Today I got up, got dressed, looked at myself in the mirror and thought: damn, you look good. Well, you can call me later, I'm not going to deny it. But I looked at myself objectively and thought: you're on it, you're really on it. And I started asking myself: what does being on it mean? What does dressing well mean?

I've thought about this for years and paid attention to what dressing well is and how to do it. I think dressing well is important and people underestimate it, confusing it with superficiality. A few years ago, very rarely did I have that feeling of "yes, you're dressed exactly as you should be, everything lines up." Over the years I've refined it. I've found what you might call my style. But what is style?

There's a Bukowski poem called Style, in which he talks about what style is. He talks about how very few people have style. That style is something very rare. He talks about how even opening a can of sardines you can do with style. He talks about how dogs have little style, though some of them do. Cats have more style. And he ends with a line about femininity and style.

And it's true. There are people who, no matter how they're dressed, have style in the way they move, the way they speak, the way they look at things, the way they relate to the world. What is that spark we can't quite identify but we know is there? How much time in your day do you spend thinking about style? Do you ever think about it? How am I moving? How am I dressing? Why?

Because dressing well is not trivial. It has a lot to do with our interpretation of beauty. Why are some things beautiful and others aren't? These are questions that might seem banal because they're so deeply rooted in our psyche that asking them sometimes feels trivial. But no — it's not obvious. Why does this combination of colors work? Why doesn't that one? Just like music: why is this particular set of sounds pleasurable and that one is noise? Both are acoustic vibrations, both can have a pattern. Yet some are harmonized. It's not easy to identify what beauty is or where it comes from. My sense is that it has an evolutionary-psychological component — understanding psychological and evolutionary to include culture and how the history of humanity has led to generating what we value and what we don't.

So, how do you dress well? How do you have style? And why does it matter?

I think dressing well is a way of demonstrating your value and worth to yourself. It has many components. First: it's not easy. And second: you have to practice it. Style takes time to find. One of its components is definitely the psychological influence that dressing well has on you yourself. Not just on others. What do you feel when you're well dressed? Generally you feel like you can take on the world. You feel perfectly aligned with what you want to be and communicate.

And this is interesting because dressing well isn't just something aesthetically pleasing. It's a communication. When you dress a certain way, you're communicating with the world. You're communicating who I am, what I am, what my values are, what my vibe is, what I value in life. It's no coincidence that if you're punk you tend to like punk girls. Someone who dresses that way communicates certain values to you.

But it's not just a way of relating to society. It's an exploration. In exploring there are times when you're going to end up looking like a mess. But if you're communicating who you want to be, first you have to identify who am I, what values do I have, how do I want to communicate them, what kind of person do I want to be. And in figuring out how one wants to dress there are deep questions. Questions that speak to who you are. Not just who I am, but who I want to be in the future. Where I'm aiming.

Learning to dress is part of the process of growing up. It's part of figuring out who you are, who you want to be, what you're aiming toward, and how you want to relate to your environment. And people dismiss it. They look down on it. But they only dismiss it on a cognitive level. Behaviorally, nobody dismisses it. People treat you differently depending on how you're dressed.

And as I said before, this doesn't only refer to how you dress. It goes further. It refers to how you relate to your environment. How you move. How you speak. What pauses you take. How you keep your room, your home. How many times have you stopped to think about how I move? Am I moving in a clumsy, stuck way? Or in a fluid way, with confidence, with security? Where am I walking on the street? Hugging the wall? Or down the middle, wide and sure?

Personally I've found it a very enjoyable game to move through the day as fluidly as possible, as smoothly as possible. If I need to go from one place to another I don't just lunge forward. I do a small weight shift and a slight hip change so the movement is more fluid. If I need to turn, I make a small arc with my foot, slide and pivot. Small dance moves throughout the day, with something as simple as standing at the kitchen counter and turning to get the carrot from the fridge.

All of this connects to awareness. To change something, you first have to notice what you're doing. You have to identify what's good and what's bad, what's beautiful and what's ugly, and you have to identify your ideals for each of those things. The first step is to observe. That's what meditation gives you. Awareness — which, paradoxically, in many spiritual traditions is considered the supreme gift of the human being.

Style matters. And why does it matter? Because it defines who you are, who you want to be, where you're going, how you're walking the path and how you communicate that to your environment. Take care.
