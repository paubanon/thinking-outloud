Hey. I promise this video is going to be short — it's just some basic reading recommendations, the books that have influenced me most in developing how I think. They're the ones I'd recommend most broadly for changing how you think, how you see the world, and one specifically about how to be productive.

I'll start with that one actually, mainly because it was one of the first self-help books I read. I picked it as my first precisely because I thought: okay, I'll read one about productivity before the others, since if I improve my productivity I'll read the rest faster. It's called *Deep Work*, by Cal Newport. I'll leave the links in the description.

It's about the importance of concentrated work: completely isolated work, giving a hundred percent, fully immersed in what you're doing. The importance of this at the level of productivity — output per hours invested — is staggering. What you can do if you set all distractions aside, and when I say all I mean all: no family around, no people, no phone, no computer, just you and what you have to do. You do it as fast as possible and as well as possible. It wraps you up a hundred percent. And the thing is, if you're completely in it, time stops passing. Suddenly you look up and it's dark, but you've been so focused that you've been able to create a ton of connections and relationships between different elements that you couldn't have seen any other way, or at least not in that amount of time, because you would have been distracted. And every time you get distracted, those logical threads are cut. It's part of why I have such a strong dislike for social media and current media in general: the interrupting.

On the other hand, concentrated work is also important for the satisfaction it brings. When you've given everything to what you're doing, without distractions, a hundred percent — whether it's training, meditating, studying, working, whatever — you have an incredible sense of fulfillment. In the book he compares it to the feeling of craft work, doing carpentry for example: since you're occupied, you don't have the option to check your phone, you're in it, and that produces a lot of personal satisfaction. The feeling of saying: today I gave everything. Maybe I didn't finish everything, which is rare because you're incredibly productive, but you gave it everything. Whereas if you're constantly interrupting yourself, at the end of the day you have an empty feeling: I did things but I don't know, I'm not satisfied. Not with the potential I could have had.

It's how I structure my time, and it's thanks to that book that I've been able to do the master's, train two hours a day, eat well, cook, and sleep eight or nine hours a day. Without that book I wouldn't have been able to do all of that.

The next one is something I think is a fundamental pillar. It's a masterpiece by Carl Sagan: *The Demon-Haunted World: Science as a Candle in the Dark*. It's a bit long and dense, and it's not easy to get a hold of — I think there's a PDF floating around, or they've reprinted it recently. It's a genuine masterpiece on critical thinking, on analysis, and on how to develop filters for discerning what's acceptable and what's not. On how Occam's razor is one of the strongest arguments you can wield — that ultimately the simplest arguments can be the most accurate. It makes you very aware of human fallibility, of our capacity to deceive ourselves and believe what's comforting to us. But what comforts us isn't necessarily the truth.

It goes through what was very fashionable at the time: UFO sightings, homeopathic medicine, mediums. And it dismisses them with logical arguments. It shows you what a line of reasoning looks like in a scientist's head: "Well, it could be, yes. But this other option requires much less artifice, and faced with two options where one is highly extravagant and you have almost nothing to support it, while the other might be emotionally less satisfying but objectively more likely, the normal thing is for it to be the second one." It's a very good way of cutting through the bullshit. Could this pill with 0.001% of a semi-active compound magically cure you? Yes, it's possible. But most probably not.

Another one I recommend unreservedly, without a doubt, is *Antifragile*, by Nassim Taleb. Also a dense book, it requires effort — but that's what *Deep Work* is for, to put in concentrated hours on it. If you're debating whether to get it in English or Spanish, in Spanish it's a bit complicated and in English it's also somewhat demanding. It's the book of life, I can't add anything. It has so many topics, treats them so well, in such an honest and brutal way, it opens your mind so much that I can only put it on this list. I have it here and as you can see it's full of bookmarks. While reading it I kept thinking: this is important. This too. This too.

In a second tier of priority but very related to the first, there's one I'm currently reading: *The Shallows*, about how the internet affects the plasticity of our brain and prevents us from concentrating. The fact that the internet is scattered information, everything demanding attention at the same time, prevents us from sitting down to do serious work. And that doesn't just make us less productive — it also makes us feel terrible. Basically: you get used to reading snippets of information from here and there, and you unlearn how to sit down, plant yourself in a chair, and do your work.

Also related to media, a book called *Reclaiming Conversation* — in Spanish I think it's *En defensa de la conversación* — by Sherry Turkle, about how media influences our personal relationships. I'm a big fan of investigating media because it's burst into our lives in a brutal way, it's modified our behavioral patterns, and while it has good things, it has very bad things that I think easily lead to depression, addiction, and fake relationships.

This book talks about the importance of in-person communication, about the difference between a conversation through social media and a face-to-face conversation. So much is lost: the classic "you can't pick up on irony through WhatsApp," of course you can't. You don't have tone of voice, you don't have looks, you don't have silences, the other person's own smell communicates things to you, their position in space, their gestures. None of that exists. And on top of that, if you add the fact that you can stop and think your response through as much as you want, it makes communication something artificial, not real.

I know a lot of people I get along great with in person, some of my best friends, who I never talk to on social media, ever. Even though I'm in another country. But when I come back home it's as if I never left. Because in the end what matters is how a person is face to face, in reality. We're getting ourselves into a situation with all this communication through networks where personal contact even starts to frighten us. We're scared to do an in-person interview, scared to meet someone we don't know for a coffee. "We'll talk on WhatsApp and get together another time." And I think that's a problem, because humans are very social and the feeling of loneliness and lack of connection with other human beings is made much worse by these platforms.

And well, if I have to recommend a fantasy book, I'd probably recommend *Harry Potter*. I spent years saying I didn't like it, but I picked it up in my third or fourth year of university and devoured it like pure heroin. It's wonderful.

In summary: *Deep Work* by Cal Newport, *The Demon-Haunted World* by Carl Sagan, *Antifragile* by Nassim Taleb, and in a second tier of preference *The Shallows* and *Reclaiming Conversation*. I hope it's useful, and if you read any of them in a given month, let me know and we can talk about it.
