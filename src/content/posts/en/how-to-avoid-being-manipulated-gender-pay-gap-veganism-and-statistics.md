I want to do a little impromptu live to comment on the stories I posted. So if you haven't seen them, I'd recommend doing that before watching this video, otherwise you'll be a little lost.

First, I'd like to point out — because it never hurts — that I believe people should be treated as people, regardless of what group they belong to. That means everyone has the same rights and opportunities to do absolutely whatever they want with their lives. That said, I also believe there are certain biological differences between different people. That makes me less capable than certain people in certain areas. And when you compute that at scale, you see there are differences between the sexes. For example, women have less muscle mass than men. That doesn't mean there aren't strong women; it means statistically they have less muscle mass and lower physical capacity than men. But it also indicates, for example, that women are better at empathizing and at social relationships.

Similarly, you only have to look at the Olympics to see how athletics is dominated precisely by Kenyans, partly for biological reasons: they have longer tendons and a better capacity to store elastic energy, and it seems, partly for cultural reasons, they're capable of enduring enormous amounts of pain.

So I made this distinction acknowledging the equality of people, not of the social group you belong to, and I think it's important to clarify that I strongly support equality of opportunity for people. Equality of opportunity does not mean equality of outcome. What does that mean? That every person should have the freedom to choose whatever career they want in life. But that doesn't mean we're going to find completely equal representation in every field. That is, we can't take any random job and expect to find a 50-50 split between men and women.

Studies support this. There's a Norwegian documentary called The Norwegian Paradox, it's on Vimeo, where they analyze all of this. And what seems to have been found is that when you build a completely equal society, like the Scandinavian countries, what you get is that the biological differences are much more pronounced. That is, when you completely remove any social and cultural bias there might be, the differences between men and women become more pronounced. There are far more men in industry and far more women in the social professions.

And it's an important distinction that this is what happens — it's not what I want to believe. What I want to believe right now doesn't matter. What we're talking about is science where you do something and you measure it. And if reality isn't how you'd like it to be, tough luck. It's reality. You measure it and it is what it is. It's on you to change your beliefs. The theoretical framework you have doesn't matter. If you measure it and it doesn't hold up, your theoretical framework is wrong. You pick it up and throw it out.

What I actually wanted to talk about in this live is statistics. It's something I see very commonly today and that drives me crazy. Statistics is not a science. It's an art. Statistics means taking enormous amounts of data and seeing how they relate to each other. To see how they relate, you have to manipulate the data. But on top of that you have to have chosen the data. And you have to have had a preconceived idea of what you're looking for. And all of that massively biases the results. Biases them to the point that in statistics you can find whatever you want to find.

That is, you sit down, add a little touch here, change these parameters, decide to include these but not those, and out comes the statistic you want. So it's a tendency that's being heavily exploited to disguise opinions as science. And it shouldn't be that way, because science is science. And sometimes science is very hard. And sometimes it's so hard to do good science that we don't know how to do it well. This is the example I keep seeing in nutrition and in today's left-wing feminist groups, which say: the statistics show that women earn 30% less. And you say: which statistics are those? How did you do them? What did you take into account?

If you take all women, take all men, calculate what each group earns on average, and take a mean, yes, you do get that women earn less. But it's a poorly done statistic in that you haven't accounted for many factors that might correct for that. If you account for the differences in career choices between men and women, you realize there's a biological tendency: men are more oriented toward things and women toward people. And it turns out that the industry dedicated to things — engineering, oil, etc. — is the labor sector that produces the most money. In large part because you can generate enormous amounts of material or money without limit, whereas in social work you can't. You can build a factory that produces a thousand cars a day, but if you work with people you can't treat a thousand people a day.

So it seems that with this, statistics — by leaving many parameters in or out — you can get whatever you want. The fact that someone tells you "the statistics say this" is being presented to you as science. And it's not, because these are the kinds of cases where we don't know how to do science well. Because it's very hard. There are an enormous number of factors. And sometimes you leave them out not out of malice, you leave them out out of ignorance. It's not the same to do statistics on the data a detector produces when you collide particles as to do statistics on social sciences where there are an enormous number of parameters.

Same goes for nutrition. What's done mostly is making correlations. Look, if someone smokes, it increases their probability of getting cancer. If they take creatine, it seems to improve their athletic performance. There are correlations and there are correlations. There was a funny one that said there's a correlation between global temperature and the number of pirates that exist. And if you actually do the statistics, there is a correlation. But it's a correlation that makes no sense.

To do good statistics, you have to go in without preconceived ideas. You shouldn't know what you're going to find. You have to take all the data you can, accounting for everything you possibly can, touching it as little as possible. You can't do statistics looking for something because you'll find it. And you can't do statistics trying to defend your moral views. For example: you can't do statistics saying "let's see if meat causes cancer," because you'll find it. I can argue to you that yes, meat is in fact more anabolic than lettuce and therefore will tend to promote the growth of almost any tissue, including cancerous ones. But what meat are you taking? What individuals? What population? A more honest approach would be to say: let's see if this specific type of meat has any kind of correlation with cancer in this specific population. Then you get statistics that are valid for that population.

Very large claims are being made on very sensitive topics that can have a major influence on the health of the population, and claims are being thrown around that shouldn't be made.

What I partly wanted to do was clarify this point and, above all, urge those of you lone wolves who watch these videos to be very, very conscious of: where did this data come from? Who processed it? What economic or moral interests were behind it? What population was it done on? What did it include? What did it leave out? When you start questioning all of that, you realize you can't just swallow "the statistics say X."
