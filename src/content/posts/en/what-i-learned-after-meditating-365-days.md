Good morning everyone, seven thirty in the morning. The sun is starting to come up over the dry lands. And my circadian rhythm has me waking up earlier and earlier. Anyway. Today I wanted to talk to you about meditation. I already have a video on the subject that I'll link here. It's called, if I remember right, "my experience with meditation." And today I'm going to talk about what changes I've noticed after a year of meditating every single day. Absolutely every single day. Well, that's not entirely true — I missed one day, in October or November, because I was really sick, I had a fever and couldn't meditate. Every other day, without fail. I'm going to share what I've learned. Not just what I've learned rationally, but how I've changed emotionally. Which is even more important. And at the end of the video I'll talk about how I managed to stick to meditation so consistently.

The first thing you notice when you start meditating is a feeling of calm. You start to notice that it settles you, that you focus better and that your stress levels drop quite a bit. Said like that it sounds pretty generic, I know. But what you notice is that your mind stops thinking about possible futures non-stop. It stops that constant background hum of: what's going to happen in the future, what's going to happen next, I feel uncertain, I don't know what to do. And it settles into something a bit more zen, a state where you're completely at peace with yourself. And little by little you reach a point where you say: whatever happens, I'll handle it as best I can. It centers you a lot.

It's also helped me sleep better. Those nights where you lie down and your head won't stop spinning and you can't fall asleep — since I started meditating consistently, they've completely disappeared. My sleep was already very good, so I can't really say it improved the quality. But surprisingly, even on nights when I have worries, when I go to bed anxious, worried about something... I still fall into a pit of unconsciousness super fast and super deep. That's fantastic.

I've also become aware of the different effect meditation has at different times of day. For me it serves mainly three purposes.

One: productivity and concentration. When I have to do something that requires focus, and I'm coming from doing lots of things at once — answering emails, jumping between Excel and WhatsApp and this and that — doing a meditation session between that and the deep work lets me sit down and focus for two or three hours. Without meditation, I have to work really hard to push past the barrier of my brain being in multitasking mode. Meditation is like putting a period, a blank line and a new paragraph. It also works before training: if I come from work very stressed, sitting down to meditate means I can focus much more on the session.

Two: managing days of high excitement. There are days when I'm carrying incredible hype, super excited about something at work or with the thesis, to the point that it's actually annoying to be that wired. On those days, sitting down to meditate calms the waters. Especially before sleep: it brings everything down, including cortisol after a tough training session.

Three: emotional management of difficult situations. Whether it's a breakup, worries about the future, anything that's hit me hard emotionally, I've been able to sit down and say: you're sad about this. Let's sit here as long as it takes and think about it. Just about that. If you're sad, you sit down and wallow in your sadness: how sad I am, how miserable I am. And then your mind starts doing the work: absolutely miserable because I still have all my limbs, even though this person has let me down I still have twenty who believe in me, I have a home, running water, food. But it's not that you tell yourself these things. You try to push it aside and focus on how sad you are. There comes a point where you can't. And after fifteen minutes, you're done being sad. I guarantee it.

I've also learned that there are bad days. Especially if you meditate after eating, the session is very bad. Meditating on an empty stomach usually goes very well: right when you wake up, at the end of the morning, after training. Since I fast in the mornings with just a coffee, it works great for me.

It's also helped me a lot in accepting unforeseen problems. In understanding that the world doesn't have to be okay — I have to be okay. That my interpretation of the world is equal to or more important than the world itself. An example: this past Christmas, the day before catching the flight back to Spain they cancelled it. And I surprised myself because it didn't affect me emotionally at all. That after three months here, in the dark, at Christmas, not being able to go home... it made absolutely no difference to me. I saw the message and said: what a pain. What do I do? I spent ten minutes thinking and said: very simple. You look for another flight at a reasonable price. If there is one, you buy it. If not, you stay at your place. And if you can't stay at your place, you ask your girlfriend if you can go with her to Italy to spend Christmas with her family. And it's fine. I knew that whatever happened, I was going to be okay. And that surprised me. In the end I found another flight and left three days later. But that initial moment that I know would have caused me frustration... it wasn't there.

Another thing you learn is to separate yourself from your feelings. Generally we operate under the dynamic of: we are what we feel. I am angry, I am happy, I am frustrated. When you meditate, you learn to separate yourself from that. It's no longer "I am angry," it's "I feel anger." And then you learn to immerse yourself in certain feelings and to completely withdraw your attention from others. You learn not to get swept away. When there's a situation that enrages you, you learn not to act impulsively. To override that reptilian brain and say: I'm frustrated with this person. Does it help anything to yell at them? It helps nothing. How could I approach this clearly without offending someone I care about? And you act accordingly.

But on the other hand, when you're very happy, the switch flips too. And you say: wow, how lucky I am to be able to spend two hours with this friend talking about the mysteries of life. And you bring it into your awareness and you revel in that moment. You experience it much more fully. Sometimes people say to me: but I don't want to become a cold, emotionless robot. That's a very wrong interpretation. You don't feel less since you started meditating. You're more aware of your emotions and you can act on them. At first it's very hard to think of a self separate from emotions, but it's possible. And I assure you it's wonderful.

Finally, how do you stick with it long enough? There are lots of hacks out there: anchor meditation to another routine, do it at the same time every day. While those aren't bad recommendations, they've never really worked for me. What has worked for me is convincing myself to have a total and absolute commitment to a decision. Total and absolute. What does that mean? That once I say "I'm going to meditate every day," the sky can fall: you meditate. It's priority number one.

That means sometimes meditating at three in the morning. It means you've had a crazy day, you get home at two-thirty and say: damn, I haven't meditated today. And you sit down and do it. What you learn from that night is: next time you do it earlier. It means meditating with people around you. It means sometimes you've been surrounded by your family all day and at some point in the car you say: I'm going to do my meditation now, don't talk to me for ten minutes. And you do it. It means sometimes arriving late to meet up with friends because you meditated. That hundred percent commitment is a switch. It's on or off. It's not a spectrum. Anything below a hundred percent is not committing. And a hundred is a yes. Period.

An app called Habit Bull Tracker also helped me a lot — you put in habits and check them off. Having something as stupid as having to press a little button, seeing the streak and the percentage... it's helped me a lot.

What you learn when you commit a hundred percent and meditate, it's not only that you're meditating. You're practicing your hundred-percent-committed self. The self that said a month and a half ago it was going to meditate every day and is still doing it. That self transfers to other things. It's the self that says: I'm cutting out sugar. And cuts it out a hundred percent, radical, absolute. Sugar doesn't exist. There isn't a day where you can indulge. It's binary: yes or no. And that's the self you want to practice.

I've also learned that there are bad sessions, no matter how experienced you are. There are days where you sit down, you know it's going to be terrible, it is terrible, your mind is completely wandering the whole time. And that's fine. The important thing is to do it. Is to sit down.

As for apps: of everything I've found out there, most of them seem pretty bad to me. With little gong sounds, water sounds, birdsong through a speaker, which is terrible because it's not real. Meditating surrounded by real nature is fantastic: the birds, the sound of water, the wind in the trees put you into a zen state very fast. But through a speaker, no. The ones I have liked are Headspace, Waking Up, and Seven Mind. Headspace was one of the first I used, I really like it, it helps you concentrate and demystifies meditation. Waking Up is my favorite by far: Sam Harris does great work presenting different techniques, both for beginners and advanced practitioners. I've done the fifty-day course and it's fantastic. Seven Mind I discovered recently and it's very good, but a bit more advanced: I wouldn't recommend it for beginners because it goes deeper into topics faster than the others.
