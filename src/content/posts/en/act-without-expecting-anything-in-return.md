Good morning. Today I wanted to talk to you about love, relationships, and attachment. Specifically about attachment to outcome.

One of the reasons I think I handle working under pressure well, or demanding a lot from myself, something meditation has taught me, is not worrying about the result. It's living by pointing in a direction and letting myself be carried along. Trying to get there, but if we get there, great; if we don't, that's fine too. As long as I'm at peace with how I've acted, the result doesn't concern me, since I can't control it.

With training, for example, it shows very clearly: not being constantly obsessed with hitting that mark, that skill. Just doing it. Detach from the result and walk the path.

The same goes for big projects, like my thesis right now. I've discovered, through the Learning How to Learn course on Coursera, that I work much better if I don't set daily output goals for writing. Because writing is a gargantuan task when you have to write a lot. There are better days and worse days, and you can't always control which are which. If you set yourself a goal of: I have to write a chapter, there are days when that's not going to happen. And it's not in your control. There are days you've slept better, there are chapters that will get stuck, there are chapters where the act of writing them makes you realize you didn't understand them as well as you thought and you'll have to rethink and rewrite them. I've learned that if I detach from the result and instead of working by I have to do a chapter I say: I'm going to sit down and write, every day... some days I'll write four hours, some days two, some days ten pages, some days one that I'll rewrite a hundred times. Doesn't matter. It's part of the path. What matters is to keep walking it.

When I did the Camino de Santiago, you're not worried about reaching the next village. You want to get there, but you don't have a target; you just walk the path. And paradoxically, walking the path takes you to your goal, without the anxiety of achieving a milestone every day. One of the things I loved most was walking knowing I was completely free. That if one day I didn't feel like it, I could stay in the previous village. That if one day I felt like it, I could go to the next one. That I could arrive at three or at seven. I just had to start walking. And you don't worry about whether you'll get there the moment you start. You just walk. It's one step after the other. But you have to forget about the result. You have to detach completely from the idea that your goal is Santiago. Because if you hold that goal, every day you don't get there will be a bad day.

I'm smiling because I'm making an analogy with the Camino de Santiago, when this concept comes from Taoism, which means the way.

It's the same with goals in life. You have to have a goal as a direction, an orientation. You don't have to feel attached to that goal. I've been working to get a planche for many years, I started in my first year of university. If I'd been emotionally anchored to that goal, I never would have made it this far. Every day I didn't progress toward it would have been a bad day. You have to detach from the goal, understand it as a direction. And if it doesn't happen, because there are things we can't control, that's fine. You can let it go.

If the stars align to screw me over and make it impossible to finish my master's thesis, I'll have to accept it. What matters to me is having walked the path. Knowing I gave everything I had. That I did what I had to do. I did it well. I'm proud of my work. And if circumstances align to keep me from finishing something... accepting it.

This brings me to the topic of love. Which is what I actually wanted to talk about today. And relationships. And how this idea of detaching from the result applies.

It's going to sound very hippie. But it's very striking that when different philosophies converge on the same concept, explained with a different name, you know there's something important there. This idea in Taoist philosophy is detaching from the result. In hippie culture it's free love. And in seduction literature it's known as the abundance mindset.

What is this abundance mindset? It means giving without expecting anything in return. It means you contribute to the world without expecting anything back. You express love toward your partner or the people around you without expecting love in return, without expecting them to say me too. You're affirming something intrinsic to your person and you detach from everything else. This means people don't feel pressured to express or communicate something. Because we have quite a strong aversion to doing things we're told we have to do. We feel like it's a trap, like it's not our own decision. If you do something because you have to do it, even if you want to do it, your brain interprets that you're doing it because you have to. And it doesn't like that at all.

The same happens when you do work you love but it's your job. Suddenly you're questioning whether you're doing it because you love it or because you have to. And that can lead to existential crises.

What I wanted to tell you is that when you take this attitude of giving without expecting anything in return, you're taking exactly the position of someone who has no need. Someone secure in themselves, in their actions, in their thinking, who only wants to share, wants to give. And we really like people who do that. We want to be around them.

And it's very hard. But when you do it, the first thing you'll notice is that you feel good. You miss wanting something in return a little bit, but you feel good. And once you're at peace with that... The typical nice guy mistake is doing something for someone expecting something back, now or in the future. It's a kind of emotional blackmail. If you don't give me something in return, I'm not going to express my love for you again. And obviously we don't feel comfortable when we're being blackmailed, because we want the freedom to express something if we want to, and not express it if we don't.

The idea of free love, conceptually, setting aside the polyamory part: I feel a very deep love for any person. I think people are good and that the world is very hard, and that makes them make bad decisions. But in general I think having a predisposition to wish people well is positive. And ironically, the less you expect, the more you receive.

When you can't convince someone, the person convinces themselves. However much you think you know something that's going to be very useful to that person, you won't be able to convince them. You have to be yourself, be whole, and give. When people ask you, you answer. But you don't try to funnel it in. And above all, you don't do it expecting something in return. You give. You have to become a source of goodness and light that radiates regardless of the outcome. And that will bring you a lot of calm and peace within yourself. It will improve your relationships a lot. And it will make you more attractive, more charismatic.

It can show up in something as simple as sending a message on Telegram or WhatsApp without expecting anything back. Without expecting a reply. I wanted to share this with you. If you read it and feel like replying, great. If not, I'm at peace. And in the world of seduction, when you do this, you become very attractive. When you invite someone to go out because you feel like getting to know that person, not expecting them to give you their number afterward or go back to their place. You do it out of pure generosity.

These thoughts come from having realized I wasn't acting the way I should. That I was expecting things in return. And it doesn't work. It doesn't work well.

So I encourage you to do some introspection. To think about which areas of your life you expect something back for what you give. And ask yourself if that's how you want to live it.

Take care.
