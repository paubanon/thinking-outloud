Hey everyone, good morning. Today I wanted to talk to you about knowledge and how to categorize and classify it.

On this channel I've talked before about the existence of unknown knowledge and the difference between unknown unknowns and known unknowns. Today I wanted to talk about the difference between two types of what we already know. It's an idea that came to me this morning on a walk, and it has its roots in a reflection on the practice of physical exercise.

The idea is that what we know can be classified into what we know conceptually and what we know in an integrated or practical way.

But first a brief overview of all knowledge. The unknown unknown is what we don't know that we don't know. It's the ideas we haven't had, the knowledge that hasn't reached us and that we don't even know exists for us. Whereas the known unknown is what we know we don't know. I know I don't know 19th century history. However, I don't know what ideas might occur to me in the future. We don't know what new revolutionary inventions might appear. That's part of the unknown unknown.

The categorization of what we know is a bit more fine-grained. We can divide it between theoretical knowledge and practical knowledge, or conceptual and integrated knowledge. Theoretical knowledge refers to the theoretical acquisition of knowledge: the study of a subject or a gesture or anything in the abstract. While integrated knowledge refers to the practical application of this theoretical knowledge.

For example: knowing a recipe doesn't mean you know how to make the recipe well. Only through actually preparing the recipe will you learn how it's done, and it'll become deeply part of you. This is because in the application, in the step from theoretical knowledge to integrated knowledge, there are a myriad of details and nuances that are indescribable at the theoretical level and can only be acquired through practical application.

Another example: knowing how to do an acrobatic movement doesn't mean you know how to do it. For those of us who have done any sport of that kind, this is crystal clear. You can watch all the tutorials you want. Knowing the theory has nothing to do with being able to execute it. In the execution you learn to feel how your body moves through the air, you have to learn to manage the momentum, you have to feel the movement. It's much richer.

Another example might be knowing what meditation is and how it's done. That doesn't exclude the fact that you have to do it to know it firsthand and feel it. I can argue about it all I want. But actually doing it is different, it takes on many nuances.

Another very common one: having studied the benefits of a diet or fasting, knowing what happens when you apply it. That's still completely different from when you actually apply it, when you feel it in your own body.

And at the academic level: knowing how to solve a problem doesn't mean you know how to solve the problem. This is very common with physics, math, or engineering students. You say: yeah, in this problem you do this, this, and this and it's solved. Okay, but you have to do it. And in the process of doing it many nuances will come up, many particular features of the problem that will trip you up. How do you solve this specific integral? What tools do you need here? That's part of integrated knowledge.

The point I want to make is that this integrated knowledge will always be far superior to purely theoretical knowledge. Because it will be embedded in you. Something you know in a very deep, very detailed way, with many nuances, in a rich and complex way. It hasn't just been a thought experiment — you understand perfectly the small details.

For example, with the recipe: a recipe can tell you the steps to follow, but it's impossible to transmit in it the smell each ingredient needs to have in order to be at exactly the right point. You can't include in a recipe the nuance that this vegetable is fresher than that one, that this one has more flavor. You can't include the particularities of your own oven, the strength of your burners, the material of the pan — there are materials that caramelize less. It's not the same to put an ingredient straight from the fridge as at room temperature.

And the same applies to any discipline worth mastering. There comes a point where you have to stop reading about it and start doing it. Because knowledge you haven't integrated through practice is, at best, incomplete knowledge.
