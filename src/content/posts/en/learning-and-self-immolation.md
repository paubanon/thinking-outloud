I want to talk about something I call self-immolation in the context of learning. Not in the literal sense, but in the sense of destroying a previous version of yourself to make way for a new one.

Real learning, the kind that changes how you think or how you act, is not comfortable. It's not adding information to a container that stays the same. It's more like the opposite: it's undoing something you thought you knew, recognizing that you were wrong, and building from there.

And that hurts a little. Or hurts quite a bit, depending on how central the belief is that you have to give up.

Think about when you learn something that contradicts a belief you held. There's a moment of resistance, of discomfort, of not wanting to accept it. Because accepting it means acknowledging that you were wrong. That the previous version of you, the one who held that belief, was less right.

And there are people who can't do that. Who prefer to ignore the new information rather than revise their worldview. Those people stop learning.

People who learn well have a different relationship with being wrong. They see it as useful information, not as a threat to their identity. "I was wrong about this" simply becomes "now I know more than before."

This requires letting go of ego around knowledge. Not clinging to your ideas as if they were part of you. Being willing to say "I don't know," "I was wrong," "I had this wrong." And doing it lightly, without drama.

The metaphorical self-immolation I'm talking about is exactly that: the voluntary death of a version of yourself that no longer serves. To grow, you have to be willing to stop being who you were.

And there's something freeing about that, if you look at it right. You don't have to defend every idea you held before. You can change. You can improve. You're a work in progress, not a finished product.
