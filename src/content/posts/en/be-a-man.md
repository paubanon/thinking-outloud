Good morning. I think this is an important video, honestly.

Right now, as you can see, I'm in Spain. And you generally know that when I'm in Spain I'm pretty comfortable and I like staying here. But you also know, if you've been following the channel, that going back to Germany usually feels like a drag, and that when I get there I feel very at home and really like the responsibilities I take on by being there.

This time has been different. I've moved my return up. I was going to leave on May 9th, which is a month from now. I've been here two weeks already and I've decided to change it. Change it to Tuesday, which is in five or six days. And I've decided to do it because I've realized that here, even though I'm fine, I'm not the person I should be.

It's because here I'm in Neverland. This is the town where I grew up. And being here carries echoes of that. It evokes that person in my subconscious. Not someone who does nothing, but someone who doesn't have to take care of their own chores, who doesn't have to struggle to earn a living, because you're thinking of mom and dad taking care of you. Someone less hardened. So here I'm not productive. And I become a person I don't want to be.

My real self, the self I want to feed, is the one in Germany, doing a master's degree, living alone, being very happy and pushing the project forward at Show Movers. So I've decided that, given the lack of responsibility I can take on from here, I'm going to go back and be the person I want to be. After a couple weeks of vacation you're good, go back and make your life count for something.

And that has made me reflect a lot on what I do, how I do it, why, my maturity.

It's come together with several turning points that have made me think a lot about myself and my character. One of them is the need I had this past term to sit down with my roommate and talk about serious things, because his attitude wasn't right for the apartment to work well. The decision to determine that it was that way, the decision that I don't want that kind of attitude and me taking responsibility for it, picking him up, sitting him down at the kitchen table and saying: we need to talk. This was really hard for me to do, but afterward I felt very good, I felt very adult. It's a good feeling.

Also the decision to go back to Germany. Breaking up with a girl I was seeing, and not sinking into misery, but redirecting that discomfort into productivity and saying: you're worth a lot, keep moving forward. And along with the death of my uncle's mother: I was training when she died, my parents went to the funeral home and I didn't find out about any of it until I got home and my sister told me. The funeral home is in Elda, so I couldn't go, and I was faced with a dilemma. Call or not call. Part of me was saying: he's your uncle, you love him a lot, but you're the youngest in the family, what are you doing calling? You've never called anyone, let alone someone in your family, to offer condolences. And letting them lean on you, which is what condolences are — it's saying: if you need me, I'm here.

And the other option was to call. And it was a harder option than it sounds, because in the family I've always been the kid. They've watched me grow up since I was little and for them I'm still the kid, more filled out and more mature, but still the kid of the family. So it was an opportunity to show them that I'm no longer a kid, that I'm an adult and responsible person, and that I'm capable of carrying that weight, that responsibility.

So, as soon as I found out and thought about all of it, I picked up and called my uncle, and I told him I'm so sorry, stay strong, if you need anything call me. And I think it was strange, both for him and for me, for someone in their mid-twenties to call someone in their sixties and say: if you need anything, I'm here. Because the roles are usually reversed. But I think it's a sign of maturity and composure and robustness. And I think it's an important step, one that going forward is going to change how they see me.

And all of this together — going back to Germany, killing dragons daily because I'm making a very strong emphasis on: every time something scares you, face it. Every time you dread calling someone, call them. Every time you want to avoid a conversation because it might be difficult, have it. Doing this daily. And going back to Germany out of the responsibility of saying: stop wasting your time. You're not at an age where you should be wasting time. Waste your time when you're eighty-nine years old and can't move anymore. Not now.

And take responsibility also because in a few years your sister will be going to high school and there'll be financial things to deal with. You have to push hard at your work, at the master's, so that in a year or so you're financially independent and can help your family. Putting all that weight on your shoulders is sometimes hard, I won't lie to you. There are times I've woken up and said: God, this crown is heavy. But it's gratifying because you know it's a sacrifice you make willingly and because you believe it's worth it.

So it's a very big step. And what it's made me think about is at what point a person becomes an adult, at what moment a person is a man of substance. And the only conclusion I've been able to reach is: when you think this way, when you behave this way, when you take responsibility for the people you love, when you do it voluntarily and make an effort to make tomorrow a better place for yourself, for the world, and for the people around you. When you do all of that, it doesn't matter how you feel inside. You are a man.

And you feel like a fraud. Impostor syndrome hits you hard. And the only thing I've been able to reach is that maybe we all feel a little like that inside. Maybe all the adults we idealized as children as secure, strong people who face life like it's nothing — maybe that's not real. Maybe we all have fears inside. Maybe we all feel terrified of letting down the people we love.

And when you realize that, it changes your perception of what it means to be an adult. And when your perception of what it means to be an adult changes, you realize that maybe you already are one. That you can't live in Neverland. That you have to make decisions in your life, some harder than others, but you have to make them. And you have to do it.

And I think it's a great step, realizing this, for a person's maturity. And starting to kill dragons as you find them, because you can't let them grow. Take care.
