Good morning everyone. Today I'm bringing you a slightly unusual video because I'm not going to cover a topic from start to finish — it's more of an extra, an appendix, an addition to a conversation I had yesterday with a couple of friends. At one point I didn't know how to answer or properly connect my response, and after sleeping on it and rethinking it this morning over coffee, I think I have a satisfying and interesting answer. And since the answer is interesting, I decided to share it with you instead of sending them a 15-minute voice message.

The conversation was about whether free will exists, freedom of choice, whether you're completely free to make a decision when you make it. It came up from Robert Sapolsky's book, Behave, or Compórtate, I think is the Spanish translation. The point of the book is that by showing the influence of biology on different scenarios and aspects of your life, one of the conclusions you draw is that freedom, free will, all points to it not existing.

The conversation flowed from there to the justice system, what that implies for personal responsibility, and so on. And at a certain point my friend David asked me: last week we were talking about cases of depression and you told me you believed the best thing someone can do is take ownership of it and say "no, I'm going to change and fix this." Take ownership of the problem and deal with it. How does that fit with the idea that free will doesn't exist? I gave an answer but not a fully satisfying one. I'm going to connect that answer now.

I'm not going to tackle the whole topic of free will because it's enormous, very complicated, and I don't have my position perfectly worked out or all the gaps covered enough to stake it out in a video. But, as always, making these videos helps me express myself better.

The idea is: if free will doesn't exist, when someone has a depressive episode, is that person responsible for what happened to them or not?

Robert Sapolsky's point is no. A person is not responsible for a depression that happens, given that a depressive episode ends up being the consequence of the type of receptors you have, the neurotransmitters, how much dopamine you secrete, how your serotonin reuptake pump works, plus genetic factors, plus how you were treated as a child, plus your diet, plus your amount of sleep, plus past experiences. Sapolsky's point is that you can't escape biology, you are a biological system, and the brain is exactly as biological as a leg. It's just much more complicated and our predictive capabilities are lower. If you break a leg you know you're going to limp. If you have brain damage, we don't know exactly what the consequence will be.

You can't simply tell someone with depression "stop being depressed, get up, snap out of it and get going." Sapolsky shows that with certain medication the person stops being depressed. It's a completely biological process. Just as you can't tell someone "hurry up and fix your leg, it's fine already."

And I completely agree with the idea that everything is biology, that you can't escape your biology and that your behavior, as much as we feel otherwise, is the product of the amount of hormones you have, your neurotransmitters, your entire biological system. I don't dispute that.

But here's the point I couldn't articulate yesterday: even if free will doesn't exist in the strict philosophical sense, accepting the narrative of "I'm not responsible for anything" has very concrete and very real consequences in your life. And those consequences are worse. The belief system you adopt literally modifies the biological system you are. If you believe you have agency, if you believe you can change, that belief activates different biological mechanisms. It secretes different hormones. It generates different behaviors. And those behaviors produce different results in the world.

It's not a contradiction. It's that the narrative of personal responsibility is, regardless of whether free will exists philosophically, the model that produces better results in practice. Not because it's metaphysical truth, but because it works. Because by adopting it, the biological system you are shifts into problem-solving mode instead of victimhood mode.

And besides: nobody actually lives as if they have no free will. Nobody wakes up in the morning and says "I'm not going to have breakfast because free will doesn't exist." Everyone acts as if they're making decisions. Because in practical terms, they are.

That's what I couldn't say yesterday.
