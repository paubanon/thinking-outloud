I want to talk about time. About how we manage it and, more importantly, how we invest it.

Time management is a topic that gets talked about a lot, but I think it's often approached wrong. It's not just about being more efficient, doing more things in less time. It's about doing the right things.

There's a distinction I find fundamental and that isn't always made: the difference between urgent activities and important activities. The urgent is what demands your attention right now: a notification, a message, a call. The important is what has a real impact on your life in the long run: learning something new, working on your projects, taking care of your relationships, exercising.

The problem is that the urgent tends to crowd out the important. If you don't have a system to protect the time you dedicate to what's important, the urgent is going to eat it all up.

A way of thinking about this that has been useful to me is the idea of investment. You don't spend time, you invest it. Some activities have a high return: learning a skill, building something, developing relationships. Others have a low or negative return: scrolling through social media, watching television passively for hours.

This doesn't mean all time has to be productive in the narrow sense of the word. Genuine rest, play, social relationships: all of that is an investment too. What isn't an investment is the time you spend anesthetized, avoiding thinking, filling the void with noise.

Another aspect I find important is energy. It's not enough to have time, you need energy to use it well. A two-hour block with a fresh mind is worth much more than four hours dragging yourself along because you haven't slept well.

That's why sleep, exercise and nutrition are not extras you add when you have time. They're the foundation on which everything else rests.

And finally: learn to say no. Every time you say yes to something, you're saying no to something else. Time is finite. Protect yours more carefully than you'd protect money.
