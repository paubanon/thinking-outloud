Our way of interacting with the world is through goals. We have to have them, and according to those goals we behave, and according to how we behave we interpret. The problem is this: achieving a goal you value, what we'll call success, is an extremely narrow line within the vastness of possibilities. And it's subjective. That place only has value when you give it value. That same goal, for another person, may have no importance. But the moment you set it as your goal, is when you're able to move toward it.

Now, Jens's story. Why did I bring up Jens? Because the problem with a lot of people is not identifying goals. And they do this for two reasons. One is that identifying goals is hard. If you don't set any goal, you can say: oh well, it doesn't matter, you weren't really trying. Then you won't have the frustration of failing. But most likely you won't achieve your goal either.

Reaching your goal is hard. It requires focus, effort and planning. Your goal is usually something so precise that, unless you consciously aim toward it and trace a route map, the chance of doing random things and ending up there is practically zero.

To reach your goal you have to make a plan. And this is the same for training as it is for studies, as it is for personal development, as it is for cooking skills, as it is for reading habits, as it is for meditating. Any goal you set has to come with a roadmap.

What do you need to do to achieve a goal — whether it's the press to handstand, whether it's finishing a master's, whatever it is? First, identify that you want to value that goal. Second, calculate how much work achieving it will require. And third, consider how many things you're willing to not do to achieve it. Time is limited and you can't do everything. Be aware of what you're not going to do, because there's a lot of disappointment and failure due to people orienting themselves toward a goal, then realizing they're leaving other things behind, wanting to do those other things too, abandoning their goal, and ending up doing nothing. And ending up frustrated because they obviously didn't achieve anything.

The method for creating the roadmap is this: you start from very large time scales down to very small ones. Say you have a goal a year out. What should the optimal progression be each quarter so you'd be happy with how close you're getting to the goal? Okay. So if this is my goal, in the first quarter I should have done this. Good. Now, first quarter: what should I do each month to achieve the quarterly goal? Okay, in this first month I should focus on this, in the second on that. Perfect. Within this first month, how should I divide up the weeks? Okay, well this first week it would be good to do this, week two this other thing. Perfect. Within this week, how should I structure the days so they align with the weekly goal, which aligns with the monthly goal, which aligns with the quarterly goal, which aligns with the overall goal for the year?

Good. You've just defined what you have to do each day to get closer to your goal. That's called a roadmap. And it's essential for anything you want to achieve.

A lot of people fail from lack of planning. Once you've done this for one of your goals, take another one in a different area of your life, and another in another area.
