Good morning. It's 7:30, 8 in the morning. I was about to start working on the Enso Movers programs and I started thinking about a program I did last night to get a bit ahead. And inspiration hit me and I realized there's a big difference. It's not two types of clients — it's two types of attitude in the people who work with us. And not just two types of people: the same person can have this attitude in one area and not in another. Some people hit their goals, they progress incredibly fast, and others don't. Or the same person in different areas. People who get to a certain point and stall, they're just there and there, months go by and they're still there. We offer them the same service, we come at them with the same program with even more attention trying to help them break through that plateau. And they don't break through.

I've realized the problem is intentionality, which I've talked about before on this channel. The problem isn't whether it's a good program or a bad program. The problem is how invested that person feels in that particular goal. If the goal is truly theirs, if that person says "I want to get this and I want it at full throttle," they get it. They don't just put in the hours, they don't just do the same reps and sets — they work to make every rep and every set bring them a little bit closer to that goal. Whereas someone who wants to achieve something but not really, someone who doesn't fully own that goal, puts in the same number of sets, gets just as tired, but doesn't give that extra, that small push past the limits.

Any goal you set for yourself — if it's not truly your goal, if you're not emotionally invested in achieving it, whether it's something as mundane as lifting twice your bodyweight or something as serious as finishing your degree — if you don't care, you probably won't get there. If it doesn't obsess you, if it doesn't drive you crazy every hour of the day, you won't make it. Especially the harder the goal is. Everyone can finish high school without too much effort. But how many people become professional researchers? How many become the head of a company? How many companies actually get off the ground? Very few. And a huge part of that comes down to how invested that person is in the project.

If you're a hundred percent invested in a project — whether it's starting a company, building a website, hitting a training goal, learning to cook, improving your relationship — if you're not fully committed to that goal, if it's not something that eats at you from the inside every hour of the day, it's not going to happen. Because the moment difficulties show up, you'll use them as an excuse to check out.

I see it constantly with my students. People who take on a goal — let's say a split — and want to achieve it. Some of you can't even imagine how badly they want it. They put in their training sessions, they push them to the max and concentrate all their attention on the moment, on their sensations, on how they can make micro-adjustments. They don't look at their phone because they know it'll pull them away from their goal and because being present is far more rewarding than looking at a screen. And on top of that, when they're not training, they're thinking about that goal. They do micro-visualizations throughout the day. People who've worked on handstands — if any of you are on that path, you know you spend the whole day visualizing. Sometimes in the middle of the day you just stop, you lift your arm, you play with the shoulder, the hand, the elbow, the position, and you picture yourself readjusting. It's literally obsessing over a goal. And it has to excite you — you, specifically — because it won't excite anyone else.

The same has to happen with your studies, with your career. You have to be dying for the next training session, dying for a moment to sit down and study, dying to grab your company and push it forward. Because that's the only way. The friends I have who are doing their master's and thesis — the ones getting the best results are the ones who, when they talk about their thesis, can barely contain themselves, they can't find the words for how insanely interesting the topic is. I have a friend who said to me the other day: "Dude, I've fallen down the rabbit hole. For four days all I was doing was waking up, studying until six in the morning, and sleeping." And he said: "It's just, I love it so much, I find what I'm studying so beautiful that if I let myself go, it swallows me."

That obsession can sometimes produce an existential crisis, it can drag you into a hole of nothing but that. But you need that obsession. And at the same time you need the wherewithal to put a leash on it and say: you're a very powerful horse, but I'm the one guiding you. You need a powerful horse. Because otherwise it's not even about putting in more or fewer sets. There are ways and ways to get through difficulties. There's a way to be in a relationship and say: well, there's this problem, I can't be bothered to deal with it with my girlfriend, more or less when things seem okay we say we're fine. Or there's another level of engagement, another attitude: no, things aren't quite right, there's something underneath we haven't dug up, and maybe that something is really painful, but it has to come out because that's what needs to happen. Because otherwise we're going to have the same argument a month from now. And that makes all the difference between couples who've burned the rotten wood again and again and again over many years, and couples who haven't, where the rotten wood accumulates little by little until it's a garbage heap.

I'm using exercise as an example because it's very tangible, but what matters isn't the exercise — it's the attitude behind it. It's not about whether someone gets their splits. It's the attitude of the person who goes all in.

And what's more important: this isn't something you either have or you don't. It's something you decide to do. It's true that picking up an instrument has never called to me. Never. But I know that if I picked one up and committed to it, I'd be capable of getting so invested that I'd be desperate to improve. It's an attitude. A "I need to commit to this." And I say it a lot to my clients: these aren't my goals, they're your goals. And nobody in the world cares whether you achieve your goals if you don't care. As a coach I can tell you it would be good to achieve this, that we should work on that. But it's your work, your goal. No matter how much effort I put in, it doesn't matter as much as it should matter to you. Because it's not my life. I can help you, I can inspire you, I can show you the way, I can walk alongside you. But you have to be the one who feels that fire.

If you're someone who puts in their sets and then goes to play video games, you're going to stall. The gap between ordinary people and excellent people is stark. It's those people who are willing to take every set as far as it needs to go. They can be on the exact same program as a beginner and improve three times faster. Just from the attitude. Just from that hunger to push through.

My personal example is my master's thesis. I was frustrated with it because it wasn't moving forward. I went through a period where I couldn't put in the hours I wanted and I grew to hate it. Genuinely. I don't want to work on this, I just want it off my plate. Then I had a moment of clarity and told myself: you don't hate it. You hate not making progress. And if you want to make progress, go all in. Let it consume you. Let it take root inside you and become something that eats at your head all day.

I went at it like crazy, throwing hours at it, working through all those things I used to say "man, I just don't feel like sitting down to study." I'd sit down and after twenty minutes I'd find my rhythm and suddenly three hours had passed. There were derivations I used to tell myself "those probably aren't that important" and I'd say: no, no, do it. And wow, now I get it, now I have that satisfaction. When I pushed like that for a week, two weeks of eight hours a day on the thesis, something magical happened: every moment of the day I was thinking about the thesis. Same as when you get the split, same as when I've worked hard on handstands, same as with every goal I've seriously achieved.

In two weeks the thesis advanced more than it had in four months. It sounds like magic, but it's obsession. And it makes all the difference.

There's something very well established in psychology: there's a brutal difference between facing something that scares you voluntarily versus involuntarily. Not just at the level of character — it's been shown that it changes your genetic expression. When you face something voluntarily, you change as a person. Your brain physically changes, the connections change, your hormonal balance changes, everything changes. Because you say "I have to get through this" and you dive in on purpose, with every intention of going through as much pain as necessary to come out the other side. And the people who do that get things done.

So if you see an area of your life that's stalled, think very carefully about whether you need a new program, whether you need guidance, or whether you simply need to get invested and give it what it takes. As I said, it's not something that falls from the sky — it's something you decide to do. You decide to pour your whole soul into that goal because getting hard things done is incredibly difficult, it requires enormous effort, and if your life doesn't depend on it, there's no way to get there.
