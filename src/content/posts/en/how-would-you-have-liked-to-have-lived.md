Good morning everyone. Today I grabbed this. It's my notebook of random thoughts, of random things, from June 2016 to December 2017. I was flipping through it this morning. My own thoughts surprised me. In general. And there were a lot where I said: I like this, I want to put this out there. So I'm going to read you a short excerpt.

I was putting a title on the notebook. I called it "Thoughts and notes of Pablo Bañón Pérez from January 2016 to December 2017." And then underneath I wrote: available for public reading. Because a lot of times I have these notebooks lying around the living room and when someone comes over they obviously don't feel comfortable snooping through your notes. So I want to make it clear that people can pick it up and read it. Any time a friend or my partner has picked something like that up and read it, there's been a strong connection afterward, a moment of emotional openness, and we've connected a lot. It's led to really good conversations and you feel more connected. That's what vulnerability does. Exposure. Opening up connects you with people.

It's a little like Mark Manson's way of thinking in his book Models, which I've always believed is important. Well, not always. Up until recent years, which is why I started this channel. I think it's important to put what you do out there. Always. And I think it's very hard and very embarrassing and it takes a lot, but I think that exposure, that vulnerability, knowing that people can make fun of you, is what gives it flavor. It's what makes a conversation not be bland. What makes someone interesting. What makes there be contact.

This also comes with a book I read called Originals, which talked about how original people, people who've achieved things in life, weren't special. They were scared to death, they had their problems. They simply dared to put that thing out there.

This notebook contains thoughts, some more intimate, some less, more vulnerable, less, more emotional, less. It could just stay there, lying around my house. It would be something for me. I could die and it would stay there and get lost and thrown in the trash and it serves no purpose. So I thought: what if Bécquer had done that with his poems? What if he'd been afraid to put that burning, omnipotent love his poems express out there? What if he'd said: wow, what a great poem this is, but I'm a little embarrassed to put it out there? We wouldn't have Bécquer. And if Edison had said: this light bulb is fine but it's not quite there, I'll keep it, I had fun designing it but nah, it was just a hobby. We wouldn't have had... well, we'd have had them eventually, but we wouldn't have had, for example, the anecdote in which he made a string of light bulbs, they turned them on and they all burst because the model wasn't ready yet.

Putting yourself out there is important for connecting with people. And when you put yourself out there people don't respond with sharp, fierce criticism. They don't make fun of you. Instead they connect, they empathize. I like people knowing me this way and knowing people this way.

So I thought maybe I'll do a series of readings from different excerpts I like from this notebook, to put it out there. This excerpt got to me a little. My past self says this:

You have to go down, you have to descend into that mudhole that is life and not escape from it, not isolate yourself in reflection. You have to go down and get smeared, dirty yourself and wallow in life, stumble and come out dirty and bruised, covered in black and blue marks, scratches, but with a radiant smile from having lived. To reach old age wrinkled like a raisin, tanned by all the sun you've soaked in, with scars and gray hairs, one for each adventure. Wrinkled, old and tanned but with tender, tender lips, from not having wasted a single opportunity to kiss, to fall in love, even if just for a night or an afternoon. You have to go down, get dirty, kiss, get bruised, make love like a madman killing the neighbors with envy, so that when you die they can only say: you bastard, you lived the way I would've liked to.
