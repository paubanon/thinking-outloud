They say we live in the age of information overload, but I'm more in agreement that we live in the age of data consumption. Information is a term that's, to my taste, a bit too generous for what we consume. We're not consuming information, we're not informing ourselves — we're consuming data, sometimes ideas, sometimes garbage in audiovisual format, constantly and repetitively and with no real depth.

This constant consumption, even when we're sometimes doing it with things that could be considered productive — like audiobooks, articles, and similar — often leaves us with the feeling of: I don't remember anything I consumed. I read eight or ten book summaries and remember nothing. Or those challenges people do of I'm going to read thirty books in a month. Without realizing that what matters isn't how many books you read, but how much information you absorb. How much have you retained? How much have you integrated? And here I'd like to make a case in defense of books themselves.

I get pretty heavily targeted by apps that give you ten or fifteen minute summaries of books. And these are books that many of them I've read, like Deep Work, The One Thing, Headspace. Typically self-help or productivity books, condensed into fifteen minutes. Also audiobooks, which people have mentioned to me more than once. And summaries in article form. People, instead of reading Mihaly Csikszentmihályi's Flow, read an article about what it's about and say: I've got it, I know it now.

I'd like to raise my hand in defense of reading, for several reasons.

Our brain has some fairly complicated and peculiar systems for deciding which information is relevant and which isn't. Two very important factors are: one, how much the information you're processing matters to you; and two, the time you dedicate to it. They're obviously intertwined. If something matters a lot to you, you're generally going to spend a lot of time on it. And if you spend a lot of time on it, the brain can interpret that it's something important and therefore absorbs it.

The problem with distilled information formats is that precisely because they're formats to which you're not giving your total attention — as would be the case with audiobooks, which people listen to while doing other things — it doesn't mean you've read the book. It means, literally, that you had the book on in the background while you did something else. And I think there's a huge reduction in the level of information absorbed when you consume this kind of content, because you're not paying attention to it.

I can summarize Why We Sleep, by Matthew Walker, in one sentence: sleep is very important for every aspect of your health, physiological, hormonal, mental, academic performance, the immune system. And absolutely everything you want to do better for your health, sleep is the answer. And you'll hear that and say: that's true. And in an hour you'll have forgotten it. This also happens with YouTube videos that try to summarize a book. If you've read the book and then see the summary you say: what a great summary. But if you only see the summary, the most likely outcome is that nothing sticks at all.

Where am I going with this? To the value of the written word. Which I think resides not only in what's said, but in the fact that the format itself is optimal for absorbing information. The book forces you to read and only read. You can't read and look at your phone at the same time. You only have a narrow field of vision to focus on. If you're reading, you're reading.

And it's slow. Reading a book, even a short one, takes several days. That means that for several days, or several weeks, you're thinking about what it says. And every morning when you sit down to read you think: right, deep work is this and that. And then over the course of a couple of hours in the morning you turn it over here and there. And the next day, again.

Reading fulfills two of the fundamental requirements for absorbing information. One: it requires your complete, uninterrupted attention, which means your brain interprets that information as important. That alone is a huge boost to retaining it. Two: it takes time. It's information that repeats and spreads out over time.

I really like the idea of visualizing ideas as three-dimensional objects. But you can't see the three-dimensional object, you can only see its shadow with a light source. What you do when you try to share an idea with someone is illuminate it from different points of view, so the person can build the object itself in their head. I can't communicate the idea to you directly, I can communicate the projection. And by projecting it from different angles, I can make what you have in your head very close to my conception of the object.

I think that's what a book does. It takes a thesis and illuminates it from many angles, with different levels of detail. And that repetition — because ultimately it's the same idea but you're seeing it from different lenses spread out over time — makes your brain say: we've been thinking about this idea from different points of view for a month. Therefore it's important, because we've dedicated this time to it. The brain often does this in reverse: it's not that it matters to me and so I dedicate hours to it, but if I dedicate many hours to it, the brain interprets that it matters.

I'd like to make a case in defense of reading, especially on paper, as the optimal medium for absorbing information. And make a case against audiobooks, summaries in article format, video format, audio format. Because if what matters to you is learning, don't be in a hurry. The brain likes the unhurried pace of learning without pressure.
