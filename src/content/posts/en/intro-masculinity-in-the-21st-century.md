Hey everyone. Today I'm going to talk about masculinity. Masculinity in the 21st century. This is going to be an intro video to a series I want to do on the topic. Last week I started reading a book called The Way of the Superior Man, by David Deida. It's a book I've been wanting to read for a while and I'm really enjoying it. Recommended by many people I follow. And it has some very interesting ideas I want to share.

The topic of masculinity matters to me. And I think there's a lot of confusion around it. When I say masculinity, a lot of people immediately associate it with machismo, aggression, negative things. And that association is wrong. Masculinity is not that.

What Deida proposes, and what I find interesting about the book, is the distinction between masculine energy and feminine energy. And the first thing to understand is that these energies are not exclusive to men and women respectively. All human beings have masculine energy and feminine energy. The difference is in what proportion. And that proportion varies from person to person.

Masculine energy has to do with direction, purpose, clarity, strength. Feminine energy has to do with flow, emotion, connection, presence. Both are necessary. Both are valuable. Neither is better than the other.

The problem Deida points to, and which I share, is that we live in an era that overvalues feminine energy and undervalues masculine energy. And this has consequences. Because if a man has a masculine essence, if his predominant energy is masculine, and he lives in an environment that tells him that's bad, that he needs to be different, that he needs to suppress that part of himself, that creates confusion, insecurity, lack of direction.

It's not that masculinity is good and femininity is bad. It's that every person has an essence, and living against your essence has a cost.

The book uses a very illustrative analogy. Imagine two children. One has a more masculine essence: more direct, more goal-focused, more independent. The other has a more feminine essence: more empathetic, more emotional, more connected to others. Both are valid. Both are healthy. But if you constantly tell the first one that they need to be more like the second, and vice versa, you're going against who they are. And that causes harm.

I want to do this series with guests. I'm planning some Skype conversations with people who have interesting perspectives on the topic. Men who have thought about this, who have experiences to share, who can add something to the conversation. The idea is for it to be a series of videos that explores the topic from different angles.

Today is just the intro. I wanted to lay out the framework, the general idea, the why behind the series. In the next videos I'll go deeper.

Take care.
