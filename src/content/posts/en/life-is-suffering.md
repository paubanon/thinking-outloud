Good and snowy morning to everyone. Today I wanted to talk about a concept from Petersonian philosophy that has helped me a lot and that's very much in line with other videos I've done: taking responsibility, a stoic stance toward life, anti-Mr. Wonderful. And the idea is very simple. It's common to many religions. The message in question is that life is suffering.

And you can hear that and say: well that's a pretty useless piece of advice, shove it. Because you say, okay, life is suffering, what do I do with that? You haven't fixed anything for me. But when you think about it twice, you realize the power of being told this.

We live in a society that has tried by every possible means to make people happy. It tries by every possible means that nobody gets offended, tries by every possible means that people can reach their dreams, tries by every possible means that we're all as comfortable as possible. And I'm not saying that's a bad goal, I think it's a good goal, but I think it has side effects we maybe haven't accounted for.

The problem shows up like this. Human beings are not as rational and objective as we'd like to be. Sometimes, even when everything is going well, we feel bad. And sometimes, when everything is going badly, we feel good. Today we're bombarded by a series of messages from the Mr. Wonderful school of thought — and I'm not just talking about the ads, I'm also talking about education, the way parents behave with their kids, the abundance of everything we have today. A series of messages that basically tell you: the world is wonderful, enjoy it, today is going to be a great day, you can do anything, grab your dream and go for it, travel as much as you want.

And yet we find ourselves in an age where existential emptiness is pretty common. An internal feeling of something's not right, something's off, what's all this for? And it's something that has hit me more than once and it's very unpleasant. Because you feel bad and you say: I have everything I could need and... it doesn't add up. You feel bad for not valuing what you have, even though objectively you do. Emotionally you don't.

And I think part of the problem is this dissonance. Everyone is telling me life is wonderful, so I have the feeling that life can be wonderful and that I have a duty to feel amazed by it. But I don't feel that way, so there must be something wrong with me. And that pressure is pretty unpleasant. Because you feel like you're doing something wrong because how you feel doesn't match how you've been told to interpret it.

So the message of "life is suffering," contrary to what it might seem, is very hopeful because it changes this point of view. It tells you: no, no, no, life is still rough. We're better off than ever and that's true and we have to be grateful for it, but life is still rough. You're going to have to work, you're going to have relationships that go wrong. If it's not with a partner it'll be with a friend you have a falling out with, or a family member, someone will get sick, the degree will be particularly brutal, your landlord kicks you out of the flat... There'll almost always be something. So being told: no, life is rough and you're going to have to work really hard to get by and get where you need to get... And that phrase "if you do what you love you'll never work a day in your life": that's a lie.

And when you're told that life is suffering, something switches. Because what was before "I'm experiencing some kind of suffering that doesn't fit with my expectations of a world that should be wonderful"... instead of changing myself to make everything wonderful — because we've tried that and it doesn't work — we change our expectations of the world and say: no, the world is rough. It is. And it's hard. And you're going to have to put in the hours sitting at a desk, whether you like it or not. And that's fine. And you'll do it with energy. Because once you accept that life is rough, you say: oh, that was the problem. It wasn't that I was messed up. We're all messed up. Oh, okay. Alright. Now I know where the problem was.

And that's what makes you happy. Not that the world is wonderful and I'm the one who's wrong. No. It's that the world is rough. We're all struggling to push through in this competitive world. So you say: right, that's what needs to be done.

First of all, when you're suffering, you accept it as normal. There will be moments when I'm sad, there will be moments when I'm happy. But knowing that there are moments when you'll be sad and struggling means that the moments when you're happy, you're really happy, and you're more aware of appreciating that happiness. Because it's a peak. Whereas if you're told the average level is up here and you're always below what's supposed to be wonderful, occasionally you go up, but you don't feel particularly lucky.

I think that's how most people feel. And it's not real. Most people are struggling most of the time. They wake up at seven in the morning and work an eight-hour job they don't like, to support a family they love dearly, but who they probably can't give everything they need.

And even those online entrepreneurs you see on Instagram, saying: money online, I travel whenever I want, look at my photo in Thailand. Even those people are projecting a facade of ultra-happiness, as fake as a carnival mask. Because the reality is that the internet is a good niche to work in these days, yes. But you also have fierce competition from billions of people. And that means that no matter how mega an entrepreneur you are, you're going to have to put in a ton of hours. Behind that photo in Thailand there are almost certainly countless hours in front of a computer saying: man, I have to switch this software, I have to reprogram this website, I have to launch this new product, I have to record it, edit it, put it together... Hours and hours, and they're not pleasant.

So don't think that everyone is super happy and that there are people at extreme levels of happiness and that you're not getting there because you're doing something wrong with your life. No. We're all fighting to try to make it.

Even — and this is worth pointing out — there's a book called Confessions, by Leo Tolstoy, in which he talks about how when he was at the peak of his life, praised by his contemporaries, had a lot of money, was happy with his family, had everything anyone could want, and despite all that he felt terribly miserable, to the point of being close to suicide. In the book he explains how he got there and his way out of that existential emptiness.

Once you accept that life is suffering and say: we're all struggling, you stop and ask: so what can I do? You have two options. The nihilistic point of view, where you say: nothing matters, I'm going to suffer anyway, I'm not going to do anything. Which usually leads to your suffering being even greater, to feeling empty, to letting life pass without doing anything that has value. Or the other option is to say: okay, life is suffering, what do I do? Try to reduce it. I'm going to try to do everything I can to make my life as good as possible. My life and the lives of those around me. My partner's, my family's, my friends'.

And that's going to require effort. It's going to require getting up at eight, hours studying, making time to see those people, doing favors... But all of that has one wonderful thing: even though it costs you effort, you like it. You see how the things you do reduce, even just slightly, the suffering in the world. Whether it's your own suffering, because you see that even though you're struggling, you're less in the hole than yesterday. You also make other people's lives a little bit better. Inviting someone to dinner, getting them out of the house, listening when they have a problem, telling them: hey, don't worry, let's talk it through. Those little things make people's lives a little bit better. And they appreciate it a lot.

And that feeling is what's called meaning in English. I like the English word, not the Spanish equivalent. Because meaning and meaningful go hand in hand. Meaningful — for those who aren't very strong in English — means full of purpose. And when you have a meaningful life, it's filled with purpose. That's good and bad. The good part is that you feel quite fulfilled, you feel really good about yourself, you're proud of what you're doing. The bad part is that it requires a lot of effort and carrying a great responsibility on your shoulders: the responsibility that everything you do matters. Good and bad. You don't feel completely free to do whatever you want, because you know that every time you procrastinate, every time you mess up with someone, every time you're not the best version of yourself, it's your fault. And that weighs on you too.

A life full of meaning is worth living. But it also weighs more on your back.

And that's where the need for projects comes in. Career projects, projects with your friends, with your family, in your intimate life. How would you ideally want the relationship with your family to be? Think about it. Act accordingly. Have a long-term goal. And at the same time, learn to constantly polish each vertex of your life: train better, eat better, sleep more, read more, learn to cook, tidy your room. Everything is a project. And it's constant polishing. The moment you reach a level, you have to set yourself a new ideal to keep progressing.

I used to think I had to work really hard to reach a point where I had enough money that I'd never have to work again. But I've come to realize that I don't like that point of view. I'm not happy if I have nothing to do. I can stop for a week or two and say: I'm going to the mountains, I'll bring my book. But there's a point where I need to have projects going. Or I climb the walls. That's partly why I like construction work, working with my hands, wood, DIY. Because you have an idea of something you want to improve and you throw yourself into it.

And with this constant battle, with this constant: I have a new project and I put the hours in and I see it come together, that's how you go through life.
