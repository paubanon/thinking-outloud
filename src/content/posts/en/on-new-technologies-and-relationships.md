The other day I went on Facebook, which is the only social network I have left. Well, no, that's not quite true. I went on Facebook because it basically keeps me up to date on events, some party that's happening, some trip. And for that it's very useful. I have it set up so I'm not following my friends basically. It's a news feed, which I'll talk about another time. It's a terrible news feed because it basically shows things in the order Facebook decides to keep you hooked as long as possible.

The thing is, every time I go on Facebook I have some notification. I always have at least one or two. And most of the time they're junk notifications. I don't care. I don't want a dopamine hit from that garbage. So I head over to the notification settings, which you can do, by the way, and I recommend it. You go to notification management: what things do you want Facebook to notify you about? It's a bit hidden because obviously Facebook wants to notify you about as much as possible, because every little red number is a dopamine hit. And the more dopamine hits you get, and the more control Facebook has over those hits, the more time you'll stay engaged on the platform.

Once you get to the notifications menu, I already knew Facebook was going to have a lot of things to notify you about. Because in recent years they don't just notify you when you get a comment, or when you're tagged in a photo, or get a like. They notify you that Pepito has changed his profile picture, that someone is celebrating a year of friendship with So-and-so, that someone visited your page. And you think: I couldn't care less about any of that. I don't want to know that.

When I went into notification management I was surprised — and I already went in expecting this — by the enormous amount of nonsense Facebook notifies you about. And not just that: for every single piece of that nonsense, it notifies you in the app, by email, and by SMS. Just in case you miss the fact that your friend Fernando liked a page. The event of the century, people. I already had all email and SMS notifications turned off, and three quarters of the app notifications too, but I went ahead and disabled a whole bunch more.

And it made me think again about the attention business that is Facebook. That is Facebook and that is every digital platform. They all want users to spend as much time as possible hooked in. All of them. Some do it with more style than others. Social networks, since the social approval component is so strong in human beings, tend to be especially addictive. We're especially vulnerable on that flank by pure evolutionary design.

Having the constant sensation of being liked, of constantly having notifications, is literally like jerking yourself off. For your brain it's a circuit that's there biologically. In one case it's the reproduction circuit, and in the other it's the circuit that seeks social approval, because that maximizes your survival. And instead of feeding it with a real stimulus, complete in all its dimensions, and which is generally relatively hard to obtain, you feed it with a substitute.

To sleep with someone you have to meet them, make plans, get a drink together, physically escalate, get to know each other personally. When you need social approval, you have to go out, meet people, get their number, make plans, become friends, start meeting up regularly. Little by little their opinion starts to matter to you because they begin to form part of your tribe. And together you support each other, look out for each other, and survive better.

In the first case, instead of going through the whole courtship process, you pull down the blinds, jerk off, and have a sex substitute that tricks your brain into thinking you're doing something valuable. In the second case, it tricks your brain into thinking you have a big community supporting you. It's not true. Nobody gives a damn about you on social media. Not even slightly. Nobody spends more than a second thinking about you, which is the amount of time their attention passes over one of your posts. Doesn't matter if it's the mega-post. Doesn't matter if you spent 30 minutes on it. We like to think that when we do that people are going to see it and say: wow, what a post. Not true. People go like this and say, oh nice, like, and keep scrolling. That's how much time people spend thinking about you on social media. Doesn't matter how many followers you have. Nobody cares about you. Period.

You know when someone cares about you? When someone calls you and you spend an hour talking. When that person has thought about you enough that at some point during the day they say: I need to call Lucía. When they have a free moment they say: let's go, I'll call her. And they're willing to give you an hour. Those people care about you. The people you say: hey, coffee, to and spend a week trying to line up schedules. Those people care about you. The people on Facebook don't care about you.

But we trick our brains into believing they do. And deep down we know it's not producing the kind of contact we actually want. And exactly the same thing happens with pornography.

Since I got off social media, my personal connections across the board have improved a lot. I don't have Instagram. I only talk to and contact people by phone, or by WhatsApp for specific things: hey, want to meet up? hey, want to watch a movie? hey, let's go to the mountains, hey, coffee. That's what builds things.

And deep down we know it. We know it because if you spend a whole day on a social network making friends, afterward you feel empty. You feel empty, miserable, like you haven't done anything productive. Because it's a substitute. It's not real connection.

A phrase that might seem very simple but that has resonated a lot with me is: people crave deep connection. People long to connect with another human being. For real. Not: I'll add you on Instagram and maybe we'll reply to each other's stories. That's garbage. Connecting for real. Meeting up. Turning off your phone. Spending however much time it takes discovering the other person's mind. Looking each other in the eyes. Paying attention to each other. Having moments where the conversation dies and you're both just staring into space until another topic appears, completely unplanned, completely spontaneous. Catching up on how work is going, the worries, the good stuff, the bad, the relationship with family. Maybe a movie you both saw the other day. Reading body language. Hearing the voice, the tone, the pauses, the other person's smell, the interest they bring to the conversation. None of that exists on social media.

And the same goes for pornography. You can masturbate all you want and in the end you're left with a feeling of: okay, that took the edge off. But that wasn't exactly what I wanted. What actually gives you what you were hoping for is when you meet someone for real, when you make plans, when you spend a wonderful night disconnected from work, from your phone, from your family, from your friends, just for each other. Having dinner together. Dedicating real time to each other. And then you have real sex. And you're done and you say: tonight was like an oasis of peace in my week. This moment let me completely forget about work and my worries. Because I'm totally absorbed in the time with this other person. I reach a maximum level of intimacy without thinking about notifications or whether I'll get more followers on Instagram. You're completely immersed in what you're doing.

The problem is that our brain has gotten used to the substitute. And not just that: it's gotten used to its frequency. It's gotten used to rewards that are very frequent, easy, always there. Likes. Pornography. Followers. The feeling of being the star of the world because you have 5,000 followers on Instagram. Not one of those 5,000 followers gives a damn about you.

So when you try to change that, suddenly your brain goes from having constant, infinite feedback to a state where you can go days without social approval. You can go days in your own world, doing what your life should actually be. And you can go weeks without any romantic or sexual contact.

The choice is clear. Either keep being an addict, sinking into the pit of substitutes. Or be an adult and say: this is not the way of life I want to be in. And learn that life is not the life you've been living until now on social media. Learn that work, friendships, relationships, yourself — they require time. They require effort. And they move one step much slower than technology has made us believe.

It takes a lot of time to build a relationship, whether friendship or personal. It takes a lot of time to become the person you want to be. A lot of time for work to progress. You're not going to have instant reward. If you accept that... you're badly educated. With every part of that word placed exactly where it belongs. And now you have to educate yourself again. And it's going to suck. Because you're used to having 10,000 notifications like Facebook, and you're going to go through days where you feel like garbage because you feel like nobody cares about you. Because you're not getting that feedback.

So you have to change your mindset. It's not that nobody cares about you. What's happening is that before you had that substitute. But that substitute is fake. Now you're craving social approval. And what you have to get into your head is that notifications don't count. They're fake. If you want sexual connection, pornography doesn't count. It doesn't work. You're not getting what you actually want. The only way to get what you want is to go out and meet people. If you want to build a friendship, call someone, make plans, make the effort to create a real connection. That's what will satisfy you.

And at first it won't satisfy you. At first you'll say: damn, I only talked to one person. I was used to interacting with 500 a day. What you have to change in your head is that that interaction is a real interaction and it's worth infinitely more than any of those likes. Because that person actually cared about you. The rest didn't.

So you have to take that frustration that comes from the lack of instant stimulation and use it as fuel to go out, meet more people, talk to someone in a café, make space to meet up. Because that's what's going to give you meaning in your life. Not the substitute.

It's very hard to do. It takes a lot of time. But once you do it, you'll learn that you can have a much fuller life than you expected. It's a slower life. It's a slow-cooker life. A stew can't be rushed: it has to have its three hours. You can't build a relationship faster. Trust takes time. It takes effort from both sides. And the sad thing is that a lot of people today don't dedicate that time. But I encourage you to. Anything you value in life takes time. Think about someone who wrote an important book, someone who developed a theory that changed our understanding of physics. Really think about the time that requires. The hours that person spent alone, working, without positive feedback, without anyone patting them on the back. That's how things worth doing get done.

So stop seeking instant social approval. Stop seeking substitutes. And seek real connection. With time you'll find it, and you'll discover that your life can be much better than you expected. Even if first you have to go through a period of frustration.
