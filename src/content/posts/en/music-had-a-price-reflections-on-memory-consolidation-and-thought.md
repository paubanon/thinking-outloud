Hey. Today I wanted to talk about music. First of all: I love music, I listen to a lot of everything, I think it's wonderful, a great form of art and a way to stimulate the senses and creativity. However, I think that with the arrival of portable music players, music gets used without considering the price that sometimes comes with listening to it. Let me explain.

Our brain needs time to consolidate memories, to move them from short-term to long-term memory. Long-term memory requires new physical connections, the creation of new dendrites between neurons — please, I'm not that well-versed in neuroplasticity, so if someone knows more and wants to correct me, go ahead and send me sources. Short-term memory, on the other hand, is simply chemical reactions. That is, long-term memory requires neuronal growth, and that takes time. For a memory or thought to consolidate, neurologically it needs a period after the initial input, a time to knead and ruminate that memory or thought.

Now, if we instantly, as soon as we've finished doing something, disconnect and jump to something completely different, or fill our heads with noise, I believe — from personal experience and from logic, as far as I know it's not backed by scientific studies — that it interrupts that circuit. If you're in class and the moment it ends you put on headphones and head home, your brain doesn't stop to assimilate what you just saw. It says: "Music! Disconnect. Shutting down." That memory doesn't consolidate as well. You don't go through that phase where you're walking home thinking: "I didn't understand that part very well, I should look at this step I'm not clear on, and that argument wasn't clear to me either." Maybe twenty or thirty minutes of walking home turning it over makes all the difference.

The same when studying. You finish, put on music, your brain completely disconnects. It doesn't go through that assimilation phase. The brain needs a relaxation time in which it processes the memories and thoughts generated in the study session, without you feeding it new input.

For certain areas this has no repercussion. If your work doesn't require high concentration, you can finish, put on music and be perfectly happy. However, for students or for work that requires concentration, innovation, or creativity, overusing music can be a problem. I myself often put it on out of reflex to go shopping or go to class, and sometimes I have to tell myself: "No. Don't put on music. Walk home in silence and let your mind turn things over."

The same with the car. People get in the car and automatically put on the radio or music. You can drive in silence. You can go without background noise. Because both long-term memory consolidation and deep thinking, creativity, and philosophy require time alone, letting the mind wander, letting it explore new topics, reflect on ones it already has, look for interconnections.

It's not just music. It's the possibility of putting on music ubiquitously, anywhere, that interferes. The same as I talked about in the video on mobile phones.

And as I said: I have nothing against music, but I think you need to be able to be without it. This also connects with the topic of facing your demons, of reflecting on your life. If you don't have moments of silence and stillness, that doesn't happen. And if it doesn't happen for long periods of time, what you have to face keeps growing, the doubts are greater, the feeling of not having control over your life is greater. The feeling of "I don't know how to be alone" goes hand in hand with the fear of facing yourself, your life, the decisions you have to make, adulthood, the passage of time, a little bit of death too. These are topics that can only be approached when you're calm, in silence, with yourself.

And on top of that, the more stressed we are, the more we tend to add noise. If you're on a beach with a cocktail, lying down, listening to the sea, maybe you don't even think to put on music. But if you're stressed, if you have to study, work, run around, buy groceries, in the in-between moments automatically: "Go on, headphones." When those same tasks would surprise you with how calm they can become, and how productive you can be, if in those small pauses you put in nothing. You go from one place to another in silence, enjoying the transition. Your brain kneads the last thing, relaxes, doesn't process new inputs. And then you enter the new activity with more energy.

I love cooking, precisely because of that. It's a manual activity: you can't be doing something else at the same time, or at least not comfortably. If you put on headphones, something usually burns or you cut yourself, because it's so multisensory. Your hands are busy or wet. You're smelling what you're cooking. The smell tells you if it's browning, if it's burning. The sound too: a desired sizzle sounds different from when it's boiling, or when there's too much heat. And I love it because, requiring so much concentration, you have to be very present. Also, it's a discipline in which you have to give it care and time, because you can't speed up the process. If a piece of meat needs three hours to cook, it needs three hours. If you want excellence, it requires time, it requires care, it requires doing things carefully. And in that time you have space to be present.

It's a bit the mindfulness philosophy — which I usually don't like because I don't like these commercial names, but anyway — that mental emptiness where you've just finished studying, you start cooking and say: "Ahhh, silence." A great moment. And sometimes I myself make the mistake of saying: "Well, I'll call home since I haven't called them, I'll reply to the WhatsApps." But the days I completely skip that and say: "No, cooking is also an activity I enjoy," the difference is enormous. It's the importance of rest: work hard, but also know how to rest hard. Taking your pleasure seriously. Taking seriously that your brain, to be productive afterwards and assimilate what it's done now, needs to rest. And rest is total disconnection.

It's the opposite of the system we have in Spain, where work gets diluted across all the hours and bleeds into every area of your life: you're resting but thinking about work, you're working but scrolling on your phone with your mind wandering. Without being able to say: "I need to do this work, I sit down and do it, period." My personal experience is that you're more satisfied with the work you produce, you do it in much less time, you do it at better quality, and you learn more from it.

I had a client in the training world who told me he was going to cut back on training hours because he didn't have enough time with university. I gave him these pieces of advice, and a month or two later he told me: "Man, one of the best things I've ever done. Now I study in a third of the time, I have time to train, I have time to go out and rest."

All of this started from the topic of music and the need to, when you work, work hard; but also be aware that when you've worked hard and thoroughly processed your brain, you have to let it go. You have to give it time to assimilate, for that short-term memory to move to long-term and become not just something in a book but part of you, with a deep understanding of the topic. That leads us to being more efficient, to having greater control over our lives and understanding them better.

Tell me in the comments because it's a bit of a controversial topic. Music is usually said to be very good, that it stimulates the brain. Like everything, it has its other side, and I'd like to know your opinion.
