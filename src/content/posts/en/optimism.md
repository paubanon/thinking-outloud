I wanted to talk briefly today about optimism. Because I think it's misunderstood and I think I can offer a different take on the usual concept.

Personally I consider myself a very optimistic person. Very optimistic, extremely optimistic. I believe things will always work out. And that's the usual idea of optimism: someone who tends to think things will go well. But I think there's a little more to it than meets the eye.

When I say I'm optimistic, it's not a blind belief that things will work out by magic. Not because I deserve it, not out of cosmic wish-granting. I think being optimistic, at least from my perspective and experience, has deeper roots.

I like to split it into two categories: things you have control over, and things you don't. And depending on which it is, you have to approach it differently at the psychological level.

Over things I have control over, I don't have blind confidence. I'm not optimistic. What I have is evidence. I have evidence that in every aspect of my life where I've worked hard to get a result, I've gotten it. I have evidence that if you have an important goal you value, you make a map, you make an action plan, you apply it consistently, you regularly update the map, you study, you work, you advance, you set micro-goals and quantify progress — you achieve it. And that's evidence. Something I've done in many areas of my life and therefore I know that if I do it in a new area it's going to happen.

It's not naive optimism. It's optimism based on evidence that I've done this in my training, in building my personality, in my career, in my businesses, and it's always worked. Sometimes it takes longer and sometimes less, but it works. Because it's something quantifiable. It might be hitting a certain number of reps with a certain weight, it might be changing a character trait. If you look at what people with that character trait do and what you have to do to work on it, you take it, work on it, refine, repeat — you get there. There's no other option, as long as it's in your hands.

Gordon Ryan talked about this — the best BJJ fighter in the world. The blind confidence you see in him, in Muhammad Ali, in Bruce Lee... when people talk about them they say: "they're arrogant." And when you talk to them they say: no, it's not blind arrogance. I didn't think I was going to be the best fighter in the world. I climbed step by step. I was the best in my gym, then in my region, then in my country. I entered internationals and kept winning. I beat a world champion. I beat another. And when you've beaten five world champions, you say: maybe I am the best in the world. Because it's evidence. Not arrogance. Accumulated evidence.

Over things I can't control, there I am optimistic in the more conventional sense. And I think there's no other rational option. Because if things are outside your control, worrying about them doesn't change them. It just makes the time worse. So optimism about the uncontrollable isn't naivety. It's simply the most intelligent posture available. Worrying about what you can't control is a huge cost with zero benefit.

And there's a third category that's the most interesting: the things you think you can't control but actually can. Here optimism is especially useful. Because if you start from the premise that you can't control something, you never try. And you'll never know if you could have. Optimism, in this case, is what lets you start. And starting is 90%.

So when I say I'm optimistic I'm not saying I believe the world is beautiful and everything is going to be fine because the universe conspires in my favor. I'm saying I have evidence that when I act well, things go well. And that for what I can't control, optimism is simply smarter than anxiety.
