Good morning everyone. Lately I've been reading about Taoism. I've been reading the Tao Te Ching by Lao Tzu and I've been reading The Tao of Pooh, which is a book that pleasantly surprised me, I'll say that much. And I've learned certain things from it, certain thoughts I wanted to share with you.

In today's society we're pushed to do, to do more things, always do more. We're pushed to study more, work more, train more, take care of ourselves more, sleep more, eat more vegetables, have more friends. Always more, always doing, never enough. Yet by the very nature of the world, you're not always going to be able to have more or do more.

Something I've learned and am really enjoying about Taoism is that it reflects very well ideas I had arrived at through meditation. And this is one of them: the importance of not doing. Knowing when not to do. Understanding that there are times when it's better not to do than to do. Not always is more better, not always is more work better, not always is more effort better. Sometimes it's not just that it's not better — it's that it's worse.

I like to think of it as water flowing in a river. Water doesn't do — water lets itself go. And if you fight the water, you generally come out very badly. Whereas if you let yourself go, it can be a very pleasant journey.

Examples of situations where you have to let go. It can be when you're trying to fall asleep. At what point do you go from wakefulness to sleep? If you try to catch that moment, chances are that moment won't come. Because you can't force sleep. The harder you try to fall asleep, the worse you'll sleep and the longer it'll take. It's like a plant growing: you can't intervene more in a plant's growth, you have to let it happen. You have to plant the seed, water it regularly, care for it, repot it now and then. But you can't force the growth. You can lie down, you can try to go to sleep, but if it happens, it'll happen on its own. You can't force yourself to sleep. You can do your routine, you can make it easier for it to happen, but in the end it just happens.

When you meditate something similar happens. The initial state of meditation is very different from the final state. The final state tends to be a very quiet state where you suddenly say: have the ten minutes already passed? And when you finish, you realize you'd been in a state of near unconsciousness of yourself, of peace, of emptiness. At what moment did that shift happen? You don't know if it was at minute one or minute nine. You only know that it happened. And the more you try to force that moment, the more you try to identify it and say "there it is!" — you'll prevent it from happening. You have to let things happen on their own through non-action.

A practical example I find very cool is in acrobatics. Acrobatics aren't about doing more. It's not about jumping harder, spinning harder. It's about knowing when to do and when not to do. Doing too much, being too rigid, prevents the natural development of the acrobatic. It prevents you from spinning in the air the way you need to spin. So knowing when to push, when to jump, when to hold tight and when not to do creates a really beautiful balance. It's like you give an impulse in the direction you want and then let it go. You let the ball roll on its own once you've given it that push.

Another example is love. You can't force someone to fall in love with you. You have to plant the seed and let it happen. You have to water it now and then, you have to show this person you're interested, that you'd like to get to know them better, that that possibility exists. But you have to let it go. And if you try to intervene too much, if you try to force it to happen through excessive interaction, it's not going to happen. We all know that if a person finds you interesting and you're constantly after them, that person loses interest. You're overwatering your plant, you're drowning it.

This leads me to another very important point of Taoist philosophy: letting go. Which can be interpreted as detaching yourself from the final outcome. In non-doing it's very important to detach yourself from the outcome. Because if you don't detach from the final result, you'll always be fighting toward the result you want. And maybe that's not the result that's supposed to happen. You have to show interest, you have to show that you want to get to know that person. But you have to let go of the possibility of something happening or not. You have to fully accept that you might not be interesting to them. And that's okay. And ironically, when you accept that, you get the best results.

One of the things I learned during a period when I had insomnia is that I used to fall into the loop of: ugh, I can't sleep, damn, what time is it? It's getting late, I have to wake up early tomorrow, I'm going to be tired, I'm going to have a terrible day. Come on, hurry up, go to sleep. And I'd fall into a loop where I wouldn't fall asleep until four or five in the morning. It took me a while to get it rooted in my psyche: knowing how to say "I'm not falling asleep and that's okay, it doesn't matter, I need to be at peace." If I don't fall asleep, I don't fall asleep. I'll be more tired tomorrow, I'll take a nap when I can or I'll go to bed earlier. It doesn't matter. Maybe I fall asleep in the next minute or maybe I'm awake until five in the morning. And that's okay. The clock doesn't exist. Being completely at peace with the possibility of not falling asleep. Ironically, when you reach that point, you generally fall asleep.

In acrobatics, the earlier example: you have to be okay with the possibility that you might fail, that maybe you'll fall and hurt yourself. You have to let it go. And paradoxically, when you let yourself go, when you give the right push and release it, you land well. But if you give the push and then force things in the air, you break the chain of movement, you collapse, and that's when you're most likely to get hurt.

Knowing when not to act and detaching from the outcome are two very important things. Because not everything is in your hands. You can't always control things. I also don't think you always have to not act or not care about the result. You have to choose a direction and give yourself a push. You can set your rudder. But you can't go against the wind. If the wind blows in one direction you can't say "I want to go over there." The world tells you: you're not going over there. So you'll be better off saying: I'd like to go there. And if the wind says you can't, you ask: where can we go? Where does this lead us?

Not long ago I applied for an important grant and was turned down. I realized I was at peace with whether they gave it to me or not. I was calm having done everything possible: I wrote an excellent cover letter that I worked on for a long time, I have the CV that I have, I had the most excellent references I could have. And I was at peace saying: I gave this everything I had, my conscience is clear, and they might not give it to me. Because the world doesn't reward the effort you put in. Sometimes you put in everything and the world tells you no. And you have to be okay with that.

The same in relationships. Something I learned early is that when a relationship ends, what you want is the peace of knowing you acted as well as you possibly could. That if events were to repeat themselves, you'd act exactly the same. That you haven't left anything untied that would later eat at your conscience. Knowing you did everything you could. And letting it go. And being grateful for what you had and the opportunity you had.

This is completely opposite to what society teaches us today: do, do more, fight harder, work hard, hard work beats talent. And doing — fair enough: yes, you have to give your life a push, you have to give it a direction. But if you want to go there and the wind isn't blowing that way, chances are you won't get there. Learn to change your goals when the time is right. Learn to jump and let yourself go.
