Hi everyone, good morning. Today I wanted to talk about how to stick to your goals and what I think is the right way to approach a new habit. This comes from the nofap video I published a couple of weeks ago, in which I talked about masturbation and how it affects you. And there was a comment saying that masturbation is positive or can bring you things.

There are two important points I'd like to make here. The first is understanding that, of course, masturbation or social media have something positive. We all know that. You can learn about your sexuality, you can learn many things from pornography. Social media can be tremendously useful for staying in touch with people you care about when you're far away, for putting information out there, for getting information. They have positive aspects.

However, what I often do on this channel is highlight the negative aspects. Why? Because we tend to forget them. We usually only see the positive side and forget the price we're paying. What you have to understand is: yes, it has positive aspects, but there are these negatives. Let's not forget that masturbation and pornography cause addiction. Let's not forget that social media can affect how you think, how you work, how you relate to others, the expectations you have of the opposite sex. Let's not forget that all of that is there.

What I'm proposing is: look objectively at what it gives you and what price you're paying. And think about whether it's worth it. In thinking about whether it's worth it, you don't have to be binary. It can be a "I'm cutting it out completely," as I did with Instagram. I thought: what does it give me, what does it take away? And I said: it's not worth it. Facebook, on the other hand, I still have. Why? Because I don't find it addictive and I find it a useful way to organize my news feed: I follow pages that publish articles I find interesting. It doesn't cause addiction, it's useful, I keep it. Same with YouTube: it doesn't cause addiction, I think I'm putting out quality content, it lets me express myself the way I want. I keep it.

However, I've also noticed when I cross the line. For example, recently I noticed that when I was writing my thesis and got stuck on some problem, I'd open YouTube almost automatically. At that point I said: you're going to do a YouTube fast for a week. I realized it was problematic because I wasn't pushing through problems as hard as I could. Instead of sitting down and saying: no, you have to do this, you have to fight through it to the end, there was YouTube.

This is the middle-ground fallacy, which I want to talk about. The everything-is-fine fallacy: since this small benefit exists, let's ignore the price we're paying and try to take a little of everything. In nutrition, "eat a little of everything" is a terrible approach in the context of a hypermodern society with supermarkets, because 90% of supermarket products are sweets, refined carbohydrates, or processed items. A little of everything in that context doesn't work.

What I see making a lot of people fail when they set out to do something is spotting a small benefit and being unable to accept that they're giving up that small benefit to avoid paying this price. Because when you have Instagram, Facebook, masturbate, eat sweets, get into all these cycles of addictive behavior, you're generally not getting the small benefits of each one and adding them up. You're paying the price of each one. And it's not purely additive. The price of using all social media isn't just the time you spend on them. It's the mental cost of having to post on this one and this one and this one. The fear of missing out. The inability to concentrate on your work. Not having a focused workout because between sets you check your phone. The lack of mental calm and the inability to be alone with yourself and be happy. That's the price you're paying.

In the end, the time we have is limited. You can't give time to everything. You have to know how to leave things aside. When I was thinking about it, I didn't want to give up being able to sit and read a book for three hours on a Sunday afternoon, quietly. Why don't you do it? Because opening a social network is more rewarding. It gives you more instant satisfaction. But if you didn't have access to those things, what would you have done? Maybe you'd have done one more workout. Maybe you'd have been reflecting on your life, your relationship, your work. Maybe you'd have picked up a book. The things you don't do, you leave those aside too.

An example I really like: going out without earphones. You don't realize what you're missing because you can't make the comparison. Imagine you're out shopping for clothes. You spot an attractive girl, through a twist of fate you exchange a comment, you start talking in the queue. Hey, I don't have anything on this afternoon. Feel like getting a coffee? You go. Another example: you're walking through the woods and you happen to be walking at the same pace as someone. Eventually you start talking. They mention there's a beautiful spot in the woods you didn't know existed. You swap numbers, make plans to walk every now and then. Now imagine both of these situations if you'd been wearing headphones with music or a podcast. Most likely you wouldn't have noticed that girl, you wouldn't have heard the comment, you wouldn't have made plans with her. You wouldn't have met that person in the woods. When you get home with headphones in, you're not aware of the lost opportunities. That's the price you paid, but since it didn't happen, you don't see it.

And the same happens with quitting social media, quitting masturbation. You don't see the benefits until you cut it out for a good stretch. I didn't realize how pornography and masturbation were affecting me until I cut them out for many months. After six or eight months without them, you take stock, look back in retrospect, and you notice that new behaviors have emerged that weren't there before. You're not aware of how it affects your standards of beauty to constantly see girls in sexual positions on Instagram. You're not aware of how it changes your attitude on a date until you quit masturbation and pornography. You're not aware of the flavor you're missing in food until you cut sugar. You're not aware of how at peace you can be with yourself, how calm you can be, the fullness that comes from having empty time to think. You're not aware of the stress that being constantly connected produces until you cut it out.

That's why I urge people to try it before making a decision. And how to stick to the changes you decide to make in your life, I'll leave that for the next video.
