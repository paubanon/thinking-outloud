Good morning everyone. Today is a sunny Friday here in Germany. Doing push-ups in the sun — remember, it's important to get sunlight for your mood, for vitamin D, for physical and psychological health. Make the most of it whenever you can because sometimes... it's a very precious thing we took for granted in Spain. One of the things I learned when I came here is not to take it for granted. I love it even more now.

This video is a small reflection that came to me because my roommate is a disaster. A complete disaster, both in his personal life and academically, and in how he organizes his house, his room, cleaning, everything. And a couple of times I sat down with him to talk about this and I told him seriously: we need to talk.

First, to say that it's something very difficult and I think it's a tremendously useful skill for everyday life: the ability to take someone and say "we need to talk," sit down and talk about the things that bother you or the problems you want to change. It's very hard to sit down with your roommate and say: you've let me down with this attitude of taking my food without telling me, or skipping the cleaning rotation, that's no way to live. The easy option is to stay quiet and say: it's fine, it's fine. But letting that resentment grow inside you is not a good idea. It accumulates over time and eats you up. It makes you not want to talk to them, makes you not want to eat in the living room, you start retreating to your room and start developing negative feelings toward your flatmates.

So what you have to do is take a leap of faith and think that only by verbalizing reality can we bring it into the conscious realm of both parties. And that means sitting down to talk, however uncomfortable and unpleasant it might be. The leap of faith you have to take is believing that sitting down to talk and facing problems head-on, however hard it might be, is going to be less problematic than letting them run.

What I've discovered is that doing this is very hard, but I think it's incredibly useful for later learning how to do it with your girlfriend, with your parents, with your boss, with your flatmates. And the other thing I've learned is that if you do it well, you learn to articulate your case, people listen, they don't go into an irrational and emotional mode and start accusing you, they listen and say: you're right, that's no way to behave, I'm going to try to change it. And in the long run it works. In the long run, sitting down to talk pays dividends and you end up with a much more solid relationship.

And I think today, partly with the rise of new technologies, this has been somewhat lost. We're afraid to sit down and talk with people. Today, and this is something Sherry Turkle elaborates on in the book Reclaiming Conversation, because it's hard and human beings tend to take the easy path, we tend to fall back on WhatsApp, on emails, on resentment, on silence. And that hinders the development of our personal skills. And I promise you there is nothing more important in life than personal skills.

But what I was going to say, and what was the starting point for this video, is that from these conversations my roommate has been slowly improving. But he still has setbacks and sometimes he disappoints himself because he wants to improve at household tasks, he wants to stop being chaos, but he finds that suddenly he's burned all the food. And he says: damn man! This is a step backward.

And it made me think about how to move forward when you're lost in the tangle of chaos you can't control.

The first thing is not to play the victim. Don't think, and this applies to nofap too, when you have a relapse: "that's it, everything's gone to hell, this is pointless because I burned the food, I'm not getting better!" No. You have to look at it long term. You have to see it as a gradual but monotonically ascending construction of yourself. There are going to be moments when you fail. But what you have to try is for those failures to be smaller each time and less frequent. Own your failure as the human being you are and keep moving forward.

And the other thing I wanted to say to minimize failures is this tool I've found very useful: break down your goals or your improvement steps into the smallest possible steps. So small they seem ridiculous. Assume you are a completely useless person who needs to take baby steps. Take those baby steps, and when you see that you're taking them well, then you can take a slightly bigger one and a little bigger.

If you're chaos and you burn the food, what you have to do is not try to do it right from the start. It's to say: I'm going to put the food on and I'm going to stay here, next to the food, so it comes out right and doesn't burn. That's the goal. It sounds stupid, because we all tend to think: I'm adult enough to go to my room and not have the food burn. But it's not true. Because we see that it's not true. We see that people forget about food and it burns.

So, whatever your goal is, break it down into the smallest steps necessary for the success rate to be ninety percent. And stick to it to the point where you say: this is ridiculously simple, but I'm going to start here. If you do it well and it genuinely is ridiculously simple, next time you make it a little harder. And you reach a point where you say: hey, this isn't so simple anymore. And you realize that that threshold of "this isn't so simple" is generally below what you thought you were capable of.

We all make a schedule and say: man, this is an amazing schedule, I'm going to be incredibly productive. And then we don't come close to sticking to it. Because we demand too much from ourselves. Make a schedule you can actually stick to. A schedule you know you're going to follow. And follow it. And if after following it for two weeks you see it's going well, then, if you see it's appropriate, bump up the workload a little.

We're terrible at estimating our own capabilities. If you start with easy, first psychologically you're going to feel like: okay, this is easy, but I'm doing it well. And we're making progress. But if we overcorrect, then psychologically we feel: I've failed, I'm a disaster, I'm no good. You're not a disaster. You just need a method. A progressive, differential, continuous method.

If you want to learn an acrobatic move, start with the most basic version of that move. Same with strength exercises. We all think we're very strong, very flexible, very everything. Then you try it and you injure yourself. Start with the basics. And once you can do the basics well, safely and consistently, move on to the next thing.

Start the first day with what you know you can do safely. That's my advice. Take care.
