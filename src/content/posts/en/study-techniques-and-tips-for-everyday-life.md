Today I want to talk about study techniques, something I think a lot of people need and that nobody teaches you properly in school or university.

The first thing I think is important to say is that most people study badly. Not because they're stupid, but because nobody has explained to them how learning works. Studying is not reading and rereading. Reading and rereading is one of the least efficient techniques there is, even though it's the most used.

The brain learns through active retrieval. That is, you learn much more when you try to remember something than when you're reading it. This is what's called the testing effect. If you want to learn something, close the book and try to explain it from memory. That's what consolidates learning.

Related to this is the Feynman technique, which consists of trying to explain what you've learned as if you were explaining it to a child. If you can't explain it simply, you haven't understood it yet. That's the signal.

Another important concept is spaced repetition. Don't study everything in one day. Study a bit today, review tomorrow, review in a week, in a month. Forgetting curves show that knowledge consolidates much better this way than doing a study marathon the night before the exam.

Rest is also fundamental. The brain consolidates memory during sleep. Not sleeping in order to study more is counterproductive in the medium term.

Another thing that helps a lot is studying in the same spot whenever you can. The brain associates the environment with the mental state, and if you have a dedicated place for studying, just entering that place puts you into concentration mode.

And finally, something that doesn't get said enough: start. Procrastination is the biggest enemy of studying. And the way to fight it is to start even when you don't feel like it, even when you're not in the mood, even when you're tired. The first five minutes are the hardest. Once you start, inertia works in your favor.

This isn't rocket science. These are things that are known, backed by research, and that almost nobody applies. Apply them.
