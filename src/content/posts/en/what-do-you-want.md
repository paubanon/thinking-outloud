What do you want? It's been a while since I've posted anything on this channel and I'm going to kick off the return to autumn and to philosophical thinking with this topic. The importance of goals and setting them.

I think one of the reasons we have such a high success rate in Enso Movers programs is because the first thing we do is set goals. Before anything else we ask people: what do you want? What would you like to be able to do? And we set some goals and based on those we put together a plan considering your current situation. It might sound completely obvious. Any reasonable person you ask will say: well, of course you need to set goals. But my experience tells me people aren't good at setting goals. People don't think about having goals. People put off setting goals as long as possible.

And not just with training. I think training is a particularly useful tool for changing your mindset with respect to life because it's tangible. The effort is direct. Here and now I put in the effort and the reward is direct: tomorrow I lift more weight. It's straightforward, clear, and measurable. However, in other areas of life it's more complicated. How much have I improved my social skills? How attractive am I compared to last year? How much have I improved my career prospects? It's hard to quantify and the effort dilutes. And all of this — the lack of quantification, the lack of sticking to your intentions — all comes from a lack of goals.

Because if you have goals you can make a map. And I've talked many times on this channel about the importance of having a goal and a specific map with respect to dopamine. Dopamine, the neurotransmitter of motivation, is produced when you advance toward a goal you value. Not when you reach it. Not just from setting the goal. It's produced when you take an action that moves you toward a goal you value.

So the first thing is to set a goal. What do I want? What life do I want? How do I want to live? How much do I want to earn? Where do I want to work? Who do I want to work with? Who do I want to spend time with? Who do I want my friends to be? What kind of person would I like to be in the future? All of those are goals that the vast majority of people never ask themselves. They move through life wherever the wind takes them and with any luck end up on some shore. But you're not going to end up on the shore you want if you don't decide in advance.

Why don't people set goals? One, people don't know what they want. Many people come to us at Enso Movers and when we ask about their goals, the answer is very vague or they don't even know and they're thinking about it for the first time. And it's a hard question, because it forces you to stop and reflect: where am I? What would I like to be? And when you have that, you might realize that what you are and where you're heading doesn't match who you want to be. And that's a difficult moment.

I think there may be situations where a lot of people are in a position where it's too late to change. After a certain age, a certain stability, a certain security, you can no longer hit the reset button. That's why I think it's very important to ask yourself these things from the beginning. What do you want? Where do you want to be? What do you want to do? This doesn't mean living in a fantasy world where everything you desire and work toward a little will come true. It's possible that you set a goal, work toward it, and don't progress. That can happen. But if you don't set it and don't make a map, you definitely won't get there in any case.

Another reason people don't set goals is because as soon as you set one, you're defining the conditions for failure. If you say to yourself — using the concrete training example — I want to achieve this goal and to do that I'm going to do this program of this many sessions, this many sets, this many reps... Every time you fail, every time you skip a session, by having a plan you feel bad. You feel like you're failing the goal. And if you don't have a plan, training one day or seven: so what? There's no overall framework, no direction to aim toward. So setting a goal ties you to frustration. And that's hard to carry, especially when you don't have one goal but twenty across different areas of your life.

And I also think people avoid setting goals because it's a form of vulnerability. If you show what you want, that person — or you yourself — has a certain power over you: you know what you don't have and what you want. Saying that you want to advance in an area and that you're not happy with yourself in that area takes quite a bit of maturity and solidity to accept.

So what to do? Think and write. Stop and say: what small thing in my life produces frustration? Where is there something nagging at me that I've been looking away from? That's the area where I'd put the first effort. Take that. It can be work-related, emotional, related to personal relationships, physical, behavioral. Whatever. How would I be satisfied? What kind of person would say: this is how I'd like to be? And you think it through, write it down, detail it.

You don't need to detail it to the millimeter, but be clear that if you want to be a physicist, you'll need to do an undergraduate degree, a master's, probably a PhD. That's the path. And it's possible that in walking it you realize that's not what you want. That can also happen. But that's okay, because there's value in the journey. Not just in the final goal. In the walking there's a lot. And there are processes that are more important than the final reward. So it's necessary to have set that goal, to have walked it, to be able to say: this isn't what I want. Now you know one thing you don't want.

At Enso Movers lots of people ask us for one-arm pull-ups, planche, one-arm handstand... And they ask with an enthusiasm that always surprises me. And all I can think is that they're not truly aware of how much work those goals require. Once you lay it out for them, many people say: no, I want to get it training three times a week. And then you have to tell them: then you don't want it. Well, in the same way, there are jobs you think you want until you're actually there.

An example that's always struck me as funny: people who criticize those at the top of companies or political groups with a certain envy, sort of saying, look at them up there earning so much money. When in reality many of those people are doing it by sacrificing their entire life. A consultant can earn five, six, seven thousand euros a month, but working sixty to eighty hours a week, with high levels of stress and a heavy demand for mobility. Are you willing to do that? A lot of people would try it for a month and say: I can't do this. Then you don't want it.

Always be willing to change the plan, change the route, change the goals. Don't change them arbitrarily, but keep a small process running in the back of your head where regularly, every so often, you say: is this what I want? What's the next step? Where does it lead? How do I feel about it?

It's very important to set goals in order to aim toward them and make progress. We're terrible at this.
