Today we're going to talk about a book called The Mind Is Flat, by Nick Chater. I have to thank Ramón Nogueras for the talk he gave with the same title for opening up this rabbit hole for me. It's an idea I had briefly toyed with a few times but had never really gone deep on, and it's one of those books that has radically changed the way I think the brain works, the way I perceive myself and perceive others. It's been a long time since a book did that to me.

The central idea is that there is no depth in our person. There is no subconscious self from which our actions emerge. There is no coherent internal entity we can ask things and that will answer us. The idea that each of us is like an iceberg, with a visible tip and below it a great unknown mass that has to be explored throughout life — well, it turns out the evidence is completely contrary to that idea. The evidence tells us that only the tip is all that exists. Everything else the brain makes up each time you ask. The brain turns out to be a master of improvisation that, at every instant, gives you what you ask for. But what it gives you was not there before.

Let's start with the visual field. We are only capable of observing in a sharp and colored way an extremely small region of our visual field. Extremely small. Corresponding to, let's say, four or five words at a typical reading distance. When we want information from another point in our environment, our brain makes an extremely rapid eye movement, looks, and returns. And because it can gather this information instantly at any moment, it creates the illusion that you were already seeing it. The brain makes it up in every moment. It constructs our visual field out of patches, giving us the illusion that it was already there.

Experiments with pupil trackers show that a person can be shown a text made up of x's and @ symbols, where only the words they're looking at at that precise moment are coherent text. As the person moves along reading, the text appears. Not a single person noticed that the text was made of symbols. The same idea applies to the feel of clothing. Until I mentioned it, none of you were perceiving the pressure, the sensation it generates. In that moment, you quickly jumped from one to another. Creating the perception, the illusion that you were already perceiving it. But that's not true. You only perceive what you pay attention to. And you can only pay attention to one thing at a time.

This connects to multitasking. You can't do several things at once. Your brain processes one unit of information at a time. One by one. The feeling of multitasking is an illusion created by the fact that it does it very fast. I like to think of it like a video game where the main character moves through the map and the computer doesn't render the whole map at once because that takes too much processing, but only renders the cone in which you can interact. The moment you turn around, the world is rendered again and what was there has stopped existing.

The same happens with our internal beliefs. We all believe we're coherent people, rational beings, that we have a set of intrinsic values from which our actions emerge. It turns out that when you look carefully and try to find those fundamental values, they're as volatile and illusory as our visual field.

They ran an experiment where they took people from the left and right of the political spectrum and gave them a questionnaire arguing for or against different policies. Some time later they returned their questionnaire to them, but with the answers swapped, and said: argue for your answer here. A large number of people didn't realize the questionnaire didn't reflect their internal values and instead created a rationalization for why they had answered that way. Your brain invents a story consistent with the label it has of itself.

Another example: if any of you have kept a diary and look back at it a few years later, the most normal reaction is to say: who is this idiot? I feel zero identification with this person. Because we evolve throughout life, we change our thoughts, and we don't perceive when that change happens. And it's not just over time. As the book Behave explains, if you put a putrid smell in a room and ask people questions about racial groups, people become more racist. Because the part of the brain through which racism is mediated is related to disgust. Our ideas are not fixed, they change according to the situation, the context, whether you've slept more or less, what the room smells like. And if at some point you're presented with evidence of your incoherent response, you make up a reason why that's not the case.

Memory isn't what we think it is either. Many of us have the feeling that memory is something fixed, that I can remember a moment in my life, I can recall it, bring it to my conscious mind. The evidence clearly shows that we manipulate our memories every time we bring them forward. To the point that false memories exist. Cases where the work of a psychotherapist has induced in a client the idea that they suffered sexual abuse from their family members, and the person is completely convinced and can tell you details of the event that don't actually exist. What the brain does is make it up in the moment you ask for a detail. It puts it there as if it was already there.

Picture a tiger. Create a very vivid visual image, with as much detail as possible. Do you have it? Tell me: how many stripes does the tiger have? Can you count them? What are the stripes on the legs like? Are they horizontal or vertical? Does it have stripes on its face? If you suddenly realize you can't answer these questions, don't worry, because it's normal. The brain patches imagination. It gives you the feeling that you're imagining a super vivid tiger, but the moment you ask for a detail your brain stalls. It was making up the story that you were visualizing a tiger, and that's what it does with your own sensations: it invents them at each moment, layer after layer of patches.

There's something very interesting when this connects with meditative practices. One of the things you perceive when you meditate is: where does thought come from. When you stop doing anything and thoughts suddenly start to appear, where do they come from? You can't perceive where they come from. And when you observe the observer, when you ask: who is this that is observing the thoughts? — when you do this with enough attention you realize there is no self that observes. The current self is nothing more than the sum of my current sensory experiences and my current thoughts. That's what my current self is made of. Nothing exists beyond that. The whole illusion that there's something deeper, that there was more before or will be more later, is the illusion of asking and the brain answering very quickly as if it was already there.

There's also something neuroscientists call the default mode network: when you're not paying attention to anything, what is the brain doing? It turns out it has a very particular kind of activation, different from when you're concentrating, which is what they call diffuse thinking. When you go for a walk and don't put on music, don't put on a podcast, aren't talking to anyone, the brain uses that extra capacity to process ideas, concepts, situations in the background. And the moment something there clicks, it launches it onto the screen of perception and we have the feeling that something has emerged from nowhere. The eureka moment. This means that if you're paying attention to something, if you're looking at your phone, if you're listening to music, your brain won't be able to do this diffuse thinking exercise that is generally more creative and more open. And that's why it's so important to walk, to cook with nothing on your mind, to have empty spaces, to meditate.

One might think all of this is a big problem, because much of what exists in our society is based on the idea that we're coherent entities, that there's a self inside. One might think of psychoanalysis, where you try to go deep within yourself to learn who you really are and what you really want. When it turns out the evidence says no such thing exists. You're no more real or deep than a character in a book.

However, there's no need to throw everything out. Even though an introspective practice won't reveal a hidden inner self, it can make you aware of your default thoughts, your habits, those habits that aren't aligned with the person you want to be. The brain is a collection of habits, one after another. So it's very important to regularly stop and think about what your mental habits are. What's my default mode? What do I think of myself? How do I behave with the people I love? How do I behave with myself? Is that default way of thinking, that default behavior, the kind of behavior I want to have? Is it aligned with my goals, my ideals, the person I'd like to be? By analyzing this you can force new habits and bring to the conscious screen those behaviors you want to change.

So even though psychoanalysis and the whole idea of searching for an inner world make no sense, there is a lot of value in an introspective practice: looking, thinking, and deciding in a rational and strategic way what the best behavior is to get the outcomes I want in life. And this aligns very much with the only form of therapy that has scientific validity, which is cognitive-behavioral therapy: change your habits, and that will change everything you need to change in your life.
