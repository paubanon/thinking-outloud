Today I wanted to talk about writing. I've been writing a lot lately, both for the master's and for Enso Movers. I've put in many hours on writing and learned a lot from it. And I've also been digging into how to write, which is far from trivial. People think they know how to write, think writing is sitting down and writing, but it's a very complex task. Very rewarding, but very complex.

On top of sitting down to write, I'm dedicating time to learning how to write. To learning how to write the way I want to write. And among the people who have come up, someone I'd already had on my list, is Steven Pinker — along with a couple of articles on writing, clarity, and science.

The central point of both ideas is thinking about why there's good writing and bad writing. And how does that happen? A question that might seem a bit strange, right? You might say: well, good writing is writing that conveys the ideas it wants to communicate well, or that evokes the emotions it wants to evoke, or that uses literary devices well, has good structure, is coherent. Sure, we all know that from school. But the question goes further. It's why those tools, why those structures, make us understand certain texts better than others. Why one way of writing makes certain ideas stick in our brain and others don't, even when the information is the same and only the way it's structured changes.

The hypothesis that seems most logical to me is that our brain has an innate structure for understanding, for extracting information from the environment. It has, let's say, slots where ideas and concepts have to fit. Writing well is shaping your ideas and arguments so they fit that innate brain structure produced by evolution, so that your brain retains the information as efficiently as possible.

It strikes me as an incredibly elegant theory. Because what you're saying is that you're taking into account the biology of the organ responsible for understanding and creating knowledge, so that when you write, that information is absorbed into the reader's mind as quickly and easily as possible. So that it costs the reader no effort to understand your information. Because when you grab a piece of information and it doesn't fit together, you have to work to make sense of it because the structure the author used isn't suited to your systems of understanding. You have to take that information, break it down, pull it apart, and reassemble it correctly. That's when someone reads something and says: hold on, what is the author trying to say? And then goes: okay, they mean this, this, and this.

If an author is good — and good here doesn't just mean making it pretty — good at the structural level of the text: the ideas, where they land, the commas, sentence length, order, visual sections within the text... all of that makes the information require minimal conscious processing and go straight to comprehension. And I find it beautiful. And I find it artistic, in the deep sense of the word. Because it requires a craftsman's work: you write, read, rewrite, change, organize at different levels. At the level of: is this the word I want to use? Is the comma where I want it? How coherent is this sentence within this paragraph? Are these paragraphs in the right order? Doing all of that requires polishing and repeating and going over and getting the details right. You literally feel like you're sanding wood, adding little touches here and there. And it's a very satisfying feeling.

One might think writing is nothing more than putting your ideas down. I've done a school paper and writing is just sitting down and getting what you think onto the page. But that couldn't be further from the truth: you have to put your ideas down and you have to choose how. And there lies the art.

Steven Pinker is an experimental psychologist specializing in language and he talks about this, I think, in one of his books I haven't read yet, but it's on the to-read list. I came across him through The Blank Slate, which I've discussed in other videos. And if you stop to analyze not just what he says, which is great, but how he says it, the structure of the book and so on, you realize he writes very well, that he's put tremendous attention into how things are structured. It's a little bit difficult to absorb at times because the argument is very tightly refined and threaded, and he occasionally uses words that are a little complex for average English. I'm reading it in English and it's a bit challenging — one of those books that's very dense with information. I need to read one or two sections, stop, let it ferment, the next day read another two. It's taking me a long time, but I'm learning an enormous amount.

This brings me to the next point: why use paper books instead of ebooks.

Part of how our brain structures information is spatial. Our brain organizes information hierarchically in the form of schemas: I have this main argument, with this point, this point, and this point, based on these conclusions. In the form of a schema or hierarchy. And for organizing that, there's nothing better than a visual structure.

When we read a nonfiction book, an argumentative book, in which something is argued from different angles, what your brain does is: this first paragraph on this side is this first argument, this second paragraph is this second argument, and all of this is part of the set of chapters, which forms part 3 of the book, which argues about the political implications of the discovery of human nature — to use something from Pinker's book. When you read a paper book, your brain structures the information that way. It uses the spatial layout of ideas on the page and in the book to create mental schemas. This is in a research article whose author I can't remember, so if anyone's interested, let me know in the comments.

When you pick up an ebook, an ebook doesn't have that structure. It has a flow of text. It starts and it's floating text. You can resize it, reshape it. So your brain is unable to organize the information correctly and you don't absorb it. And it's literally very curious because you read it, you understand it, it makes sense, but when you compare the level of comprehension between a written text and text on a screen, you retain the screen text less. You're less capable of having clearly in mind: this idea, this idea, this idea, connected by these arguments that lead me to this conclusion. That structure you can't achieve with an ebook.

Books like The Blank Slate, for example, have the book divided into four or five parts, each part has three chapters, each chapter is divided into sections with a symbol that signals a topic change. That doesn't happen in an ebook. I tried reading it in an ebook in Spanish at some point and didn't understand it. I'd read it and think yeah, yeah, yeah, and then try to think it through and say: I don't understand what it's telling me, what is...

A very clear example: imagine you pick up a book you haven't touched in a while and you want to read a specific passage, an idea where you think where was that? And your brain knows: it was around the middle of the book, roughly in the lower left corner, because you remember that when you were reading it, you were on that side. And in thirty seconds you've found the page. If the book didn't have that physical structure, it would be very hard to find that passage. And that's what happens with an ebook: even if you know it was around the middle, your inability to flip quickly through pages makes it very hard. Either you use the search function or it's extremely difficult to recover fragments.

Now, there is a case where ebooks and audiobooks work really well: books that don't have this kind of structure — meaning novels. A novel on an ebook is fantastic. Why? Because it doesn't have that structure of this paragraph is for this, this one is for that, and in your head you have to take these three paragraphs and combine them to reach a conclusion. It's a constant linear flow of what's happening to the hero or heroes. Because it's completely linear, your head doesn't have to form a complex structure. For these cases an ebook is very convenient: you start reading, keep reading, and understand it very well.

I still prefer paper because it has a charm and a higher level of immersion, but I use the ebook a lot for books I want to read but don't care much about having in physical form. So maybe the debate today about ebook versus paper isn't which is better but which for what? And anyone who's studied at university level knows this. You know you can't study on a computer. People pay money to print something they could look at on a computer. They say things like I just prefer paper, I study better on paper, without thinking about why. And the answer is that your brain has biological structures for understanding information, and those structures involve spatial hierarchy. When you pick up a physical object, the information is structured spatially, your brain organizes it, and you retain it as knowledge — not memorization. As in, in your brain you have diagrams of this idea, this idea, this idea, argument, argument, argument, conclusion, and the lines connecting the ideas are relatively clear. That you can't do with a digital format, or it's much harder.

Audiobooks don't have that spatial structure either, because you're not using a physical object where the information is structured. It's like trying to use your arms to run. They're not designed for that. You can learn to do a handstand and run on them, sure, but you're not going to run as fast as using your legs. Because legs are designed for it.

Coming back to connect with the beginning: this is what a good writer has to achieve. They have to understand all of this and structure the information with the reader's brain in mind. And part of the art comes not just from this, but from understanding how a non-specialist reader will see that information. It's very common for the person writing an article or book to skip very simple steps because they're obvious to them. You've spent months working on a topic, you know it inside and out, and it's obvious to you that one thing follows from another. But for the reader it's not. So part of the art of writing well is putting yourself in the reader's shoes and understanding how to structure those ideas so their mind absorbs them with the minimum possible effort.

This leads me to why writing is so important — why in your head or why speaking. Speech is similar. Steven Pinker is great at giving talks, actually. The ideas are super clear and well-structured, he makes it engaging with touches of humor exactly where they belong. The way he speaks, the way he gives examples, how the slides are designed, how the transitions appear so they don't distract you. It's great. And it's really cool to watch something like that not just paying attention to what he's saying but to how he's saying it.

Because as human beings we work through the negative: we notice when something grates, when you think god this is boring. But when something slides into your brain without friction, you don't notice that that's what's happening. You don't appreciate coming home to a clean house. You only notice it when you come home and there's something disgusting in the middle of the floor.

And when writing, something that's very useful is that it forces you to take all that jumble of ideas and arguments that you have more or less clear in your mind, that you think you understand, and put them on paper. And putting them on paper forces you to have their structure very clear: what ideas do I have? How are they connected? What arguments take me from one to the other? What conclusions do I draw? How do I order them logically? And sometimes it surprises you, because you realize you didn't have the ideas as clear as you thought. Maybe you're connecting ideas enthusiastically, saying: this leads from here through this through this, and suddenly you realize this one idea is floating and doesn't come from any other, and you realize there's a gap you have to fill and defend. That's why it's important to put your ideas out there and talk about them. That's why this channel works like therapy for me, in a manner of speaking.

As advice for those of you who want to write: tip number one, read on paper. And tip number two: it's very hard to write, it's really off-putting to sit down in front of a blank page. The answer is: don't start, just write. To get past that initial barrier, simply start writing. Don't agonize over the form, over whether that's the sentence you want at the beginning, over images, format, tabs, whether you're repeating something, whether the sentence is well-ordered. Don't worry about that. Write, vomit your ideas onto the page in a more or less logical order. Zero attention to fonts, to all that. Just content. What you're doing is picking up the clay and slapping it on the table.

Before that, make yourself a quick outline: I want my text to have this section, and this section is divided into this and this, and this other one into this and this. Then you dump it out non-stop. And then you read what you've done, refine it, fix the spelling, check if the sentences are where they should be within the paragraph, if there are repeated words, if the paragraphs follow the order you want. You do that two or three times. I know it's tedious, but you're kneading the clay, shaping it. And each time more. At the end you're adding the fine details, making tiny marks, polishing, smoothing edges. And in the end you get a work of art.

This is how I've been writing lately, because it's the first time I've had to write texts this long and complex. It's enormously satisfying to finish, because you have the feeling of having created something through effort, something you can read afterward and say: I'm proud of what I've made. The more complex the text, the harder it is to create this. But I can assure you that if you keep all of this in mind, writing stops being torture and becomes a pleasure. I want to write, the way you might say I want to carve wood.

Of course, when you write: deep work. No distractions, no music, you sit in silence and write. After two or three hours your brain will no longer be functioning, you pull your head out of the deep work and rest.

I hope you found this interesting. I find this topic fascinating, because it's a very illustrative example of how culture comes from biology. Because everything comes from biology. And how what seems like merely culture — because it's a good text — can be traced all the way back to the innate structure of our brain. Which is not a blank slate. Take care.
