I've been thinking about something that worries me for a while now. We've turned productivity into a religion. And like every religion, it has its dogmas, its rituals, and its heretics.

The dogmas are clear: more is better, free time is wasted time, if you're not optimizing something you're failing. The rituals too: the perfect morning routine, the task management apps, the methods with English names that promise to revolutionize how you work.

And the heretics are those of us who sometimes ask: what for?

I'm not against being productive. I'm against turning productivity into an end in itself. Because when you optimize for the sake of optimizing, when you measure your day by the number of tasks completed and not by how you feel at the end of it, something has broken.

I've met incredibly productive people who are deeply unhappy. And I've met people who apparently don't do much but who live with a peace I wish I had.

The problem is we've confused being busy with being valuable. And it's not the same thing. Not at all.

I think real productivity isn't measured in tasks completed but in questions answered. Am I doing what I want to do? Am I going where I want to go? Or am I just running very fast in a direction I never chose?

Sometimes the most productive thing you can do is stop. Sit down. Think. And decide whether the race you're in is yours or someone else's.

That's it for today. No neat conclusion, no list of tips. Just a question rattling around in my head that I wanted to share.
