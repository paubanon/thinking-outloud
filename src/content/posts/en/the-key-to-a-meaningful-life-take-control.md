I was here talking with Pablo Vázquez, my colleague at Enso Movers, and we were working through a really interesting reflection that I want to put into words. I wish I'd hit record on my phone. Actually, maybe in some future video I will, because the conversations are incredibly good. Not just my opinion — the statistics say so.

We were talking about how the path to happiness is not comfort. It's total and absolute ownership of your life. Meaning: being in control of your life, doing what you know you need to do, even when you don't want to.

And that's a very hard thing to do, because we constantly sabotage ourselves. Taking ownership of your life means taking responsibility. And being responsible for your life — knowing that all your actions have consequences and you're the one responsible for those consequences — is hard. As an individual you are the one to blame — the creator, the one accountable — for your actions. If something goes well, it's your doing. If something goes wrong, also. And that puts a lot of weight on the individual, and society today makes it so easy to dodge that weight that we often skip out.

And then we feel bad, because we've shirked our responsibility, we've lost control of our lives. And not having control of your life is what makes us unhappy, because you notice you're at the mercy of the wind, that you're not getting where you want to go, that you're not reaching your goals. And the answer is simply that you need to take the reins.

This might sound like a motivational sermon, but it's true. Deep down you know what you need to do to get where you want to go. You know. Do I want to get there? Is this taking me there? No, don't do it. Does this other thing take me there? Yes. Is it uncomfortable? Yes. Tough luck. It's that simple.

Our brain is a magnificent weapon of self-sabotage, of dodging responsibilities, because it doesn't like being responsible, it doesn't want to feel like it's your fault if you fail, and it's very afraid of failing because failing makes you lose value. Or you have the idea that failing will diminish you socially and personally. But that's not true. The truth is that failing is just another stage of learning. The key is to fail with a learner's mindset: I failed, why did I fail? Well, because of this. So I'll fix it.

When I went to Barcelona — this is a detour into personal history — I was going to do a master's degree there, and following a pile of problems with my landlord, which eventually led to me calling the police, I couldn't do the master's in Barcelona and had to come back after a week and a half. And what could initially seem like embarrassment, because you've left and told everyone you're leaving, and in the end you had to come back, I felt no embarrassment at any point. I had done everything possible to be there and it hadn't worked out. In that case it wasn't my responsibility, I'm not going to stress about it.

But when I went to Germany that year, I was scared out of my mind. I constantly wanted to call the whole thing off. I'd say: you're in Elche, you're with your family, you're with your dog, you're teaching classes, you're making money... why are you going to do a two-year master's in another country? No, no, no, stay here. And deep down, deep deep down, I knew those were bullshit excuses. They're excuses to avoid taking responsibility for your life. If you want to get a master's in astrophysics, you want it, shut up and do it. Face the consequences, be an autonomous human being, capable of handling whatever life throws at you. And it's going to be hard, and you'll feel uncomfortable, and there will be nights you cry because you'll miss your family, and you'll feel completely lost in a country that isn't yours, far away, knowing you're not coming back for three months, with two years ahead of you. But it's your responsibility. Grab it and do it.

And the same has happened with Enso Movers, with getting my driver's license, with starting this channel. Fear of failing, fear of what people will say, fear of not being good enough. But you have to pause — after a good night's sleep, after eating well, without a mobile phone for 24 hours — and think about it seriously: what do I want? Do I want this? Do I want to invest this time in my future? Yes. Do it.

As strange as it may seem, taking ownership of your life is what will give it meaning. I've never felt completely happy when I've had too much free time. When I came back from Barcelona, for a few weeks it was great, I'd think, ugh, nice, vacation. But then I felt stuck, useless, with no purpose. And the only moments I noticed I was doing something I loved were the days when I was going full throttle: in the morning I had to study, then driving practice, then four hours of private lessons, then train. Running from one place to another, on a tight schedule. When I had a life full of things, goals, responsibilities, I'd think: damn, I love my life.

And that's the key: being totally and completely responsible for your life. An ideal job that gives you a lot of free time, which might seem like it will give you the life you want, probably won't. Because you'll feel empty, purposeless, with a life that does nothing for anyone, that has no meaning. You can't underestimate the importance of stability, of work, of taking responsibility. Responsibilities are uncomfortable, but they're what gives life meaning.

And the same applies to relationships. These days free love is very fashionable, not putting a label on it, not wanting to set limits because you don't want to take responsibility for the fact that the other person has strong feelings for you and it's your fault if you hurt them. That freedom has a price: you're unable to build a relationship with meaning, with depth. When the other person shows you their vulnerabilities, you are responsible for their emotional stability. You're responsible if you hurt them. And that responsibility is a burden. You have to carry it. A lot of people today don't have the guts to carry it. Don't have the guts to say, this is a relationship, we can trust each other, we're not going to hurt each other. And that, in return, gives you a far deeper connection and a trust you won't have with anyone else, except your closest friends, who you can count on one hand.

Think about which areas of your life you're not taking responsibility for, how you're avoiding them, how you're sabotaging yourself. And take ownership of them. Be responsible for your words. And that way, over the years, you'll start completing the projects you've set out to do. Imagine if you do this for five years: if you stop postponing every project, if you tackle it, and then the next one, and then the next. Ten or fifteen years from now you'll be a much more developed human being, better educated, more responsible, with a life that feels much fuller.

So think about which areas you're avoiding responsibility in. Take the reins of your life.
