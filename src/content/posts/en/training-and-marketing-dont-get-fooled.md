Hey everyone. As you can see, it's an absolutely gorgeous day here in Heidelberg and there's no way anyone's studying in this. I was having a productive morning and I thought: screw it, why not take a minute and record a video?

This started because this afternoon I wanted to go climbing and the soles of my climbing shoes were a bit dirty. So I started cleaning them, and I stopped to think that the shoes I have are the cheapest ones in Decathlon, or the second cheapest.

That brought back to mind a thought that always makes me laugh every time I go to buy sportswear: consumerism in sports, and especially running, is a funny thing. A lot of people get obsessed with buying gear to insane extremes because the marketing promises it's going to improve your performance. And in most cases that's simply false.

When you go to Decathlon or any sports store, the advertising is designed to make it look like they're selling you a superhero's suit: a windbreaker jacket with fluorescent stripes that conform to your anatomy, and the model in the ad runs like a god, no red face, no getting winded. And you get the feeling that that's what you're missing. You think: "I'm out here grinding like a mortal and what I needed was a two-hundred-euro Garmin that tracks my heart rate to work in the exact interval and saves my lap times and uploads them to the computer and makes charts and graphs."

Look, we've only had that technology for a very few years. And up until then we already had very, very good athletes. If you're going to compete at the Olympics, fine, invest, because you want to be the best in the world. But if you want to run regularly to stay in shape and improve your times, don't spend money on little gadgets thinking that's what you're missing. What you're missing is training. When you reach the level athletes had in the eighties, then start refining. Because those little things are a very, very delicate refinement.

It goes in layers: first there's the bulk, which is training and nutrition. Then the programming gets a bit more complex. And then, at the end, you start with the fine-tuning: "If I adjust this shoulder position I can exert a tiny bit more force that lets me perform the set more effectively." But those are fine details. Pure fine details.

In running specifically, the whole shoe topic is its own thing — I'm a big advocate of minimalist footwear, but that's another point. The thing is: before spending more money on something, before going for the latest technology, the most amazing leggings that give you quad compression with a special rotation because they squeeze more here than there, all of that is nonsense. A piece of fabric on your thigh is not going to change your movement patterns. The tangential force a pair of leggings can exert compared to muscle force is completely negligible. Any gimmick about "muscle activation" is pure garbage.

What I mean is: before investing more money in that kind of stuff or thinking "I'm not progressing the way I'd like, let me buy something more," think it through carefully. With very basic stuff you can go incredibly far. You have to be aware of your level and what you actually need. My climbing shoes are the cheapest ones in Decathlon — why? Because I climb maybe four times a year at best. I don't have the level to get any benefit from a hundred-and-twenty-euro pair of shoes. If I put them on I'd probably say "wow, better grip." Of course they grip better. But is it worth spending a hundred euros more when I'm going to use them four times a year? I could use that money to pay for three months of gym membership and have access to full equipment.

I have two good examples. One is my friend Beppe, who ran really hard. He trained in a pair of futsal shorts someone had given him, a technical t-shirt, and the cheapest watch in Decathlon, ten euros, no heart rate monitor or anything. But he trained hard. Really hard. And when he went to popular road races, he'd eat everyone alive. Everyone who had fluorescent tights with little lights and the two-hundred-euro Garmin and the chip with whatever features. He'd eat them all.

The same thing happened when we went to an aquathlon — run, swim, and run again. We showed up with our swim trunks and a tank top. We got there and saw the triathlon teams with their sponsored triathlon kits, very serious-looking. We said: "Damn, they look like they've got real level." The race ended and my friend Pep and I finished fourth and fifth, and all the triathlon club people finished behind us. They were in a club, they had their kit, they had all their fine-tuning. But we had trained harder. That's all it comes down to.

What you need isn't more stuff. You don't need more money. With reasonably flexible clothes you can drag around and wear out, a pair of shorts and a technical t-shirt, or if not then train shirtless like I do, and that's it. My equipment: some rings which are the most expensive thing, forty euros; a couple of resistance bands, thirteen euros each; some parallettes, five euros; some wooden blocks, five euros. A hundred euros total over five years. And I have much more strength and a much higher level than a lot of people who spend a hundred and eighty euros outright on minimalist shoes.

Be aware of your level, what you need, and how far you can go with what you have. And when you've been grinding away at that discipline for four or five years, then you think about buying fine-tuning stuff.
