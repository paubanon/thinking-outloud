The other day I gave you a challenge: choose a YouTube video between 5 and 10 minutes long and watch it uninterrupted. Phone on another table, airplane mode. All tabs closed. Full screen. No comments, no related videos. Just being conscious that you're watching that video, finishing it, and then moving on to the next thing.

Many of you commented on how useful the exercise was for making you realize how distracted your mind is. And that's normal, that's what it was for. It's a very good exercise because it shows you how the internet shapes your mind for a kind of fractured attention where you're jumping from one thing to another. If it was hard for you to watch a 5 or 10 minute video without thinking about what to put in the comments or opening another app, what kind of brain are you building? How are you going to be able to sit down to read a book, to write, to work for 2 or 3 hours uninterrupted?

Your brain has neural connections that modify depending on demands. If your brain has a demand for concentrated, serious, deep work, it will get good at doing that. If you give it the message that what it has to do is jump from one task to another constantly, it will get very good at that. But that type of work produces no value, except in a few very specific jobs. When you want to generate something of value, you need time, effort, and concentration. And you have to teach your brain to do that, because you can't teach it to do both things at once.

Today I want to propose an extension of this exercise: for one week, everything you do, do it consciously. It's a pseudo-meditative practice; if you combine it with meditation it's going to be easier and you're going to see better results. What I want is for each thing you do throughout the day to be conscious and not interrupted or mixed with something else. If you're reading an article, you can read it straight through or read a section and check your phone, I don't care, but make it conscious.

When you cook, I want you to be thinking about how you're cutting the onion. About feeling the knife cut through it, the impact, the rhythm. To be paying attention to the smell, the sound the pan makes. When you write, paying attention to the shape of each letter, to what arguments you're putting down and why. When you eat, be eating, not looking at your phone until suddenly the plate is empty.

In all these activities, having your phone nearby is not allowed. Putting on a show is not allowed. Be single-tasking individuals for a week. If you're going to listen to music, sit down and listen to music, then stop it and go do something else. If you're going to check your phone, say "I'm going to check my phone because I want to reply to this and this." Be conscious of how easy it is to suddenly be looking at something different from what you intended. Because a notification comes up, you say "oh, I have to look at this too," and suddenly you stop and ask yourself: what am I doing? This isn't what I wanted to do.

I want you, when you're with someone, to be conscious of being with that person. Without your head at work or on your phone. Look them in the eyes and be conscious that you're enjoying that company. If you have sex, be conscious of the connection with your partner, of the intimacy. That way with all aspects of your life for a week. Brushing your teeth, the shower, everything.

A philosophical detail I like to see in this type of task: imagine you're a craftsperson. Think of yourself as one of those people who dedicate their lives to manual tasks and are masters at them. Think about the concentration, the attention to detail they put into their work. Think of yourself as a cabinetmaker who is trimming all the profiles of one of his wooden pieces. And think of yourself doing each thing in your day-to-day that way. Pay attention to detail when you write. Enjoy the act of cooking itself. Live your day-to-day as if you were a craftsperson. It's incredible everything you gain from it.
