Today I'm going to make a special video because it's a request from a friend who told me I've talked a lot about relationships on this channel, but not about breakups, about heartbreak. And I thought about it and said: she's right. It's not something I've covered much. So I want to address in this video my experiences and my tools, above all, for dealing with this.

I'll start with a warning, as always with relationship topics: relationships are extremely personal. A relationship is, I think, the most personal thing there can be, and therefore the breakup will depend a great deal on the relationship you had, on who you are, on who the other person is, on your previous experiences, your personality, whether you were the one who ended it, what the relationship was like before. All of that affects enormously how a breakup hits you and how you manage it. Let that be said up front. On the other hand, I'm not a psychologist or anything like that, so all I can do here is share my experiences, my thoughts, and my tools, which have worked for me and which might work for you too. Take it for what it is: a video about personal opinions based on what's worked for me, based on what I know about physiology, psychology, evolution, and my philosophical reflections.

One of the most important things is understanding that how the breakup goes will depend a great deal on what love means to you as a concept. If you're someone who has love figured as something fundamental, as something central in life, who has romantic love very deeply rooted within themselves, a breakup is going to be very difficult. Because for you, conceptually, love is a super important part of life, you give it a central place, and therefore a breakup means the possible loss of that ideal love.

I think romantic love is very problematic. It can be disabling and very counterproductive to conceive of love through the ideal that there is only one person in the world we are destined to be with, that love is the ultimate purpose of life, that without love nothing is worth anything, that love is won through enormous heroic feats, that it has to be extremely passionate... All that story we've been told in movies, in books, in songs has a historical reason, is primarily cultural, and has to be taken with great care because it leads to unhealthy relationships.

Personally, I come from that tradition, from that idyllic and romantic love that has been instilled in us, and I've been evolving. I've been exposing myself to relationships, to other ways of thinking, to other stories. I've thought about how I want love to be for me, how it's functional, how it forms a positive part of my life. And I've ended up with a theory of love where love is important — in the end personal relationships are fundamental in our lives — but it's a very humanized love. I fall in love with people, not with ideals. When I notice I'm starting to put someone on a pedestal, I try to bring them back down to eye level. We're on the same plane, they're a person, they have their good parts and they'll have their bad parts.

You can be in love and care for someone and be completely at ease without having to make grand gestures to prove it. A relationship can be totally healthy and have moments where there isn't that burning love. Maybe there's a moment where someone is very stressed and says: right now I'm not in a place to give and receive love, I need to focus on this project. And that's perfectly valid. In the same way, there are times we fall in love passionately and completely irrationally, and we have to recognize that it's beautiful when it happens, but sometimes it's with people who aren't good for us. So there's a part of love that has to be rational, intelligent and clear-headed to say: this person isn't good for me and, even though I care about them a lot, that's okay. There are many more people out there, there are more relationships, more possibilities.

So, how do you handle a breakup? I really like what I call the pillars theory, which consists, following this line of non-romantic love, of not centralizing love as the ultimate purpose of life. Life can be incredibly rich in all kinds of areas and the best thing you can do is diversify. Be a diverse person, with a rich and varied life, with different pillars. Key elements that support your life. For example, your family, your friends, your work, a hobby you dedicate time to, training, and a personal relationship. The more pillars there are, the more solid your base will be.

If you're someone who builds their entire life around a single pillar, when that pillar is taken away, your world collapses. On the contrary, if you have many pillars, when one wobbles or falls, you have the others. So what you have to do is, first, have pillars, and when one falls, put more weight on the others. When a breakup happens it's the moment to give more attention to friends, to throw yourself into hobbies, to give a push to work, to take better care of yourself, to train hard.

What to do in case of a breakup? The first thing is to make peace with it. Accept that the breakup has happened and that it's okay. That it is what it is and that life goes on. For me, I achieve that through regular meditation with respect to the breakup. Literally, when I find myself sad, one of the things that's proven least recommended but most useful to me is to sit and observe that sadness. Stop and think about how I'm feeling and say: I'm sad because this ended. Let's look at that sadness. Let's dig in, observe those feelings, where they come from, where they go, how they evolve, whether I'm feeding them. And you look at it. And when you voluntarily and properly confront it, you'll probably find peace. You'll be at peace for an hour or two. Then you'll be sad again. But you're at peace for a couple of hours. It's important to accept the breakup, not to ignore it, not to deny it, but with assertiveness and with compassion for yourself.

As a second point, not creating resentment when going through a breakup. Not demonizing the other person. Because it's very easy to focus on the bad things about that person, on how terrible they were, on everything they did wrong. Or to invent reasons. To try to rationalize negatively the relationship to create sides: one good and one bad, and the other person is the bad one. Understanding that life isn't like that, that it's not as simple as sides of good and bad, that everything is shades of grey. Accepting that the relationship had good moments and bad moments and that it ended for whatever reason. And being grateful for what you had. It's a chapter that got this far. I thought it would last longer, but it's ended. I still care deeply about that person, I wish them the best, I think we both did the best we could, and we move on with our lives.

Neither demonize nor idealize. The other extreme is also a mistake. We don't have to say: how wonderful that person was, I was going to have kids with them, we were going to get married, and look what a piece of garbage I am, now nobody will ever love me. Put things in balance. It was a person who had good things and bad things. And I'm a person who has good things and bad things. And we made it work. There were beautiful parts and bad parts. Let's not ignore either of them. Remember the bad moments too. And approach it from a positive perspective: what am I going to do with this now? Every breakup is an opportunity to find someone new.

Talk. For me it's super important. When there's a breakup, being open to dialogue. But if you notice that talking to that person pulls you back into the hole, then it's probably not the most ideal.

Be conscious and learn to cut contact when necessary. Learn to discover who you are without that person. You have to rebuild yourself without that pillar. And this is especially difficult and important in long relationships, when you've shared a lot of time, when that person has occupied a very large space in your life. Who are you without that person by your side? What are your interests? Who are your friends? What do you like to do? How are you capable of handling time alone?

Get your physiology in order. In situations where life is hard, dealing with it often means making sure your hormones and neurotransmitters are as tuned as possible, so you don't have a single extra ounce of unnecessary suffering. It's already enough to remember that you're going through a rough time without your hormones also being out of balance on top of it. Eat well, sleep well, train regularly, go outside every day, don't lock yourself indoors, don't waste time on things that aren't worth it like TikTok, Instagram, social media. Do things that are worth it. Keep a regular routine, maintain your personal hygiene, relate to others. All of that helps put your system in optimal mode and helps sorrows be less sorrowful. If you have a reason to get out of the house every day, you get yourself clean and looking good, you train, you talk to people, you eat well, you sleep well, you'll be sad, but you'll be a lot less sad than if you stay home in your pajamas all day with nothing to do but ruminate on that person. Cold showers. They help a lot in moments of deep low. Get under a stream of cold water and it clears your head in five minutes.

Strengthen your personal bonds. The research is very clear: having close personal bonds with people is extremely positive for psychology and for managing stress and grief. It's the moment to spend more time with your family, with your friends. It's important to share when you're not doing well, to let them know you're not okay, to be able to put it outside without shame. But at the same time, not to wallow in the mud. Don't meet up with people every day to tell them over and over again how bad you're doing. Do it for yourself, because it doesn't serve you, but also do it for them. Say it if you're not doing well. If at some point you're in a very deep low, you pick up the phone, call a friend, say: hey, I'm in a really bad place, let's talk for half an hour. No problem. We've all been there. And aside from that, do other things. Go play volleyball, go for a hike, go camping, go to the movies, try a new restaurant. Do things together that aren't sitting around looking at each other and lamenting. That will distract you, strengthen the bonds, and also reinforce the element of: there is life beyond this grief.

Do things. I'm not going to get tired of repeating it: do lots of things. Put yourself in situations where it's hard to be sad. I remember a breakup that happened when I was in Tabarca, a small island on the Alicante coast, with my family and my dog. In September. A paradise island, crystal clear water, still sunny, good temperature. And that's where a breakup happened. And even though I was sad, I was less sad than I would have been somewhere else. Because it's relatively hard to be very sad when you're on a paradise island with people you love, with your dog, with sun, eating well.

And let time pass. You have to accept that things are going to take their time and their space. The only comfort you sometimes have is that you have no control over it. You simply have to wait. You can wait by focusing on it and wallowing in the mud, or you can wait by doing other things. Know that no matter how bad you feel: tomorrow you'll feel better. And in a week you'll feel a lot better. And in a month you'll be way past better. Time heals everything, as they say. Be compassionate with yourself. Neither wanting to feel bad forever, nor wanting to feel good right now. Being completely at peace with the fact that you're feeling bad right now, and that it's okay, and that you'll feel better in time.

Right now you are the way you need to be. Tomorrow you'll be the way you need to be. And time will pass. Meanwhile, I'm going to live my life. I think that's a good way to have compassion for yourself and accept that a breakup is something almost all of us have been through. It's something that hurts, but it's something you learn from. It's something you come out of. It's not the end of the world. Life is, and will be, full of positive things, rich things, experiences to have, people to meet, butterflies in the stomach to feel. It's going to keep being full of things.
