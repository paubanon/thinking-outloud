Right, I wanted to make a quick video. I was reading an article about the effects of technology on our brain and how we're overfeeding it with technology. It talked about new therapies for treating attention deficits and how all of this seems to be part of a new wave of a large-scale problem in our cognition. Not as individual problems, but as: how many problems are there now of dementia, of attention deficits.

The article was going well until at the end the author started proposing that we redesign technology to help improve our cognition. He talked about how great it would be if there were an artificial intelligence that knew better than you what was good for you, that oriented a video game to boost your cognition, that gave you feedback on your cognitive state and taught you to maintain your attention.

And that's where he lost me. He lost me because I realized he'd fallen into the same trap everyone falls into when they look at training. And the trap is that we want someone to do it for us. And the answer is that nobody does it for you. You have to earn it. You have to do it yourself. There's no trick to it.

It reminds me, for example, of when a client asks me: do you have an exercise to learn to activate this muscle? And the answer, the vast majority of the time, is: concentrate on activating that muscle. That's it. Understand that your muscle isn't firing when it should be. Identify when it should fire. Focus on firing it. Focus. Repeat. Explore. Do it yourself.

By default, we want a gesture that we do and then we've learned to activate it. We don't want to learn to activate it. And it's the same mistake this author makes. There's no need to invent a complicated and wonderful technological system with artificial intelligence that learns to guide your attention where it should be. The author doesn't get it, in my view: you don't need a system of that scale and complexity. What people need is not a system that teaches them to direct attention. What people need is to learn to direct attention. To do exercises as stupid, subtle, and low-tech as identifying when your attention drifts from what it should be paying attention to, like a book or some studying. And saying: I got distracted. I'm coming back. You don't need an artificial intelligence to orient the virtual environment around you. You don't need a Google assistant to dim the lights when that happens.

What if instead of all this performance, all these exercises to dodge taking responsibility for ourselves, we told ourselves the truth? That the responsibility is to say: it's all reps. There's no trick to it. Do you want to improve your attention? Make the effort to improve your attention. Make the effort to identify when your attention drifts. Take back control. And focus. There's no trick in how to activate a muscle in an exercise beyond: what if you focus on activating that muscle? No trick, nothing fancy, no shortcuts.

It's all work. All of it. And I'm more and more aware of that.
