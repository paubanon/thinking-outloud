I've developed a set of rules for phone use that have worked well for me and I want to share them.

They're not universal rules. They're the ones I've built through personal experience and through reflecting on my relationship with technology. Each person will have to find their own.

The first is not looking at my phone first thing when I wake up. That first hour of the day is very valuable for thinking, for planning, for being present. Opening Instagram or messages as soon as you get up puts your mind in reactive mode from the start of the day.

The second is keeping my phone on silent most of the time. Notifications are interruptions. An interruption costs you much more than it seems, because you don't just lose the time the interruption takes, but also the time it takes you to get back to the level of concentration you had.

The third is not using my phone while I'm with other people. If I'm at dinner, in a conversation, in any social situation, the phone doesn't exist. This seems to me a matter of basic respect, but also of the quality of the experience itself. You're in two places at once and nowhere fully.

The fourth is not looking at my phone in bed. The blue light from screens interferes with sleep. And also, it's a habit that makes the bed associated with stimulation instead of rest.

The fifth is checking messages and email at specific moments, not continuously. This takes practice and sometimes generates anxiety at first, but it frees up a huge amount of mental energy.

The sixth has to do with what apps I have installed and with social media. I've been reducing the number of apps I have. If an app doesn't give me real value, I delete it.

I'm not telling you to do exactly this. I'm telling you to think critically about your relationship with your phone and to establish your own rules. Because if you don't set them, the phone sets them.
