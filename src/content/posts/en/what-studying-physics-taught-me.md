Today I want to reflect on what studying physics has taught me, beyond the actual content of the degree.

The first thing I want to make clear is that I'm not talking about physics as a scientific discipline — which is also worth discussing — but about what the physics degree as a process of formation gives you as a person.

A physics degree is hard. It's objectively one of the most demanding degrees out there. And that difficulty, properly understood, is a gift.

The first thing it teaches you is to deal with frustration. In physics there are problems with no easy solution, that require hours of work to advance an inch. And you learn to sit with that discomfort. You learn that the fact that something is hard doesn't mean you can't handle it. It simply means it requires more time and more effort.

The second thing it teaches you is to think in a structured way. Physics trains you to break complex problems down into their most elementary parts, to identify the relevant variables, and to build models. That capacity for thinking transfers to any area of life.

The third thing, and maybe the most important, is that it teaches you to live with uncertainty. In physics you never have absolute certainty. Everything is approximation, everything is model. And that gives you an epistemic humility that I think is fundamental for anyone who wants to understand the world honestly.

There are also things the degree doesn't teach you, or teaches you badly. For example, it doesn't teach you to communicate. It doesn't teach you to work in a team effectively. It doesn't teach you to build something. Those things you have to go find elsewhere.

But overall, I think studying physics has been one of the best decisions I've made, not because I want to work exactly in physics, but because the process of learning physics has shaped me as a person in a way that few other things could have.

If you're thinking about studying something hard and you're not sure whether it's worth it, my answer is yes. The difficulty is the point. Not in spite of it, but because of it.
