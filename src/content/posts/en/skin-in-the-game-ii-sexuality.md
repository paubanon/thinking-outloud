In the previous video I talked about the difference between action and intellect, between practical knowledge and theoretical knowledge. And I wanted to use sexuality as an example of this, because I think it's one of the fields where this difference becomes most apparent.

Sexuality is something that moves us deeply. At a biological, evolutionary level, sexuality is the force that moves us most. We are sexual beings. Everything we do, to a greater or lesser extent, has a sexual component. Seduction, status, money, power. All of it is tied to sexual attractiveness and reproduction. It's one of the parts of human nature that people most try to rationalize, to intellectualize. And I think it's one of the fields where people fail the most.

We live in a society that tends to desexualize, or tries to. Japan is the most extreme example. Japan has a very low birth rate. There's a phenomenon called herbivore men — basically men who have renounced active sexuality. Who are disconnected from their masculinity, from their sexual desire. And the result is a society that doesn't reproduce, that ages. And this is interesting because Japan is a very technologically advanced society. Very intellectual. Very rational. And at the same time it's producing a generation of men who have lost contact with their most basic nature.

The opposite exists too. There are theories that talk about masculine energy and feminine energy. David Deida in Way of the Superior Man talks about this. It's not about men being one way and women another in a rigid sense. It's about the existence of sexual polarity. And when that polarity exists, there is attraction.

A very interesting example of how sexuality operates at an instinctual level, below the radar of the intellect, is romance novels. Romance novels are consumed in their vast majority by women. They are written in their vast majority by women. And the masculine archetype that appears over and over in romance novels is the dominant, powerful man with an element of danger. A man who has that beast inside him, that animal character, that fire, that desire, but who controls it.

It's a pattern completely opposite to what you'd expect given the current social narrative, where men and women play equal roles, sexuality is a social construct, and what's valued is equality. I have very intelligent, very attractive friends, with their master's degrees in neuroscience and things like that, who have told me: I'm a strong, independent woman... but in bed I like being submissive. I like the other person to take charge. I like there to be that difference in roles at a sexual level.

I've heard feminists defend all this narrative about sexuality and then tell you: but I like assholes and I can't do anything about it because I like assholes. Watching a feminist lay out the whole narrative of gender as a social construct and then you put a man with that kind of profile in front of her and they melt. And you put someone who fits with their intellectual narrative and they're unable to feel attracted to him. I find it very amusing to observe this kind of behavior because what I've learned is that you have to judge people by how they act.

The Beauty and the Beast archetype illustrates this perfectly. The Beast is someone extremely large, masculine, animal. And through the love of Beauty, he becomes a human, a prince, who is still powerful, strong, muscular, handsome. But that animal sexuality has been tamed through love with the woman. It's a story with the sexual archetype stitched into it perfectly.

My experience with sexuality and romance is that women don't want a nice guy. And the closer you are to that, the worse your results will be. And the worse you'll feel about yourself. I've spent many years of my life without integrating sexuality into my person. Without letting that competitive drive, that cockiness, that swagger, that desire to show strength, to feel strong, powerful, that desire for dominance — which most men have — without letting it form part of who I am.

You look at little boys and they compete with their toy cars because mine is bigger and beats yours. And they do it as children who haven't even gone through the testosterone flood of puberty yet. And it's transcultural, throughout the entire world.

Sexuality is one of those things where I think you have to live it. You can rationalize all you want, but you have to live it. In the book Book of Puck, the author mentions that he's talked to university professors who knew everything about philosophy but had no idea how to approach an extremely attractive woman and express their sexual interest in her. And I assure you that those university professors, because they're human beings, feel very attracted to extremely attractive women.

A lot of people who study seduction get stuck in the thinking, in the reflecting, in studying more and more and going over things again and again. While what you need to do is live it. Get out there, play, try, experiment and decide then.

My experience is that the more masculine I am, the more I integrate my masculinity into myself, the more comfortable I feel with myself, the more at peace I feel with my desires, the more honest I feel with my thoughts. And the more I attract women. By a wide margin.

I laugh now at the over-rationalization of sexuality in society. Because people tell you one thing and act in a completely different way. Men like feminine women. And feminine women like masculine men. People act that way. And all advertising reflects that.

The advertising industry has understood that underlying sexuality in human nature and expresses it at the ad level because it knows it's there. If you put very attractive women in your products you'll sell more to men. And if you put high-status men in products for women you'll sell more to women. They can tell you whatever they want, but that works. Advertising has no interest in pushing society toward role stereotypes. It's only concerned with selling. If it sold more by selling tampons to men, it would tell you to put one in. And it works.

The same is true for children's toys. The wrong reading is that blue, trucks, missiles and superheroes are for boys because we want to impose that role stereotype. They've got it all wrong. If you take children and from a very young age give boys dolls and girls trucks, you discover that the boys make the dolls act like missiles. And the girls make families out of the trucks. It's stupidly obvious when you don't over-rationalize it.

Studies have been done where gender differences have been completely eliminated through culture. And what you get is an even more polarized situation. The famous Scandinavian paradox. There's a documentary about it I think called Brainwash. If you search for gender Scandinavian paradox brainwash I think it comes up. Fantastic.

Sexuality is something to experience. To live. You don't need to overthink it. You have to put it out there. Because action beats potential.

The more time I've spent acting and the less rationalizing, the more I've learned. And you notice the masturbatory tendency of the intellect to feed itself, to think about how it thinks, to believe it knows everything. When in reality it knows nothing. Because if your theories fall apart when you put them against reality, maybe you should think about whether those ideas are true or not.

We're reaching a point in society where intellect is valued far more than experience is valued. And I think that's a big mistake.

I really liked a post by Ido Portal in which he said: you want to debate training methods, nutrition? Take your shirt off. Show me what's underneath. Show me the path you've walked. Show me how much effort you've put in. Show me that your arguments have a solid foundation behind them, supported by reality, by the experimental test.

If you have an idea and you put it to the test and it doesn't work, you take your theory and throw it in the trash. You see that cutting carbs and doing strength training and eating unprocessed food makes people lose weight. Well, dare to tell your theory to go to hell.

What Quique told me after daring to experiment with all of this is: man, it's magic. My change in attitude, how my life has changed, how I feel. It's magic. And there are things you can't understand deeply if you don't experience them. I can verbalize it for you, I can detail it. But if you don't experience it, you don't understand it.

So I encourage you to dare to experiment with your life. To dare to take off your shoes and run barefoot through the forest. To dare to take ownership of your life and set aside intellectualizing — or develop it if you want, it's great. But dare to fill yourself with life. Dare to sleep on a mountaintop. Dare to get cold. Dare to come home soaked from running in the rain. And you'll learn much more about yourself than you could ever learn from watching YouTube videos.
