Hey everyone. So, I've had a rough few days. Rough in the sense that I've been tired, low on motivation, struggling to work. My body felt heavy. Physically I wasn't performing — it wasn't that I was sore. It was a more pronounced tiredness, like having fatigue, like someone had drained my energy.

I've been thinking about it and finally figured out why, and I wanted to share it with you. It was this feeling of: I need to work but I don't feel like it, I sit down, I work, but I'm not particularly productive either. I go to train with some drive but not much. I train and I gas out fast, each set is a struggle. Instead of going in like a beast saying: let's go, let's crush it. I'd go in feeling like: ugh, fine, the session. Just wanting to say: I want to go home and eat and lie down and rest.

Turns out I've been spending quite a bit of time with my girlfriend lately. And in all that time together, things run late and we end up going to bed a bit later than is usually normal for me. It's not dramatic, but I was losing about an hour, hour and a half of sleep every day. At first I didn't notice anything: I wake up fresh, go work. But this past weekend I noticed my legs were heavy. I said: I haven't recovered from Friday's session. And I realized it was the sleep.

Yesterday I managed not to see my girlfriend and I went to bed really early. Had a good shower, a good dinner, meditated and went to bed. I slept nine hours. And today I woke up and, exactly as I already knew, the world felt new. The world feels new. So does my drive. My motivation to go work, to go train, the number of ideas, the desire to take on the world, my plans, my goals, all at full speed, all my projects, full tank.

It was the sleep. It was the sleep. And it's incredible the effect it has on us. I've said it many times. Sleep is irreplaceable. If I had to choose one important thing for health, sleep. If you want to read more about it, there's a book called Why We Sleep by Matthew Walker. It's been translated. I can only recommend it. In it he compiles all the existing literature on how sleep affects everything. And sleep affects everything. That's the answer. At the metabolic level, hormones, inflammation levels, reflexes, driving ability, how you relate to others, your motivation, your physical performance. It affects everything badly if you're short on it and everything well if you have it.

We're not talking about gaining a little bit of fat if you don't sleep enough. No. We're talking about not having the energy to work, not having the energy to train, not having the motivation to advance at work, not feeling like socializing, being on edge with your partner, not dealing with problems properly.

In the same way that a 0.1% daily improvement compounded over 5 years turns you into a completely different person, a 0.1% daily decline, a 0.1% of not making the decisions you need to make, means that in 5 years you look back and you're not the person you wanted to be. Because, as we know, the person you want to be is built day by day. Step by step. That's why it's so important to invest in everything that feeds back into us positively, even just a little. Like sleeping.

You can say: I'm just tired, I can't afford to sleep, I have to do this and that. But not sleeping that extra hour means that all the other decisions of the day are worse afterward. Your attitude toward all your interactions and decisions, both with other people and with yourself, is worse. The work is worse. And when your boss says something, instead of using that feedback as fuel and saying: this wasn't where I want it to be, I raise my standards, I get on it, I know I can and I'll get it done. Instead, you resent it, you get angry. And that's not the way.

Same with training. You train, sleeping little means you go in without drive, that means you don't push yourself, you don't push yourself and you don't improve, not improving makes you lose the will to train, you start skipping it, you stop training, that makes you sleep even worse on top of it. And training becomes a burden, one more stressor, instead of a moment of activity and release.

Sleep is massively important. And it's very hard to identify. Because sometimes you say: it's the stress, it's just a complicated period right now. The more stress you have, the more important it is to sleep. Because stress, as I say a lot, is optional. A situation isn't stressful. You make a situation stressful depending on how you take it. So if you sleep badly, a stressor is going to upend your life, put you on high alert, make you want to throw everything out the window. But if you've slept well, that stressor stops being one and becomes a challenge. It's something you're eager to tackle, wanting to ask: will I be able to do this? I think so. And I'm going to do it as well as I can.

What do I recommend? First, prioritize sleep.

On a practical level, some tips on how to sleep well. First: train and get sun exposure during the day. That signals to your body that daytime is for activity and nighttime is for sleep. Creating a big difference between day and night, in terms of activity, social interaction, and input, means that during the day you're more active and during the night you're calmer.

Meditate. An incredible tool for improving sleep quality. On one hand, because it teaches you to approach things differently and that means you have a lower stress level. And more concretely, it teaches you to guide the focus of your attention. It teaches you to decide what you center your mental energy on and what you don't pay attention to. It's learning to observe your consciousness and direct it. In terms of sleep, it means that on those nights when you go to bed thinking about other things you're able to say: now's not the time to think about this, I'll do it tomorrow. And you're able to put that thought to one side. Very important tool.

Another tremendous tool, you knew I was going to say it: get rid of screens, phones, iPads, at least 2-3 hours before going to bed. Not just because of the blue light that affects circadian rhythms. But also because these things tend to be stimulating. If your brain notices you're interacting with a lot of people, it notices it's time for activity. Being on WhatsApp, opening Instagram, seeing very fast-changing images... your brain activates and gets stimulated, and kills your desire to sleep. Once you cut that out and get used to it, you notice that when you get home you don't feel like picking up the phone. Because you don't want to hear from anyone, because now it's time to rest.

Another tip: don't use overhead lights at home a couple of hours before you go to sleep. Use indirect lights that illuminate the wall and bounce off, ambient lights. And about an hour before bed, use only candles. I use them while I eat dinner, then while I brush my teeth and then while I read. You drop like a rock.

And another point that has helped me a lot: reading before sleep, and reading fiction. I think some of you believe — because I went through that phase — that reading has to be something productive. And that fantasy novels are useless. I want to read productivity, nutrition, how to be more productive. It's a good motivator, but I don't recommend it at all before bed. Because before bed you want to switch on sleep mode. And a personal development book can do two things: the book is so good you want to learn what you're reading, and since learning is an active thing, you lose the desire to sleep. Or you get sleepy and forget what you're reading because you're not paying attention to learning, you're paying attention to sleeping.

A novel helps you switch gears. This isn't productivity time, this isn't time to think about work, this isn't time to think about my problems. It's time to stop my productive life and rest. For me and for many people I know, it's the best tool I've found.
