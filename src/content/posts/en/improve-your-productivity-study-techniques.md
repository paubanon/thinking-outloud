Good morning, once again, to Pau's advice column, where we talk about whatever we feel like. Since I've had a few philosophical videos, I wanted to make a more practical one. You know I'm someone who wants to do a lot of things: I work with Show Movers training people, I'm doing a master's, I train twice a day, I read a ton of books. There are twenty-four hours in the day for everyone. I wanted to give some tips that have worked for me and that I've picked up over the years to be productive and, not just that, but to be more content with what you do. To make it more satisfying. Things like studying, which can be psychologically very tough — how to make them a little more bearable.

The first thing you have to do is get your head in the right place. See it coming and understand what this period calls for. It's a period where you need to work. And since the end result is something I want, I'm going to work. Be clear about it. Don't let yourself be carried away by moaning and complaining and whining. Cut all that crap because it does you no good. Accept that it's a month where you have to work hard, and that's that. You don't go out partying and your main occupation is going to be sitting down studying. If someone says "come on, let's go out," and you have the exam in three days, you say no. Not "ugh, being a student is messing with my head." If you've accepted it beforehand, the answer is already made. As I mentioned in the decision-making video: if the decision is already made, you don't have to decide, and you don't stress about it.

The most important thing you can do to study — and while we're at it, to improve your life in general — is sleep well and sleep enough. When you sleep, your brain resets. Maintenance tasks get done. It's the only thing that gives you enough rest to gather energy for another study session. And it's essential for consolidating memory. If you don't sleep, you don't perform, life is greyer, you're less productive, you get distracted more, you study worse, you need more time, and those hours aren't productive, which frustrates you. Sleeping well is imperative. Basic stuff: avoid devices an hour before bed, follow a routine, be in a cool room.

The next point is important: train. You're going to be sitting for many hours. Training relieves aches and pains, keeps you healthy, tires you out so you sleep better, gets you outside, which clears your head. It generally has a social element if you train at a club or gym. And it releases endorphins. Makes you see life better. Even if it's hard to go train after a full day sitting down — with your mind stuck and not knowing what you want — trust me, force yourself. Once you get into the habit, you even train harder than usual because you have energy to release.

We're not a mind and a body stuffed in the same box. We're an organism that works as a whole. The fact that you train modifies the structure of your nervous system, stimulates the creation of new hormones, keeps you young, stimulates circulation, oxygenates the brain. Training makes you smarter, or at least gives you more capacity to stimulate your nervous system. It's important that the training has a technically complex factor: doing things that are difficult for you. You're not going to become Einstein doing bicep curls, but if you do a complex type of training that varies and forces you to learn new things, it's interesting not just physically but neurologically.

Tip 1: tidy your room. Imperative. You can't be consistent and structured if you're surrounded by chaos. The room, as Jordan Peterson says, is a reflection of your mind. If your room is chaos, your mind is chaos. Coming home to find it a mess is coming in saying "ugh." It doesn't have to be perfect, but having things in order, no clothes on the floor, the bed made, helps a lot.

That leads me to the point about structure. For your brain it's important to have structure: it gives you security, order, calm. My personal routine is: I get up at 8, make myself a coffee, while I have breakfast I read the book I had going, I get to my room, tidy it up a bit, sit down, study. Break at midday to air out. Do some pull-ups, a walk. Sit down again, make lunch, eat, nap, get up, coffee with a book, study again, go train, come back, dinner, repeat.

Always get up at the same time. Your sleep schedule has to be long enough to wake up automatically, but don't sleep until eleven. Set the alarm for 8, every day, weekends included. Eventually you'll wake up without an alarm, and your body will make small micro-adjustments according to its needs.

Leave the window slightly open so light comes in when the sun rises. That tells your body it's time for activity. Your body starts to adjust its circadian rhythms to the natural frequency: when there's sun you're active, when there's no sun you rest.

A nap is imperative for me. It's the element that lets me perform in the afternoon. Without a nap, the afternoon becomes "I'm loaded, I resent the desk, my mind wanders." My naps are exactly one hour. I sleep for real, deeply. I lie down, read ten minutes, put in earplugs, and fall asleep. Sometimes I even dream. If your naps run three hours because you're not used to it, set an alarm, but be consistent.

Make your bed. You start the day doing something well. It takes very little. And if you do it, you start the day with an objective accomplished. Psychologically it's a positive reinforcement.

Dress as if you were going to university, even if you're studying at home. This gives your brain the signal of "activity mode, work mode." If you sit down to work in the same clothes you sleep in, those are slightly contradictory messages. You notice the difference enormously.

Watch the caffeine. It can be an important boost, but don't overdo it. It can mess with your sleep cycles and if you get to the point where you're slightly wired even though you feel very active, that agitation is tremendously counterproductive for concentration. I have one coffee when I get up in the morning and one after my nap. If I have any more during the day, it's decaf.

On how to study: when you're doing something, dedicate yourself completely and absolutely to it. No distractions. The book Deep Work changed a lot about how I approach this. It compares it to craft work: when you're doing carpentry, you can't be on your phone, because you need your hands. What happens when we make our work into craft? We focus completely and we're very productive. And we feel good about being completely immersed in the task.

To work like this: the phone has to be off and away from you. Not on the table "on silent." Off and away. The pull of a phone is brutal. You also need a quiet environment. I can't study in the library: the fact that there are people walking around or whispering breaks me. I need complete isolation.

How much do I study per day? Between five and six hours. I can't do more. About three in the morning and two or three in the afternoon. If you're really performing, at three hours you're fried. This is the other side of deep work: when you work, you work. But when you're not working, you're not working. When you've finished studying, you've finished. You don't skim the notes a little more before bed. No. Off. Because if you don't, when you sit down to study you won't perform.

This lets you be fully present when you're doing something else — also in deep work mode. If you let studying bleed throughout the entire day, on one hand it's inefficient, and you also notice it's not getting done, which leads to listlessness.

Reward yourself. Say no to procrastination in the moment, and reward yourself when you finish. I'm going to study two hours seriously with the phone off, and then I'll look at Instagram for twenty minutes. What's going to happen? That when you finish studying you don't even want Instagram, because you didn't want Instagram, you wanted to not sit down and study. When you do something well, reward yourself: eat really well, go train, any activity that gives you pleasure.

Before going to bed, make a list of what you need to do tomorrow to feel satisfied. It has two purposes: one, it gets the worries out of your head, you don't have to be ruminating "remember to put the washing on, remember to call home." It's written down. Two, the next day you have a mini plan. You don't have to map it hour by hour: put the washing on, have breakfast, study, eat, nap, study, train. You know what you have to do. There's no time for the procrastination of "what should I do?"

Before you start studying a subject, make a complete index. Bird's eye view: how many topics are there? What subsections do they have? How many problem sets are there? Don't sit down with "topic 1, that's it," not knowing how many topics are left. Always.

Set limits on sessions. I'm going to study two hours. Two hours, break. Even if at the two hours you're in the zone. The idea is to never reach the point of burnout, because burnout is what makes studying something unpleasant that your brain rejects. And yes, studying is unpleasant, it requires effort and concentration. That's why it's important to set the limit: I push hard for two hours and then I can do something else.

Finally: in the morning, don't turn your phone on before you sit down to study. The world can go on without you for five or six hours. When you pick up your phone, check WhatsApp, Instagram, and so on, before you know it it's eleven and you say "damn, it's so late." Then you try to sit down and study but you've spent three hours on trivia and it doesn't work. Be diligent from moment one: you get up, have breakfast, and because you're not distracted by your phone, you know what's up. It's time to study. Turn the phone on at midday, reply to whatever needs replying to, and continue the cycle.

I hope you liked it and that it's useful. If you have comments or personal tricks for being productive, comment them. I'm always willing to hear new ideas.
