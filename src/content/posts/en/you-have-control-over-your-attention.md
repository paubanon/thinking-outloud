Hey everyone, good morning. It's 8:30, 9 in the morning. Today I woke up at 7 anxious to work. Lately I've been in monk mode full throttle. Just work, train, eat, and sleep, which as some of you already know is where monk mode comes from.

I did my morning ritual: coffee in silence looking out the window. Today I didn't have a book in hand, I just finished the last one I had. I don't pick up my phone until I leave the house. It's a practice I really like because waking up and having your coffee in silence, doing nothing, lets you think about what you're going to do, where you're going to focus your energy. It lets you make a plan for the morning. Even if you want to write it down in a notebook that's fine. No rush. Not having my coffee to rush into work, but enjoying the coffee, stopping for a moment, thinking about what I'm going to do, how to structure things. Even if the answer is work, what am I going to do first and then. And then you say: ah no, I'm not going to do that because if I do I'll get scattered and then won't be able to work well.

This morning, while I did that, I made a plan: now I'll meditate, now I'll go for a walk, reply to some messages I have, then meditate, then come back, work on this, then go to the city center, buy such and such, sit in a café, finish working, and if I do all of that well, at the end of the morning I'll have some calculations I wanted to do. The point is it lets you direct your attention. Plan where you're going to direct your attention. Think about what you want to do this morning and have it very clear. Because if you don't have it clear, the moment you hesitate, the moment you finish an activity or are halfway through something, you're going to say: I don't know what to do, I'll open my phone. And that's the recipe for not having a good morning.

You want to have a good plan and you want every moment of doubt to be answered with: don't hesitate, we already dedicated the time before to thinking about what to do. Now we know what to do, let's do it. I'm a big fan of planning on one side and acting on another. Never both at the same time. That's why I always say you have to have your workouts written on paper before going to the gym. Never, never work and at the same time decide what to do.

In today's meditation I'm using an app called Seven Mind. I've been liking it a lot lately, along with Waking Up and Headspace for beginners. The Seven Mind one is a bit more advanced. Worth it. One of the points to reflect on in today's guided meditation is: I have control over my attention.

And it's something important. Something I emphasize a lot on this channel and that I think can't be emphasized enough. We... We have control over what we pay attention to. We generally don't realize it. And I think it's one of the things that helps most in meditation. It abstracts you, lets you look at yourself from the outside and direct your focus toward what you want. It can be directing your focus toward ignoring negative thoughts. Or it can be the opposite: enjoying how lucky I am to have this person in my life. But generally, even if you've been meditating for a while, it's very easy to go through daily life driven by mundane desires or impulses without realizing it. Very rarely are we conscious that we have control over what interests us, over what we pay attention to.

A very clear example, focused on productivity: when you're working and suddenly have the urge to pick up your phone and check whatever. Stop. Stop and think: do I want to focus my attention on this? I'm in the middle of my work. I just stopped because I have a problem in front of me that's so big it feels daunting. And I picked up my phone. Do I want to focus my attention on the phone right now? The answer will probably be no. In that case, decide to focus your attention back on the problem you were working on. Decide that what's happening is that the problem feels daunting. That happens to me too.

When deciding where your attention goes, you can do several things. On one hand, you can limit yourself: trap yourself in a situation where there's no choice but to work. Like leaving your phone at home and going to a café with only what you need to work. Then you have no choice but to work. Or you can learn to focus your attention.

But knowing that you can focus your attention, that attention is not some magical thing that happens to you — just like motivation — it's not something that happens in you and guides your desires. Both motivation and attention are practiced and you decide where they go. And that empowers you tremendously. If you get used to combining reflection on the present — on what am I doing, what do I want to do, what do I need to do — together with focusing your attention, you become something very powerful. Because you not only know what you want in the short, medium, and long term (and just knowing that is already half the battle). You're able to identify it, name it, and act accordingly.

For example, I can say right now: what do I want in the short term? In the short term I don't want to sit down and study, I want to pick up my phone and spend time there. What do I want in the medium term, this week? I'd like to finish the calculations I need to do and have a good meeting at Enso Movers. What do I want long term? To have the master's finished, for Enso Movers to have double the revenue, to have monk mode up and running. Okay, what do I need to do today to meet certain objectives? And some are mutually exclusive: I can't have what I want today and have what I want long term. I have to sit down and do those calculations I don't want to do.

How do I redirect my attention? I've turned off my phone. I'm in a silent room. I know that I'll finish this video and sit down to work. And since I know there's nothing else in the room that can distract me, and I know I want to do this because long term I want that, I'm going to sit down and do it. And every time my attention wanders, just like in meditation, I'm going to notice and act accordingly. Sometimes the answer will be: shut up and refocus on what you were doing. And other times it'll be: you've been focused on this problem for an hour and a half, you're not that fresh anymore. Stop. Take a walk. Go buy something. Change locations. Refresh. Then come back and sit down.

Being aware of when your attention wanders and what you're going to do with it is crucial for work. And on top of that, deep work is not just productive and efficient — it produces a lot of satisfaction. Because at the end of the morning I'm going to have those calculations done. And I'm going to look back and say: it was worth it. Even though right now I don't feel like doing it at all.

Keep this in mind the next time your attention wanders. Maybe you wander a lot not because you want to, but because you don't pay attention to when it happens. Try making that practice a regular thing. Take care and have a good morning.
