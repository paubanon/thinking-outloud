On this topic, about the importance of the question forming in someone's mind before they receive an answer, it occurred to me while I was putting the eggplants in the oven that it's very similar to seduction. As you know, it's an area I find very interesting.

In seduction you can't directly say what you think, your values, or your genuine interest in the other person. You can't. If you do, you've lost. That's not how it works. It's about generating interest in the other person so that they can discover you themselves. But you can't reveal yourself.

It's as if Brad Pitt walked up to you and said: "Hi, I'm Brad Pitt and I'm the shit because I've done this and this and this, I'm hyperfamous and I'm a millionaire." Sure, yes, it's Brad Pitt, and because you already know who he is you'd think: ugh, this kind of opportunity doesn't come around twice. But if someone like that approaches you from zero, does it really attract you? Or would you give them a pat on the back and tell them to get lost?

Whereas imagine you're getting to know someone and you don't know what they do, and you keep wondering: what do they do for work? Why are they talking to me? Do they have money or not? And then you go to the car and they've got a Jaguar, and you think: damn. But if they walk up and tell you directly: "Hi, I have a Jaguar," you say: great, enjoy it. It's an art. It's the art of seduction.

Mind you, I'm not talking about when two people meet and the interaction is mutual and the discovery is mutual, which is very beautiful. I'm talking about when you want to meet someone on a romantic-sexual level, because you're attracted to them, but you don't have that connection yet. That's when you have to learn not to lay out your values, your achievements, your personality. You have to generate the other person's interest so that person says: "Hmm, seems like they might be interesting. I'll make a bit more of an effort to get to know them." That's it.
