Hey guys. I was thinking this morning. I was reading Digital Minimalism, a book by Cal Newport. He put it out very recently. Cal Newport is the author of Deep Work, which I've mentioned so many times, which has influenced me so much in how I think and structure work and how to learn to concentrate and perform at your best. Grouping your tasks into blocks of uninterrupted, focused, tremendously productive work, and how that pays dividends not just in terms of productivity. In the end what we all want isn't to produce a lot — what we want is productivity, because if you produce a lot but you're working twenty-four hours a day that sucks. The money-to-hours ratio is what matters most.

So I was reading this new book of his, Digital Minimalism, which talks about a strategy for using social media: optimizing their use, maximizing the benefits while minimizing the problems they cause — psychological, emotional, and practical in terms of lost time and attention.

And I was in the early chapters where he explains the science behind social media and how they're designed to be addictive. We think the web is neutral and it's anything but neutral. Social media is designed by companies that make money from you spending as much time as possible on those networks. And they have job positions that are addiction engineers, who basically study how the brain responds to addiction and design the best strategies to maximize that addiction.

That they do this at all might seem immoral, but it's the natural evolution of advertising, except now we're smarter and we know how to affect people better. Advertising, which is the archetypal example: I have my company and I advertise because I want people to buy. Well, with social media today we simply know more about how to influence the brain. If I put a red button or a red notification, you're going to interact with it much more than if it's blue. If I don't give you all your notifications at once, you're going to open the app more often — because if you post a photo and in half an hour you already have all the likes you're going to get, that's it, you won't touch it again. But if I split those likes and give you 50% in the first half hour, then 20%, then 10%, you're going to spend more time in the app. If we make an infinite feed, you never reach the end and that keeps you in the application longer. Anyway, it's all just evolutions of advertising: we've simply learned how the brain works.

And this was helping me think about how all this circuitry of addiction and motivation has biological roots. It's the dopamine circuit, which is the circuit that motivates you to do things. In principle that circuit evolved so that you'd be motivated to do the things that help you progress in life. One of the most important things we needed to find was information. And that's why we're addicted to information, because in the wild any piece of information could be crucial: it might tell you your wife is being unfaithful, it might tell you a rival tribe is approaching, it might tell you where prey has gone so you can get food. Information is vital.

It's interesting because the reward your brain gives you for information has been studied, and it's the same as the reward it gives you for food. For your brain, getting information is just as important as getting food — partly because the information might be about how to get more food.

The dopamine circuit, like any other hormonal circuit in the body, has receptors with a level of sensitivity. And excess dopamine, excess stimulation of this circuit, desensitizes you to its effects. You need bigger doses to feel motivated to do something.

And I was thinking about the world today: I was thinking about nofap, about not masturbating and not watching porn. I was thinking about social media. About junk food. About the world of instant gratification we live in today. And about how achieving what you value most is the exact opposite of everything we're exposing ourselves to.

Because if you're constantly exposed to all these constant, shallow stimuli — constant access to social media, hyperpalatable food, series, pornography — you're not going to feel that drive that would make you start a new business despite how hard it is. Because that motivation comes from the dopaminergic system, and the impulses that kind of task provides are minuscule compared to what masturbation and pornography give you, for example.

I think that's partly why it's so important to be aware of all these types of addictions in our daily lives. Because if we want to get where we're trying to go, it's going to require a lot of effort. Have you ever thought about how hard it is to write a book? Sitting in front of a blank page and starting to write. The number of hours it requires in solitude in front of a computer or a notebook, writing and rewriting and deleting and thinking alone. To write a book, to achieve something great in life, you're not going to have little red lights making you stupidly happy as you write it. You're not going to have smiley faces. And if you're not used to that, if you've been exposing yourself to other stimuli, you're going to find it incredibly hard, incredibly unstimulating. That's going to make you not give it time, not make progress, get even more frustrated, and you'll end up in a hole of no motivation. Why am I not enjoying my work? Why am I not enjoying my career? Maybe this isn't my career, maybe this isn't my job. And you enter a loop of I don't know what to do with my life because nothing motivates me enough.

And maybe it's not that there's nothing that motivates you enough. Maybe you've burned your dopaminergic systems so badly that what you need to do isn't find something more stimulating. You need to increase your sensitivity. And that's where meditation comes in. That's where giving up masturbation and porn comes in. That's where reading on paper in silence comes in. That's where learning to be alone at home in silence without music and without any stimulus comes in. Small tasks, small rewards. That's also where eating well comes in, enjoying eating well, the habit of food that's not ultra-sugary, not processed, the yogurt is bitter and that's how it is and so is the coffee, and you enjoy them as they are.

And then, let's say your baseline, your usual level of stimulation, has dropped. And because it's dropped, the things in your life that provide some stimulation have more value, because you've removed everything that was suprastimulus. And you find yourself discovering that music is much more pleasurable. That you value your relationships with your parents, your friends, your partner more. You learn to value people more as people because being with them produces an intrinsic joy and a deep connection.

And then, when you've lowered your baseline, your career, making progress in any personal project, becomes something enormously fulfilling. You feel full, you feel like you're doing something useful at the same time. And you didn't get that satisfaction by searching for a career that satisfies you more — you found it because you increased your sensitivity by removing all those unnatural superstimuli.

I said: masturbation, excess noise and auditory stimulation, excessive series and movies and TV, processed food, pornography. Cut them out of your life. They're not doing you any good. Social media, very important. Social media in particular has positive aspects, but they still carry a very high price and you have to keep that in mind. I have a specific rule of mine which is that you never open a social media app to check if you have messages, comments, or likes. That's forbidden. Because if you post a photo, uninstall the app, and two hours later open it again to see if there are likes, you're posting the photo and giving your brain the reward of doing it for the likes. If you open an app, it should be with a purpose: I'm going to open Instagram because I want to share this. You post it, share it, delete the app. And you don't open it again until you have something important to share. You have to learn to distinguish between the craving — "will I have more likes? will there be a comment? will there be something important I've missed?" — and a real, non-anxious desire to share information.

Everything that we understand very clearly — like how junk food affects our hormonal system — seems very obvious because food is tangible: it has this compound, this compound, they interact this way with these hormones and this is what makes me feel bad. Because it's something objective you can touch, it seems more real than the psychological changes produced by misusing a social media platform. Because you're not physically touching anything. You're not sticking your hand in radioactive waste. But you're bombarding yourself with information structured in a very specific way to alter how your brain works. And you have to be aware of that and take a step back and analyze this whole industry the same way we took a step back and analyzed the sugar industry.

I look forward to your comments and your experiences. And best of luck if you decide to embark on this journey. Because it's definitely not easy, it's hard, you pay a price for all of this. But what you have to keep in mind is that the final reward, the feeling of satisfaction with your own life, is more valuable in the long run. Take care.
