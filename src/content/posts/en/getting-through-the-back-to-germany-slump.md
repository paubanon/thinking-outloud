This is a video I want to make about coming back to Germany. I came back for Christmas and, well, the holidays ended and honestly I didn't want to come back. And it wasn't just a mild "I don't feel like it" — I genuinely didn't want to come back, not seriously. The three days before leaving I fell into a pretty heavy depression, about not wanting to come back, about questioning whether I wanted to keep doing the master's.

I'm also saying this because a lot of the time people only share the positive stuff, and I'm the first to do that, but I also think it's important to say that we all go through hard times. I spent three days with my head spinning way too much. In the end I talked to several friends — thanks to everyone who put up with me — and family members who also put up with a lot. And in the end I managed to get my depressed self under control. It turned into a "I'm okay, I'm sad to be leaving, but I'm okay."

The thing is, I didn't want to come back because the first time I came things were really rough that first week: it was overcast, I still didn't have a flat, I was staying at a friend's place, I felt super far away after having spent a year back in Elche with my family. It wasn't easy at all. And I think part of me was afraid of having to go through all that again.

But it turned out I arrived here to my mansion, after spending the whole day traveling and having spent the whole afternoon chatting with friends so I wouldn't go straight home and lock myself in. And when I got home around 8 or 9 at night, instead of saying "god, I'm here alone," what was curious is that despite coming in with the attitude that that was exactly what was going to happen, my reaction was different. It was arriving and thinking: I'm home. I know this furniture, this room, this smell. I lay down on the bed and thought, ah, I know this mattress, these sheets. I felt comfortable. And it surprised me, because I'd spent three or four days dreading coming back and not wanting to, and then I arrived and felt at home.

Analyzing it now, a few days later, I wanted to share a couple of reflections.

The first is that if you're depressed, don't know what to do with your life, your head is spinning and you're lying awake at night: talk. Talk to your friends, to your family, open up. It's not easy to tell your father or mother "I don't know if I want to keep doing the master's," but if you have a good relationship with them, they'll understand and they'll help. And if you don't have that relationship with your family, do it with friends. Tell them: guys, I don't know if I want to do this anymore. They'll help. You don't have to go through all that crap alone, because you'll end up spiraling and not seeing any way out.

When I was going through it, I objectively knew that things here were fine because I'd been here three months and it had been great. I thought: damn, how lucky am I, what a beautiful city, what a great room, I'm meeting so many interesting people. But emotionally my brain wouldn't show me any of that. It only wanted to show me the image of me waking up alone in the room, looking out the window and missing my family. That was the image my brain kept putting in front of me.

And this leads me to another conclusion: your brain is terrible at imagining your future self. Even if you know that in the future you'll be fine, if you're struggling right now, you're struggling. You're not going to avoid the rough patch just by knowing things will be okay later. Your brain doesn't understand that in the future you'll be a different person with different experiences. "I don't know if I want to work abroad." You don't have to know that now. You have to know it when they make you the offer. And for now, since that problem doesn't exist yet, you shouldn't be spinning your wheels over it. When they offer it to you, you'll be a different person, with different experience, a different story, and you'll make the decision with that.

Letting your head spin a little always produces something useful. I came out of it with more reasons to keep doing the master's and now I'm handling it better. At first I got into it a bit because it felt like the thing to do after the degree, but I didn't have solid reasons. That made things feel shaky. Now I have a clearer picture.

And something else: sometimes you have to do what you don't want to do in order to feel good. When I arrived I felt comfortable, felt good. The next day I got fully stuck into the master's because I had to hand in a problem set I hadn't done and it was due the next day. I spent the whole afternoon locked in going full throttle. And I thought: damn, that felt great, I was so productive, so satisfied. Having spent three or four days before that questioning whether I wanted to keep doing the master's.

Today I was home and I had to train, go to uni, 20 minutes by bike. Didn't feel like it at all. But I knew it would do me good and I'd feel better afterward. And so I did it, I got on the bike, set off with not much enthusiasm, and there I was thinking: this feels so good, it's been so long since I trained here. Because the other option was to stay home doing nothing.

Sometimes even when you don't feel like it, you know what you have to do to make progress, to feel better. You know. And even if you don't feel like it, you have to do it. And once you start without enthusiasm, the enthusiasm comes. That's how it works: you start and then the motivation arrives. You don't always have to feel like it before you begin.

And something else that helped me a lot with the spinning out of this depression is trying to see those negative thoughts as something separate from you. A tip from a good friend, Lucía: don't claim them as your own, think that it's not you thinking like that, it's something else.

I was lying in bed and my brain was picturing me alone, overcast, sad. And I kept telling myself: Pau, shut up. Stop whining. You have to go, no choice. Stop now. And you repeat it however many times it takes, which is a thousand, throughout the day. You try to beat down those negative thoughts by force.

The most important thing for me has been talking to friends and family, and doing it honestly. Saying the truth: I'm worried about this. If you have a good family, they'll back you up. If not, friends. Sleep well, that always helps. Train, that helps a lot too. And talking about yourself and understanding how your brain works doesn't free you from these things, but at least in hindsight you understand them.
