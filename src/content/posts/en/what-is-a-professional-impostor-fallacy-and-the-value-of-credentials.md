Hey. Today I wanted to talk about what it means to be a professional. And the funny thing about this video is that it's not because I am one.

It all started when I was writing a post for Enso Movers and I wrote: "if you want professional help, hire us." And I stopped to think: but what is a professional? When does someone become a professional?

Professional, to me, comes from profession. So someone is a professional in a field when that field is their job. But that brings other problems, because you can work at something and be completely incompetent. And yet we all have this idea that a professional is someone who knows a lot about a field. So the etymology of the word doesn't help us, because I can get paid to do something without being competent at it.

If you define it as someone who knows a lot about a subject, the question of when comes up. And there you find that there's no clear threshold, no line where someone tells you: now you're a professional. There's no certificate of professionalism. I've seen people finish a physics degree and be completely incompetent. And I've seen people who've failed exams and are barely scraping by, who know an enormous amount and who I'd trust with my training before people who've graduated. I know completely incompetent physicists with the degree, and brilliant physicists who are struggling with one class. The degree isn't an indicator either.

When you start getting interested in a subject, you realize there's no line telling you: now you're a professional. I don't know if I consider myself a professional in fitness. All I know is that I know a lot more than many people and a lot less than a huge number of others in that field. So I just try to teach and help people who know less than me, the same way I know they could help me in many other areas.

And you realize that the term "professional" doesn't make much sense, beyond being a bit pretentious. Claiming to know more than others and calling yourself a professional. But there's never a point in your life where someone tells you: now you're a professional, now you've got the keys to this. In the end, everyone — from Albert Einstein to the guy working construction on the corner, through every specialist and self-proclaimed master of any field — they're all the same regular people who started learning about something and kept learning. There came a moment where they said: hey, I can share this, or I can get paid for this, because I know more than most people. But there's no inflection point.

And that can be a problem. It's what psychology calls impostor syndrome: because there's no established cutoff for what being a professional means, you sometimes feel like a fraud. You feel like you can always learn more, that getting where you've gotten doesn't earn you the right to say "I'm a physicist, I'm a coach" — instead you say, well, I don't know, I've been doing things and I ended up here. And you feel you have no right to teach. When you're told "to get in here you need to pass this exam" and you pass it, you say: okay, I've earned my place. But when it's something blurry, you've been climbing and sometimes you tell yourself: I'm here, but I'm not sure I should be here.

It's a very common mental block. Being in research and saying: yeah, I'm doing things, but I'm not sure this is what I'm supposed to be doing. I got here through my master's and, well, I kept researching a little and here I am. And I don't feel the way I think I'm supposed to feel. And the problem is exactly that — a lot of the time how you actually feel doesn't match your expectations of how you should feel. And you have to accept that.

And this leads to another important point: since there's no line, in the end we're all the same regular people putting in hours in different fields. The recognition a certificate gives you often loses its validity. I don't have any paper saying I'm a coach. I didn't have one for English until a couple of years ago either, but I knew a lot of English. And I knew exactly the same English before taking the exam as after. Everything you learn in a degree can be learned on your own today. You just have to be the resident nerd and put in the hours.

I know nutrition, biology, anatomy, loads of things, because of that. Because I studied it. And I know there are plenty of people who know more physics without a certificate than physicists who've finished the degree.

I think you just shouldn't take certificates too seriously. Take the person seriously. When deciding whether to trust someone, you look at whether they're motivated, whether they have results, whether they have a solid foundation. If they pass your filters, you can trust that person regardless of whether they have a degree. If someone comes along and proves they can speak English, don't ask them for a certificate that they speak English. They're handling themselves in English — what else do you need? What's the certificate for?

This would kind of dismantle the university system and today's economy. But at least in Spain, the way I'm seeing it work, being a person of value and demonstrating that you're worth it counts for a lot more than having a degree. A lot of people complain about this and call it professional intrusion. But if there's someone without a degree who can do a job much better than someone with a degree, which of the two has more legitimacy to hold that job?
