Good morning. In today's video I wanted to talk about why I quit Instagram and the changes I've noticed, plus a bit of philosophy as always.

My relationship with Instagram has always been a bit tortured. Not so with Facebook. Facebook I couldn't care less about. It doesn't cause me stress or addiction. I have the option to not follow my friends even if they are friends, because most people on Facebook don't give you anything relevant about their lives. They generally share third-party content and in the end it's all a garbage pile. For me Facebook is basically an organizer for some magazine or blog I want to follow. No emotional stress.

But Instagram, yes. Instagram has always been very addictive for me. And I've learned a lot from Instagram, I have to admit. But emotionally it's always hit me hard. It's always gotten to me more than other platforms.

Instagram is different because it makes you believe that what you share matters. You're much more tempted to share on Instagram than on any other platform. On Instagram you have to put out your content: your photos, your videos, your likes, your stories. And part of what I found so emotionally hard to explain is that, because you have to share your own material, you feel like what's on Instagram is the measure of your life's value. And you think it's very important that people see what you do.

The feeling I had inside wasn't that I wanted to share. It was that I had to share. Because if I didn't, what was I doing? It might sound repetitive, but think about that. If I don't share it, am I at peace with myself? Or do I have some strange restless feeling?

When you're about to post or story something you genuinely love, something you say "this is so cool I want to share it," ask yourself: if I didn't share it, would I be at peace with myself? I encourage you to sit with that feeling and think about it. Why should living an experience without sharing it make me uneasy? Not sharing an experience takes away pleasure from the experience itself. It's very strange.

One of the things I've learned from quitting Instagram and coming back occasionally — because it is a good platform for business — is that seeing it from the outside, from someone who's stepped back for a while, it strikes you as a very strange behavior for a human being. Very normal that it happened. But very strange. It's not natural.

In quotes, there are people who publish really good content on Instagram. And I've learned things from Instagram, especially from coaches — techniques and progressions. In that sense, for training content it works really well because it's a small description paired with a video or photo. So it's completely audiovisual content.
