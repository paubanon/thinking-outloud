Good morning everyone. Today I had a different video planned, but another one came to me and I think it's important. It's about this. Here, you can see it: my bed. And I wanted to talk to you about the importance of making your bed.

I've talked to you before about the importance of getting dressed for work. Whether you're someone who works from home, someone who has to go to an office, or you're just going to study, you have to get dressed for work. By this I don't mean you should wear a suit. I mean that to be productive and put your brain in now-is-not-the-time-to-rest mode, you have to groom yourself and get dressed. It doesn't matter so much that it's formal — it can be jeans and a t-shirt. But there's a huge difference between sitting down in your pajamas right after waking up with a coffee, versus getting up, showering, making yourself a coffee, making your bed, tidying up the room a little, and then sitting down to work. It makes a huge difference and I think it's important for mental health.

Making your bed is one of those small things your mother and grandmother have always told you to do. And if you live alone you probably don't do it. Or your mother does it. A lot of people think making your bed is purely aesthetic, so the room looks tidy. Few understand that the state of your room is a clear reflection of your mental state. And you cannot have mental clarity, be calm, and be completely relaxed in a messy room. It doesn't work. The brain doesn't do it.

Those of you who know a little about my influences will know that this is practically the message Jordan Peterson promotes in his rule: keep your house in perfect order before criticizing the world. He's right.

One of the arguments I used to make when debating, when my mother told me to make the bed, was: but no one's going to see it. It's my bed and if I want it like this, I'll have it like this. And now, with time, I see it differently. I make my bed every day. And I wanted to share why.

Making your bed doesn't just make it look nice. It has many purposes, when you think about it and especially when you're conscious of them. The first one I heard from a military person. In the navy they make you make your bed every day because it's a sign that when you get up, the first thing you do, you do it well. And you have to do it well. A quick yank won't do. You have to do it the best you can. Why? Because it's a set. The fact that everything you do is a set. A set toward the person you want to be, or a set away from that person. Making your bed well is a set of doing small things well, no matter how tiny they might seem. You're doing something well, as well as you can. And that brings you a tiny bit closer to the person who does things well.

That mindset then carries over to when you train. The person who says: well, I'll do this set just to get it done and then I'm out. Or the person who does the set and says: no, this set, like all the previous ones, like all the ones to come, has to be perfect. My mind is here, I'm going to be aware of my body, my fatigue, how far I can push with this set. That's the kind of person who doesn't go to the gym to check off a box on a list. That's the person who goes to train because they want to train. And the same then carries over to work. Someone who makes their bed well is someone who when doing anything at work doesn't settle for a quick fix. They want to do it right. Maybe it takes more time. But they pay attention to detail. And those details add up over time.

That report you have to write, that project, that thesis. You can do it and say: well, it's fine. Or you can produce something that you read and say: I'm proud of this work. I want it to go out with my name on it, it's that well done. Because I want people to see it and say: how well done, how elegant, how precise. Because every sentence is exactly right, every word fits, each paragraph flows seamlessly into the next. The whole thing has a structure. And that takes more time. In the case of the bed, doing it well or badly takes you an extra minute. In the case of a project, doing it well or badly can take an extra month. But it matters. It reflects how you do everything in your life.

It can be a relationship. Someone who says: well, I'll put together a dinner for the two of us, whatever, it doesn't matter. Or someone who says: I want the dinner to be excellent. I want the relationship with my partner to be impeccable. And for that I'm going to put in effort, I'm going to pay attention to details. And that's the kind of person you want to be.

And on the other hand, you also become the kind of person who doesn't let details slip by. Small things matter. They matter much more than they seem. Your relationship with your family, with your friends, with your partner is generally not determined by one big thing that happens and makes everything go well or badly. A relationship — whether friendship, family, personal — is forged in small acts. Tiny little things in daily life. That day when you're frustrated and tired from work and someone talks to you. The difference between saying: honey, I'm genuinely exhausted from work and my brain isn't functioning, it's not that I'm not listening to you. That can be very different from just snapping at them.

That small thing of noticing when your friend is emotionally a little off, just below the surface. Instead of letting it slide, being the kind of person who says: come on, let's grab a coffee and talk. And you get it all out and give them a pat on the back and say: hey, push forward, don't stress. Without them asking. And then that person says: wow. Those small details matter.

And the bed is a small detail. But if you can do it consistently, you become the kind of person who sees something out of place, sees something they don't like in their life, and is able to identify it. That's the first skill. And second, you become the kind of person who sees something that's wrong and fixes it. Or tries to fix it. And tries to fix it as well as they can.

And that translates tremendously into your life. Because it means you're the kind of person who says: I don't like the way I dress, I'm going to learn how to dress well and do it. I don't like the way I look physically, I'm going to train, I'm going to eat well. Instead of hiding their fears, they take them on. You own it: this is your bed. And it's your problem. No one else's.

And finally: the reason for doing things. One argument for not making your bed is: no one's going to see it. It's my room, no one's going to come in. But if you're doing that, it means you're not making your bed for yourself. That implies that if other people weren't out there, you'd be a slob. And that carries over to other areas of your life. If no one else were putting pressure on you: would you train? If no one's watching: would you eat a muffin? If no one's watching: would you do the work you're doing if nobody cared?

The truth is nobody cares. Nobody cares about anything in your life more than you do. You are the most important person in your life. Your bed, your room, how clean your house is — it's not so that when someone comes over they say wow, how clean. It's not for social recognition. You have to do it for yourself. Because if you make your bed, you're doing something that appears to be useless, that no one's going to see, and you're going to do it as well as you can. You want to do something useless, as well as you can, that no one will see. And you want to do it for yourself. Because it's your bed. Because it's part of your life. And you want to keep order in your life.

That's the attitude you want to bring to every area. You don't want to train so people see you looking strong at the beach. You don't want to train to post that photo on Instagram. You want to train because it's part of your life and you want excellence in all areas. You want to train because you want to train. And if you were living alone on a deserted island, you'd still train just as hard.

You want to be that kind of person. The kind of person who does things, no matter how small. Who if they notice something is wrong, fixes it. And does it well. And does it because they believe that's the right thing to do. And that carries over to other small things. Like lying. You can tell a small lie and you know it's a lie. But lying only makes sense if you don't get caught. That's an attitude of: lying only works if others don't catch me. I'm subject to other people's opinions. But that's not a good attitude. You want to not lie because you believe it's right not to lie. You want to be the kind of person who is honest, who is clear, who knows what they want, who is capable of accepting their vulnerabilities and their weaknesses.

And all of that is encoded in small daily gestures. Keeping your room tidy. Getting dressed when you have to work. Keeping yourself groomed. Brushing your teeth every day. Keeping your kitchen clean. Every time you cook a recipe, trying to make it as well as you can. Doing small favors without expecting anything in return. Small gestures like making your bed every day.
