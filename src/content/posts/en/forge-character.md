Good morning. Today I went to the forest, it had been a while since I'd gone. And as always it was pretty fruitful. I was a bit melancholy this morning, because yesterday I went out a bit and I woke up late. I wasn't in a great mood and I didn't feel much like going to the forest either, but based on past experience, objectively, I told myself: if you go, it'll be worth it. Because it always has been. It changes your mood.

So I woke up, took a cold shower, made myself a decaf coffee so I could sleep during the afternoon nap later, and went outside. I got as much sun exposure as I could and that keeps my circadian rhythms in line. And I went to the forest. The first thing I noticed was that the moment I stepped outside the house I felt great. The light clears your head, wakes you up and gets you going.

I only take my notebook and my keys when I go to the forest. I was thinking about my things. It's one of those moments where you have time to think. I think those are moments that are usually lacking nowadays. People are very focused on doing things and don't give themselves any time to not be doing things. To walk. But go where? Doesn't matter. You're not going anywhere. You're going for a walk. Because walking in itself is already the reward. The prize you need.

And so it was. I went in with my head a bit scrambled, I started walking, and man, it clears you out. Nature connects you and makes you think about your problems, but not in a negative way. It makes you think about your problems in a positive, reflective way.

And I also went barefoot in the forest for a while. If you do it stubbornly, instead of listening to your first instinct saying "cover it and protect it," you say "tough it out and keep going." You start thinking: what would happen if I were on a deserted island and had to go barefoot? What would happen if I had to walk X kilometers barefoot because there's no other option? Well I think all these kinds of situations put things a bit in perspective for you.

Because when you finish you realize you made it through and you didn't start crying or get paralyzed by the pain. It hurts more or less, but here we are. And what you also realize is that generally the pain scale we have isn't so reliable. You can't trust it as much as you think. Because I, for example, was in the forest saying "my feet are hurting a lot," but then you think about it and say: well, but I didn't freeze up, I didn't throw myself on the ground, I didn't cry. Maybe what I was saying hurt a lot in that moment was a 6 on the absolute pain scale. Or a 5. And if something I said hurt a lot I made it through with no damage to the soles of my feet... how long could I actually go?

And that's what makes you think. The perspective that giving yourself these kinds of challenges gives you. Same as cold showers. Same as exposing yourself to everything that scares you. They give you the perspective to say: if I got through this thing that in the moment felt brutal, and I made it, and it wasn't that bad... how far am I actually capable of going? And the answer is: you're capable of going much farther than you think.

These constant small exposures to discomfort, to pain, to cold, to any kind of stressor, what they do is tell you daily: you think you're giving 80%, but that's not true. You're much more capable than you think. You can do many more things. And you're able to tolerate much more responsibility and much more pain than you think.

And it's good to know that because it extrapolates to other areas of your life. For example: how much work can you handle before burning out? Well maybe you're capable of working much more and enjoying it more too. Instead of studying 5 hours, you can study 8. And maybe you start doing it and say: you know what, it wasn't that bad. I thought I was going to burn out completely, but I didn't.

It's a bit the message of a small book, an ebook that's out there called The Flinch, which I found through a YouTube channel recommendation. It's precisely about this. The flinch, according to the author, is that feeling of fear, of "I want to leave, I don't want to do this," that shows up before any difficult situation you don't want to expose yourself to. A cold shower. Getting hit in martial arts. Talking to a girl. A job interview. That feeling of "no, I'm out" before a speech.

What the author argues is that this reflex, which makes evolutionary sense because if a bear came along you said "see you later," today it's overactivated. It fires too often. And it makes us feel like what we're about to do is going to be terribly dangerous, and generally it's not. Generally it's just a cold shower. Generally you go out and give a speech in public and everyone likes it.

These kinds of things — cold exposure, pain, meditating, exposing yourself to anything that scares you — are what teach you. They reset the flinch and teach you that it's not so hard, it's not so tough, you just have to start. Which in everyday language is what people have always called forging character.
