Well, this is the classic cooking-while-I-film video. I'd been wanting to do it for a while. It's a bit radical, and I decided to do it because of a story about a friend who was reading an article and put on his Instagram story: "What am I doing reading an article instead of watching Game of Thrones?" And I told him: that's obsession. Like, as a joke. But I always think it's necessary to be obsessed and truly put in effort toward something.

When I say obsessed: if you want to improve, if you want to learn, there comes a point where it's not that you're making an effort, it comes naturally to you to not waste your time. The only way to achieve many things in life is to dedicate time to them. And if you want to be good at many things, you're going to have to dedicate a lot of time to those things. The point is understanding how much those things matter to you and accepting the sacrifice you're making.

By sacrifice I mean that, if I wanted to finish my master's, wanted to train and wanted to study nutrition, there were no TV shows. I've barely watched any series. I don't play console games. Movies happen once on the weekend, at night or something. And I try not to waste time on social media and all that.

And that's what it comes down to. You want something? Go for it. Work for it. Make that something interest you so much that the irrelevant trivialities that bring you nothing, like watching a series, stop mattering to you. You know they bring you nothing. You know it's throwing hours away. How long does a series take? Let's say an average of seven seasons, ten episodes per season, being conservative, one hour each: seventy hours. Do you know how much you can do in seventy hours? And then you all freak out because you don't have time to do your university work. "I don't have time to train, or cook, or study." And how do I do it? There's no magic. The day has twenty-four hours for everyone. If you want to dedicate hours to one thing, you have to take them from another. The key is that what you do has to interest you so much that cutting the other thing isn't a sacrifice.

For me it's a pleasure to skip watching a series and train instead. At no point do I say: "Damn, I wish I was watching a movie." And if I put on a movie, there are times I've had the feeling: "Man, I don't feel like this, I'm going to train. I feel more like training. I find it more interesting." That's the level of obsession with what you want to achieve that you have to accept.

The question is: do you really care that much? Do you really want it enough to make those sacrifices? If the answer is yes, go ahead. If the answer is no, that's okay. Everyone has their things, everyone is interested in certain things. But if you're doing something only to prove something to someone else, you're not going to last. You're not going to find the intrinsic motivation to put in the necessary hours just to say "look how much I know." It has to genuinely interest you. And when it genuinely interests you, you don't mind making those sacrifices.

There's a Bukowski poem that said: *Find what you love and let it kill you*. Well, that's what you have to do. Find what you love and let it kill you.
