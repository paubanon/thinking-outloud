Good morning. I just finished today's meditation session. I'm using the Waking Up app. Well, I'm not doing the course now, I finished the course a long time ago, about a year ago. And since then I've tried different apps for meditating, but I always end up coming back to this one. I like the tone of voice, I like the rhythm of the meditations, I like the length, I like the content. And I've realized I don't need more. That despite having finished the course, I don't need another app with a different course.

I was thinking precisely about the tendency we have, when we pick up a practice, to look for more and more information about it. With the meditation example: you take a course and you want to take another one, you look for another app, more books that explain it in a different way. But I wanted to talk in this video about the idea of understanding when enough is enough. Of saying: I took this course, this is what meditating is, it is what it is. I can enjoy what it is without expecting more from it. Without always waiting for some new novelty, a new course, a new book that explains it differently. Just appreciating that you've learned what you've learned. Being at peace with that amount of knowledge.

And not only that, but also allowing yourself that if your practice deepens, let it. But if it doesn't happen, that's fine. It reminds me of the Bukowski poem So You Wanna Be a Writer. Which says that if a fire burns inside you asking you to write, if you can't sleep, if you can't eat, if ideas keep raining down in your head, write. But if that doesn't happen, let it go. Don't write.

I also see it quite a bit in cold exposure. People start with cold showers and then cold baths and then start buying ice tubs and then go on cold retreats. And for many people that will be great, but maybe you're also fine knowing how to say this far and no further. I take my cold showers, I've learned what cold is, I'm good with my level of practice. I don't need to keep pushing it further.

In so many areas we force ourselves to look for more, more and better. We try to be the top of everything. The one who eats cleanest, the one who trains hardest, the one who has every skill, who also knows how to code, the best lover, the best friend. And you can't be everything. And wanting to do everything is a big cause of frustration.

To connect it to the movement culture, which I'm so close to: you don't need to be Ido Portal or Roy Goldsmith. You don't need to try to be. You can be yourself and be at peace with who you are, with your characteristics, with your level of practice, and decide it doesn't interest you to dedicate that much time to it. And that was something that took me quite a while to realize.

These people dedicate an enormous number of hours of their lives to training and moving. And I realize that level of performance is attractive, but it doesn't bring me satisfaction, it doesn't make me happy. I'm happier with my level of training and my physics studies. And I'll get a tremendous joy from discovering how the universe works that they'll never even glimpse. And it's fine that they're like that and I'm like this.

That doesn't mean one doesn't want to aim for things. More that you can want to improve, but you have to know when to stop. Because if something as complex as breathing can take you years, how long could handbalancing take? How long could learning to cook take? You have to know when to say enough.

In the fitness world it's very common: I want to learn more about nutrition, I need to fine-tune my diet a bit more, I'm still missing some micronutrient, some superfood, a new fasting schedule. And many times no. Many times you sit down for a period, you study, you learn what it is, you learn how to eat, you implement it. That's it. You've already learned how to eat. You don't need to understand the mechanisms of every hormone and protein in the body to know how to eat. It's not necessary. And today it seems like everyone has to know all about that and more.

When someone gives you an external certification and tells you: this is the content you need to know to say you're a nutritionist, a trainer, a physiotherapist, you tend to accept that that's what you need to know. It's an external standard. But your life is yours. You have to know how far you want to go. Maybe with what you learn in a physics degree you say: I'm good as a physicist, I don't want to know more, I'm going to go work. Or you say: no, I feel like I have gaps in my knowledge I want to fill. And then you go deeper.

I've seen people who aren't nutritionists with far more knowledge than nutritionists. And they don't have a degree. But they know so much more. So, knowing how to set your own limits, knowing when enough is enough.

I just wanted to explore the idea of when enough is enough when it comes to something. When it's enough to say stop. I've learned to fast for 24 hours. I'm happy. I don't need to do three-day fasts. I'm not going to do it. There are people who do very well with it. Wonderful. It's not for me. Knowing how to say that takes a lot of self-control, a lot of self-knowledge. And I think it's a great sign of maturity. It gives you a lot of peace. So I encourage you to develop it.
