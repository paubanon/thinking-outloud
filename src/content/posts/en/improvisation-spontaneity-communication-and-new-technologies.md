This last video on this topic brings us to the fact that many times our brain is terribly bad at analyzing complex systems. Terrible, terrible. It's very good with simple systems, but what complex systems have is that the relationships between their parts are non-linear. Many times the interrelations within the system lead to unexpected behaviors. The system is not the sum of its parts, it's much more, because the parts interact with each other. If we add complexity: 1 plus 1 isn't 2, 1 plus 1 is 3, and plus 1 is 7, and plus 1 is 17, and plus 1 is 39. And so it keeps growing as you add new elements because they interact with all the ones already there. And our brain finds this incredibly hard to accept, and we're terrible at making predictions in complex systems.

As human beings, since we don't like this, what we've done is try to fit everything into a theoretical framework. We like to have a perfect theory that explains how everything works and tells us what to do. Uncertainty drives us a little crazy. The brain doesn't like uncertainty.

Partly, I personally think it's the fault of society today, which hands us everything ready-made, pre-chewed, organized, very un-spontaneous. Everything is arranged. From the moment you have any awareness, practically, you know what you have to do: go to school, to high school, to university, get a job...

Social relationships, making friends, chatting with people, both your own sex and the opposite, trying to flirt in plain terms, all those situations require intuition and require instant reaction. You can't stop to think. If you're with a person and they've just said something, you can't say, hold on, let me think about what I can say back. And this is something digital communication breaks, because as we communicate more and more through WhatsApp, what it creates is a lack of spontaneity and an inability to process stimuli instantly.

Learning to play with what's there. You're a thief in the street and you've just been stopped. What do you do?

When you talk on WhatsApp, people always talk about how identity gets modified, how people project who they are. On the internet you can be whoever you want. But what almost never gets talked about is one of the many elements that disrupts communication, which is response time. In real life, if you're dealing with someone, you can't think of the ideal response to what they just said. You can't come up with that perfect romantic line that you say, wow, that was amazing! Because maybe it doesn't come to you. Because it's right now, right now.

And so everyday life, talking to people, involves screw-ups. It involves putting your foot in it and saying, oh, that's not how you do that! It involves offending the other person, involves them offending you, involves this human feedback. But when you have basically infinite response time, you have time for them to say something, you wait and think, what should I reply? Because we've all done it. And ten minutes pass and you say, well, I think this is what I should reply.

But life isn't like that. That's not a real human interaction. It's fake. Because now you have time to think about it. But then, when you meet that person face to face, the conversation doesn't flow as smoothly. Because you can't do that. You generally notice much more the mistakes someone makes when you see them in person if you've been talking a lot through WhatsApp.

And partly it's because of that, and partly because not looking at the person, not having them nearby, completely eliminates many of the elements of communication that aren't language. When you're with someone, even if you're not conscious of it, you're analyzing their smell, their gestures, their posture, their gaze, the silences, the pauses, the intonation. All of that is information you're processing. I can give you the same sentence and you might say, wow, what spark, how natural. Or you might say, this guy is a total dud. Just by the way of speaking. Or just by body position. So all of that gets lost.

And this came up in connection with the need for spontaneity. A hyperprotective system with a very well-defined life trajectory, combined with the fact that one of the most important factors of improvisation — human relationships — is going completely off the rails with the disruption of social media and fake communication, makes us very bad at improvising and managing spontaneous stimuli.

Another example of how terrible we are at this: presentations. In university presentations, speaking in front of people, I see more and more people who are completely useless at it. It terrifies them. It paralyzes them. Because unless they have a very tight structure — which is what most people resort to — they grab everything, they prepare all the points, exactly what they're going to say, how long they have for each slide. Everything under control. They arrive, they dump it all out and that's it. But what if that day you forget a few lines? What if someone asks you something in the middle of the explanation and interrupts you? Suddenly they make an observation that makes what you'd prepared no longer valid. All of that is very fragile. Perfection is very fragile and very unnatural.

A much more robust approach is one that has to do with improvisation. With saying: I know the topic very well, I know what I have to talk about, I know what key points I have to hit, and I don't need to prepare in detail what I'm going to say. I'm going to go out and tell it. I'm going to tell a story. The same way I do these videos. I have an initial topic and I let it loose. I start ruminating on it and go this way, that way.

And that ability to improvise is one of the most important you can develop in your life. It gives you character and it gives you confidence. As stupid as that might sound. Because if you're used to playing with something closed and perfect, it seems very safe. But in reality it's very fragile, because the moment someone blows on your structure, everything collapses. On the other hand, if you know you're capable of handling the situation regardless of whether you've prepared it or not, if you know you can play with what's there, improvise... What have I got here? I can handle it. I'm self-sufficient. I'm capable of generating new resources. You have control of any situation regardless of whether you've prepared it.

And when it comes to problem solving, I think it's the most effective way. When I solve a problem, instead of following a linear structure, I have like five sheets at once and I'm writing mainly on one, but then I suddenly stop, look at another one, what had I written here? And suddenly I see something I can incorporate into what I'm doing now. And in the end it looks like chaos. But it's like a dance. It's improvisation.

As advice to those of you who are followers of this channel: develop it. In any situation try to be more spontaneous, more loose, more open, less control. Try to establish spontaneous conversations with people, when you go shopping, on the street, when you go out. At first you'll be very clumsy. But little by little you'll develop an ability to improvise, to not be afraid to fail.

Because this way of operating works like this: you walk, you bump into things, you turn, you bump into things. You're like those robot floor cleaners, bouncing off all the furniture. And in the end they clean pretty well. Because if it had a perfect map of how to operate and you suddenly moved a chair, it wouldn't work anymore. And the opposite: by bumping into everything, to know where to stop.

You have to lose the fear of failing. And see that when it fails, nothing happens. That you can improvise. You can pull logical threads out of nowhere. You don't need to have prepared them in great detail.
