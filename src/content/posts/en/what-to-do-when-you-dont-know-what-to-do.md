Good morning. In the information age, in the age of multitasking, the age of productivity and self-improvement, one of the hardest decisions we have to make throughout the day is deciding what to invest our time in, what to give our attention to, and why. Every day we're faced with an enormous range of possibilities and we have to decide what to do.

And many times we find ourselves in the situation of not knowing what to do. Or more precisely, we know but we don't want to accept that that's what we have to do. But we experience it as not knowing what you need to do.

The feeling can take two forms. One is total apathy: I don't know what to do, you pace around, you procrastinate, you open Instagram, you stare at your screen, you stare at your desk, you look at your notes, the hours pass and you've done nothing. And the other form is feeling overwhelmed by an enormous amount of tasks, projects, ideas, and not knowing which one to tackle.

I want to share with you how I approach it. When I don't know what to do, I've discovered that the best thing I can do is sit down and think. Sit down for ten minutes with a notebook in front of me. If possible outside — it tends to clear my head more. Or go for a walk. And it's very important that during this walk or while sitting outside with a notebook, the only condition is that you have no people around. You can't do it in the kitchen while your family is nearby. You can't go for a walk with someone. And you can't have your phone, can't have music, can't have anything except emptiness and a notebook.

Why? Because when you experience this emptiness, when you completely blank your mind and tell it "okay, whatever comes up," your mind starts to surface things. It starts surfacing the clutter you have in the background. It starts surfacing ideas, projects, things you're doing well, things you're doing badly, priorities. What I want you to do with this practice is notice how ideas appear in your mind. Some ideas you thought were forgotten might even show up after months. And you write them down in your notebook.

I don't want you to judge whether now is the right time to do them, whether you should do this one first or the other one. Whatever comes up, just blank your mind. Don't judge whether you feel like doing that idea, whether now is the time, whether it's for later. Just write them down. In a notebook. And it's very important that it's a notebook or a piece of paper. Because if you use your phone or computer, the fact that it has lots of colors, lots of icons, lots of available options means your brain scatters its attention and you end up doing something else.

This practice should last between ten and fifteen minutes, roughly. Because it's a duration where you have time to get bored, to empty out, and to say: I don't know what other ideas I have. And it's important to stay with that feeling — sit with it a little longer. But it's also short enough that you can do it every day without feeling like it's affecting your productivity. It's ten minutes, you can find them under a rock.

Once you've written down what you need to do, I recommend setting a timer with a wristwatch or a kitchen timer, anything that isn't your phone. Because even in airplane mode, you'll be tempted to look at it. We all have some degree of phone addiction and when your attention wanders, your brain will tend to look at that black square to see if there's a little light on it.

Once you've written everything down, you'll notice two things. First, that certain ideas have a logical order: there will be things where you say, I can't do this if I haven't done this other thing first. And second, you'll notice that some ideas push up with more force. There are ideas where you say: I have to do this, this is number one today. So you decide that today you're going to do that. Regardless of whether you feel like it or not.

And you sit down and do it. At no point do you stop to judge whether you feel like it, whether you're in the mood, whether you're not. You say: today I'm going to do this because I have to, my brain is telling me I have to. And you don't think about it, you don't feed the thoughts of yes, no, but I feel like. Because when you're thinking at the same time as you're doing, things get tangled up.

This happens to me a lot when I have to write my master's thesis. There are days when I say: I have to write this chapter, and I sit down and say, I absolutely do not feel like it. I'd clean the room, the kitchen, I'd go shopping, do a load of laundry, I'd spend time going over my accounting, I'd make doodles on a piece of paper, I'd reorganize my entire computer, reformat it, install updates, call a friend — I'd do all of that before sitting down to write. But I know I have to do it. I know it because my brain tells me so. Because it aligns with my long-term project. So, regardless of whether I feel like it, I start. I don't think about whether I want to or not. I sit down and say: what's the first thing I have to do? Open the document. I open the document. What's second? Read where I left off. I read where I left off. What's next? Keep writing. And I start.

Over time I've learned that it's incredibly valuable to separate the time for thinking and planning from the time for doing.

In training it works exactly the same way. You never decide what to do while you're training. Why? Because while you're training you tend to get caught up in the feelings training produces in you, and sometimes they're negative. You're five sets into squats and you think you still have five more to go and you start questioning whether ten sets of squats is really necessary. Because you're tired. Because maybe today was supposed to be a deload. Because ugh, I'd like to get to dinner early. You start going through all of that mid-set and you end up lowering the weight, convincing yourself it's time to deload. And you end up doing a lot less.

Whereas if you, before training — or even better the week before — have put together a plan for your weekly and monthly training, when that day comes you don't stop to think about whether you need to do that mobility work or not, whether you feel like it or not, how many sets, how much weight. You've already thought about it and whether you feel like it or not, whether you're tired or not, you have to do it. And in the long run that means you work a lot more, you push yourself a lot harder, and you progress a lot more.

Exactly the same thing happens with work and studying. When you have to sit down to work, in the moment you're at your desk you don't have to think about what you need to do because you've already decided. If you sit down without having decided what to do, your brain is going to put a thousand tasks in front of you before the one that makes you uncomfortable. And you'll end up doing what we all do: well, I'll check email, I'll empty the spam folder, I'll organize my files. Most of the time it ends up being: I open Instagram and lose the rest of my time. Simply to avoid the anxiety the indecision causes me, I completely escape into social media or some kind of addictive behavior.

So if you separate the time for thinking from the time for doing, in the thinking time you don't produce anxiety because you say: I'm not thinking about whether I feel like it or not, I'm not going to do it now, I'm just going to write down whatever comes to mind. And on the other hand, when you sit down to do something, you don't think about whether you should be doing other things. You've already decided what you have to do. You have a list. And therefore you just have to sit down and do it.

And it's also important to know that once you sit down to do what you have to do but don't feel like doing, chances are, once you get past the fifteen-or-twenty-minute discomfort barrier, you'll start to feel like it. Simply because you're making progress on a task you'd had pending for so long that it was weighing on you. The act of pushing it forward will produce positive feelings.

I hope this is useful. Personally it's brought me quite a bit of productivity and quite a bit of happiness. It requires a little self-awareness and spending about ten minutes each morning thinking. Even on empty days it's important to say: what am I doing today? Stop and plan. Maybe the decision is: I'm going to sit and read all day, that's what I feel like. Or I'm going to clean the house. Great. But let it be a conscious choice. I think that's the key to a satisfying life: that your decisions aren't made by your environment but by you saying, I want to do this because I think it's the right moment. So try it and let me know what you think.
