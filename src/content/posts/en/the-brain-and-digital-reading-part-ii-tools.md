Alright, digital reading part 2. In this second video I wanted to talk about the tools I've found useful for dealing with the problem of information in digital format and the difficulty of absorbing it properly. If you haven't seen the previous video, I'll link it here, or in the description because I usually forget to add links. It's important to watch it to understand why it's necessary to develop strategies for dealing with information in digital format and to understand the theoretical framework I'm working within.

Given the need to properly absorb information in digital format and the fact that the vast majority of information is by default in that format, I've been forced to develop certain strategies to absorb it as well as possible and in the most ecologically responsible way. Especially since my work involves reading large amounts of information in a concentrated way, absorbing it as best I can, and connecting it with other ideas.

The first tool I usually turn to — and I turn to different tools depending on the type of text, the length of the text, how much that text matters to me, and how I feel like dealing with it — is some kind of reading assistant in browsers. Firefox has a reading option, and if not there are extensions, both in Chrome and Firefox, Opera, almost all browsers, that do the following: when you take a website, you hit that extension and it eliminates everything that isn't the text. It removes the menus, the sidebars, the hyperlinks, the videos, the images, and leaves you with nothing but a white or sepia-toned page with the text.

That helps tremendously with reading, and I also usually go into fullscreen mode. I made a video a while back where I talked about how to properly watch a YouTube video in Deep Work mode: you start the video, go fullscreen, and sit down to watch it. You don't look at your phone, you don't pause it, you don't look at the comments at the same time, you don't look at the recommendations, you're not checking your email — you start the video and watch it from start to finish in fullscreen without touching anything. It might seem a little odd, but it would surprise you how much it improves attention, how distracted we are when we're on the internet. I do the same thing when I want to read a not-too-long article on the computer or the phone. Fullscreen, distraction-free mode, clear away all that junk surrounding the text.

The next tool I find very useful is the Pocket app. Pocket lets you send articles to the app and the app saves them in a distraction-free format, with fonts that are quite decent for reading, and automatically downloads them offline to your device. When I come across an interesting article — but not interesting enough to want to save permanently — I send it to Pocket. The app downloads it offline automatically and when I have a quiet moment, I open the app, look at the pending articles, and read them. Usually two or three at most — I don't like reading more than that because then it starts to be too much. Calmly, from start to finish, without opening any other notifications.

A functionality of Pocket I find very interesting is that you can read in infinite scroll mode or in page mode. My experience is that page mode helps me retain information much better, because it lets me remember roughly where things were, whereas scroll mode is like "it was somewhere around here."

Then, when I want to read an article that matters to me a bit more, I use an app called Push to Kindle. It's a game changer, I'm telling you. For Kindle and other e-reader users, you can send an ebook to the device by email and the e-reader downloads it. What Push to Kindle does is take an article, convert it to ebook format, and send it to your device. When I want a long article — one that takes 15-20 minutes to read, or that particularly interests me — I grab it, send it to the ebook, close it, forget about it, keep doing what I was doing. And then Saturday morning, with a cup of coffee, calmly, phone off, in the sun, like a proper person, I pick up my Kindle and say: let's see what articles we have from this week. I connect to wifi, the four articles download, and I read them calmly, taking my time and reflecting as much as I need.

Also, when I don't just want to read one article but the whole blog of a person I find interesting, I use Push to Kindle together with Calibre, which is my e-book manager. Calibre lets you add plugins and one of them is ebookmerge. Ebookmerge takes a series of ebook files and combines them into a single document with a table of contents, organized like a proper digital book. When I want to read someone's blog that has 30 or 50 entries, I download all their articles in ebook format, combine them into a single document called "So-and-so's Blog," pass it to the ebook, and read it calmly, one or two entries at a time. This way I turn a blog post — from which I'd get nothing because I'd be thinking about other things, it has links, videos, pop-ups, junk — into a concentrated, deep reading that I enjoy much more and retain much better.

And finally, when I really need to retain information, when it's a complicated text, when it's a complex text that requires assimilation or that I want to keep, I print it. As I said in the previous video, nothing beats paper. All my scientific papers from university I print. It's impossible to read a complex, deep article, with interconnections, with scientific and technical language, in digital format. It just doesn't work. I absorb less than half the information. And the same applies to books. If it's a book that deeply interests me, I'm going to buy it. Because it's very different — especially if it's an expository, informative, and complex book.

For all of you more interested in how to apply these strategies to work, I recommend the book Deep Work by Cal Newport. In this book he talks especially about work and how to work in a concentrated and deep way. One of the things he talks about is that when we work in a focused way we get much more satisfaction from the work. If you do the same task while scattered, you finish with a feeling of tedium. If you take it from start to finish and do it as well as you can, you finish satisfied, proud of your work. The same happens when you read. If you take a text from start to finish, you finish feeling good, with a good taste in your mouth. If you read it by skipping around, you neither enjoyed it nor retained it.

These are my strategies for dealing with the digital world: fullscreen, eliminate all distractions, send documents to the Kindle, combine them into a single file or print them. All of this has increased my productivity a lot and above all helps me enjoy what I read more, retain more information, and live with less stress. I hope all of this is useful to you.
