You don't want free time. That's garbage. And it's something that took me a while to learn.

You don't want free time. You want occupied time. And this is something that's been sold to us badly. The intention was good, but it's been sold to us badly. And what that's created is false expectations about what to do and how to organize our time. We've been told that we work to earn free time. And that you're happy in your free time. Work time, well, you just get through it. And free time has been sold as what you have to do to be happy — watch movies, series, lie back and stare at the sky, do nothing. And that's not true.

What we want is occupied time. When you spend an afternoon watching a series, or an entire afternoon wasting time on your phone, you don't feel good. You feel like you wasted the afternoon. When do you actually feel good? You feel good when you've done something you value, put in effort and pulled it off. And you had a project and that project moved forward. The problem is that sitting down to work on the projects you have is hard.

So, you don't want free time. You want occupied time. Full of things that when you finish you say: this made my life a little better.

And I don't mean working sixteen hours a day. Not at all. I mean time dedicated to certain activities deliberately. Spending an afternoon with a friend can be very valuable time. If you're at a café and you haven't seen each other in a long time, you're adding value to your relationship and that adds value to your life. Spending an afternoon on Instagram doesn't add value to your life. Going to eat with your family and spending the afternoon with them adds value to your life. Watching two seasons of Game of Thrones doesn't add value to your life.

It's hard to say this because I'm the first one who, if a day or an afternoon isn't planned, lets it slip away. You can even lose an entire afternoon to just meditating. You can meditate for hours, but it has to be something conscious: I'm going to spend this time meditating. Or an afternoon reading: I'm going to dedicate this time to reading. Consciously, deliberately.

If you let things happen on the fly, whatever comes comes. Things don't happen. Time slips through the cracks. Before you know it the morning is gone.

I've found myself in this situation in Spain, since I finished the first year of my master's and came back. As I mentioned recently, I have very few friends here with free time right now. And some mornings I'd say: well, I'd like to do this, this, this. And I'd start messing around, looking for something else on the internet. And before you know it it's one o'clock. It's practically lunchtime. And that can't be. That can't be because it's not satisfying.

One way to work on that is what I mentioned in the previous video: get out of the house. Don't stay home. Do what you have to do, but do it outside. At a library. In a study room. At a café. Doesn't matter. That already shifts your mindset. At home the mindset is: sit down and rest. If you're outside, the mindset is: social interaction, work, a different environment. Plus you get a lot more light, which clears your head.

And the other is to plan. Take what you need to do and write it down. And when you see you're going to have a free morning, say: I'm going to tackle this project and this one. They can be simple things. It can be going to buy certain things, sitting down to write for a bit, reading, building a website, painting, taking the dog to the beach. But whatever you do, do it deliberately. And you'll realize that very rarely will you consciously say: I'm going to sit and binge this season of a show. Because when you do you have a feeling of emptiness, of "this didn't give me what I expected."

We've been sold the idea that we want free time. And it's not true. I was reading about it in Cal Newport's book Digital Minimalism. When he argues for reducing technology use, he says you have to fill that time with something. And one of the things he advocates for is filling your life with little things that bring you pleasure and that you have to do by hand.

Some people have retired very early from the job market because they'd earned enough that, by cutting their cost of living, they could get by. These people sometimes went off to cabins and so on. And what was common to all of them is that their free time — which they had a lot of — wasn't spent watching movies or doing nothing. It was spent doing things. Often physical tasks. They had to chop wood for winter because they didn't pay for heating. They had to read. And all of that is stuff you have to do, but that doesn't happen if you don't plan for it.

I've changed my mindset. I don't want to retire. Because I do what I do because I enjoy it. I like taking projects and developing them. The project can be building a shelf. It can be designing a training program. It can be writing. It can be developing my Python skills. I need something on the go. I need a project, or several. And that's what gives me satisfaction and meaning.

Think about time that way. Don't think about work and then free time. Think about projects. Work is a project. And as a project it has to move forward, both economically and psychologically. You need to earn more, but you also need to be doing things that motivate you. And don't think of your free time as the satisfaction coming from tequila on a beach. You'd be tired of that in two days. The satisfaction is going to come from having your own projects and investing in them.

Let's shift that thinking. Let's say we don't want free time. We want occupied time. Take care.
