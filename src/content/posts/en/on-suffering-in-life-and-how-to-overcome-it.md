Good morning. Today I decided to record out here because the sun is coming out a little. I like sitting on the ground a lot. Let's see, where do I start?

Suffering is inherent to human life. I think that's the first thing you have to recognize to approach this topic. It's necessary by simple contrast: to have moments of great happiness you have to go through some rough patches. But it also makes the moments of happiness more valued, more important, it helps you recognize what's good about what you have.

Suffering here understood not just as vital suffering and depression, but also as the effort to get what you want. To set aside pleasures and suffer in order to get what you want. To get something you have to put in effort. Whether it's physical exercise, whether it's building the career you want, whether it's having a stable relationship. That's indisputable. And on the other hand, as the limited individuals we are, we have to deal with a very variable, very random, and very complex environment over which we have no control. That's inevitably going to lead to suffering.

How does a person face suffering? And how do they take it on voluntarily knowing there will be growth on the other side? First: by accepting that it's necessary. Understanding it as one more element of life, knowing that life is not light and color and happiness at every moment, certainly not. But it can be wonderful. And for it to be wonderful you have to make an effort, you have to go through hard times and you have to build yourself as a person in those moments. And also, when you have good moments, value them, enjoy them and know that they're finite. But just as the good moments are finite, the moments of suffering are too. That's precisely what I wanted to talk about.

Staying and lamenting how much you're suffering doesn't take you anywhere useful. It's not a super optimistic point of view, the "no, you just have to think positively at all times." Yes, of course you have to see the positive side, but you have to accept that at certain moments you're in a rough place. And right now it's your turn to be in a rough place. That's okay, you accept it, just try not to stay swimming in the mud. I'm in a rough place right now, this is going to last me a while, I'm going to try to get out of it as stoically as possible.

It can apply to a breakup: you can languish in the corners of your apartment, or you can say "I'm not doing well, so I'm going to start exercising, I'm going to get into personal projects, I'm going to see my friends more. I won't be at full capacity because I'm emotionally hurt, but I keep going." That's a positive perspective. Not the "nothing's wrong, I'm always happy." That's false and counterproductive, because you're lying to yourself, and the lie creates a dissonance between reality and what you're saying. And our brains don't like that.

The second point, and here I want to give a lot of credit to Héctor, a friend from Valencia: the limitation of suffering. Just as moments of happiness are finite, so are moments of suffering. And being conscious of that finitude helps us face them. You know this is going to last until around here, more or less. And that makes it much more bearable.

An example of mine: I didn't want to go back to Germany and I had to go back to Germany. I knew it was going to pass, because when I was here I was comfortable, and I said: Okay, you're in a really bad place right now, but you know this is going to last three days. Is it going to pass? It's fine, get through it. I was in absolute misery for three days. And it passed. But knowing it was a temporary condition helped carry it. In fact, what can hurt most and most destroy a person's morale is temporally unlimited suffering, not knowing when it's going to end or whether it will. That's very hard. It's part of the psychological torture of Nazi concentration camps and similar.

If suffering is voluntary, having a deadline helps you push harder, stay more motivated, go through it with drive to come out the other side. A period of exams with no end date would be impossible to bear. There has to be a limit: I'm pushing as hard as I can because I know in three weeks I'm done. If you knew it was going to drag on indefinitely, forget it.

This leads me to a very important point I took from Deep Work: the need that when you work, you work, but when you rest, you rest. You can't be at full capacity all the time. You have to set stages: now I'm going to put infinite emphasis on this project, and stop. Rest. There has to be that cut. You can't be on the front line indefinitely. You burn out. This goes especially for entrepreneurs or those of you who want to start something and are very motivated: you need to stop. I don't know anyone who gets past that. And if you don't stop voluntarily, your body stops you. In the form of demotivation, lack of drive, frustration.

The combination of hard work and rest is important, and there is no one without the other. In the same way, if there's only rest, you get tired of resting: it becomes boredom, empty time that produces nothing. Let's not fall into the lie that a life with infinite free time would be wonderful. We need responsibilities to enjoy life. It can be a job, it can be anything. But we need a certain routine and certain responsibilities.

To summarize: suffering can be overcome by being conscious of its necessity in life, of the fact that it's unavoidable and that everyone goes through it. And on the other hand, by being conscious of its finiteness in time: that it ends. Either because emotionally you know you're going to get through it, or because you set a limit yourself.
