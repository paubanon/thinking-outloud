It might seem very trivial, very obvious. Yes, okay, you have to look people in the eye. But reflecting on it, I realized I had a tendency not to. Reflecting on seduction and important patterns — just as I did with voice — I arrived at eye contact. And there's very little information, very little attention paid to eye contact. In seduction circles it's usually said that you have to be a confident person. But it's hard to explain exactly what that means. Eye contact has turned out to be a key element of that confidence for me.

The first thing is the sensation of holding someone's gaze. At first it's uncomfortable — you feel uneasy, you feel observed. And you feel like you're making the other person feel observed. For insecure people this can be a problem, and for me it was. If I caught someone's eye on the street, I'd look and then mind my own business.

But a look can say many things. It can completely change your game. Not just in relationships and seduction. With your boss, with your family, with your friends. A steady gaze conveys confidence and self-assurance in yourself and in what you're saying. A look can also convey warmth, it can generate intimacy, it can tell the other person on a subconscious level: I'm here, fully with you, I don't have my head somewhere else, I'm giving you my full attention.

Why does it make us uncomfortable? On one hand, it makes us uncomfortable when someone looks at us. The difference between a creepy look and a confident look is incredibly subtle. We feel observed. And lately we don't feel observed very often, we don't feel that real human connection very often. We're used to dealing with people without really engaging with them. If every interaction on social media counted as a personal interaction, you'd never have a person truly talking to you.

There are evolutionary theories that say human beings have so much white around their eyes because the gaze, for us, is more than a tool for seeing — it's a sign of trust. If someone doesn't look you in the eye, you get the sense you can't trust that person. We've developed eyes with a lot of white to identify the people we can trust. And our brains are tremendously good at detecting when we're making eye contact and when we're not.

Something that gets said in the seduction community: if you're uncomfortable looking someone in the eye, look at their forehead instead. Terrible. You can tell when someone is looking at your forehead. Don't do it.

You want to be the kind of person who, when at work talking to their boss and wanting to make a point with emphasis, puts it on the table, looks them in the eye and says: this is how it is, I think this is how it is. That conveys that you're grounded, solid, that you're a pillar, that you firmly believe in what you've said. If you have total conviction that something is right, why would you harbor doubts?

It also serves to tighten the screws on someone a little. If you stare someone steadily in the eye you can make them feel more or less uncomfortable, because that person feels they're under your scrutiny and it can make them open up more.

We all have in our heads the archetype of a powerful person: they don't avoid the gaze. A steady, penetrating, calm gaze that makes people tremble. You want to have that gaze. Not to use it as a tool of oppression, but to have the capacity to do it. And also so that when someone turns that gaze on you, you can hold your ground. I'm here, and you might be my boss, but I'm not your subject. We're collaborating. You want to be able to look your boss in the eye and hold it. That can sometimes be very uncomfortable because it can be interpreted as a sign of defiance. Be careful how you use it.

How do you develop a steady gaze? The first step is to notice whether you look the other person in the eye or not. Because if you never look people in the eye, you're never aware that people are looking for yours. You never realize that you're not giving it back.

So you have to become aware of this. At first you'll be hyper-aware, you'll try to hold the gaze a little longer than feels comfortable. But in a relaxed way, calm, talking normally.

A really good practice — one you can't do right now because you're locked in because of coronavirus — is: when you're walking down the street and you catch someone's eye, wait for the other person to break eye contact. Don't break it yourself. Especially with attractive women, because it's harder. Don't stare at them in a creepy way, not with a face like you're devouring them with your eyes. It's a curious, open gaze that says: you caught my attention, maybe in another situation I'd like to meet you, and I'm looking because I find you interesting. If the other person looks away, you go on with your life. But if they hold it, hold it back. Sometimes you cross paths and look at each other, and look at each other, and then you smile. It's difficult, but it's going to start building your confidence.
