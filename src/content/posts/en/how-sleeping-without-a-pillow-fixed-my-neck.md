Hey everyone, good evening. For a while now the channel has been mainly philosophy videos and reflections on everyday life, but as I said at the start, this channel is for posting daily reflections and comments, and it had been a while since I'd shared anything on health and training. Those of you who follow me know that cold showers, meditation... various everyday tools, the deep squat, all of that are techniques I use.

And there's one I've been doing since my third year of university, that I implemented and haven't changed since. There are habits I've implemented that worked, and others I tried and dropped. For example, I didn't like Wim Hof breathing exercises and stopped doing them.

This one is very simple: sleep without a pillow.

Why? Evolutionary coherence. We've been sold the idea that we need a pillow to keep our neck in the correct position. But evolutionarily that falls apart completely. It's exactly the same way cushioned footwear falls apart. Once you look at it a little, you go: this is unnecessary. And when you pull on that thread and start filtering your whole life through that lens, you see there are a lot of things that are unnecessary. Sleeping with a pillow was one of them.

I wasn't that deep into the whole evolutionary coherence thing back then, but I simply realized that from studying and spending many hours hunched over a desk, I had developed a pretty uncomfortable cervical overload. I decided to try it: I got rid of the pillow. And it was immediate. Two days, and the neck pain and discomfort I had disappeared.

When you sleep without a pillow, at first it feels strange. The most comfortable position is generally on your back. A lot of people don't sleep on their back, although that's also something you can get used to. One of the things I was looking into is that many people say sleeping on your side is a bit complicated without a pillow. And I partly get that. But I've gotten used to it and I'm not sure how right or wrong it is. There are positions some people are more comfortable in.

When sleeping on your side, for example, a lot of people suffer from a lack of scapulothoracic mobility. The scapula is the shoulder blade and it's connected to the humerus to support itself in place. The scapula moves over the thoracic cage and is capable of vertical sliding, horizontal sliding, and tilting. People generally don't know this exists and only perform glenohumeral movement. However, scapular and glenohumeral movement should be synergistic to maximize range of motion.

Because people lack scapulothoracic control, they generally struggle to sleep on their side on hard surfaces. But if you have adequate scapulothoracic mobility, that problem doesn't exist. If you lie on your side, the scapula is able to slide forward, the neck barely undergoes a small deformation and you're not lying with your neck completely twisted. If you add that a mattress generally has a bit of give, we find that proper scapulothoracic mobility allows us to sleep on our side too.

Sleeping on my back is very comfortable for me, although it makes mouth breathing easier — that's another topic for another video.

Something I also recommend quite a bit, and there's nothing mysterious about it, is lying on the floor. Find a clean hard floor and lie on it both face up and face down in a starfish position, and feel how the floor presses to align the vertebrae of your thoracic cage. Notice how the pelvis can feel uncomfortable and the lower back too, but that's something that builds over time and is simply a lack of mobility. And it's incredibly relaxing to feel the weight of your own body, face up and face down, and notice how your body pushes against the floor. The floor is completely non-reactive to your pressure, unlike a mattress, and it makes you adapt the position of your scapulae, your thoracic cage, your neck, your hips to the floor. You get a complete relaxation. And the same face down, with your head turned to the side, doing soft but very relaxed breaths, noticing how with each breath the muscles of the body relax more and more.

Going back to the pillow topic, which is what I wanted to talk about: you simply have to understand that today's comforts make life easier for us, we agree on that, but sometimes that ease comes at a price. Sometimes that price is negligible compared to the benefits it gives us. And other times that price is very high. And the only way to discover what the price is for each thing is: you try it, you remove it, and you decide whether it's worth it.

That happened to me with nofap: I was dying to try it, and after a while I fell back into old habits, and I realized it wasn't worth it for me at that point, it wasn't what I wanted. Instant satisfaction, but then an emptiness, having let myself down. I was better off before even though it was hard.

And in the end, all of this is each person's endless journey: of discovering yourself, of trying things, of learning, investigating and thinking hard. Above all of asking yourself every day what you want to do, how you want to do it, why you do it that way. And sometimes it can start with something as simple as: I have neck pain from studying too much, how can I change it? With the pillow, let's try it.
