Hello, ladies, gentlemen, monkeys, snakes and leopards. The other day I was listening to a talk by Sherry Turkle, who is the author of a book I've mentioned before called Reclaiming Conversation. It's an essay that digs into the effect that social media and new communication methods have on the way we relate to each other. Basically it lays out the problems that come from overusing the mobile phone in everyday communication: how it weakens our personal relationships and that makes us unhappy. And this unhappiness causes us, ironically, to turn to our phones even more to try to fix it, when that's actually not what we need.

She also talks about the importance of real communication with people, of in-person dialogue, as a tool for strengthening relationships, for creating them, and for exposing our vulnerabilities to others.

After listening to this talk, my brain made a very interesting connection between this concept and Jordan Peterson and his discourse on responsibility. And since if I don't write these things down they slip away, I immediately paused the talk and sketched out these ideas.

The idea Sherry Turkle presents in the talk, and also in the book which I'll link below, is that today the overuse of the mobile phone is incredibly common. The lack of real presence I think is making us tremendously unhappy without us realizing it. It makes it harder to find a partner, destroys friendships, weakens the bonds with your family. The phrase "Alone Together," which is another earlier book of hers, I think is perfect to describe all of this.

Having rules for phone use, being conscious about it, really encouraging getting together with people in person, even when you say: just tell me here by phone. No. Meet up, have a coffee, accept the effort, it's worth it.

A very good example: you go to a work meeting, you arrive a little early, let's say five minutes, and you say: well, while the meeting starts I'll check my phone. You look, reply to a couple of things. The meeting starts, you put the phone down, great. But the problem is that if you didn't have your phone, those five minutes before the meeting started, instead of doing that, you'd stop and look at the colleague next to you and say: hey, what's up? How's it going? And they'd say: a bit rough, I have to finish this report, my boss won't stop with this. And you'd say: yeah, mine's been giving me hell too, but don't worry. And you start chatting about nothing. And another day it turns out their dad was in the hospital and they'd been having a hard time. Then a third day you meet up and you just say: how's your dad doing? And they say: really well, thanks for asking. And little by little like that you establish a relationship of friendship and trust.

Those kinds of connections with people, which can happen with the baker, with someone on the bus, with your coworker or labmate, even in class, don't happen if you have your phone. And not only do they not happen, but our brain, because it has no sense of having missed something — since it never even surfaced — isn't aware of it. You go through the day walking down the street with your phone and you're not conscious of all the potential opportunities you've had to start mini conversations with people. Which maybe are just four sentences you exchange with someone. But maybe, to get dramatic, it's the love of your life. And that cliché cartoon that says "someday the love of your life will walk right past you and you'll be busy on your phone complaining about how you can't find love"... I think it's very apt.

By the end of the week you can feel a little empty. And then what do you do? You go back to your phone to tell your friends how empty you feel. And you don't realize that the solution is often to turn off the phone, learn to be alone, learn to enjoy solitude as something necessary and positive. And learning to be alone teaches us to be with others too.

But in this video, the point I wanted to get at was this connection I made between Sherry Turkle and Jordan Peterson. Sherry Turkle, in the book or the talk called Alone Together, said that we like technology because it gives us the illusion of companionship without the responsibility of friendship. That is, it gives you the feeling that you have someone there to listen to you, but you have no responsibility, none of the demands that a real relationship entails. It's like — let's say it's like the porn of relationships. Porn in the sense that it's just the sex extracted and distilled from a complete romantic-sexual relationship with a person, with everything that involves, good and bad.

This is the illusion the new technologies give us. The feeling of being surrounded by friends, but not having to take responsibility toward any of them. And that word, responsibility, even though she didn't use it in the talk, clicked in my brain. Because Jordan Peterson, part of his discourse, says that part of the problem with humanity today is that we want rights, rights, rights, but don't want any responsibility. People keep whining because I want more rights, I want to be less oppressed, I want things to be made easier for me. And they don't realize that rights and responsibilities are two sides of the same coin.

And people don't realize that hidden in responsibility is the meaning of life. It's the feeling of: as useless as I am, at least I can do this. And I can do this thing that I value. In taking responsibility for your life that element is there. Giving meaning to your life because you say: okay, life can be tough, but at least I'm doing this.

I think part of the reason we feel so alone with social media and the excess of technology is that... it doesn't matter what you say, but you take on no responsibility. And by responsibility I mean that a relationship, of any kind with someone, involves mutual support. It means you put up with my shit and I put up with yours. And that way, supporting each other, it's easier to move through life.

In a real, face-to-face relationship, if only one person complains and dumps and pours everything onto the other without listening to the other person, that other person is going to get tired and say: I'm not staying here just as a spectator. I'm out. So in a real relationship, the duty of each individual is to listen to the other person, to make the other person feel important, to help them, to share. And their right or privilege is that the other person listens to them too.

What I think is missing, and this is the connection that clicked in my head, is that just as Peterson says that in responsibility, regardless of what it costs you, lies the meaning of life... I think friendships are the same. Part of why it's worth having a relationship with someone is the burden it places on your shoulders. Knowing that you are important and useful to that other person. And you're only important and useful to that other person if you're willing to carry the burden they place on you.

We don't just want to be listened to. People think they want someone to talk to, an artificial intelligence that pats them on the back. But that's not reality. Reality is harder. A real relationship is hard. And it's going to cost you to understand another person. And sometimes it's going to be unpleasant. And sometimes you're going to think: god, I want to knock this person's head off. But in all that richness, in all that plurality, in all those dimensions in which a person exists, lies the beauty of a relationship. We don't want to be listened to. We want to feel important to other people.

And it's in taking responsibility over another person where the real meaning of a friendship lies. Knowing that the wellbeing of that other person depends in part on you. On your listening. On your empathy. On sending a message every now and then and saying: hey, how are you doing? And that person knows you'd also be willing to do the same for them.

It blew my mind. How these ideas are so connected, and how deep they are for human happiness.

La incomunicación en la era de las comunicaciones.

We proudly assert that technology brings us closer together. We have built a collection of tools that unite us. Information technology has worked the miracle of bringing us closer to our neighbors. That's why we worship it. Because it's the closest thing we have to God. But are we right? What if technology separates us? What if the promise of connection disconnects us? What if the closeness technology guarantees is an illusion?

We are going through a paradoxical era. The omnipresence of electronic communication devices has generated new relational habits that, from a certain perspective, could be understood as fictitious unions, ways of relating falsely or of being alone together.

Wally is a fable about the hidden reverse side of progress that draws from the wellspring of literary dystopias. It's not only an ecological manifesto with the well-worn moral about caring for the environment. It's a denunciation of how economic power impunity usurps the functions of political power. The close-ups of the Axiom show us that all the tasks performed on the ship are carried out by machines. Humans, true servants of things, have delegated their responsibilities and tasks to devices. In that state of apathy and inaction, human beings have banished the simple act of walking as if it were a detestable plague. The loss of bone mass and the grotesque obesity of the Axiom's passengers are the most visible consequences of that radical cult of sedentarism. But perhaps not the most tragic.

The ideal of passivity has not only brought consequences in the realm of morphology. It has also substantially altered the basic patterns of human communication. Constantly slumped in their hovering chairs, the space travelers don't even make the effort to turn their heads to hold a face-to-face conversation with their interlocutors. They converse with each other through their screens, even when they're a meter and a half apart. The act of talking to the person right next to you has also been swallowed up by the monster of ones and zeros.

In one of the film's most revealing scenes, we see Mary talking to another woman through a screen, saying: "Going out on dates... all the dates I've been on have been a disaster. I wish I could meet someone who isn't so superficial." Immersed in the banal exchange of opinions, Mary doesn't notice the crushing logic underlying her complaint. That is, the fact that people are superficial in a way of life that reduces modes of connection to a digital surface. Can the relationships established in the flat space of screens possibly avoid superficiality?

What the communication technology in Wally compromises is not so much living as coexisting. Wally presents us with human beings who live under the same roof without truly sharing their lives. They are ghosts to one another. Solitudes moving alongside other solitudes. If they don't touch, if they don't smell each other, if they don't look each other in the face, can we say they are relating?

From Wally we jump to Her. Her tells the story of the sentimental relationship that develops between a man and an artificial intelligence operating system that, as the company's ad promises, "listens to you, understands you, knows you. It's not just an operating system. It's a consciousness." Theodore Twombly, the protagonist played by Joaquin Phoenix, works at a company that provides the service of writing all manner of personal letters, love declarations, and greetings on behalf of its clients. Devastated by his failed marriage, he spends his free time with a holographic video game.

In Her many couples, families, and groups of friends appear moving through the city, but a good part of the pedestrians walk alone through the streets talking through their phone's earpiece, gesturing to no one. Dialogues are already conducted with total naturalness between smartphones and their users. In the end it's a city of solitary people, a metropolis in which its inhabitants seem allergic to dealing with others.

Nothing summarizes Her better than an apparently irrelevant shot. It's an association of frames in which Theodore remains oblivious to the video playing on the enormous screen behind him. Due to perspective, we the viewers see on the screen an owl spreading its talons to catch Theodore. With subtlety and making use of superb symbolism, Spike Jonze sends us the message: we are the defenseless prey of a hungry technology that, like a bird of prey, swoops down on us with the intention of devouring us. Technology, that pet we thought was domesticated, can turn into our predator. Are we raising crows that will peck out our eyes?
