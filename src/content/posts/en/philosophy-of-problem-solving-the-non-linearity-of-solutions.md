This is a short video based on a conversation with a friend named Pablo Carrera. We were talking about problem solving. The main idea was that when it comes to solving problems, the idea people have of how you solve a problem is: you sit down, you start step by step, ta ta ta ta ta, and you reach the end.

Well, that's not how it works. It never is. I was thinking about this the other day watching an exercise class at university, seeing the difference between a structured problem, which follows a logical order — which is the solution to the problem — and the search for the solution to the problem. Which is, philosophically speaking, how you approach a problem and how you deal with the uncertainty of which way to go.

Because when you first pick up a problem you have a bunch of paths to choose from. And what people think is approaching a problem they picture as a linear process: you start from the beginning, step 1, step 2, step 3, step 4... It can be longer, harder, but they don't consider that reality isn't like that. That you never approach a problem that way. And it's curious because even though it's happened to all of us, we keep thinking that problems are tackled like that. Our mind is very bad at understanding these things.

The way you actually approach a problem is much more intuitive, much more natural, based on trial and error. First you ruminate on it in your mind, about where you could start. You lay out all your initial doors. And then you start down one. And you see it leads you nowhere. You hit a wall and go back to the beginning. And then you try another path. And on that other path, halfway through, you stop and say, hold on, this thing I did before on the first path, now it's useful. So you take that thing and bring it into the second path. And that takes you to another dead end. But then you go back, only halfway back. And you find a path 2.1, or 2.2. You start down it. And then you realize that doesn't lead anywhere either. But that with what you figured out by taking that detour, you can go back to the first path and advance. And in the end, that way, branching your options, you reach the solution.

And only then, only after, do you know the path. In the end it's like a maze with many options. When you know the path, it's clear. But when you have to figure it out, you have no choice but to walk, hit walls, backtrack, a step forward, a step back, sideways.

And this is also interesting because it makes you think about how the human brain works to solve these problems. And how the information society interrupts the logical process. When you have to take all these steps, you have to constantly keep in your head, first, a theoretical background about what to expect from the problem; whether what you're doing makes sense; plus, where this problem is taking you. And as you make more attempts, you need to have in the back of your head all your failed attempts, where they've led you and whether they've contributed anything.

All of this is a very complex process with several cores. You have the theoretical, you have what you're doing right now, you have what you've already done and didn't work, what did work, and the doors you haven't taken. And all of this requires supreme attention to keep it all coordinated at once.

The moment a ding goes off from your phone, a WhatsApp, all that clockwork mechanism breaks and the gears come apart. And when you come back it's that feeling of saying: shoot, where was I? I had done this here because... ah right, because this fit with this and this. You have to re-engage everything. If you're constantly breaking that spinning of the wheel — you go to Instagram, you go to Facebook, you go to WhatsApp — every time you get back you have to re-engage, you have to rebuild the house of cards that is logical thinking. And the task becomes much more tedious.

How do you approach a problem? It's very different from how we think it is. In the end solving a problem is very messy. It's not elegant at all. The elegant thing is the solution, but to get to the results, as almost always, you have to get your hands dirty.

It makes you think about how we want to work, how to be efficient, and why social media and the communication society prevents constant, concentrated work.
