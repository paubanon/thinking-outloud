"Creativity exists, but it has to catch you working." Those were Picasso's words on creative work. We're talking about someone extraordinarily creative, someone who painted three or four works a day I don't know how. And we tend to think of creativity or inspiration as something ethereal that appears outside our control and sprinkles magic dust over whatever task we're doing. And magically we have ideas. I'm here to argue against that.

Part of what I'm going to present comes from a book called Rest, by Alex Soojung-Kim Pang. Specifically, about the need to rest in order to work deeply and creatively. It's a great complement to Deep Work because they're two sides of the same coin: Deep Work talks about how to work efficiently in a very intense way in a small window of time; Rest talks about the same thing regarding rest and how important it is for recovering mentally and boosting your productivity and creativity.

In the first chapters he talks about the need for routine in creative work. And the concept of routine is something that's kept coming up in my thinking lately. We tend to think of routine, discipline, schedules as something bad that you have to swallow to be productive. "Ugh, a routine! The same thing every day. A schedule! I have to do this at this time. The discipline of doing what I don't want to do!" And I think all these associations we have with schedules and structure are completely wrong. A routine shouldn't have any purpose other than automating the things you can automate so you don't have to think about them.

The book argues how countless writers, mathematicians, physicists, and entrepreneurs have a routine. And the goal is simply: I'm going to automate these morning tasks so I don't have to think about them and they leave my head free to focus on my stories, my company, my projects. And the schedule, which is also very interesting, is usually conceived as a tyrant. When I think it's more interesting to see it as a refuge, a fortress. It's what I hide behind to block out the world out there and focus on my own stuff. If I don't protect myself, the world interrupts me.

And this time-blocking lets you free up mental space in each block. If you know that when the moment comes to do each task you're going to do it, you can in each block forget about everything else. And you can free up the mental space to work now on what matters.

But there's also the discipline to sit down and do what you need to do. Even if the right mindset is "finally I can sit down and do what I want to do, without distractions from the rest of the world," it always costs. And it doesn't cost to sit down because concentrated work is easy. As Cal Newport argues, doing quality intellectual work is hard, just like it's hard to go to the gym. But it's imperative for producing creative work to go through this.

And here the book argues from the example of countless creative geniuses who talk about the stupidity of the idea of creativity as something that happens to you and you can't do anything about it. For example, Raymond Chandler, the icon of detective fiction, said there should be a block of four hours in which a professional writer does nothing except write. In that block you can not write, but you can't do anything else. You sit down and either you write or you think. You can't do anything else. And in those times when you sit down and can't get started, force yourself: you can't do anything else either. You can't pick up your phone, you can't answer emails, you can't open Reddit. See what happens. The answer is that you end up writing.

Ingmar Bergman said it's necessary to sit down regularly every day at a specific time, regardless of whether you're in the mood to write or not. Tchaikovsky said that any self-respecting artist should not sit with folded arms on the pretext of not being in the mood to work. Waiting for inspiration to come before working is as absurd as a cobbler waiting to be struck by inspiration.

I really loved the example of Stephen King. Instead of thinking of inspiration as an ethereal muse that arrives and sprinkles magic dust over your typewriter, he has something he calls the "muse." The muse is not a Michelangelo's David elegantly carved that comes to bring you inspiration. The muse is a man who lives in a basement, smokes cigars while admiring his bowling trophies, has a belly, and lives in a tank top. Very unpleasant, a grouch, you'd rather be anywhere else. But you know that in the magic bag that man has is what you need: that article you have to write, that idea you need to find. And you have to sit down and work regularly until that man, grumbling and reluctantly, maybe gives you something useful. And only through an enormous amount of effort will you manage to please him enough for him to give you what he has in his bag.

I also remember an interview between Joe Rogan and Jocko Willink where they talked about the need for discipline in creative work. There are days you go to work and produce garbage. But the next day you take that garbage and say: I can salvage this and this. And in the end you get a decent piece of work. Because creative work isn't that inspiration strikes you and you suddenly write the work of your life. It's through producing a lot of garbage that by sheer statistics something decent comes out. And in that process of writing and repeating you learn from that garbage.

The same happens with physical training. Last week I went to train with zero motivation. Absolutely zero. I said: there's no way anything comes out of this today. I feel tired, scattered. I thought about skipping the session. And I said: no. No headphones, in silence, alone in the workout, just do it as fast as you can. It turned out to be the best session in two months. With zero inspiration to work that day. The lesson: you have no control over that kind of thing. The only thing you can do is show up and work.

And this is very important: you can't be creative in fields you don't know. You need tools to be creative. You need to know what tools are available to you, what's been done, what hasn't, why, how it could be broken. I remember a client we had at Enso Movers who did contemporary dance and had a lot of resources on the floor and standing, but in intermediate ranges she didn't have as many. And the answer was that she didn't have enough strength and mobility to work there. She didn't work there, didn't develop movements there, couldn't be creative. By strengthening that range she was able to start moving there and creating in that space.

The same with guitar: you can't be creative if you don't know how to play. You need to know the chords, how to position them, the rules, the norms, so you can later forget them and break them. Back to Picasso: "It took me a whole lifetime to learn to paint like a child again." You have to go through it: learn and then unlearn. This is the path of the artist.

And the cooking example, which hits closer to home. A chef before working does what's called mise en place: before putting together a dish, you arrange the ingredients you need and the tools around you in an organized way. So you can focus solely on creating the dish and forget about everything else. There's no reason to think our brains work any differently. If you want to optimize the creation of a piece of work, the best thing you can do is have everything around you — space, time, tools — in a setup that lets you free up the maximum amount of mental energy to focus on what matters.

And only that way can you maximize your productivity, your creativity, your output, and your quality of life. Because if you do four or five hours of that kind of work, it's more productive than most people are in three days straight. And it lets you free up time to rest afterwards. Which is fundamental for the brain to process things in the background and produce new ideas.

But creativity and motivation come after working.
