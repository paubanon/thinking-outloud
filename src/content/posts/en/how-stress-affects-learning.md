This is the second time I'm making this video. The first time, I hit the lock button on my phone and cut the video a minute in. I must have been talking for about five minutes without recording anything. But it helped me organize my thoughts.

What I wanted to talk about is how stress affects the brain's ability to retain and learn.

Let me start with an anecdote. I tend to arrive late to things, and that includes classes. And I noticed that when I arrived late to class, I basically sprinted on my bike, and as soon as I got there, you take your stuff out, sit down, copy what's on the board, the last ten minutes you missed. But it turned out I hadn't just lost those ten minutes — for the next half hour or forty-five minutes I was unable to retain information or follow the logical threads of the class.

And this got me thinking until I arrived at this: when you're stressed, when the brain detects it's a stressful situation, it's unable to retain information. When you have a stress spike, your brain goes into fight or flight mode — fight or flee — and it's completely unable to retain information because all it wants is to get out. It's like a lion is chasing you: you don't stop to check if it's a spotted or striped lion. You just want to escape.

And I've tested it and it works really well: when I arrive late to class, it's much more efficient for me to walk at a calm pace. Even if I arrive five minutes later than I could have, instead of sprinting I just go at a relaxed pace, trying not to worry about being late. Because even though I arrive a bit later and missed a bit more of the explanation, for the rest of the class I'm perfectly capable of understanding and absorbing it.

This connects to an interview on a very interesting podcast where they talked about how to face challenges. For learning and adaptation to happen, exposure to stress, to confrontation, must be voluntary. Because if you want to overcome a fear, you can't be thrown into it, because you'll panic and you won't overcome it. You have to decide yourself that you want to be exposed to it and that you want to get something out of it.

When it comes to overcoming a fear, you don't do it by reducing how scary the situation feels, but by increasing your courage. The difficulty of getting through a situation has two components: the situation itself, and how you approach it. If you're afraid of elevators, you can't modify the elevator to make it less scary. What you can modify is your conception of yourself: I'm braver than I think and I can get through this. That's how you overcome things.

I find it remarkable how enormous an impact the way you mentally approach a situation has on the final outcome. The same situation, the same person: if you think you're going to get through it and there's no problem, you grow much more than if you're thrown into the void and have to fight without having chosen it.

And I've said this in other videos: the way you change the story you tell yourself, your perception of yourself, changes the results in a real way. It can be something as simple as dramatically improving your university grades just by being aware of this effect. Because when you face an exam and that exam terrifies you, your brain doesn't retain what you're studying properly. The hormones responsible for keeping you in panic mode are maxed out, and it's impossible for a brain in stress mode to concentrate and relax enough to learn. It's completely at odds with evolution.

So asking for maximum performance during periods of stress is very hard for a student, unless they have the ability to accept the situation for what it is and subdue the fear, to accept that the exam is just a small test and de-stress — with whatever story you want to tell yourself — to make sure it doesn't stress you out. Because if it stresses you out you won't learn, and if you don't learn you'll stress out more because you're not learning.

And all of this is just visualization. What you tell yourself. And it's very powerful. How you visualize yourself, how you see yourself. Do you see yourself as someone who can't handle challenges? Or do you see yourself as someone perfectly capable of dealing with life's situations and learning from them? That gives you power over your life. When you're able to learn from situations, you face life differently and you learn not to be afraid.

The key is not to expose yourself recklessly to many stressors. The key is to expose yourself to many stressors with the right mindset: the mindset to learn.
