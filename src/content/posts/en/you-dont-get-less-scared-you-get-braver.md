There's an idea I find fundamental and want to develop here: courage isn't the absence of fear. Courage is acting in spite of fear.

A lot of people wait for the moment when they'll no longer be afraid to do the things that scare them. They wait for time, or enough preparation, or the right circumstances, to make the fear disappear so they can finally act.

But that moment never comes. Never.

What actually happens is different: every time you do something that scares you, and you do it anyway, the fear doesn't disappear, but you become more capable of tolerating it. You learn that fear isn't a stop sign. It's just an emotion. And emotions, including fear, pass.

This has a very important practical implication: the path to becoming braver isn't waiting until you're not afraid. It's doing the things that scare you, repeatedly, and noticing that you're still here after. That it wasn't as bad as you feared. That you could handle it.

Every time you do it, you're recalibrating your relationship with fear. You're telling your brain: "this thing that seemed dangerous, actually it wasn't that dangerous." And the next time you face something similar, the threshold for triggering fear drops a little.

You don't get less scared. You get braver. That's the difference.

This applies to almost everything. To public speaking. To having difficult conversations. To starting something. To opening yourself up emotionally in a relationship. To trying new things where you might look bad.

The first step is always the hardest because it's the first. After that, the next one is a little easier. And so on.

Happy New Year. May it be a year of doing things that are scary.
