The other day I was talking with a friend, Pablo Jurado, and he asked me if I'd ever hired a personal trainer. It's funny because the answer is no. And it's funny because I do personal trainer work without ever having hired one, which is a bit contradictory.

The first reason I never hired one is I didn't have the money. And I've always been someone who figures things out for myself. If I had to pick one good quality of mine, one meta skill, it's that I'm very good at pulling myself out of trouble, at not being too lazy to say: I want to do this, I don't have the resources, I'm going to do it anyway as best I can, I'm going to invent the resources.

I remember when I was around 12 or 14 and I wanted a wooden katana, I was into the Japanese thing. My parents said no because it's an aggressive toy. I said: if you won't let me buy one, I'm going to make one myself. I found a carpenter, bought wooden boards, and spent a week and a half cutting, sanding, gluing, until I made a sword. And I still have it at home.

And same with everything. I want to learn this programming language, I sit down and learn it. I don't know how to cook, so I sit down and learn. I want to learn to train, I don't have money for a trainer or courses, so I find the courses, study them, and apply them. The ability to find sources of information for myself.

And another skill of mine is diligence when it comes to following through on a program. I think that's one of the most important elements and where most people fail. Often the personal trainer's program isn't even that good. But the mere fact that you're paying someone makes you commit, makes you stick to it, not skip it, put it in day after day. And that makes the difference.

In that I've been very, very diligent. Not because I'm naturally like that, but because I understood very quickly that that's the way. That there's no magic formula. That you just have to walk. So I set up a training program and followed it almost militarily. Two hours a day, five or six days a week, without skipping. Coming home dead from university and going to train. I think that's what has made the difference at scale. Much more than whether my program was better or worse. If I'd had a trainer I would have improved faster, of course. But this has taught me many more things that a trainer couldn't have taught me.

You learn to trust the process. You learn to experiment. You learn that many times the problem with training isn't having a better or worse program. It's the lack of confidence in your own ability to stick to it. A lot of people get a program, start putting it into practice, and since they didn't pay for it they don't take it seriously, start skipping. Or they have no faith in the program because they designed it themselves. Consequently they don't get results, and if you don't get results you don't take the program seriously. You end up in a cycle where you jump from program to program for years without getting anywhere.

The only piece of advice I'd give: consistency. Setting your sights on a goal and saying I'm going for it 100%. Understanding that the way is to walk. That basic research already gives you 80% of what you need. And the rest, diligence matters much more.

This has taken me much further than anything else. It's understanding that often what matters is consistency. I want to learn to cook: little by little, every day, like an ant, every time you cook you think about how to improve the recipe. For example, the only time I managed to make meditation consistent enough was by taking it with such diligence that I didn't skip a single day. Sometimes I've come home from going out at 2am and sat down to meditate at 2am. You don't skip it. And if you feel like skipping it at 2am, you should have thought of that earlier. Next time, first thing in the morning.

Now I'm at a level where not having a trainer doesn't bother me much because I've already achieved the other meta skill: learning to break down exercises and skills, decompose them into the most basic patterns, work on them individually, and integrate them. Once you know how to do that, everything else is putting in sets. It's making a program, following it, and following it, and following it.

Consistency in your projects goes hand in hand with your confidence in yourself. Because if you make a program and you yourself don't take it seriously just because you made it, you're undermining your confidence in yourself. You're fooling yourself. If you think you should do something and you make a program to do it, make sure you're going to do that program and take it seriously. As if you'd paid 100 euros for it. And you don't skip a single day. When you've done that program for long enough with enough diligence, then you evaluate whether it's good or not. But don't do it if you're not taking it seriously.

And beyond that it has another corollary: if you don't take seriously what you yourself do, how do you expect other people to take you seriously?
