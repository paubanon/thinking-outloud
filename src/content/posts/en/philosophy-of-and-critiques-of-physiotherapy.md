Today I want to talk about physiotherapy. And I don't want to talk about physiotherapy in the sense of telling you what's good and what's bad, but more about the philosophy behind physiotherapy and why I think that, in many cases, it's wrong.

First, a bit of context: I studied physiotherapy for a year before switching to physics. And during that year, and then with everything I've read and my experience in the world of movement, I've developed a fairly critical view of this discipline.

The first problem I see is the fascia one. Fascia is the connective tissue that covers muscles, organs, basically everything. And in recent years it's become very fashionable to talk about fascia as if it were the solution to all problems. Foam rollers, lacrosse balls, deep tissue massage... all of this is sold as a way to "release" the fascia, to change its structure, to improve its gliding. The problem is that the scientific evidence doesn't support this. You can't change the structure of fascia with a foam roller. Fascia is an extremely dense and resistant tissue. The pressure you could apply from the outside is not enough to change anything structurally.

So, why do people feel like it helps? Because there's probably a neurological effect. You're stimulating receptors, you're changing the perception of pain, you're doing something that feels good. But you're not changing the fascia. And the problem is that it's sold as if the fascia were being changed, and that's an important conceptual problem.

The second problem, and this one is deeper, is the overall model. Traditional physiotherapy starts from a very passive model: you come, I treat you, I fix you. And I think that's fundamentally wrong. The human body is not a machine that breaks and needs to be fixed from the outside. The human body is a biological system that adapts, that learns, that needs stimulus.

There's a concept in evolutionary biology that I find very relevant here: evolutionary mismatch. Our body evolved to move in certain ways, to be in certain positions, to perform certain types of effort. And modern life has taken us completely out of that context. We spend the day sitting, in positions we never would have adopted naturally, not moving, not varying positions. And then our backs hurt, our necks hurt, our knees hurt. And the response from traditional physiotherapy many times is: I'll give you a massage, I'll put some electrodes on you, I'll do traction. But it doesn't teach you to move better. It doesn't give you the tools so the problem doesn't come back.

I had a client at Show Movers who had had an anterior cruciate ligament tear. They'd had surgery, done the standard rehabilitation, and were still having problems. And the problem wasn't so much the ligament itself, but that they'd never been taught to use the knee properly, to distribute forces properly, to have awareness of how they were moving. That's what's missing many times.

The solution, in most cases, is to move. Move well, move consciously, move progressively. It's not sexy, it's not complicated, it doesn't require expensive machines or sophisticated techniques. But it works. And there's a continuum between physiotherapy and training that I think is ignored too much. The physiotherapist should be someone who helps you get from being injured to being able to train. And the trainer should be someone who understands enough about movement and anatomy to not injure you. That continuum doesn't exist clearly in practice, and that's a problem.

It's like nutrition. Everyone should know how to cook. You don't have to be a chef, but knowing the basic principles of how to eat well is a fundamental skill. In the same way, everyone should have a basic understanding of how their body works, of how to move well, of how to keep it in good shape. It shouldn't be something you completely delegate to a professional.

I'm not saying physiotherapy doesn't have its place. There are situations, especially post-surgical or in acute cases, where a professional's intervention is necessary and important. But in the vast majority of chronic cases, the back pain, the postural problems, the solution is not another massage session. The solution is to learn to move better and to move more.
