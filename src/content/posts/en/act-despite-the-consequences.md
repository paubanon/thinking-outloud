Good morning. Welcome to my office today. I really like this spot. It's a table under a huge pine tree in the shade and when it's very hot it's very cool here. I like this setup for recording a video because it feels like we're having a one-sided conversation.

What I wanted to talk about today is the ability to act regardless of the consequences. I think it's an important element in the development of character. And I think someone who is whole, someone who is complete and secure in their values and the strength of their ideas, should go through this, or should think about it, or already has.

It's a quality we see in all the masculine and powerful — not oppressive — ideals in cinema history. In the typical models of masculinity from the 50s, the 60s. And I think it deserves a closer look especially today, where anything goes and you're not allowed to offend anyone, and therefore nobody knows how to say no, nobody knows how to stand firm in their ideas.

I think there are ideas that are right, ideas that are wrong, and that we should talk about them to reach some kind of consensus.

This won't be a political video, despite how it might seem so far. I'm going to use some of the language of power, but not in the sense of someone tyrannical. Let me clarify what I mean.

The ability to act, knowing how to act, being willing to act despite the consequences, is a quality of someone powerful. It's not that it's going to make you powerful, but it's a quality of someone powerful. And these relationships tend to be bidirectional: someone powerful acts this way, and if you act this way, over time, both at a subconscious and practical level, in day-to-day life, you'll gain more power.

Power has been associated with something negative, with someone who abuses it. It's the Marxist view of the world: oppressors and the oppressed. And the oppressors are that way because they have power. Every human interaction is understood as a power struggle: your friends, your family, your partner. I think it's a deeply stupid idea that has no idea how the world works. But it's caught on, because it appeals to empathy and nobody wants to be an oppressor.

And yet there's a point in Jordan Peterson's discourse that I really like. Which is that having power is not the same as oppressing. And that being weak is not the same as being virtuous. Someone virtuous, someone morally sound, is someone who has power but uses it ethically and correctly. Someone who can't be corrupted.

Because if you're weak, you can't be virtuous. You can't be stoic, you can't be principled and act morally, because you're not facing the dilemma between being corrupted or making a sacrifice. You don't have that capacity if you're weak. If you're weak, you can only act like a weak person.

So it's a worthy goal to acquire power and still be incorruptible. That's the foundation of being a good person. It's not just how you act. It's how you act in certain contexts. Imagine someone who doesn't steal, but doesn't steal because they can't. From my point of view that's not a good person. What defines a good person is someone who faces the situation where they could steal and doesn't. That's the difference between someone with power and someone without.

And one of the ways to exercise that power is knowing how to say no. It's important to know how to say no. And most people don't know how to say no, because they're not aware of the consequences of a no. When you say no and do it consciously it means: I'm saying no because I'm putting my own person, my time, my principles or my opinion ahead of yours. And I do it knowing that it might affect our relationship, it might cause a small argument, it might create tension between you and me. But I'm willing to accept that rather than bend to a will I don't think is right.

The irony is that sometimes saying no and creating that tension is more positive than saying yes. Once that tension is resolved, the other person sees you differently. You're no longer someone weak. You're someone who can take the blows life throws and say: this is my opinion and I'm not changing it because it causes me a little discomfort. I'd rather go through the discomfort than bend my will. And that's hard to find. People value it. Your bosses value it. Your partner values it.

I'm not saying to always say no and be a completely self-centered person. What I'm saying is: be aware of when you say yes and be aware of when you say no. And be aware of the consequences. Because sometimes the consequences of a yes you didn't want to give are worse than the consequences of a no.

It's like bargaining. You have no power when you bargain if you're not willing to get up and walk away empty-handed. Once you've accepted that can happen, you bargain calmly. This is the same. You can say: I'm not willing to do that that way. Are you willing to come down? Great. Not? I'm leaving. What matters is being aware of what my principles are, how far I'm willing to give, what consequences my yes has, what consequences my no has.

And I was thinking about this a year ago. There was a critical moment in the history of Enso Movers. We were on the verge of closing. And during one of the low points, under enormous pressure to decide what to do, I was at a peak of stress listening to Jordan Peterson and his Bible readings.

I remembered the story of Abraham and Isaac. God asks Abraham to sacrifice his son. Abraham is willing to do it. He makes all the preparations. And when the last moment comes, when he's bringing the knife down, an angel appears and stops his hand. Well done, Abraham, because you trusted and showed your faith.

Without getting into religious reasons, I think the moral of that story is very important. Which is that sometimes you have to be able to act despite the consequences. And that sometimes, to achieve the greatest possible good, you have to sacrifice the thing you love most. That phrase has burned itself into me. Sometimes to get what you most want or need, you have to sacrifice what you most value.

The alternative is never clear. You don't know what's coming. Maybe Abraham would have killed his son and been left looking like an idiot. But that's not what happened. And it makes you think. Religious stories in many cases are reflections of lessons learned over the years.

The same applies to attraction and seduction. To be able to flirt, to be able to seduce someone, you have to be willing to not care about that person. The moment you depend on them, you've lost. You have to be willing to lose them if necessary. Because you meet someone and you don't really know them. You have a mental image. You have to be willing to dismantle that mental image to replace it with the real self of that person. And if the real self turns out not to be what you thought, you have to be able to say: no, it doesn't matter how attractive you are. If your values don't align with mine, out.

This happened to me recently with my flatmate. You know I've had problems with one of them. And it's very hard because you always tend to say: one more chance, one more conversation. Well, in the last one I said: if there's another one, if we have to sit down one more time to talk about this, it's over. I'm going to file a formal complaint with the university, which is the one that rented us the flat. And time passed, things went well, but we've had another one. And it was hard because, since time had passed, you're not hot from the last one. You're not doing it out of revenge. You're simply being consistent with your word and your actions. At the time I was so fed up I said: enough, one more and that's it. I could have said: come on, let's sit down again. But no. That's it. And with complete calm, no desire for revenge at all, we've had another. I filed the complaint. The process gives me far more headaches than having a conversation in the hallway would. But I know that in the long run it's worth more to deal with those headaches now than to deal with this crap every single day for the year-plus I have left here.

And when you do this regularly, when you slay dragons one way or another — saying yes, saying no, acting — your life improves dramatically. It improves because you keep cutting off the hydra's heads as they grow back. The hydra always has more heads. Every time you cut one, two grow back. But that's life. What you learn is not to let them grow. It's better to deal with the head before it grows. There are still very rough heads. There are still very tough problems. But it's better to deal with them head-on and as soon as possible.

There are two ways to not act. You can not act consciously, and you can not act unconsciously. However, acting always comes hand in hand with consciousness. In day-to-day life we don't do thousands of things unconsciously and nothing bad happens. But those who sin consciously, knowing full well what they're doing... chaos. Pure chaos. And this is even reflected in religious texts and in law: it's very different to act consciously or unconsciously.

When you face a decision: you can act or you can not act. And not acting, letting life drift along until the situation boxes you in and there's only one option left, just to avoid carrying the weight of responsibility, is not a good strategy. Because it means the rest of the world is deciding for you. And most of the time the world is going to decide based on its own interests, not yours.

It's important to act. It's important to know when you're acting and when you're not acting, and what the consequences of each are. Most of the time it's better to act than not to act. And when you act, you can do so in the positive or the negative. It means knowing how to say yes sometimes, but also knowing how to say no. Knowing how to say no is acting. And when you say no and accept the consequences, you're acting despite the consequences. Understanding that the consequences of being a person of integrity are far better than those of being someone weak and fickle who gets swept along by the tide of the world.

This has a lot to do with the idea of masculinity. The phrase "toxic masculinity" I think is garbage. A real, sound masculinity is that of someone who has power and still acts correctly. It's the stoic ideal: despite the consequences, despite how hard life is, to act correctly. Someone who constantly reflects on death, on the consequences of their actions, and decides to act according to what they believe is right, not according to what's easiest. That, for me, is a person of integrity. It's a good ideal to aspire to. A character we shouldn't lose.

Because we're not always that person. It's very easy to give in to others and value their opinion more than our own. It's very hard to put our own opinion on the same level as theirs and demand arguments when making a decision. You know your arguments, but you don't know theirs. So when someone makes a request, ask why. Why should I prioritize this you're asking me over what's aligned with my goals? And a lot of the time you'll find the arguments are weaker. And if they're weaker, you have to say no.
