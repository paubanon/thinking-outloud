This is a video about opinion. About forming an opinion and about the role of opinion in the development of a person, of character. And the role of other people's influence and new technologies in constructing your own opinion.

It starts because the book Solitude, which I've mentioned a few times that I was reading, talks about the dissolution of a person's opinion due to the flood of suggestions that new technologies expose us to. The fact that we always have reviews and other people's suggestions means we don't form opinions, simply because we tend to trust others' opinions more than our own. We tend to think the other person knows more.

I sometimes call this the frame theory. Basically, in a conversation, whoever has the stronger beliefs will dominate the interaction and tend to hold their ground against someone who doesn't have worse beliefs, but believes in them less, or doesn't have the composure to say: that's true or that's not true.

We tend to value other people's opinions more. This is because when you look at your own opinions, you see their flaws. You know where you fall short, you know how much of a mess you are as a human being and how weak you are sometimes. So you tend to trust them less. But when you see someone else's opinion, especially when it's presented with confidence, you tend to think that person knows a lot. Your brain says: if they're presenting it with that much authority, that much confidence, maybe they have really good arguments? And I'm not sure I have that much confidence in myself.

In today's world, where we're bombarded by opinions, reviews, suggestions, this means we never develop our own opinion. That we never think: what do I believe? What do I think? What do I like?

It's very rare to find someone who can look at a YouTube suggestion or a review and say: this is trash, no. It happens to me on Amazon. You see the reviews and you tend to trust them. But what if that person is an idiot? Have you stopped to think about it? What if they don't have a valid opinion for you? What if it doesn't match what you think? Maybe you should trust your own opinion.

And we've lost the ability to accept that something we consume isn't the best, and that that's okay. We want the best and we want it now. We're afraid of not making the optimal choice. It's a very bad attitude toward life because it causes constant dissatisfaction. There's always going to be something better. Just like there's always going to be some kid who can do a handstand better than you.

I see this a lot with Google Maps and restaurants. We tend to go to the restaurants with the best ratings. And this creates another problem too, which is that it feeds the Matthew principle: those who have the most will be given more. The restaurant with good reviews gets more people, gets more stars, gets better reviews, and a very sharp hierarchy is created — restaurants everyone goes to and a bunch of restaurants no one visits. But maybe in those small little places there's gold.

When was the last time you were in a new city and looked for a restaurant without checking TripAdvisor? Go for a walk, look at what's around you, ask local people, take a look, judge for yourself. Say: I'm not loving the look of this place. Look: here it seems like there are people, I like the atmosphere, the fish at the counter looks good. You ask someone: hey, how's the food here? Really good. And you try it. And maybe there was the best restaurant in the world for half the price around the corner. And it doesn't matter. Because life is a constant cycle of picking up and letting go.

And what we sometimes lose in this exposure to something that isn't perfect, that isn't the best, is also learning. With movies, maybe you don't want to watch Netflix's top recommendation. You pick a film, watch it and say: I just sat through some garbage. And enjoy it anyway. Or watch a film that's a 6. You watch it and say: it was fine, it wasn't the film of my life, I didn't need it, but it was interesting. And in that exposure to things that maybe you don't love is where personality lives.

When someone in a family setting says: I watched this Korean film the other day, god it was weird. And for a second you think: how on earth did you end up finding that film? Because Amazon Prime doesn't recommend that. And in that spark of originality, in that tiny detail of: wow, this person watched this, read this... that's where character is built. That's where a person develops taste. Because when you don't fully like something, you don't just learn what you like, you learn what you don't like and why. And that's valuable.

Because the problem is that in this loop of social media suggestions, the suggestions are built by an algorithm that boxes you in: you're this type of consumer, and as this type of consumer you might like this. And you probably will like it. But you lose the spontaneity of going to someone's house and suddenly they put on flamenco. Man, look at what this person is listening to. And maybe you'd never in your life have thought you liked flamenco. But suddenly you say: oh, this has passion, it has power. And you discover you like it. And 99% of the population that likes classic rock doesn't like flamenco. But maybe you do. And that makes you an individual. It shapes your personality.

With new technology suggestions we end up being consumer archetypes where we all listen to the same Spotify playlists, all watch the same Netflix-recommended films, all read the same books. But I think there's a lot of value in being able to go to a bookshop or a second-hand market and see what's there. Not looking for something: seeing what's there. Going with a completely open mind. I don't know what I want to read, let's see if something resonates with me. And often you leave with nothing. But other times you find a book that catches your attention. Maybe the paper catches your eye, maybe the cover, or how old it is, or the description. You don't need to see a thousand people's reviews. You don't need to know the critics' opinions. It's a book and it caught your attention. And that's reason enough to buy it and read it. And then maybe you say: unreadable. Or maybe you say: wow, I would never in a million years have bought this on Amazon because it doesn't grab me as much there and because there are a billion "better" suggestions right next to it.

In that exposure to the strange, to the randomness of everyday life, of the real world, to the absence of hyperconnection with other people and other opinions: that's where identity is forged. That's where you're the one with the opinion. You're the one who decides whether you like something or not.

And that's very rare today. Someone who truly has a strong opinion. Because people's opinions today aren't really opinions. We've delegated our knowledge to an accumulation of facts, of statements we can look up. What does the internet offer you? Facts. It doesn't connect them for you. It doesn't give you a mental map of how these facts are structured. It doesn't give you the logical associations of why these facts are connected this way. We accumulate: this is so, this is so, this is so. But we lose the point that in forging those connections is where identity and character are built.

We also defer to appeal-to-authority arguments. Saying: this philosopher said this. Sure, but what do you think about it? What do you think about what the philosopher said? Do you think it's true? Do you think it's right? Why? Argue it for me yourself. Don't just tell me this person did this and leave it at that. Because you're not opening yourself up as a person. You're showing me you're flat, that you're not capable of taking a topic, thinking about it and arriving at conclusions or forming opinions about it.

And forming opinions is something you work at, on many levels. Because what you do in one field transfers to others. Someone who can take a film with an 8 or a 9 and say: I think it's garbage. That person is interesting. That person has something to say. And has character. And that person is probably also more likely, when they pick up a scientific paper, to read it and say: these conclusions seem wrong to me, I don't think what the author says... I don't think the conclusions follow from the results. You have to have that capacity.

When was the last time you read an opinion piece in a newspaper and said: this is garbage? We tend to believe that just because it's written, because that person wrote that column and it got published in a newspaper, it's a good opinion. But it's not true. You have to be able to criticize. You have to be able to take a book, an opinion in print, from your friend, from your family, from the classical philosophers, and say: I don't agree.

When someone tells you: Nietzsche said this. And you say: okay, what do you think about that? I don't care what Nietzsche said. I don't give a damn. What do you think? And you also have to be able to take Plato and say: this is very good, but I think this isn't a good attitude toward life, I disagree with this. And it doesn't matter that Plato said it. Plato was a human being. And you, as a complete human being on the same level as Plato, have to be able to understand the arguments and be able to critique them.

Don't misunderstand me. You always have to be open to the possibility that you might be wrong. You have to always be open to changing your opinion, to looking back and saying: I used to think this and it's not true. Or to someone explaining something to you and saying: no, you're right, that opinion is more valid than mine, that person knows more about the topic. That happens to me with art or music. I know there are people who will give me opinions and I'll say: I don't agree with your opinion. But I know your opinion is more valid than mine because you have more knowledge on the subject. And that doesn't exist today either: people say that all opinions are equally valid. And that's a lie.

But you also have to understand that some opinions don't need to be argued. There are technical opinions that need to be argued. But there are opinions that simply are: I like this painting. And someone can tell you: that's a terrible painting. And you have to answer: this isn't a poll, I like this painting, I want it in my room and I don't care what you think. And that's something very rare today. When was the last time you were talking to someone and said: no, I don't think that's true, I don't agree? And held your ground?

I've done it. Taking a text by Descartes and saying: I think he was wrong. I think this argument is bad. I don't agree. Maybe in a few years you look back and see it through a different lens and say: what I used to think this way I now understand differently because my worldview has changed. But maybe you truly do, with everything in your background, arrive at the conclusion that it's not right. And you have every right to say so. And that's what enriches society. That's when you can contribute. That's when you're an individual with character.

I've always said that physicists do what computers can't do. And I find that beautiful. Because computers can calculate much faster than you, can look up information much faster, can solve differential equations with far greater precision. But you can connect ideas and derive new ideas from previous ideas. And a computer can't do that. And that's the value. That's what you contribute as a human being.

There's also an idea I find very interesting, which is the menu idea. If you give me pre-designed options, I'll tend to choose one of those options and won't stop to think about what my real opinion actually is. Because when you go to a restaurant and there's a menu, you choose from what's on the menu. But imagine you have fish, pork knuckle, stew, and soup. You think: which of those options do I want? But you don't stop to think: what do I want? If they don't show you the menu and simply ask what you want, maybe you say: I feel like paella. But just by seeing the menu, you don't think of paella. Your brain says: what do I want from this? On social media it's the same. You tend to judge from what appears, from the suggestions that are there. You don't think for yourself. And that creates masses, creates people without opinion, without character, without identity. People who don't contribute, who only repeat, carbon copies of each other.
