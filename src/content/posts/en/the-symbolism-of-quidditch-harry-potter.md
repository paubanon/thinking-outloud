Today I wanted to talk to you about Harry Potter, Quidditch, and symbolism. The topic of my recent reading has had to do with symbolism and mythology in cultural development. It mainly comes from the work of Joseph Campbell and revisiting the idea of the hero's journey. I recently read The Power of Myth by Joseph Campbell, in which he talks about those myths and symbols common across different cultures throughout human history and what they mean for us.

This is rooted in the idea that stories are not just stories. At least not all of them. Our brain doesn't remember what you want it to remember. Your brain remembers what it decides to remember, those things it considers important. Your brain has an internal hierarchy of values and based on that hierarchy decides where to invest resources. This expresses itself in the form of motivation or in the form of memories. When your brain finds something it considers important, it decides to store it as memory, important for your future survival or for the optimization of your life.

Why do we remember certain stories? Why do we remember some fables and not others? It turns out we remember some stories because encapsulated in them are pieces of information that our brain has recognized as extremely important for our survival, for the exploitation of our potential as people, and our interaction with the world.

This is how myths appear, the stories of religions. They're the stories that have been distilled over time through the retelling, over and over, of the same ones. The superficial has been stripped away until all that remains is a very condensed and very fundamental story. And because it's so dense with information, our brain remembers it, passes it to others, and because it's important and it's a good story, other people remember it too. From this we can understand that symbolism and mythology are not just stories or ideas, but fundamental tools for dealing with the world, encapsulated in the form of fable.

This is also visible in popular culture. The stories that become popular, that become massive cultural phenomena, generally contain these fundamental symbolic elements. And Harry Potter is a perfect example. Harry Potter is a story that has connected with millions of people around the world. And that's not by accident.

Quidditch, in particular, I find symbolically very rich. It's a sport with several dimensions: there are players who score points with the quaffle, there are defenders, there are beaters who dodge bludgers, and there is a seeker whose only mission is to find the golden snitch. And when the snitch is caught, the match ends regardless of the score, with 150 extra points for whoever catches it.

Think about this. You have a whole team working hard, scoring points, defending. And then there's one person whose mission is to find something small, elusive, almost invisible, and when they find it it ends everything. The snitch is not just one more point. The snitch changes everything.

This strikes me as an extraordinary symbolic representation of life. You can be working hard, doing everything you're supposed to do, fulfilling your obligations. But there is something, an element, a mission, a purpose, that when you find it, surpasses everything. What you were doing before still has value, the points you scored still count, but the snitch — that transformative element, that thing you're really looking for — is what closes the game.

And the seeker is the one who takes the most risk. The one with the most individuality. The one who is not in the collective of the other players but seeking something the others can't yet see. It's the hero in Joseph Campbell's terminology: the one who separates from the group, goes to find something, and returns with it to transform the community.

I think J.K. Rowling, consciously or unconsciously, built in Quidditch a very precise image of something that the great myths have been telling for thousands of years.
