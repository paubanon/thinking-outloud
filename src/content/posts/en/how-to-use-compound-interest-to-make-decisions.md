Today I want to talk about compound interest in an application I've been thinking about that isn't the usual one, which is the economic sphere. For those not familiar: a compound interest situation is one where interest is applied on the capital you have plus all previous interest. If you have 100 euros at 10%, the first month you get 10 euros. But the second month, the 10% is calculated on 110 euros, which would be 11 euros. The next month, 12.1 euros. And so on.

Compound interest is extremely well known by economists as an incredibly powerful tool for growing capital long-term. Short-term it's no different from standard interest. But as 10, 15, 20 years go by, the speed at which it overtakes traditional interest is exponential, in the mathematical sense of the word. And the human mind has difficulty grasping what that implies over periods of 20, 30, 50 years.

What I've been mulling over lately is that using compound interest as a metric for deciding what choices to make in life is a very powerful metric. When making decisions we can basically find three types. Those that bring immediate pleasure. Those that have a delayed payoff, where the benefit is going to be greater than if I get it now. The classic example of: do you want 5 euros now or 20 euros in a month? But there's a third type I think is more interesting: those decisions or actions that make all future decisions better. That's compound interest applied to life.

A good example is education. If you invest in educating yourself, in growing, in learning, you're not only developing the technical capacity to solve a specific problem. You're developing critical analysis skills, knowledge in that field, but above all you're learning mental frameworks you can apply to other problems. You're learning to analyze complex problems, to write, to express yourself, to deal with frustration, to advance in uncharted territory, to delay instant gratification. All of that accumulates so that the work opportunities you have in the future, your capacity to analyze political situations, your ability to observe your relationships and the world will all change for the better. You'll have better mental models to analyze and decide with.

Another example is physical exercise. Exercise doesn't only give long-term benefits, which is already a classic example of long-term investment, it also has a compound interest aspect. It's teaching you the capacity to endure physical discomfort voluntarily in order to get benefits in the future. It keeps you healthy and staying healthy, besides meaning you'll live longer, will allow you to engage better with your environment, to have more optionality at a physical level. Something as simple as moving furniture when needed, lifting your grandchildren, helping people in your community. That in turn will improve your personal relationships, will increase your mental vigor, will allow you to think more clearly. The relationship between the mind and exercise is crystal clear. The Greeks already mentioned it: mental vigor is not separate from physical vigor. We are one single thing.

A third example: choosing jobs and careers that not only involve money to survive today, but that generate learning you can use in future positions. The difference between working at something where you simply produce and working at something where besides producing you have the opportunity to learn how things are done, to get involved in how to do them better, to develop curiosity and judgment. That's going to make you better at your job, but you'll also develop along the way skills you weren't directly looking for.

A fourth example would be investing in elements that have a passive economic dimension. Things that let you dedicate time to creating them and then you can reduce that time almost to zero, but they keep paying you returns. When you build one, you build another, you build another, you take a time snapshot and you have several passive income streams that in the long run give you far more capital than you could have had if you'd simply invested linearly.

This concept connects well with another idea I've discussed in some videos: considering actions not only at a first-order level, but at second and third order. What matters isn't only the decision I make today for its direct consequences — what also matters is who it turns me into. Something this simple: today I decide to skip the gym. In an analysis of a year, skipping one day doesn't matter. But what does matter is that today, by skipping the gym, I'm telling myself it's acceptable to skip it. And if I skip it today, I'll skip it more down the line. And I'm telling myself I'm the kind of person who skips the gym. Who is the kind of person who has trouble keeping commitments. And therefore I'm also telling myself I'm a person who fails. And in every future endeavor I take on I'll carry that narrative.

If on the other hand you're very diligent about something as minor as not skipping the gym, you're telling yourself you're the person who doesn't skip it. That you're disciplined. That you're someone people can rely on. And that's very powerful long-term.

Social relationships are also a fantastic example. Investing in connecting with others doesn't just give you happiness short-term. Long-term you have a network to lean on. But at the compound level you're developing social skills, your ability to communicate, your ability to present yourself, your ability to integrate into new environments. And the more people you meet, the easier it is to meet more people, because those people have their own circles and introduce you to them.

So, I think using these metrics when making decisions is very useful: what's the short-term return? What's the long-term return? What are the second and third-order consequences? Is it going to produce something for me at a large scale, at a compound level? It's a fantastic way to analyze which path to take.
