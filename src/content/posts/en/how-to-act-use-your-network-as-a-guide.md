I was at the gym with my partner and we started chatting with a girl who was there. Later my partner told me the girl had said that, not knowing we were a couple, she found it very motivating to see me at the gym. Why? Apparently because I push hard, and that motivated her because she liked being around people who push hard. And this small, innocent comment had me thinking for a couple of days. What effect am I having that I'm not aware of in my environment?

We tend to think of ourselves as isolated individuals. I do my things for myself. But we don't understand that we actually live in a network, surrounded by people and connections, and that information travels through the network in often complex ways. And many times it travels further than you'd ever expect.

I stopped to think: what would happen if we were deeply aware of how our actions affect our network? If you went to the gym and had absolute certainty that by pushing hard today, those three people watching you would train hard too, have a good workout, and go home satisfied. Wouldn't that motivate you to behave better? To push harder? Not for yourself, but for your network.

When I shared this with my partner, she said: sure, but that's you because you do a lot of things and train in a very impressive way, but I don't have a network I can inspire. But that's not entirely true. When someone goes to a library, your behavior influences the behavior of others. And their behavior influences yours, and you know it. That's often the reason you go to a library to study: because when you're surrounded by people studying in a focused way, it's much easier for you to focus too. If one person in the library starts making noise, getting distracted, it's more likely that the people around them also get distracted.

We have an effect even on strangers that we're not aware of. The fact that you're a kind and friendly person makes your neighborhood more pleasant. The fact that you interact properly with someone at the bakery or at the bus stop influences the overall feeling of what it's like to live in that city.

And there are times when you don't feel like acting ideally, the way you know you should behave. Thinking that how you act has an effect on your environment is a good reason to act in a deeply correct way. If several people in your group are trying to lose weight, if one slips up it's much easier for the others to slip up too. I couldn't say: when I'm alone at home, nobody cares how I behave.

And here's where it gets interesting. You are not you as a solid, conscious individual. There is no self from which decisions emerge. As I talked about in the flat mind video, every time you ask yourself who you are, your brain makes it up. It looks at past actions, looks at what it would like to be according to your ideas, and creates a figure of the self. And our self changes mainly based on how we behave. The more recent the behavior, the more influence it has on building that internal narrative.

That's why when people say to me "I'm just not a consistent person," I say: there's no such thing as being consistent in the abstract. You just have to do things. When you do them regularly, then you look back and say: hey, I've been consistent. But it's not that you "are" consistent, it's that you've acted consistently in that situation.

You are the first node in the network. How do you act? The person your actions are going to have the most influence on is not the person next to you. It's you. Every action you take now is going to be a data point your brain will pick up in the future to build a narrative of who you are. Every decision you make, every instant in which you do something, is a signal to your future self: this is the person we are.

And in that sense, when you're alone is when it's most important to behave well because you're building the self you want to be. Not acting according to what I want, not acting according to what I feel, but acting according to what I'd like to be in the future. What is the direction vector of my action? Is it a vector toward the kind of person who says "I'm tired, I'll skip the workout" and skips it? Or is it a vector toward the kind of person who says "I'm tired, but this is what needs to be done"?

When you do it in one area and do it in another, and a difficult situation presents itself where acting correctly is very hard, if you've built it up brick by brick in each of the small opportunities you had before, you'll have built a person who behaves not as they want, but as they should according to their ideals. And there comes a moment when you look back and say: hey, I've done this, and this, and this. Can I say I'm this kind of person? Yes.

You're not isolated. You live in a network and information travels through the network. We have no idea how it travels. And if we could see it, we'd be completely blown away by the influence we can have on our environment. In the end, all the great people there have ever been in human history were ordinary people who decided to have an impact on the world. It's in your hands to decide who you want to be.
