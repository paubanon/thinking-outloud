The other day a friend broke her toe in a stupid race where she decided to slam it into a door. She asked me to drive her to a class the following Saturday. Like a good gentleman, I said yes. But Friday night, when she texted me to confirm, I was about to reply: "Oh, I'd forgotten! But yes, I'll pick you up." And in my second-order thinking I asked myself: why would you tell her you forgot? What's the benefit of showing that flaw? In the end, what matters is that you're going and you're keeping your promise.

This made me ask myself the following question: why do we regularly say things that work against our own interests? Another example: you're late to a meeting. You can send a message saying "guys, I'm running 15 minutes behind, sorry." Or you can say "guys, I overslept, I'm running 15 minutes behind." Why would you self-sabotage by saying you overslept? Nobody cares, it adds nothing, and the only thing it does is chip away at people's perception of you.

And that's what this video is about: why do we self-sabotage when we talk about ourselves?

The first thing that comes to mind is that you are always aware of your own flaws. Always. And that makes you vulnerable to the fallacy that others don't have those same flaws. You're aware of the areas where you're not good enough, and you want to protect yourself from people finding that out.

The second point is protection against failure. If you lower people's expectations of you by talking negatively about yourself in front of others, you're protecting yourself against the possibility that someone in the future might expect too much of you and you won't be able to deliver. It's the equivalent of saying "I could have gotten a 10 if I'd wanted to." You decide to study less so you get an 8, and you always have the excuse. Because what would have happened if you'd studied as hard as possible and hadn't gotten a 10? Then you'd have discovered that maybe you weren't capable. And that's psychologically much harder on your identity. If you speak negatively about yourself on a regular basis, you have a self-protection barrier that prevents you from having to confront the limits of who you are.

There's another related aspect: an inability to sustain the tension of a conversation when necessary. I've caught myself making jokey, ironic, sarcastic comments at moments when I should have kept my mouth shut. It can happen in a work negotiation, where you put an offer on the table and have to maintain the silence until the other person reacts. Or when you're interacting with someone of the opposite sex and a certain romantic tension builds, and instead of holding it you break it out of nervousness. It's similar to self-sabotaging to avoid meeting expectations: if I convey a certain emotional intensity with my words and I sustain it, I'm positioning myself and putting myself in a much more exposed place emotionally. And the way to break that potential vulnerability is by making a jokey comment about it.

The problem, just like with the exam example or a negotiation, is that there are times in life when you have to be serious. There are times in life when you have to put your cards on the table and, as an independent and mature adult, you have to be able to say: this is my opinion and I'm standing by it.

This leads to the second point: the relationship between what we say and our personality. We tend to think that personality is set in stone, that there's something inside that is what it is, and that from that personality things pour outward in the form of behavior. The reality, which cognitive-behavioral therapy and Nick Chatter's flat mind model defend, is that there's no such thing as personality. How you behave and who you are is the same thing. The relationship between these two aspects is bidirectional: you have an idea of who you are and you project it onto the world, but how you interact with the world directly and powerfully modifies who you are. If you behave like the person you want to be, you'll create the person you want to be.

And here we're seeing the opposite: "I don't think I'm capable of maintaining the standards of the person I'd like to be," and therefore I self-sabotage before even trying.

The clearest example of all this is the difference between saying "I could do X" and actually trying. A billion times I've seen something and said: I can do that. And I've had to tell myself: yeah, do it then, prove it. And when it came to actually doing it, far more problems came up than I'd expected. The "I could do that, but I don't want to" is actually, in many cases, an identity defense mechanism. Because accepting that maybe you can't is harder.

So one of the rules I set for myself a while ago — which was the origin of all this thinking — is: I'm never going to say things that go against my own person. I'm not going to voluntarily put myself in a position where I show weaknesses voluntarily and unnecessarily. There are times when you can show vulnerability in order to empathize, to connect with the other person. It doesn't mean faking indefinitely. It means that if it's not necessary, dare to be the kind of person others listen to, others admire. Dare to aim as high as possible. To apply for the job you'd like to have. To approach and try to win over people with the level of attractiveness you'd like to have. And if in some area you notice you're falling short, instead of saying "well, but that's because I don't want to," dare to try.

Being aware of these situations and creating the behavior that the person you'd like to be would have. That, I think, is the way.
