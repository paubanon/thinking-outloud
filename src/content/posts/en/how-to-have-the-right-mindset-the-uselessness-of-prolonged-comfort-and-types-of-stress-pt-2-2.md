This was coming from talking about how people don't have stressors in their lives. And the ones they do have are this kind — constant, without spikes and recoveries.

What we think we like — being comfortable — isn't reality. A human being wrapped in cotton is not happy. Even if you think they are, they're not. Even if you think you'd be happy if that were you, no, it's not true. Because you don't feel fulfilled. You haven't achieved things in life. You feel useless, you feel weak, you feel vulnerable.

When you're happy, when you feel full, is when you have a lot of things to do and you're doing them. You feel powerful, you feel like you're doing something of value with your life. That is being happy. That's when you feel a spiritual fullness.

You have to chew on that to change our way of life. And not think that stress is bad. No, stress is not bad. Stress, whatever kind it is, always makes you grow if you're willing to grow. If you have the mindset and the organization to say, okay, this is going to help me.

For example, when I came to Germany, the first week I didn't want to come. It seemed enormous to me, incredibly difficult, unmanageable. But objectively, I knew it was good for me, for my future. And that it was going to be a positive experience in countless ways. So even though emotionally I didn't want to, objectively I knew I had to do it. And that it would benefit me. Yes, I'm going to grow, I'm going to learn, and so on. But in that moment it sucks. In that moment it really sucks.

This leads to another video: knowing when you have to listen to your emotional side and when you have to ignore it and say, I know I have to do this objectively, even if I don't want to, because I'm going to grow. And that's what happened to me. I came here and, even though I knew I was going to grow, that I was going to have a great time, I arrived here and got depressed. It hit me hard. From being far from home, my friends, with bad weather, a different language, a master's degree on top of it all. It seemed gigantic.

Not anymore, now I'm very happy. But what I want to say is two things. One, that even when we objectively know something is going to do us good, stress is never comfortable. You're never going to want to work out, you're never going to want to fast, you're never going to want to get in cold water, you're never going to want to move to another country. Never. So these are things you have to approach objectively. You have to say, I'm going to do this because I know it's going to help me.

And two, that sometimes you have to completely silence the emotional part and tackle something solely because you objectively know you have to do it.
