Today I woke up with this idea in mind that I've been turning over for a long time and that has been sliding silently into my life: the idea of compassion. It's a hippie new-age word that gives me a bit of an allergic reaction by the way it's used in pseudoscientific and pseudomeditational circles, as a solution to all problems. But it describes a position that has been settling into my way of seeing the world and that I'd like to share here.

How would I define compassion? For me it would be a well-intentioned way of interpreting other people's actions: trying to understand that most people don't act out of malice, but out of ignorance, or trying to do their best and screwing up. Adults are often not very different from a child. We're just as stupid, just as emotional, just as irrational. And when a child has a tantrum, you don't get angry with him because he has no control over himself. He's a child. Most people understand that when it's a child.

Compassion is repositioning that lens. Seeing adults as somewhat volatile beings, not fully responsible for their actions, trying to do their best in life. When they don't know, it's out of ignorance, incapacity, or simply error. We all do things wrong sometimes.

In meditation circles this idea emerges naturally, first toward oneself. You can be meditating and your brain wanders off to a thousand other things. And with compassion you say to yourself: it's okay, brain, we're tired, come back to breathing. You don't get angry, you don't punish yourself for having gotten distracted, instead you guide yourself back by the hand, the way you'd do with a child doing something it shouldn't.

This concept has been circling me for years, but it started to take hold more strongly through reading *Behave*, by Robert Sapolsky, which I'm rereading now — and by now I mean I started it this morning. Sapolsky shows that we are more determined by our environment, at multiple scales, than we'd like to believe. When we act, well or badly, we are being consistent with what our neurons, our hormones, our genes, our culture dictate. Therefore concepts like responsibility, both positive and negative, narrow significantly when you take all of that into account. When you look at the world through that lens, you can't help but arrive at a compassionate perspective: this person who did this atrocious act probably would have liked to have been able to act differently, but in that moment they couldn't. What function does my getting angry serve then?

This is enough for a book. Not one book, two: *Behave* and *Determined*, which is the follow-up with the philosophical consequences.

This idea kept sliding into my life with ever greater intensity, but it was recently that it made a quantitative leap in a short time. It was because of a psychedelic experience that I could talk about at length. Basically I was overwhelmed by... how to put it? The beauty and ineffability of each moment. Each moment was absolutely perfect in every imaginable sense. My internal condition was obviously altered by the drug, and whenever the context changed I had a small moment of melancholy because that perfect moment was fading. But then the next moment was equally perfect. And the next. And the next again. After an eternity of perfect moments one after another, each with its corresponding instant of melancholy, I arrived at the conclusion that maybe every moment is perfect. Always. If you choose to see it through the right lens. And that it's in your hands to understand that this is so, the same way it's in your hands to see that someone doesn't want to behave the way they do, but does because they have no other option. That the only rational response, really, is to be compassionate. When something goes wrong, when someone hurts you, when something goes off the rails, there's nothing left to do but say: well, but everything is fine. One way or another it'll be okay, because it always is if you know how to see the world through that lens.

Since then I've found myself with much less resentment, much less bitterness, getting angry much less when someone makes a decision I think is wrong. I can give them my opinion and tell them I'd do it differently, but it's their decision and that's fine. If you decide one thing, it'll work out; if you decide another, maybe in the short term it goes worse, but if you know how to build and keep moving forward it'll work out anyway. It's not that relevant. Even very significant decisions: they can take you to a different life than the one you wanted, but you're going to have a life, and if you know how to focus it and build it it'll be a good life anyway.

This connects with something else that's been resonating strongly in my head: that we're not in this world to take it too seriously. Let the record show: I like doing things very seriously, training hard, working a lot, taking care of my relationships. Someone watching from the outside might think I'm a hard worker. But under closer inspection what you see is that I do it all for pleasure, like it's a playground. We're here to enjoy this life, not in the banal sense of instant reward, but in the deeper sense: doing what fulfills you, what builds you. Within real limitations — earning a living, building a future, investing in the person you want to be — there's a way to see everything through the lens that you have to enjoy the process, not the destination. Even if something is hard or boring, if someone asked you whether you'd do something else, you'd say no. The thing is, it's difficult, it requires effort. But you want to do it.

And when you catch yourself distracted, wasting time on whatever nonsense — I'm not going to say YouTube but I'm saying it — in that framework you look at it with compassion. Pau, you spent an hour wasting time. It's okay, tomorrow better. And if you see it happening very frequently, you tell yourself: this isn't making us happy, let's change it. Same with other people: don't get angry with them, guide them by the hand. Explain why something wasn't right. Reward the positive. And instead of punishing or arguing, if possible put a structure in place so that behavior doesn't happen again. Like with a child and an electrical outlet: you don't scold them to infinity. You put an outlet cover on and you don't have to scold them anymore. Same with yourself.

All of this aligns closely with the book *Behave* and the cognitive-behavioral psychology I've talked about other times on the channel. The life you live is determined by how you see the world. The same situation lived by two people has two completely different interpretations. Someone has been in the same job for twenty years and feels miserable because they're not advancing; another person comes from months of unemployment and that same position gives them enormous security. Where do we come from? That determines the state we're in. And in large part that means the narrative we create about our events.

Compassion, both toward yourself and others, has a great place in that narrative. I'm not talking about being hippies or going to hug each other in fields. Something much simpler and more everyday: when you find yourself doing something you wouldn't have liked to have done, take your own hand and say, we should have done better. And next time you try to do better. And if you can, put barriers in place so it doesn't happen again. The world would be a much friendlier place if we could all see ourselves with a little more compassion.
