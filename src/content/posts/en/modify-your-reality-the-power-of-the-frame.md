Good morning. Today I wanted to tackle a very interesting and very practically useful topic: the importance of the frame and the subjectivity of reality, and how we can use this to change the position we occupy in society.

The first thing is to understand what motivates this. We start from a place where we want to change something about ourselves. We're not okay with the image we believe society has of us in some area. Maybe you were shy as a kid and now everyone thinks you're shy. They treat you like a shy person, you perceive that they see you that way, so you behave shyly. A very hard cycle to break. Or you want to change the idea that you're not the type of person who starts businesses, or who connects with people they're attracted to. This is also often called social pressure, but if we call it a frame, it opens up a wider range of possibilities.

It's very common in the city where you were born, especially with your established friend groups, the people you grew up with. And it tends to be especially harsh in adolescence, which is a period of change where you feel like you want to be different but you feel trapped by the preconceived idea they have of you.

This happened to me when I left to study in Valencia. I noticed I wanted to be a different person from how they perceived me — back then I was shyer, more timid, let's say it that way. And I couldn't wait to move to a new city and start from scratch. This is not the person I want to be. I want to be different. Moving cities is a way to reshape your environment, so that nobody has any preconceived idea of you. And you can project who you want to be and build yourself the way you want.

The first thing is to understand that within society there's a hierarchy. A very deep and very ancient part of your brain places you in that hierarchy and places others in it too. And it makes you behave accordingly. You know, in a way, who is above you, who is below you, who is at your level. And this isn't unique to humans: there are hierarchies in lobsters that have survived 350 million years, far longer than the dinosaurs. It's deeply rooted in nature. It's not the fruit of the patriarchy. It's simply a very complex map in which we position people. It's not just up and down, it has more dimensions. And depending on the position someone occupies, they're treated one way or another. You don't treat your father the same as a friend, or the president, or a cashier.

People can move in the hierarchy as they get to know each other, but it's hard to do it with yourself. Hard to say: I'm not in the position I want. How do we do it?

A tool used a lot in entrepreneurship and also in seduction is: act as if you are until you are. As stupid or fake as it might seem, it depends on what you define as reality. If I tell you "I've sailed around the world in a hot air balloon," I'm lying to you. But if I tell you "I'm the best," I'm projecting a reality. There's no tangible reality to compare it to. But it's real because we all act as if it's real. Within all of us we know the relative positions in the hierarchy. And if there's an underlying reality which is the position you occupy in this social structure, that position can be modified by how you behave. By how you behave, others perceive you. By how they perceive you, they act. By how they act, you perceive that they treat you with a certain status. And that creates feedback.

This is hard to change, especially at first. And if it happens too abruptly, it creates dissonance for the people around you: "What happened here? You're not like this." But it can be a very important tool.

"Be yourself" — that's bullshit. Don't be yourself. Be who you want to become. Act and take responsibility to become the person you want to be, to have the job you want, the health you want. If you're not happy with your current self, you change it. Don't be a conformist.

If you act as if you are, others will perceive you that way, act that way, and you'll perceive that others see you that way. If everyone agrees that they perceive you a certain way, even you perceive yourself that way — what's the difference between being it or not? Especially when it's something as ambiguous as "I'm the best" or "I'm very high on the scale."

If we all agree there's a pink elephant in the room, we all believe it's there. What's the difference between it being there and not being there? If we all believe a certain person occupies a position on the social scale, that person occupies that position. Because what builds that social scale is all of us. Reality isn't only tangible. There's a subjective reality that is just as real, because it's what you perceive. And it's much more deeply rooted than it seems.

This applies not only to how others perceive you — seeing you as shy, as an entrepreneur, as attractive, confident, extroverted, adventurous — but also to how you perceive yourself. You act the way the person you want to be would act, and your brain says: "Huh, if I'm doing this, I must be like this."

Do you want to be productive but you know you tend to procrastinate? You get up, wash your face, the first thing you do is get dressed and make the bed. Your brain says: "Wow, we're being productive. At a very basic level, the level of daily tasks, but we're achieving goals. Seems like we're a productive person. Let's keep going." And it becomes easier to sit down and study. Start with very simple behaviors: keeping your room tidy, making the bed every day, being consistent with exercise and with what time you get up. And once you start pulling that thread, you realize you've changed. You used to see yourself as someone who procrastinates, who struggles to sit down; suddenly you're behaving like someone who follows through on what they set out to do. Your brain tells you: "Productive." And a positive loop begins.

The most iconic example is Muhammad Ali. Long before winning the title, he was saying: "I am the greatest, and I know it and you know it." Conor McGregor is the same. A phrase I really like is: "If I say I'm the best, you'll say I'm bragging. But if I say I'm not the best, you know I'm lying." These are people who have consciously projected the idea of "I am the best. There is no other option. I know it, you know it, it's a fact." And they projected it with such force that they believe it themselves. And when they believe it, and the rest of the world believes it too, everything becomes more conducive to circumstances aligning so that reality solidifies into something tangible, like being world champion.

The concept of "the best" is very abstract, and therefore can be modified. It can seep through the cracks of your mind until the idea that you are forms itself. And once everyone thinks you're the best, and even you agree — what's the difference between that and actually being it?
