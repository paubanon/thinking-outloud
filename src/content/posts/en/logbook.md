Today I want to share with you the most useful tool I've found for staying motivated, connected and with good mental health throughout the PhD. And it's this small red notebook. Or rather, what do I do with this small red notebook?

I call it a navigator's log. A navigator's log because what you're going to use it for is the same thing the log was used for on ships: a daily record of what has happened, where you are, where you're going, any problem you've run into. That small daily task, as trivial as it sounds, I've found to be tremendously powerful for staying connected.

The reason is that our brain doesn't store or process information rationally. We often need clear, objective data, where we cross-reference the good things, the things achieved and the ones not achieved, to be able to see where we are and where we're going objectively. This is one of the basic principles of cognitive-behavioral therapy. When someone enters a depressive or negative mode, one of the psychological tools is to look at your problems objectively. Because many times these problems aren't real, but dramatizations of the events that happen.

For example, a depressed person might think: I have no friends, everything is wrong, I'm alone in the world. A cognitive-behavioral approach might say: is it true that you have no friends? Do you really have no one to talk to? Let's make a list of your closest people. And maybe you start saying: well, actually I have this person and that one. Actually I get along really well with my cousin, and my family is there too. And what you perceived as "I have no friends" you have to reframe as: at this moment in my life, in this new city, in this situation, I find myself alone because I don't have as many personal connections as I'd like. And that completely changes the frame. One is a categorical, defeatist approach; the other is bounded, situational and something you can work with.

When you tackle a very large project, like a doctoral thesis, this happens very frequently. The frustration of "I'm stuck, this isn't moving, I'm a disaster, in the last month I haven't managed to do anything." These are dramatizations of the situation, and it's important to put them in the right box.

And that's what the record is for. What you do is arrive every day, before starting to work, and write how you're feeling today, the goals you want to meet and where you stand. I wrote it a bit in that navigator's log tone. "Today, July 31st, 2024, I find myself in this situation. In the last two weeks I've felt very burned out and low on motivation, I think it's a combination of stress from this and this. However, despite these situations, they've been good weeks." And then at the end, you come back and mark what you did, what you couldn't do and why.

This is key because when you advance in a large, complex project, the natural state of things is being stuck. When you're stuck, you're going to spend much more time solving the problem than when you know how to do things. Imagine you have 10 tasks: you do task 1 in half an hour, 2 in half an hour, 3 in an hour, and on task 4 you spend 4 days because you're stuck. If you measure your feelings based on how long each task takes, the feeling is always going to be that you're stuck. "This week I spent the whole time fighting with this and I haven't done anything else." However, when you record daily what you have done, you're positively reinforcing yourself.

And when you're feeling a bit low, at the end of the week, when the feeling is "I'm stuck, this isn't working," you can always go back to your logbook and check. This happened to me after Christmas. Around March I said: I haven't done anything. These last two months I've barely advanced. Is that true? Is it really true you haven't done anything? Look at the record. January 1st you did this, this and this. Monthly summary: you rewrote this section, you submitted this article, you prepared this presentation. And maybe you realize you were measuring your progress by a single metric out of the myriad that make up the project.

This is similar to keeping a training log. When you're stuck on an exercise, it's very hard to identify whether you really are if you don't have metrics, because our brain holds onto information from the last two or three sessions. Maybe the last two have gone down. But if you look at the trend over the last month, maybe it's positive and the stagnation is local and you don't need to change anything.

Keeping a logbook is essential. For me it's been an absolutely revolutionary tool for my mental wellbeing and motivation. If you have a big project on your hands, one with many aspects, I can't give you a better recommendation than to break it down into pieces as small and precise as possible, determine every day what you're aiming for that day, and mark with date and notes.

Everyone finds their own way with this. It can be done on the computer, in Excel, in task apps. Personally I love the feeling of writing on paper. It centers me, puts me in a low-arousal but focused state, and the feel of paper gives me better results than any task application. What matters is that it's quantifiable, that it's complete and that you can go back to it in the future to check whether you really are stuck, whether you should be frustrated, or whether you simply weren't looking with enough detail.
