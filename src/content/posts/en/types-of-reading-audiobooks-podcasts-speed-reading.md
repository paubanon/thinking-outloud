Good morning. Today I wanted to talk about something I've been asked: my opinion on ebooks, audiobooks, podcasts, and similar things. I've been thinking about it, because I had some gut-level resistance to audiobooks and didn't really understand why. When I have a visceral rejection or direct acceptance of something and don't know the reason, I stop and think about it. That's what happened with audiobooks. Podcasts, on the other hand, I quite like. I don't follow any faithfully, but I listen to individual episodes here and there with interviews with people I find interesting.

The first thing I want to say is that there are different types of reading. Being aware of this lets you adapt your way of approaching a text depending on what the text is. It's not all just "I'm going to read": depending on what it is, you have to read it one way or another, with more or less concentration, with one kind of syntax or another.

This first hit me reading Dickens. Dickens writes, like me, very long sentences. Many chained subordinate clauses. It's a more complex reading than we're used to today, where sentences are getting shorter and shorter, maybe influenced by social media and instant gratification. Dickens leaves the subject way back, four lines up. There's a moment where you think: what am I even reading? I was reading A Christmas Carol and found it hard to follow without knowing why. After several chapters I stopped and said: let me try not to read looking for what it's telling me, but instead look at the structure of the text, to see why I'm finding it so hard. And I realized you had to read it marking the pauses very clearly and keeping in your head who the subject was and what the verb was. Once that clicked, the rest of the reading was easy-peasy.

So we can distinguish types of reading. Simple, leisurely reading, like a novel without much complexity: you sit down and read it, without worrying about anything else. But there are other books or texts, generally the ones that teach you something, that have a lot of content and tend to stretch or shift the ideas in your head. They make you grow as a person, make you question things. A phrase I really liked: a mind stretched by a new idea never returns to its original size. That's what these books do. They require a detailed, slower reading. What does this sentence mean? What idea does this paragraph contain? And distilling it little by little. This reading requires attention, silence, a coffee, and rereading. If you're not clear on what a paragraph said, if you read over it, read it again. And again. And you read it meticulously until you understand what it means. That's what happens to me with philosophy books or when I have to read something from my degree. Twelve Rules for Life by Jordan Peterson, for example, is like that. And then there's also poetry, which goes in the opposite direction: you don't focus so much on the form but on the emotions it evokes, an emotional stimulus more than an intellectual one.

Why don't audiobooks fully work for me? A written text has a very different structure from a conventional dialogue or a spoken reflection. Writing requires a lot of ruminating on ideas, distilling them, laying them out in an orderly way, with the possibility of restructuring arguments, rewriting, deleting, reordering — none of which is possible in a conversation. That's why a written text is generally more complex. When you're reading it, you might need to reread certain parts, pause, and if it's being read to you in audio you don't quite grasp it well because the ideas are more condensed and the structure isn't the same.

Nicolas Carr talked about this in The Shallows: how the medium modifies our way of thinking and the structure of text. He applied it to how the Internet reshapes our minds, but before that it applied to how writing itself, depending on the method, even modified the writer's prose. Nietzsche, when he switched to writing with a typewriter, wrote very differently than when he wrote by hand. The medium matters. And with audiobooks it's the same: you can't take a written text, read it aloud, and expect the same comprehension.

A podcast, however, is not a written text converted to audio format. It's a genuine conversation. And the brain is prepared to pick up ideas in a conversation, with its small talk, its pauses, its repetitions, its multiple angles. But a book doesn't contain any of that, because it was made to be written and to be read. Reading and listening are different forms of communication.

One final point: speed reading. I think it's an interesting skill for certain uses, especially academic ones. If you're looking for very specific information, being able to pick up a paper and extract the basic idea quickly is useful. But I'd never make it my preferred method out of some desire to be more productive or to read more books. A reading that requires concentration — philosophy, for example — it's impossible to get the same benefits reading it carefully as skimming through it. The most brilliant people in human history have spent enormous amounts of time writing that book, condensing their ideas, their reflections, their life experiences into it. It's condensed in the clearest sense of the word, and written in a specific way on purpose. You can't skip that and expect to get the same thing out.

It's not just about having read the book and saying: done, another box checked. It's about picking it up, pulling it apart, looking at its threads, seeing how they fit with your own existing ideas, whether you've changed your opinion, where the author's arguments fall short from your perspective and where you agree. That takes time. It's not about how many books you read. It's about which books you read and how you read them.
