Do the things that scare you as soon as possible so you don't have to spend your life fearing them.

This is a line from a fictional character in Joe Abercrombie that has stuck with me. It's stuck with me because I think it's a transcendental truth, let's call it that. The book is called The First Law. And it's ironic because it turns out to be a reformulation of Peterson's rule number one: Stand up straight with your shoulders back. A command to face life directly, without evasions. To take what scares you but what you know you must do, and face it directly, exposing all your vulnerabilities. Knowing that you'll come out hurt but believing that, despite the damage you might suffer, you'll be able to withstand the blow.

And this is nothing more than a restructuring of the hero's journey. The hero is the one who faces what scares him. The hero's path is the one who decides to go to the dragon's cave and kill it voluntarily, without waiting for the dragon to come for him. He approaches the dragon when he's small and takes care of it. Because the dragon is what guards the greatest riches, as a metaphor for what you want in life: your goals. And the dragon is everything that scares you.

This line from Abercrombie has resonated with me a lot. And I've been thinking about it because I've been putting it into practice over the last few months. And it has made me mature in leaps and bounds, as I mentioned in the previous video. It has made me think and rethink about who I am, how I am, what I want. And how I approach things and my personal relationships. And it was something that scared me: taking someone I needed to talk to and sitting down and saying: let's talk. And I've decided it's better not to be afraid of that. Well, to be afraid of it, but to act anyway.

And in this sense, that's why I think — as I've talked about in some of my earlier videos — people are afraid to be in silence and solitude. Because they have to confront their greatest fears. They have to kill their dragons. And they have to face them alone.

When it comes to psychologically managing problems we might have — a tortured relationship with someone, something we're not satisfied with at work, especially a breakup, which I think is the example we'll all understand — we tend to avoid dealing with it. We tend to try to fill our time completely with things. With noise, with people, with plans, with doing things to avoid thinking about that breakup. And I think that's a defense mechanism to avoid going to the cave and killing the dragon.

I think sometimes the dragon is too big to deal with all at once. But you have to make some attempts. You have to make forays against the dragon and see when you can finally overcome it. And for that I think you need: time in silence with yourself. And I think meditation is a great, great door into that.

There are times when you don't have to avoid a feeling. You have to sit with it and work through it. Take a thought, something that torments you, and sit for half an hour in silence with yourself. And work through it. The word isn't solve it. You don't want to find a solution that fixes the problem. The idea in this sense is to process it. For your mind and your spirit to accommodate themselves to the idea in question, to all its nuances, to all its spectrum, to eliminate the possible shadows and fears that might arise. To learn that life goes on, whatever it is.

And you find a surprising peace once you've done it.

It's the only step: I know this is going to be hard and I'm exposing myself to this danger — emotional danger in this case — voluntarily. Knowing it's going to be painful. Because I know I'm strong enough to withstand contact with my own emotions, my own thoughts.

It's something I learned recently and I wanted to share. The fact that sometimes, when you need to process something — an emotion, an anxiety, a thought, whether it's work-related, university-related, and especially emotions, deep emotions you feel inside you and that sometimes emerge... some people start crying when they emerge, some people get depressed, some people get angry. And you don't entirely know why, even though deep down you do. In those moments sit down and meditate. Work through it. And see what happens. You'll definitely learn something.

This is the first law.
