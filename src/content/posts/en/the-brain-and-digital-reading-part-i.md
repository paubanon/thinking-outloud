Good morning everyone. Today I wanted to make a video on a topic I've been wanting to record for a long time and that some of you have specifically asked me about, because I've mentioned it in other videos. The topic is digital reading, the ability to concentrate, and how to make working hours more productive or how to retain more information when reading — especially digitally.

What sparked my interest in this topic again was an article in the journal Investigación y Ciencia titled "La lectura digital en desventaja" — Digital Reading at a Disadvantage. I'll put it in the description. It's a meta-analysis of what we know when comparing digital and analog reading. That is, reading on paper versus reading on a screen. The results are that analog reading is much better for retaining information and assimilating concepts than digital.

Why is that? The authors of the study repeatedly refer to the shallowness hypothesis developed by Nicolas Carr in his book The Shallows: What the Internet Is Doing to Our Brains. Which is a book I definitely recommend reading. Because all of us use the internet and technology in our daily lives, and as we know, I think they're wonderful — without them I wouldn't be talking to you right now — but they come at a price. They modify the way we think and feel. And from my perspective, it's necessary to be educated about how they affect us, how to manage them, and when we want to use them and take advantage of their benefits, and when we don't.

The shallowness hypothesis is based on the premise that the brain is plastic — meaning it adapts to whatever it's dealing with. And specifically it talks about the importance of the medium through which we receive information. In fact, at a certain point the phrase is used: the medium is the message. Meaning the medium through which we get information is so important that it completely modifies how we absorb that information and our way of thinking.

This is illustrated very interestingly with the introduction of the typewriter into Nietzsche's writing. When Nietzsche wrote by hand he had a more elaborate, more careful, more measured prose. Whereas when he switched to the typewriter, the fact that it lets you write faster and the tactile sensations of typing produced another kind of sentence structure. A more cutting text, with shorter sentences, more to the point.

Following this line of the brain being plastic and adapting to the medium, the conclusion is that the internet is making us shallow. Because the medium we're constantly navigating is devoid of depth. It's short headlines, fast information, and constant jumping from one thing to another. This makes us adapt to that type of information and become incapable of handling long, complex texts that require reading and understanding over a sustained period. And not only texts — it prevents us from thinking in that slow, deep, continuous way, connecting different elements, weaving them together, building an interconnected network of conceptual ideas. Which is precisely where I think the value of the human mind lies and where we find a great deal of satisfaction.

When we pick up a text in digital format, we're constantly exposed to a storm of decisions, an enormous quantity of options that our brain has to decide whether to act on or not. You're constantly bombarded by ads that interrupt the text, by a top menu, a side menu, hyperlinks, images, videos. And at each one of those moments your brain has to decide whether to stop reading, click on that hyperlink, whether to open that new hyperlink now or read it later, whether to read it all the way through and then come back to the text. And the same with a video embedded in an article. It might seem very modern, very multimedia: look how I'm supplementing this information with that other content. But it's terrible. Because your brain is following an argumentative thread and suddenly there's a video with its own argument, its own narrative. If we think our brain is capable of retaining the structure of the text and then sub-processing the video and then incorporating it, we're wrong.

On top of that, when we work on the computer itself or on the phone — worse still — these are devices we use for many other tasks. Even if no notification pops up, we have the urge: I use this device for these other things, I should stop reading and open Instagram, I should stop reading and check my email, I should get back to work. All of that has to be sub-processed by the brain unconsciously. And as a result, we're unable to fully focus on reading.

This is the shallowness hypothesis. And what the article shows is that, indeed, digital reading is inferior in every case to reading on paper. The only case where digital reading is more or less comparable is with narrative texts. In principle because a narrative text doesn't have as many hyperlinks, ads, or menus. And also because it's a text with lower information density — it's simpler to process. Personally I use the e-book for narrative texts. I would never consider reading a paper on an e-book. And a complex book like The Blank Slate is pointless, from my perspective, to read in digital format because the amount of information you absorb is vastly reduced.

Another hypothesis that was being considered was that this is because we're not used to it, that eventually our brain will become plastic and evolve and be just as efficient managing all these digital resources. The article looks at that too. And the answer is no. Digital natives not only continue to score worse on digital reading than on analog — they actually score worse than non-digital natives. This suggests that you get used to shallow content, you get used to fast information. And the more fast information you consume, the less attention span you have, the less capable you are of concentrating on reading, and the less capable you are of building complex and deep conceptual maps — which is what's required for serious and important problems.

Apart from the problems of digital content with hyperlinks and distraction, there's another element that influences why a physical book is better for retaining information. And that's that our brain uses the sensory experience of paper to retain information. The richer an experience is, the more it's remembered. If you accompany a meal with good music, a good conversation with someone you find interesting, and take time to appreciate the smells, you'll remember it much more than if you eat the same meal quickly while thinking about something else in the middle of the street.

When we read on paper, reading doesn't become just absorbing information. The book smells. All of us who are addicted to reading have walked into a bookstore and said: smell the books, smell the old books. Touching the book, the texture of the paper, the sound — it's pleasurable. And that's a richer experience.

On top of that, our brain structures information spatially. It structures it in two ways: in the flat dimension of the pages, and in the third dimension which is the depth of the book. It helps your brain to structure information by knowing how far along you are in the book and what point on the page you're at. We've all had that moment of saying: this thing was in a book, I don't remember where, but I think it was around here, and I know it was in this corner. And you've opened to roughly where you thought, just looked at that corner and said: there it is. That's not a coincidence. It's that the physical structure of the book influences how you retain information.

The fact that a digital document is an infinite scroll prevents your brain from creating a map of: this is here, this is back here, this is connected to this, with this here and this below, these two ideas come together to generate this other thing. A physical book allows that.

As I've mentioned before, our brain stores information in a conceptual map, an enormous interconnected network. The fact that our brain stores information this way doesn't mean giving us information that way is the best way to receive it. Quite the opposite. The best way to receive information is linearly. Our brain has a network of information, but the best way to learn isn't to jump from one node of the network to another — this is connected to this and this to that, a bit from here and a bit from there. I think everyone who has been to university has had a professor like that. And it's terrible.

The best way to take in information is to start at the beginning of a logical thread and follow it to the end. Then take another logical thread and follow it to the end. And once you've finished with those two threads, you say: look how this is connected to that. Now from those connections we'll do this and you pull out another logical thread. You develop it the same way: linear, sequential, in a logical order. That's what a book does, among other things. Because in a book you can't jump from here to there. A book has a linear structure. And that's why we absorb information so well.

The internet, however, doesn't.

The article also talks about the influence of technology in the classroom. This digital revolution that was supposedly going to allow us to teach more, faster, and more efficiently. I've always been against introducing new technology ubiquitously in classrooms because I think it's a mistake. All it does is scatter attention. And I personally have noticed a huge difference between subjects that used a blackboard to explain things and subjects that used a PowerPoint.

The blackboard is obviously much slower, but it lets you follow an explanation step by step. One thing after the other. Why this idea comes from that one, why we do this this way. While you write, you're thinking about why you're writing that. It forces you to take notes. And taking notes itself forces you to write and to ask: okay, why are we doing this? And then you draw an arrow. And that helps. Giving more information doesn't mean you'll assimilate it better. Better information, well-delivered, beats more information.

Subjects delivered through PowerPoint were extremely fast. They didn't let you take notes except for quick scribbles. And you absorbed them pretty poorly. Until you got home, picked up the book, and read it. In that case the class stopped being a class and became a quick overview of what you were going to study at home.

The article shows how the hypothesis that the ubiquitous presence of information technology in classrooms was going to allow students to learn in a more active and interactive way doesn't hold. In fact, it's quite the opposite. They get in the way. Full stop. That doesn't mean technology shouldn't be introduced at some point. It can be very useful to pause the class, use a projector to show a graph or an image, play a short video and continue the explanation. But that doesn't mean everything has to be through a computer.
