Today, for a change, I'm going to do the video sitting down because I've been doing maintenance work in the garden all morning and I'm very tired. I wanted to share a few stoic reflections on what manual work is.

Here, in this house where I'm living now — it's a university house — you can go a certain amount of time without doing any kind of community work, but at a certain point, if you want to keep renting the room, you have to contribute to maintaining the facilities. That means asking the caretaker to give you work: fixing things around the house, cleaning the basements, clearing the garden of rubbish, picking up leaves, trimming bushes, whatever. You can also be in charge of the laundry room, be a bike assistant, things like that. I've always preferred to do garden work, which is considered the worst option. People usually fight to be in charge of the laundry room, because in the end you don't really do anything. Every once in a while you go, mop the floor a little, and you go home. But the hours in the garden are harder. Yet I've always preferred physical work.

The first thing I've noticed is that I like it, and I like it for several reasons. The first is that it's about taking care of my garden. The garden is shared, it's very large. I use it a lot: I spend a lot of time outside studying, reading, sleeping, training, or watching the clouds. I like the garden, I appreciate it, and I like it to be nice and clean. When I see someone leaving rubbish, it bothers me, because it's part of my home. Having the chance to put in my own effort to make it better is quite fulfilling.

On the other hand, physical, manual work is like a gulp of fresh water after all the intellectual and computer work I do. All the time I spend at a computer, all the time I spend studying. It's exactly the opposite, like a breath of fresh air. And it's a reminder. Today, for example, it was five hours, and other times it's been four or six hours of physical work. Pulling weeds, gathering wood, trimming hedges, picking up rubbish, cleaning the basements. These are jobs that could be considered unpleasant. But having the chance to do them every once in a while reminds me of how lucky I am.

Every time I say "ugh, I really don't feel like sitting down to edit videos," or "what a drag, physics is hard, I barely like writing, I'm sick of studying equations" — every time I do this kind of manual work, I think: how lucky I am. How lucky I am to be able to do what I do, to be able to do physics, to have Enso Movers. Because there are a lot of people who do what I did today for five hours, and they do it for eight. And not three days every six months, but every single day. And they don't do it to take care of their own garden — they do it to take care of someone else's garden. It's very tiring and very hard. It gives you perspective.

Spending a morning doing this reminds you: man, you're really lucky. This is hard and there are people out there giving everything they have. You're lucky to be able to work at what you like. Your work might be intellectually challenging, a bit frustrating, whatever, but you're at home in the warmth with a computer. When I've been outside pulling up shrubs in 35-degree heat, it's not pleasant. And when it rains or it's cold and windy like today, it's not either. At least I have the option to choose which days I go out. If it's raining or snowing, I can say "I don't have to do the work right now." But some people can't choose. There are people who work laying asphalt on highways. There are people who harvest the oranges you buy at the supermarket, or the persimmons, or the lemons. Have you seen the spines on a lemon tree? Imagine ending every single day with cuts like that.

Apart from that, because that's just who I am, I went out wearing only a sweatshirt and then realized it was 10 degrees and quite cold. And instead of going back inside to get more layers, I said to myself: I'll get used to it. And sure enough, I did.

It's a reflection on that discomfort you can deliberately introduce into your day-to-day life, and that gives you a lot of perspective on what it means to push yourself, on what it means to work, on what you have. I think everyone should put themselves in this position for a bit. Now, you have to do these things and then warm up your head to give them a philosophical meaning. But I think physical effort — training very hard, very cold showers, manual labor, pushing yourself physically — blows away in one gust all the nonsense we've accumulated in the twenty-first century.
