Taking advantage of the sunshine I've come out here to the garden in front of my building to make this video. I wanted to talk about the importance of formulating questions before getting answers.

How many times has it happened that you're reading through the notes for a class, you have a doubt and it gets stuck in your head and you turn it over and over? Why this derivation? Where does this come from? And in the end you either find the answer after turning it over long enough, or you work it out yourself, or you finally ask the teacher and they answer it. And then you never forget it again. It's fixed in your mind.

Thinking about this, and thinking about Show Movers and how to give information to students in the best possible way, I arrived at the conclusion that for our brain to retain information, assimilate it correctly, understand it, know the importance of that information, it has to have already formulated the question. There has to have been a process of reflection and questioning beforehand before getting the answer.

Because if you arrive — I'll use the Show Movers example — looking for information about training, and I open the firehose and spend five hours blasting at you: training organization, mobility, acrobatics, parkour, fasting, ketosis, carbohydrates, micronutrients, magnesium, sleep, all of it point-blank... you're not going to value it. It won't stick. On the other hand, when you start with us and we talk a little, we crack open certain doors and leave them slightly ajar, so you can peek through. And if you like what you see, maybe you go looking for what's behind that door. You get curious. You question it. "Hey, how does this fasting thing work? Why the protein supplements? Why carbohydrates at night?" And when you've spent a few days turning that over and we finally answer it, you say: "Of course! It all makes sense." Then you truly appreciate it.

This applies to any scenario. In university, I'd study things I'd seen in class and say: "Right, okay." But they didn't stick. However, when you sit down and study it yourself, put in the hours, start asking yourself: "Where does this formula come from?" And it turns out it comes from here, putting this together with that, making these approximations... And the formula makes sense. It stops being an empty expression and becomes something with logic, something that came from a need to solve a question. And because your brain knows you had that question, the answer sticks. It says: "We've been worried about this, let's store it."

The same happens in history. If they just tell you history it goes in one ear and out the other. History sticks when you have genuine curiosity about something. When you're in a castle and you suddenly say: "What happened here? Who ruled this? Why did they build it this way?" And suddenly: "Of course, from here you can see the whole valley, they oriented it so that troops could only pass through there." And it sticks.

Answers stick when there's been genuine interest beforehand, when there's been questioning from existing knowledge, or when you're aware that you don't know something. As I said in another video: there's what we know, what we know we don't know, and what we don't know we don't know. That third category you've never thought about, it doesn't worry you, it doesn't exist for you. But there are areas where you know you're missing something, you turn it over, and when you get an answer you say: "That was it! Of course!" And it takes hold with real force.

The problem with education is that this isn't encouraged. If you're not taught to question things, if no one asks you: "Have you ever thought that what makes things fall here on Earth is the same mechanism by which the Moon orbits the Earth, and the same one by which the Earth orbits the Sun? Pretty interesting, right?" Then the student starts turning that over, interest is sparked, and they go wanting to know the answer. But if you open the firehose on the student, they don't care.

And above all, you're not stimulating the student to ask questions. And that's a big problem. Because in the future, if you don't question things, you don't advance. In any area. Both personally and professionally. Someone who questions everything is very valuable. In research, someone who questions everything can say: "Hey, what we've been doing for twenty years is wrong. No one had stopped to think about it." And the same in your personal life: questioning what you want, whether you want to improve, what you really want to be part of your life.

When you learn to think critically, different answers start to emerge and you have to learn to discern among them. A critical mindset develops, a person who questions things. And that in the long run, in terms of society, is very valuable. The person starts to question: "I used to believe in this political party, but maybe it doesn't have the best ideas. Maybe this other idea is also reasonable. Maybe my opinion isn't so absolute." These are very valuable people for society, more responsible and with more control over their lives.

This is the characteristic of a good teacher: sparking genuine interest in the subject. Not having students learn the life story of Fernando VII for the exam, vomit it back, and forget it. That's not educating you in any personal way. Maybe what you have to do is teach less, but build better people. People with a more critical mindset, with greater curiosity. And people who actually understand what they know, who have learned for the pleasure of learning, guided by a tutor who doesn't say "this is the way" but rather "think about how to get there." A tutor who doesn't draw you the map but gives you reference points. And you're the one who evolves, who discovers, who moves from one point to the next.

It's a bit like what Socrates said — or Plato, because that's one I was actually taught badly — that he never taught by telling the other person what they needed to learn. He taught by asking, always asking, to reveal the knowledge already sleeping inside the other person.

There's a point at which it's necessary for someone to ask you questions, because otherwise the things that are in the "unknown unknown" never move to the "known unknown," and then they don't matter to you. That's where the good tutor opens things up: "Have you ever thought about this?"

So think about this in different areas of your life. What motivates you most? What would you like to learn? Turn it over. And once you've turned it over, then go look for information. Don't fall into the trap of, just because everything is at your fingertips today, saying: "I have a question, I'll look it up, done." No. Think about it. Derive some principles, some ideas. Discard them. Sit with it. And eventually look for information. I assure you that even though it's more uncomfortable, your final result as a person who acquired knowledge is much stronger. Because on top of that you don't only know the answer they give you: you have other options you thought of, which you can compare against. And sometimes you look for a solution to something and say: "Well, I don't agree, I think the options I came up with are more valid." And that lets you judge your sources of information. That's something you can't put a price on.
