Good morning everyone. This is my notebook for writing things down daily. I always carry it with me, always, more than my phone. I always keep a pen inside. It's the notebook for writing down things, thoughts, sketches, drawings, anything. It's super important for capturing my instant thoughts and keeping them. Because if I leave them in digital format they tend to disappear. Generally, when I start a new notebook, I write some poem or phrase on the first page that inspires me during that stage of my life. And this notebook has on its first page: action always beats potential.

It's something I've been turning over for quite a while. How action is, while potential is potential. And isn't. How, therefore, despite the fact that potential — which in this video I'm going to equate with intellect — is very attractive and easily deceives, action exists in reality. And action dissolves all doubts. It's in line with the idea that people are the way they act. Not the way they tell you they are.

In the next part I'll explain this with a very interesting example, which is sexuality. But in this first part I'm going to talk about the need, as Nassim Taleb says, to have skin in the game. To walk the path. The need to get fully involved in life. To give everything. To dare to play. And stop rationalizing.

We live in a society that values intellect highly. High-status jobs are jobs where you need a large amount of intellect. And we've been moving more and more toward a society with less and less action, less interaction with the world. To the point that the vast majority of our work is sitting down moving only our hands in front of a screen, building mental constructs, modeling reality. But it doesn't matter how beautiful your model is. If you can't test it, if you can't try it, if you can't run an experiment on it, you take your theory and throw it in the trash. That's what makes the difference.

I'm very fond of Nassim Taleb in this area. In his book Antifragile he's very hard on intellectuals, and it's very fun to read. He says these people have no idea what life is. They have their jargon, their mental constructs, their complications. But they have no idea how to live, of tangibility. And when you take one of these intellectuals and put them in a real situation, life smacks them in the face and shows that maybe they weren't that smart after all.

Taleb makes this distinction between intellectual intellect and practical intellect. The Taoists call it the difference between knowledge and wisdom. Wisdom, from Taleb's perspective, requires that your theory works on a pragmatic level. You have to be able to put it into practice on the field and have it work. And that's a very hard test. Reality is more complex than it seems and people are more complex than they seem.

Personally I find myself very divided. I'm a physics student. My job consists of modeling reality, of thinking a lot. I'm a theoretical physicist: paper and pencil, literally. On one hand I'm someone who likes to reflect, to philosophize, to rack my brain. But on the other hand I've discovered enormous value in getting fully involved in life. In swimming naked in the Mediterranean. Climbing rocks. Going barefoot in the mountains. Plunging into a frozen lake. Running. Jumping. Having an active, fun and healthy sex life. Training three hours a day. I don't have a middle ground where I exercise but not too much, and think but not too much. Comfort makes us weak.

What all this has taught me is that there are many things in day-to-day life and in life itself that you can't rationalize. You have to live them. You have to put your hands in there, get dirty, cover yourself in mud. And that teaches you far more than you could have arrived at through rationalizing. And it's not that by rationalizing enough I could have reached the same point. I couldn't have reached that point. I wouldn't have acquired that knowledge without the praxis, without the action.

For example at Enso Movers, my personal training company, I never teach anything that I haven't learned well enough for myself first. I never put a movement in front of a client that I haven't done myself. Because every time I pick up a new exercise, even something as simple as a handstand push-up or a purely strength exercise, there are a million small details that it would have been impossible to conceive without putting it into practice. The collarbone that always drifts back, the scapular tilt, the need for a certain external rotation, managing the push as if it came from the chest rather than the pelvis. All of that you're never going to fully understand if you haven't lived it.

I broke my collarbone a few years ago. My whole life I'd thought that the idea of aching bones when the weather changes was a myth, because I couldn't find any rational explanation. Then I broke my collarbone. And for two years, if it was going to rain, I would wake up in bed and say: it's going to rain today. Because my collarbone hurt. And it never failed. I still don't have a rational scientific explanation for why that is. But I know my collarbone hurt every time the weather turned bad. And that's an example where life told me: you know what? You thought you were very clever. You weren't.

There are times that what I say on this channel can sound radical, and there are times when some of you respond in the comments: no, but that's not how it is. Especially on the topics of sexuality. There's a lot of intellectualizing around it. And with meditation too. A lot of rationalizing about what happens and the benefits you're going to get. And sometimes the only thing I can say is: try it. Just like with nofap: we can speculate endlessly about which hormones go up and down, but in the end, try it. Commit a hundred percent for two months. Go three months without pornography and masturbation. And see what happens. Put skin in the game. Dare to experience it, to live based on that. And then you rationalize.

What I've learned is that a lot of people fail at that step, at putting things into action. A lot of talk, a lot of bluster, complex things. And in the end life is meant to be lived. You have to dive into it completely.

One conversation that caught my attention the other day was with a girl I've met here, Selin, and my roommate. We went into town for a drink. The conversation drifted to meditation and my roommate started saying: I think meditation isn't the act of meditating itself, but a way of living, of being engaged with life. And I told him I don't think so, that meditation itself is a practice you do daily, that leads you to a certain mental and physiological state, and that it's a mental training with a beginning and an objective. He argued with me. And at a certain point I said: but do you meditate? No. It makes me want to slap him in the face. You're lecturing me about meditation when I've been meditating for five years, without missing a day for almost two years straight, and I've seen firsthand all the changes that have happened in me. And you haven't dared to try it for yourself. It's absurd. And it's the perfect example of someone who hasn't quite managed to grab life and take it.
