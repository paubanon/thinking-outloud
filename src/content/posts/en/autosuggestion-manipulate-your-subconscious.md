Today I want to talk about a concept that's been spinning around in my head for a while and that I find tremendously interesting: autosuggestion.

Autosuggestion is basically the idea that you can influence your own subconscious through repeated affirmations. It's nothing new, it's something that's been extensively studied in psychology and that also gets mentioned in classic self-help books like Napoleon Hill's *Think and Grow Rich*.

The idea is simple: your subconscious doesn't distinguish between what is real and what isn't. If you keep telling yourself something, your brain starts to act as if it were true. And that has some very interesting implications.

There's a related concept called *fake it till you make it*, which has more scientific backing than it might seem. It's not just an empty motivational line. It has to do with how the brain works and how actions influence thoughts, not just the other way around.

Amy Cuddy, in a very famous TED talk, talks about how adopting power poses for two minutes changes hormone levels. Meaning, the body influences the mind. If you behave like a confident person, you start to feel more confident.

This connects to something I think is important: many times we wait to feel motivated before we act, when in reality motivation comes after acting. You do first, then you feel. Not the other way around.

Autosuggestion works similarly. You're not exactly lying. You're telling your brain which direction to move in. You're programming the autopilot.

Obviously this has limits. You can't autosugest yourself into knowing how to fly and jump off a building. But you can, for example, tell yourself that you're a disciplined and organized person, and have that behavior start filtering into your day-to-day actions.

What I find most valuable about this concept is that it gives control back to the individual. Instead of being a victim of your thoughts and your moods, you can start actively shaping them.

It's not magic. It's work. But it's a kind of work that starts in your head.
