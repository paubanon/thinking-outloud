Hello everyone, good morning. Today I wanted to talk about self-help culture. And I wanted to talk about it off the back of a video I watched, which I'll leave in the description. I wanted to talk about self-help culture and specifically about how addictive it can become and its dangerous side, let's call it that.

You know I like talking quite a bit about social media and how it can be counterproductive. Well, the same happens with the self-help world. And curiously the social media mechanism applies to self-help. Many of you who listen to this channel will follow other similar ones. Because in the end we all drink from the same trough, where people are encouraged to improve their lives, to be more productive, to have better habits. All of this in principle has nothing negative. I'm the first who has read, not that much but a fair amount, from the books out there. And I think I've benefited from it. I've learned a lot from this kind of literature and I certainly recommend it to anyone who wants to change their life.

The problem I notice is drawing the line between consuming more self-help and working on your projects or on whatever you want to improve. And that's where a little of the danger of self-help lies. I don't think it's out of malice, I think it's a by-product, a natural consequence of how the human mind works. The same way that although social media has this component of being designed to create addiction, the bigger problem isn't social media itself. It's that our minds work this way and when faced with this type of information they're a bit defenseless.

Our mind always likes getting more information. And there's the fear of missing out. Self-help literature runs the risk of making you believe there's always one more book with those keys you need for your life. And it can be infinite. We've all seen those people who talk about self-help books and have a stack behind them: Essentialism, Deep Work, Atomic Habits, The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People, The Power of Now, this endless list of books about how to be a millionaire, how to be productive. They're endless.

The problem is they're easily addictive. It's easy to dodge taking action in your life by using the excuse that you still have something to learn. Sit down and work on my project... ugh... better I read How to Win Friends and Influence People. Because of course, then this will let me do better marketing. But if you never move to action, everything stays as potential. And you're doing nothing more than wasting your time.

There's a point where you have to know how to say enough. I've already learned certain things. I don't need to learn more. I don't need to keep reading, I don't need to keep watching YouTube videos. I need to sit down and work.

And the problem is that sitting down to work is very hard. It can be very boring. So we turn to that other thing: the flow state, the pomodoro method, concentration techniques. A bunch of things which deep inside us we hope will make sitting down to work more pleasant. It never gets more pleasant. Sitting down to work is tedious, boring, and sometimes costly. That's what it is. There's no book that's going to teach you any wonderful technique to ease that suffering. It's hard. Accept it. Period.

This goes hand in hand with what I've taken to calling the motivation culture. The idea that to work you have to be motivated. And there are people who feel part of a group and very identified and very proud to say: I use a pomodoro system to study. I set my 25 minutes and then do a tabata set. Using all these terms as if knowing what they mean makes you a better person. And it doesn't.

At the end of the day you have to sit down and work for things to move forward. Motivation is nice to have every once in a while, but it lasts 10 minutes. Motivation can last about as long as it takes to finish watching that motivational video with animals in super slow motion and a voiceover and the Transformers soundtrack. And what it takes is closing the browser and opening the work document. The key is you don't need motivation to work. You need to sit down and work. Period.

Same thing in the world of dating: another book, another video, another course, another seminar. Maybe you just need to go out and talk to girls you find attractive. And that's it. It's the most uncomfortable step there is, but it's the only one that will actually move you forward.

YouTube is especially guilty of this kind of thinking. YouTube always has the feeling that there's more. The excitement you used to get from reading a self-help book, you now get from a 5-minute video that summarizes that book. And you end up super fired up saying: that's what I needed. And then you see in the recommended list: how to improve your charisma. That's what I was missing with girls. How to improve your productivity. That's what I was missing to sit down and work. And the reality is no.

I've read Deep Work, I've read Flow. They've given me things. But there's no insanely radical difference. There's no magic pill. The only difference is I'm now a little more aware of certain processes. But at the end of the day you have to sit down and work and put in the hours, whether you feel like it or not.

I love the analogy of writing a book. Writing a book is an especially tedious, long, solitary and difficult task. There's no shortcut. In the end you have to have typed, I don't know, 80,000 times on the keyboard. And there's no productivity method that's going to make that more enjoyable. You just have to sit down.

Finding a work method that works for you: there are people who work better in long blocks, people who work better in a coffee shop, people who work better at home or in a cabin in the middle of the woods. But at the end of the day you have to sit down and do it.

I'd like with this video to demystify a little the idea that you have to be super productive and in a state of flow and isn't life wonderful. Sometimes even in this culture people go on and on about how hard they work, how much effort they're putting in, how much suffering they're enduring. That's also a stupid culture. You don't have to suffer to work. You have to sit down and work. Sometimes it's boring, sometimes you're totally in the zone and everything goes fast. But you have to sit down.

What you also can't expect is for people to give you a pat on the back for how much you've suffered. I couldn't care less. Do your work. And don't expect anyone to recognize you for it. Do it because you want to do it. And if you don't want to do it, be honest with yourself and don't do it. You're not a better or worse person for it.

Don't wait to be motivated to sit down and work. Don't watch another YouTube video. Don't read another self-help book. Just sit down and work. Do what you have to do. It'll be over tomorrow. And get on with your life. And learn to distinguish when a book or video is going to give you something, and when it's not.
