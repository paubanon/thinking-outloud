A while back I posted a video about personal relationships and the importance of taking responsibility and owning it. That video went more or less viral for the tiny channel that mine is: a lot of people shared it and it got a good number of views. I'd like this one to be similar, because when something gets shared widely it means it resonates with a lot of people and it's something important that we feel we lack.

This video is about apologizing. About knowing when you're wrong and accepting it. And it's not easy at all. Apologizing takes a lot out of me. Not compared to other people — I don't know if that's the case — but it requires a lot of personal effort, it requires thinking it through, it requires doing it consciously.

Let me tell you a little story. When I moved to Germany I wanted to sign up for judo. I went to a couple of classes, talked to the instructor, he said he was going to order some judogis and that I'd need to pay a deposit. In the end the schedule didn't work out, it was very hard to make it to the classes, and I stopped going. Which felt terrible because I had told the instructor I would be there. A while later I ran into him in a different context and decided that, even though what I'd done wasn't morally wrong because it was largely out of my control, it still deserved an apology. It wasn't easy to approach someone you've only spoken to once and say: "I'd like to apologize because I told you I was coming and I didn't, I couldn't make it work, and I'm sorry — you probably ordered those judogis." But I did it. And now I have a lot of trust with that person, and I think it's partly because of that honest moment.

Why is it so hard for us to apologize? A lot of people say it's ego. I think it goes deeper than that. When you apologize you have to accept that you were wrong or that you acted in a way that hurt someone and that you know it and want to empathize with that person. And when you accept that you were wrong in the past, it implies that your current view of the world might not be correct either. That you might be wrong right now. And that destabilizes your world. It makes you aware that the world is chaotic and that you can't always act correctly. Apologizing means accepting that not everything is in your hands.

It also means accepting vulnerability. It means we're not superheroes, we're human beings, we make mistakes, and we're asking the other person to love us in spite of those mistakes.

Another example. This past Christmas, near the end, when I was in Spain, my aunt asked me to give my cousin a math tutoring session. She caught me at the tail end of the holidays with a lot of people I wanted to see and my mind already on having to go back. I was mentally a bit worn down and had to fight that internal battle. I told my cousin I couldn't, that I had too many things in the middle, limited time, limited energy, and that taking on a class — even an hour-long one — wasn't something I had the strength or the mood for. I know I didn't do anything wrong, because given those circumstances I'd probably do the same again. In any other moment I would have said yes without hesitation. But I know it hurt him.

Two months later I was back in Spain and it was something I knew I needed to do. So I got into it and said: I wanted to apologize about Christmas. It caught me at a bad moment and I know you shouldn't have had to hear that. What happened? Instead of a deep resentment sitting there — "he's my family but he said no and that didn't sit right" — that tension dissolved. We hugged, genuinely. It was a "thank you for apologizing."

Even when time passes, I think it's something necessary to keep order in your life. You don't want to become old Scrooge, a lonely and resentful old man on Christmas night.

And hand in hand with apologizing, you need to know how to forgive too. That's also difficult. You have to accept that just as you're someone who makes mistakes, so is everyone else. Accepting an apology isn't always easy when someone has hurt you. But you have to try to assume ignorance before malice. You have to try to accept that when people hurt you they don't always do it intentionally. You have to give them a chance to explain themselves and genuinely put yourself in their place. Many times when you do that you understand that the behavior that hurt you so much wasn't motivated by a desire to cause that hurt. That person saw the world a certain way and tried to do the best they could to deal with this very difficult life we have, and as a result you got hurt. But you also have to understand that that person may know they did wrong, may want to make amends, and you have to be able to accept that.

This includes your partner, your friends, your family. It's something complementary to the video I made about relationships. Maintaining a relationship means accepting that both of us do things wrong, that both of us will hurt each other, and that both of us value the relationship above all of that. And that therefore we're going to work to keep it going in spite of our mistakes and our failures. It's the only way to maintain a relationship over a long time: accepting that you will fail, accepting that you will hurt each other, and accepting that you want to carry that hurt together.

The last story I wanted to share is about an ex, my first serious love. Like all first loves it was very intense, very felt, and in the end this girl hurt me a lot. We cut off the relationship completely and after a year and a half in which there was barely any contact, she called me just to say: I sincerely believe I owe you an apology because I hurt you a lot and it wasn't right. It was something very difficult to receive because this person had hurt me a lot, as almost every first relationship does. But I had to get past that resentment and say: this person is aware that she hurt me and was noble enough to, a year and a half later, when she could have just let the river keep flowing, make the effort and apologize. I accepted. And today we talk regularly and we get along really well.

Because you have to accept that people change, that people make mistakes, and that people are sometimes willing to make amends for the mistakes of the past in order to make the world we live in a little better.
