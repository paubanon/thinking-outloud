Today I'm going to talk to you as a physicist, which isn't something I usually do around here. I wanted to talk about a very everyday concept that we have a lot of trouble with in physics: time. What is time? How do we understand it from a physical point of view?

As people, as humans at our scale, we have a fairly clear sense of what time is or at least how we experience it. We have a clear picture of time as a stratified, constant dimension for all human beings, flowing uninterruptedly. You're standing still, you're sleeping, time flows and flows the same for everyone. However, this idea is a relatively modern one, brought by Newton, and it breaks down as soon as you start moving through different spaces.

Historically, defining time has been quite difficult. In the past, time was given by the solar clock, which is local to wherever you are on Earth. Time was something local, determined by the city's clock, which was usually in the church. The bell tolls determined local time. There was no need or capacity to synchronize one city's clock with another two days' travel away. It wasn't until the introduction of the railway, when we were capable of moving from city A to city B within a short time on the same day, that we began to need to coordinate time from one place to another. And that's how time zones came about.

How do we understand time from a physical point of view? Let's start with classical physics, then quantum physics, and finally relativistic physics. And we'll see how they interweave and where the gaps are.

In classical physics — the physics that governs our everyday world — the description of time is symmetric at the physical level. There's no concept of forward or backward. The physical laws depend on a parameter T and you can run time forward or backward. There's no distinction between past and future. This is already different from how we perceive time, because we conceive of the past as something that existed and the future as something that doesn't exist yet.

There is only one physical law that determines the direction of time: the second law of thermodynamics. This law states that systems always evolve toward increasing entropy. Entropy is a measure of the disorder of things. What the second law tells you is that there are many more ways to disorder a system than to have it ordered. A messy room: you kick something, the configuration changes, and it stays messy. A tidy room has a limited number of valid configurations. So through relatively random fluctuations driven by temperature, systems move toward the states where there's more disorder. Things tend to decay, rooms tend to become messy, things tend to mix together. And from this emerges the concept of friction and heat dissipation that gives us the direction of time.

In the quantum regime, things are very small and interact very little, and here the second law of thermodynamics no longer applies directly. At the quantum level, time is a parameter, a variable you can run forward or backward, and it doesn't have a physical meaning in its own right. What does have physical meaning are the interactions between the different elements that make up the system. So when things are very small, we don't know what time is in a fundamental sense. As the system gets larger, it starts to interact, starts to become disordered, and then we can define a direction. That's how time passes from the quantum regime to the classical regime.

What happens when we go beyond the classical? What happens with things that are very large or very fast? That's where special relativity and general relativity come in. It turns out the universe has a maximum speed at which information can propagate: the speed of light. We've consistently observed this to be the maximum speed at which any kind of information can be transmitted through spacetime. This puts a limit on how things interact, and remember that time is determined by the interaction of things.

If you and I are talking we can say we're in the present moment. However, my voice takes time to reach you. It's such a short time that our brain can't distinguish it and we perceive it as simultaneous. But if you were on the Sun, the communication between you and me would take 8 minutes to arrive and 8 minutes to return. We can calculate what "now" is through this triangulation with light. However, this only holds if we're both stationary. If someone passes by the Sun traveling at a very high speed, for that person the time it takes the photon to go to the Sun and the time to return are no longer 8 minutes and 8 minutes. They're different. Because for people moving at very high speeds, time passes differently.

So the concept of what "now" is becomes less clear. My now is no longer your now. There's what we call a bubble of the present: take the minimum time your brain can resolve as two separate events and multiply it by the speed of light, and you get a distance. Everything that happens within that bubble you perceive as simultaneous. You perceive it as "this is my present." Everything beyond it, even if it happens at the same time, you can in theory distinguish what came before and what came after. And this simultaneity is relative depending on who is observing.

So the concept of time, the concept of present, of now, starts to dissolve. We don't lose the causal relationship: if one thing causes another, that causal order will be respected in all reference frames. However, two things that are completely disconnected — you can't clearly order which comes first and which comes after, depending on who is looking at it.

Something similar happens with very large gravitational fields. When a gravitational field is very strong, time passes more slowly there than in a weaker gravitational field. If you're closer to the Earth, time passes more slowly than if you're far from it. This doesn't mean you have more time to do things: time for you will happen at the same speed. You can only see it, as in the film Interstellar, when you bring together two people who have been, one near and one far from a very intense gravitational field. For those outside, two years might have passed; for those who approached the field, one.

And this that sounds so strange we can actually measure. Today we have clocks precise enough to put one on one floor and another on a different floor, synchronize them, separate them, and see that when we bring them back together they no longer show the same time. Time passes differently at floor level than at ceiling level. Your feet will be a tiny bit younger than your head when you die. Because they're closer to the ground. The difference is infinitesimal fractions of a second, but it exists.

So when you go to very large scales, there's no such thing as time as a total and absolute frame of reference for everyone. There is causality. There is how one event causes another, and that one causes another, and that causality is respected. But for events that are not causally related, you can't say which comes before and which comes after.

We have the quantum aspect, where time is a variable you run equally forward and backward, causality is respected. As the system gets larger, we get the classical regime where time comes into play as entropy, as the ordering of things. And as you get larger or faster, you have to introduce new laws: time shifts differently for observers moving fast, and time passes differently depending on the gravitational field.

All of these conceptions we have to try to bring together and reconcile. And right now we have serious problems in physics trying to put them all in the same framework. If you were hoping to get to the end of this video with a final conclusion about how everything ties together with a big bow, I'm sorry to disappoint you, because we don't know how to do that. It's one of the most important open questions we have on our hands. We'd like to obtain a theory of quantum gravity that unifies the concept of gravity and velocity with the quantum element. And we hope that theory will shed light on how to understand time. We're working on it. Let's hope we find something, in time.
