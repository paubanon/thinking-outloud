"If people knew how hard I have had to work to gain my mastery, it would not seem so wonderful at all." These are Michelangelo's words and they reflect the topic I'm going to address today: the importance of deliberate practice in reaching an excellent level of performance in any discipline.

It's a belief that goes against the norm, but I think it's a barrier that doesn't do us any good and that we need to learn to knock down. When you reach a certain level of performance, you often find yourself in the situation where someone sees what you do and instantly attributes it to some force or kind of luck that's out of their control. It can be something as simple as watching you do a handstand at the gym and saying: "but you just have great balance." Someone watching you on the rings: "it's just good genetics you have." Same with flexibility. Same when we see some master painter, some virtuoso musician, some chess master, a good mathematician, or an elite athlete. We instantly attribute it to genetics. Or luck.

And while it's true that there's some genetic predisposition toward certain traits, 99% of their level was not obtained through genetics. It was obtained through what we're going to call deliberate practice.

Anyone who has reached a very high level of performance in any area notices one thing: improvement is the direct result of the hours you put in. And when you manage to achieve some athletic, artistic, or intellectual feat, the feeling is quite different from what you might expect. From the outside you might think that when someone achieves these kinds of feats they finish with a feeling of euphoria, surprise, and satisfaction. The reality, however, is quite different.

Consistently, what I've found is that when you accomplish something considered extraordinary, it doesn't surprise you because you see it as the natural fruit of the work you've invested. You're not flooded with euphoria. You're glad to have finished something difficult, but you understand that it was inevitable given the hours you put in. There's no surprise because it's a direct consequence.

When you manage, speaking from personal experience, to hold the time you'd set for yourself in a one-arm handstand, when you manage to do one-arm pull-ups, you look at it and think: of course this happened. How could it not happen with the attention and effort I've applied? When I finished my thesis on an introduction to quantum gravity, this might sound to someone who doesn't know the field like "wow, you just have a head for physics." But from my point of view I look at it and think: how could I not write this thesis knowing the whole trajectory I've had, all the years of studying, all the foundation I've built and the effort I've put in? Of course it was going to happen.

I remember a quote from Steve Jobs on the subject. Paraphrasing it: we grow up believing that everything around us was made by exceptional people. We grow up hearing: don't try to aim too high, don't try to be too ambitious, don't try to stand out. But your vision of the world changes radically the day you realize that everything around you has been made, thought up, designed, and built by people no different from you and me.

When you hear that and understand what it means, it has very deep consequences. The consequence is understanding that the people who have managed to do amazing things are normal people. And that means that you, as a normal person, have the capacity to do surprising and magnificent things, if you're willing to pay the price required to achieve them.

It's a difficult thought. Because if you truly integrate it, you realize you can't do anything but stop making excuses. Because if you really believe it, it means you have unlimited potential to change the world. And when I say unlimited potential I mean you don't know how far you could go if you truly dedicated the time, the effort, the level of obsession necessary. Nobody knows whether you could be the next Gaudí, whether you could be the next violin virtuoso, whether you could be the person who unifies gravity with quantum theory. You don't know. Nor does anyone. But you have to dare to dedicate the time and effort to try to become it.

And it's a very heavy weight to carry. It means that every time you do something not aligned with your real desire, you're falling short of your own ideals. And you'd be depriving humanity of something through your own fault. Not through chance.

But it's also inspiring. Because it means that if you wanted to you could be an astronaut. If you truly want it, you just have to start walking and take the steps. Do the degree you're missing. Sign up for the schools you're missing. Travel wherever necessary. And walk the path. Over time you realize there's only one thing to do: walk the path. It might be longer, easier, harder. But if there are people who do it, there is nothing, nothing, nothing that tells you a priori that you can't do it.

This reminds me of something I believe was LeBron James. When they asked him: what makes you think you could be the best player in the NBA? And his answer was: someone has to be. Why not me?

Deliberate practice — what distinguishes it from simple training — is the focus on the edge of your capabilities. It's not repeating what you already know how to do. It's finding the point where you fail, attacking that point, improving, and raising the bar. Over and over. And this has a name: continuous, deliberate, intentional improvement. Not genetics. Not luck. Work.
