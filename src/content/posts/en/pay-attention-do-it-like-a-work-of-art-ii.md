"I think that everything in life is art. What you do, how you dress, the way you love someone and how you speak, your smile and your personality, what you believe in and all your dreams, the way you drink your tea, how you decorate your home or go out dancing, your shopping list, the food you make, how you write and the way you feel. Life is art." Elena Baumkartner.

I came across this piece of text recently and really liked it. It reminded me of an old video I have, one I think is terribly underrated on this channel, because I think it's the best practice you can draw from everything I've talked about here. It was called something like "do it like a work of art." The main idea is to treat your life as if it deserves your full attention. All of it. From chopping an onion to preparing a dish. To how you get dressed every day. The way you speak. The way you move. The way you write, what you write and how you write it. The way you look at things. The way you gesture. How you smile.

This is an idea I've been consciously refining for a long time: trying to be aware of everything. And obviously being aware of absolutely everything is impossible because our brain is built to filter things out. But this is the basic principle of meditative practices. Seeing things. Not what you think things are, but looking at them and trying to observe them as they are. Trying to remove the filters. Trying to be as close as possible to the raw experience your senses provide.

And in the end I think that's kind of the message of this channel. A large part of what I want to convey is the importance of being fully present in whatever you're doing. In everything. In life. Both in the tasks themselves and in how you organize your life. Try to do it as excellently as possible. Try to plan your schedule with all the care you can. Try to be the most excellent version of yourself in every area. And reflect on them and notice them.

A very clear and beautiful example is social relationships. It's a very interesting practice: try to be completely present when you're with someone. It doesn't matter whether you know them or not. It can be someone you see every day or a total stranger. And if you pay enough attention to them, if you really look them in the eyes and you're not thinking about all those other things that person has said to you at some point. If you're not seeing that person through the filters that form and the expectations you accumulate from years of knowing them. If you see them directly, without filters. If you truly listen to what they're saying. Then conversations are magical. All of them.

And this idea can be generalized: everything you do, if you pay enough attention to it, stops being boring and becomes something special. Because making something magical isn't dependent solely on the task itself. It depends on how you see the task, how you interpret it, and how much attention you give it.

This can be understood as the cure for boredom. Because if you pay enough attention, you'll find that repetition doesn't really exist. Everything you think is repeated, is so only at a certain level of resolution. But if you look closely enough, everything is different. All the time.

And this applies to everything. It applies to any task that might be considered routine. It applies to the people you see every day. It applies to the places you walk through without looking. The world is infinitely rich in detail if you learn to notice it. You just have to learn to pay attention.
