A lot of people go to university for the degree. And that's fine, the degree has value. But there are many more things university can give you, if you decide to take advantage of them.

The first is the environment. Surrounding yourself with young people who are thinking, who are learning, who have projects. That has enormous value that's often not recognized until you've lost it. Conversations in the hallway, debates in class, people you meet casually who turn out to be important in your life.

The second is learning how to learn. A well-used degree teaches you to study complex material, to process a lot of information, to prioritize, to work under pressure. Those are skills that transfer to anything you do afterwards.

The third is independence. If you study away from home — which I recommend if you can — university is your first laboratory for living alone. You learn to manage your time, your money, your food, your relationships. You make mistakes, you learn from them, you grow up.

The fourth is exposure. University exposes you to ideas, to disciplines, to people you wouldn't have found otherwise. A philosophy book you read for an elective can change the way you see the world. A talk you attend because a friend dragged you along can open a door you didn't know existed.

And finally there's the time. University, especially if you do it in a residential setting, gives you a relatively protected period of time to explore. To try things, to make mistakes, to discover what you like and what you don't. That's a luxury very few people have afterwards.

Of course university has problems. It's expensive, sometimes it's disconnected from the job market, some degrees don't make much sense. All of that is true. But if you decide to go, go with the mindset of making the most of it, not just of getting the degree.

The degree is the minimum. Everything else is up to you.
