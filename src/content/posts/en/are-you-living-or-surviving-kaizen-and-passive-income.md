Hey everyone, I've brought you here to the Misty Mountains Cold. Well, not really: I haven't brought you, I didn't come here to make the video. I came because every week I go to the mountains and it clears my head. I recommend that if you can and you're somewhere quiet, put on headphones, because I've set the mic to omnidirectional and I'd like you to hear the silence here and the sound of the drops falling from the high branches and hitting the leaves on the ground.

To give you some context for today's video: this morning I had one of those moments where I realized that, Pau, you're not like the rest of people, there's something different. And that different thing could be called monk mode, this whole life setup.

What happened is that yesterday I threw a party at the flat and we went to sleep at 4 in the morning. Obviously the kitchen was dirty and everyone was sleeping. And since I'm so in sync with my body and the sun, I wake up at the same time regardless of what time I was put to bed. So while the rest of the world was sleeping as is normal, I woke up at 9 in the morning after going to bed at 4, made a coffee, got dressed, and went for a walk. I think about it and it's that old-man attitude: when you're young you stay in bed, when you're older you get dressed and go out. A friend told me: man, you have an old man's mind. Maybe.

Then I came home and went straight to cleaning the kitchen. And I think about it and it's a slightly unusual attitude for someone my age: throw a party, go to sleep at 4:30, and the next day wake up at 9 perfectly clear-headed, lucid, and go out for a walk. The thing is I noticed that when we sleep well habitually but have one day of sleep deprivation, we don't feel it as much. It seems that generally when we lack sleep we're more irrational, more emotional, more impulsive. More animal. And in a very small dose that awakens a certain level of creativity. Good ideas come to me when something like this happens. Although it could be that all of this is a fallacy and I currently have a considerable sleep deficit and I'm thinking I'm making a good video when it's actually terrible.

The thing is, on this morning walk I was thinking about two things. The first: I started thinking about what it would take to buy a Tesla. That's 50,000 euros. If you have a net gain of 2,000 euros a month after all your expenses, that's about 24,000 euros extra a year. It means that to buy a Tesla you have to spend two entire years of your life. And that's already having a salary that lets you live comfortably, pay rent, and have 2,000 left over each month, which is a considerable amount. So I thought about the kind of projects I like to carry out: projects to which you sacrifice your free time, your vacations, to build something that later will require very little time and will give you constant income. What are known as passive income. I don't like that term because I can't stand the entrepreneur community. It all seems very fake to me, everyone with gold watches, a lot of pretension. I don't want to get into that.

These kinds of projects are obviously a fantastic way to live. On paper it's very clear that it's a win: you're going to sacrifice your vacations for two or three years and then for the rest of your life you're going to have an extra income of 1,200 or 2,000 euros a month putting in 5 hours a week. And along this line of thinking I got to asking myself: if it's so obvious, why don't people do it? I'm not going to give a motivational speech here. We just assume they don't do it because it's very hard to see the long-term reward. It's very hard. And I have to admit that's something my degree and my master's gave me, because they're investments on a much longer timescale than this type of project. They're investments on the scale of your life. You don't do a degree just because you like it: you do it with the hope that after many years, that effort will pay off and pay dividends.

A passive income project is the same. You're doing something that will pay you for the rest of your life with minimal work. But it's very hard to see that long-term return. And therefore it's very hard to sit down and say: I'm going to sacrifice this year's vacation. Completely. Because I know the future will be better. And that's the difference between the people who dream and the people who do things. However corny and motivational-poster that sounds. It's the difference between the person who says: you don't feel like it but you shut up and do it because you have to, you sit down and do it. And then, three or five years later, you look back and say: wow, I was back there and I've made it to here.

Which leads me to the second thought of this morning: a reflection on what you've done in the last year. Starting with looking back one year. If from a year ago until now you haven't changed... You're still in the same job. Still with the same friends. In the same house. With the same partner. Your numbers in the gym are the same. You haven't done any major travel. Your bank account is in the same place. If you've spent a year just surviving it, working to earn a certain amount of money that you spend by the end of the month and repeat. If you've spent a year tiptoeing through life. You've lost a year.

And it's a thought that scares me. That scares me and that I'm grateful for having had. Because having thought it gives me reason to say: watch out, don't stay here. That every year, that every action you take, has a positive impact on your future. That you've built something that from now on will earn you more money. That you've taken a trip that has marked you personally and emotionally. That you've moved to a new city. That you've made new friends. That you've advanced in your degree. All of those are forms of progress.

I think the problem is that most people don't live with a mindset of progress. They don't live in kaizen: a little better each day, a little better each month, a little better each year. They live with a survival mindset: I want to be able to have a house, a car, my partner, and that's it. And if there are people who are happy with that, great. If they're genuinely happy. But I think you can't stay happy without progress, without advancement, without change.

Life is a path and you have to walk it. The purpose of life is to walk, not to arrive. You have to be moving. The moment you sit down, you lose perspective. What is this? What am I doing here? Why am I doing this?

And related to passive income projects: I think there's a big fallacy in that approach. It's Tim Ferriss's 4-Hour Work Week approach. Work a lot now to build a business that gives you automatic income in the future, so you don't have to work. And it goes hand in hand with the idea of ultra-early retirement: work is suffering, I don't want to suffer, therefore I'll invest a lot of work now so that in the future I won't have to work. And you go live in Thailand.

I fell for that fallacy and for a while I thought this way. But now, with meditation, with time, with studying physics, with doing the master's and Enso Movers, I've realized that's not what I want. What I want is to do projects. I don't want to retire. I'm not walking to arrive. I'm walking. I don't want to build three passive projects and then say: there we go, I'm earning 5,000 a month, I can lie on the sofa.

For now I do it because I want to reach a certain level of financial independence. But I'll keep doing projects after that. I do them because I'm not doing them to survive. The mindset isn't one of surviving. The mindset is one of living. And to live you have to change. And to change you have to work, you have to put effort into something.

I do the projects because I like doing them. If I'm earning 5,000, great. If I end up earning 10,000, great. If I end up earning 2,000 and am very happy, that would also be fine. It's a question of approach. It's a question of saying: it's a path, not a destination. I'm dedicated to physics not because it's the thing that's going to make you the most money in the world, but because I like doing it, because I enjoy it, because it's something I have fun with. It's one project more. And after that project will come another. And another. And another.
