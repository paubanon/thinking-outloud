Today I wanted to talk about tools for debating with people who don't want to be convinced. People who are ideologically possessed, people entrenched in their position, people who haven't really stopped to think through their stance. It could be arguments with a stranger at a bar, with a friend, with family, with a partner. And these kinds of arguments, which have become very common since COVID and also in the world of nutrition, are debates where generally both sides feel they're in possession of absolute truth, and all they do is fight with no intention of reaching any conclusion.

Personally, I'm someone who loves to debate and search for the truth behind arguments, so I tend to get into these debates easily. Over the years and through various courses, I've arrived at certain tools I use regularly — tools that are not manipulative, meaning they don't force the other person into your position, but instead honestly encourage discourse and make the debate context healthier and more productive.

The first tool I've found fundamental is agreeing on the purpose of the debate before it starts. It may seem obvious because we're debating about climate change or veganism or vaccines. But no. We have to narrow down as much as possible what each person's goal actually is. Let's take the example of veganism versus a diet that includes meat. It's not really one debate, it's many debates. Is the goal for you to convince me to stop eating meat? Or is the goal for both of us to analyze which of the two diets has a greater or lesser climate impact? Or we could debate the morality of killing animals, or intensive versus extensive farming.

This matters because if we go into a debate with the intention of "I want you to stop being vegan and you want to convert me to veganism," the debate will most likely go nowhere. If instead we choose an objective metric, like the impact of both diets on the climate, that has a root in truth. And in that area, if both of us go in with a reasonably honest attitude, we can agree that one of us is right. We can talk about studies, what they account for and what they don't, and we can agree that objectively one of the two options is better. Agreeing on the topic before you start fixes everything.

The second point is to ask: what evidence or what would need to happen for both of us to change our minds? And this question is posed exactly as stated, literally. For example: you think a vegan diet is better for climate change and I think a diet that includes meat with an adequate production chain is better. What evidence would need to exist for you to stop believing the vegan diet is better? And what would need to happen for me to accept it? That doesn't mean I stop eating meat or you stop being vegan. It means that at this point, what are we looking for in this debate? What are the premises? If there were a study that controlled for this and this and this, I'd have to concede and say: you're right. That's essential to establish beforehand.

Another important element is agreeing that the debate will be honest, with the intention of understanding the other person's point. For this I really like the tool Peterson proposes: when you paraphrase, before continuing with an argument, make sure that what you understood is what the speaker meant to say. You need to say: "so what you mean is..." and the other person has to give you the green light. If they say you didn't understand correctly, you rephrase until they say: "yes, that's exactly my argument." That way we make sure what I understood is exactly what you meant to say.

This is very important because in arguments we often talk and assume the message lands because we said it. But what one person says and what reaches the other can be very different. It depends on the words we choose, our tone of voice, the emphasis, the looks. And it depends on the receiver: on the attention they're giving you in that moment, their preconceived ideas, the subjective connotations of each word. You'll be surprised how many times simply clarifying this already fixes the debate. Often one person is using terminology with one definition and the other is using the same terminology with a different definition. You just have to agree on what we define as reality or truth, and then proceed from there.

And finally, a tool I use a lot in debates is not to speak in statements, but to speak in questions. Once someone has laid out their argument, I advance with questions, trying to get the other person to arrive on their own at the ultimate consequences of their arguments. This often breaks their arguments, because they've never stopped to think: what happens if I take this position to its extreme? What happens if I hold it exactly as it is? And many times they arrive at conclusions they never thought they'd reach.

If you directly tell them "that position implies this," they'll say "no, you didn't get it." But if you get them to take small logical steps, little by little, you reach the point where the person says: "well, yes, that's true, but that's not where I wanted to go." Which is basically a way of saying: I just realized my main argument has this consequence I hadn't thought of. So I need to rethink it. It's a much healthier and more constructive way to debate.

If you do it honestly, the debate can be incredibly enriching for both sides, very constructive and can lead to a greater understanding. And more than that: you might end the debate both of you feeling grateful toward the other person and richer for it. Because you weren't trying to tear the other person down as a person. You were arguing about specific points where you had a dissenting opinion, and so you both learned and grew.

And in case any of the initial conditions aren't met — I've had arguments where, raising all of this, I asked someone: what would it take for you to change your mind? And the answer was: no, I'm not going to change my mind. And at that point I stop and say: then there's no point in us sitting down to talk about this. Because you're not willing to receive information. The argument loses the point it should have: that we both make an effort to find the truth.
