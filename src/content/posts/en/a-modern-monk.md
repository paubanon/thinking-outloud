There's an archetype that fascinates me and that I identify with more and more: the modern monk.

I'm not talking about literally becoming a monk. I'm talking about adopting certain principles shared by monastic traditions across different cultures and applying them to modern life.

What do monks have in common, regardless of tradition? Daily discipline. Simplicity. Spiritual or contemplative practice. Few possessions. Focus on what's essential.

And what's interesting is that many of these principles make sense even from a completely secular perspective.

Daily discipline, for example, is something that appears constantly in the lives of the most effective and fulfilled people. Not motivation, which comes and goes, but discipline: doing what you have to do regardless of how you feel.

Simplicity too. Living with fewer things, fewer commitments, less noise, frees up mental space and energy for what really matters. There's research showing that making decisions depletes cognitive capacity. If you reduce the number of trivial decisions you have to make each day, you have more energy for the important ones.

Contemplative practice, whether meditation, prayer, or simply time for silence and reflection, also has documented benefits for mental health, concentration, and general wellbeing.

And reducing possessions isn't just aesthetic minimalism. It's not having to worry about maintaining, cleaning, protecting, insuring things you don't actually need.

The modern monk doesn't live in a monastery. They live in the world, work, have relationships. But they consciously choose what to let into their life and what not to. They take their own development seriously. They protect their attention and their time.

It's an ideal I aspire to. I don't know if I fully achieve it, but orienting my life in that direction seems worth it to me.
