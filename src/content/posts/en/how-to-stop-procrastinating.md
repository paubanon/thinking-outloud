Hey little monsters, welcome back to Germany. As I mentioned in the last video, I came back early and it's been a tremendous decision. When your gut tells you it's enough, leave — that's generally the one you should listen to, even if it scares you a little.

I didn't want to talk about that today, as you can tell from the title. The idea for today's video is that since I've come back to Germany, I've gotten back on track with the master's. Now I have to do my master's thesis, which is a year-long project, fairly ambitious, and I've had to think hard about how to organize myself. That changes the schedules I need to dedicate to working on Some Movers, training, meals. I have to review my schedule and figure out how to spend the time I want on the things I want. A project that big and that important requires daily effort. And that requires planning.

And that's what I wanted to talk to you about today: procrastination, how to avoid it, and a couple of small tricks I've been using over the past few years that are especially helpful right now.

Basically, the problem is that I procrastinate when I'm in a situation where I don't know what I need to do. If I have my schedule clear and transparent, I jump from one thing to the next. If I know I need to get up, have a coffee and meditate for ten minutes and then leave, I don't turn on my phone. It's like when you have class at eight in the morning: you don't stop to check Instagram because you think there's no time. So you get up, make breakfast, and bam, out the door.

That's the power of habits. If you know what you need to do each day and do it without even thinking about whether you want to do it or not, you go straight to work. That's the key: not thinking. Forcing your brain into a situation where you don't have to think because everything is already defined. Because the moment you have to think: do I need to do this or that?, and you say, I don't know, but while I'm thinking about it I'll get my phone out... that's where you're lost.

What you need to reach is a situation where you spend a little time planning, so that all the rest of your time is relatively organized and you don't have to think about what comes next. Because the moment you think about what comes next, that's a mistake.

This applies to training too. You don't have to decide if you need to train. That's: time to train, period. And you don't have to decide what to train either. You have to arrive at the training session with the training already done: prepared, I need to do this, this, this and this. If not, it's a disaster.

What have I found that's very useful for organizing time? Not just planning it in your head. Planning it on paper. I have my desk with a giant stack of post-it notes. And what I do every morning is say: what do I need to do today? What would I want to have happened this morning to consider the morning productive? Without getting carried away with how far you want to go or how many things you want to do, because then you'll probably fall short. You can have a specific notebook for that, the cheapest one you can find. And you just write: today I want to spend the morning reading. Or: I want to read a bit and then go shopping and make lunch. Today, for example: check email. Meditate. Film this video. Clean the bathroom. Do the Enso programs. That's what I want to do. That took me, I don't know, a minute to write down. But the act of writing it means that every time I found myself like: ugh, I need to go clean, you say: come on, go clean, otherwise you won't have time to do the programs. Bam. Done. You finish cleaning and say: ugh, I'll open YouTube and see if there's anything interesting. And you say: no. No, I need to do the programs. Bam. Programs.

And it's been tremendously useful to me.

You can also write it the night before. And that's another tool that's helped me a lot to sleep well. Writing the night before what you have to do the next day means you sleep calmly. Because you're not lying there thinking: I need to remember to buy this. All of that is noise in your head. And noise weighs. And noise stops you from relaxing.

So write everything down. I use a ton of paper. Papers that have a little drawing I need to explain to someone and I send them a photo. Then I use the rest of the paper to write the shopping list. Then some thought came to me and I say: well, right here. Many go in the trash. But having it written is essential.

Tip number two: how to avoid procrastination on the computer. Because that's a big monster you have to fight. Because we all know that your phone and your computer are windows to the world. And I'm sure many of you — because it's happened to me — sit down to work and end up watching Netflix. And that's not good.

So, after reading Digital Minimalism by Cal Newport — I recommend it infinitely — along with Reclaiming Conversation by Sherry Turkle and The Shallows by Nicholas Carr, which together make a good set on the philosophy of optimizing the use of new technology — what's worked for me is: every time you turn on the computer, ask, what do I want to accomplish with this? What am I going to do? You write it on a piece of paper: write the university paper, buy sheets on Amazon, check email. Since you have it written down, you know what you have to do. And even if you still open Facebook, since you have the paper in front of you, you'll see the pending tasks and say: no, come on, bye Facebook. Let's keep writing.

And when you're done with those tasks, if you want to use social media, you do it — but by saying: I'm going to use social media. That is, don't finish writing the article and save it and by instinct open YouTube. No. I'm putting a lot of emphasis on doing things with purpose, with constant deliberateness. And if you want to open Facebook, you take your little piece of paper and say: I want to open Facebook. And many times you realize you actually don't. It's a form of: I don't have anything to do right now, I'll waste some time. And no. That's when someone says: actually what I want to do is go train. Why did I open Facebook? To hell with it, I'm going to train.

With the phone it's much harder, because the phone is much more accessible throughout the day. For the phone, you already know my strategies: not having social media apps installed, not using it before leaving the house, keeping it in your bag instead of your pocket, keeping it on a different table than the one you're working at. The video about my phone strategy is out there, it's one of the most popular ones.

And do everything consciously: why do I want to do this? How am I doing it? Am I sure this is what I want, and am I not doing it out of inertia, because it's my default mode?

Planning also doesn't have to make you your own slave. You can say: Sunday is my free day and I'm not doing anything. And you loosely plan Sunday too. You say: Sunday morning I'll go for a walk in the mountains, I'll eat when I get back, and then I'll spend the afternoon reading. Keep it in mind. But don't waste time on social media.

Work time should be work time. Training time, training. Sleep time, sleep. And the time you spend with the people you care about, make the most of it. Enjoy it fully. Be present with them, not on your phone and not thinking about anything else. Enjoy even the silences that happen with friends when you're having beers and you look at each other and a new topic will come eventually. But resist pulling out your phone then. Enjoy even that. And you'll see that life has a wonderful taste. And your relationships improve enormously. And your sleep. And your work. And everything.
