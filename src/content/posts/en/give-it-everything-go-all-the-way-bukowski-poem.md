*If you're going to try, go all the way. Otherwise, don't even start. This could mean losing girlfriends, wives, relatives, jobs and maybe your mind. It could mean not eating for three or four days. It could mean freezing on a park bench. It could mean jail. It could mean derision, mockery, isolation. Isolation is the gift. All the others are a test of your endurance, of how much you really want to do it. And you'll do it, despite rejection and the worst odds. And it will be better than anything else you can imagine. If you're going to try, go all the way. There is no other feeling like that. You will be alone with the gods, and the nights will flame with fire. Do it. Do it. Do it all the way. You will ride life straight to perfect laughter. It's the only good fight there is.*

That's the poem. It's a poem by Charles Bukowski called "Roll the Dice." It's a poem that hits me, and I wanted to recite it.

The message it carries is the importance of one hundred percent. The thesis the poem defends is that if you're going to do it, do it one hundred percent. And if not, don't do it. And that the good things in life, the things that are worth it, can only be achieved when you commit one hundred percent to them.

There are many people who know they want to do something and don't do it because they're afraid of committing one hundred percent. And what generates that fear is the possibility that, giving it everything, it might not work out. Because if you don't fully commit, if you don't give one hundred percent, you always have the excuse: it's not that it didn't work out, it's that I didn't give it my all. And a lot of people prefer not to give it their all so they can keep that excuse. So they don't have to face the possibility that, having given everything, it still didn't work out.

I have a thesis to write. And there's a moment where, to write the thesis, you have to commit one hundred percent. And when I say one hundred percent I mean that, for a week and a half, you get up at eight in the morning, shower, get dressed, sit at the desk and don't get up until seven in the evening. Phone off. No distractions. With one objective: finish this.

And there are people who can't do that. Or rather, who don't want to do that. Because that's what the poem says: it could mean losing girlfriends, wives, relatives, jobs, maybe your mind. It could mean not eating for three or four days. It could mean not sleeping enough.

Commitment has a cost. And that cost is real. And the question is: are you willing to pay it?

The answer, for many people, for many things, is no. And that's fine. Not everyone has to commit one hundred percent to everything. But if you really want something, if there's something in your life you feel is important, that's yours, that's what you want to do, then you have no choice but to give it everything.

The dragon metaphor. There's a moment in life when you have to face your dragon. Your dragon is that thing that scares you, that you know you have to do, that you've been putting off. And the difference between the people who get what they want and those who don't is that the first ones face their dragon. They stand in front of it and say: okay, this has to be done. And they do it.

And the paradox is that when you commit one hundred percent to something, when you say yes to that, you're paradoxically saying no to many other things. And that's scary. But that's also what makes it real.

Isolation is the gift, says the poem. Isolation is the gift. And it's right. When you're alone with your work, your project, your goal, without distractions, without noise, something special happens. The mind concentrates. Time becomes yours. And the work flows in a way it doesn't flow any other way.

And suddenly. All the existential emptiness disappears. Because you're giving everything for a fight you believe is worth it.
