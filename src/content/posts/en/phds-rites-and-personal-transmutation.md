A few weeks ago I successfully defended my doctoral thesis. As befits such an occasion, I decided to throw a party. But at the same time I went to certain celebrations for other friends who also defended their theses in the weeks before mine. Some of them were reluctant to make a big event out of it, and it was only through social pressure that we ended up celebrating such a significant moment.

When you finish a doctoral thesis, it can mark a major change in a person's life. And many of us accept that major changes need to be accompanied by some kind of event. Why that is got me thinking about a topic I've already touched on here before: rites. Rites, ceremonies, the moments in life when you need to make a psychological transition to a new level. The need to make these transitions through a celebration or a ceremony helps us make that transition stick, it helps with commitment, and it helps us psychologically with change.

My personal example. Finishing the doctoral thesis came with a mix of satisfaction and joy at work well done, relief at being able to step away from a relationship with my supervisor that had been slowly souring over the last few years, and a lot of happiness at having finished. But at the same time — and those of you who have finished a degree, a master's, or a doctorate will know this — there's fear and anticipation about what comes next. Suddenly the decision of where you're going is in your hands. Up until now you had something to do, every day you had an objective, you were fully invested in it, and when the end comes the question inevitably surfaces: so what now?

In my case I've been doing physics for 13 years and I've decided, for reasons I'll explain in another video, to leave academia. And the question emerges: who is Pau without physics? Who is this person? Who will they be in two or three years? Will it be someone who keeps following this trajectory, who is trying to be the best possible version of themselves? Or will it be a rocket that fails mid-air and ends up having a normal life? Well, it's in my hands right now. And to help us in these situations, I think it's very important to have rites.

Even though we consider ourselves a secular society, in many ways — not mythological, supposedly — these are so integrated into our psyche that they emerge in our society in different forms. Hence the massive success of TV series, fantasy literature, films. All these forms of fantastical narrative tell us myths, tell us stories we already knew that help orient us in life. And they do it in a different way than self-help books, which tell you what you need to do. Rites and stories are a little more subtle. They're a caress, an idea that lets you understand the character emotionally, empathize, and move forward.

A rite is some kind of ceremony or event that, consciously or unconsciously, helps us make a psychological transition to a different stage. This is illustrated very well by a wedding. A wedding is a transition from the individual to the couple. What's being said isn't just that we're going to celebrate love, but that it's a social declaration: before we were two individuals, from here on we are a couple. We make a public commitment in a big, serious event that carries enormous economic, psychological and emotional costs. Why not just say "we're a couple" and leave it at that? Because all that investment — spending months planning the wedding and making it a public event — is what helps us make that change, to become that new person.

Changing, stopping being the person you were in order to become a new person, is always a painful process of grieving. And that opening up toward what might come brings fear, brings doubts about whether you'll be capable of getting where you want to go. It's very comfortable, and very common, to refuse to make that transition. To refuse to make the transition into adulthood — which is why many societies have coming-of-age rites: it's a way of socially declaring that from here on we're going to treat you as an adult and there's no going back.

Rites are a social thing because you're showing yourself that you're not alone, that you're part of a community. One of the things that moved me most when I held the event for my defense — where people came from Spain, from France, from all sorts of social circles — is that it demonstrates the importance of these rites: subconsciously we know it's something important and it's somewhere we need to be for the people who matter to us. And those people support you and show you that whatever you are in a few years, wherever you go, whoever you transform into, they're going to be there supporting you. That's something truly precious.

For me, being conscious of the rite — being aware of what it means, where I'm going — and celebrating that psychological transition has helped enormously. It can be big or small. It can be a major event like a wedding, or something smaller like a dinner with friends when you hit a goal you've been chasing for a long time. Another example is those people who take a big trip to find themselves after a painful situation. That's a rite of passage: an event outside the normal that carries us to that new stage of psychological evolution.

Identify and embrace the rites. Or, to put it more lightly: allow yourself to celebrate the good occasions. Allow yourself to go out for dinner when something goes well. Allow yourself to invite all your people to a weekend together when one of you has gone through a breakup. Allow yourself that connection with the mythology we carry inside. And you'll find that it takes you to new aspects of your personality, new horizons, that will help you grow and know yourself better.
