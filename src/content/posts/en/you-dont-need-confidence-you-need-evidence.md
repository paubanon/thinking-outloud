Good morning, everyone. Today I wanted to talk about a distinction that I think is very important when it comes to building confidence. I got the title from an article called "I Don't Have Confidence, I Have Evidence."

A lot of people have sometimes labeled me as arrogant, self-centered, "acts like he knows everything." And other people have said the opposite: that I have a lot of self-confidence, that I go around as if I know things are going to work out. Two sides of the same coin.

If you're on the path of self-improvement, you'll have noticed that you are your best investment. That means paying a lot of attention to yourself, and for a lot of people that's morally wrong. Paying attention to yourself and loving yourself is bad. They'll label you as selfish. Accept it and learn to know whose criticism to consider and whose to let go. Sometimes, honestly, it's come from my own family. And I've said: no, you're not right.

We humans tend to think that others know more than we do, even about ourselves. It's a very fine line between listening to others and listening to yourself. But that wasn't exactly what I wanted to talk about today.

Today I wanted to talk about self-confidence when it comes to facing anything that scares you. Mark Manson talks about this really well, and it's especially clear in the world of dating.

There's one type of confidence that is bluster: without knowing anything, believing things are going to go well because you're special, superior, divine. The ones who go through life praising themselves until they're brought back to earth and fail catastrophically. Because they've built up so many expectations, the crash buries them.

But then there's another type of confidence. One that isn't blind faith, but evidence.

When I was about to take an exam, I knew I was going to get an 8 or a 9. Not because I thought I was super intelligent and others weren't, but because I'd looked at my track record throughout the course. I knew how I'd worked, the time I'd put into the material, how the checkpoints had gone. So I knew, roughly, what I was going to get. And when the exam was over, I knew how it had gone. It wasn't blind faith in myself. It was confidence based on facts.

The same happens when you've been investing in yourself for a while. When you see that all the projects you start move forward, that everything you set out to do you're gradually achieving, you start building a track record. You finish high school with a good grade, you get into the degree you want, you finish the degree in four years, you stick to training, you improve your diet, you meditate. When you've achieved a lot of things you set out to do, you'll achieve the next one too. Because you're the type of person who puts in what's needed for things to work out, or who analyzes problems and looks for solutions until they do.

A while back, when I was talking about the rough patches I went through before coming to Germany, I mentioned something about this. The first and second ones were pretty catastrophic. But later on, instead of thinking "I'm not doing well, I'm depressed, I'm going to feel terrible," even though I felt that way, when someone asked how I was doing I'd say: "Honestly yeah, I really miss being there. I love it in Germany, I have so many friends there." Even if I didn't feel that way in that moment. But I knew it was true. I knew I liked being in Germany, that I had friends there, that when I was there I was happy. I just found the moment of leaving Spain and going back hard.

And it's very curious how, by categorically stating out loud what you don't feel, you can start to feel it. Your brain is a bit of a bastard and works retrospectively. If you're depressed but you do everything as if you're not, your brain starts to say: well, I thought we were depressed, but we're going outside to get some sun, seeing friends, laughing, having dinner with family, telling people we're doing well. Maybe I was wrong. Maybe we're happy.

There are quite a few psychological studies on this. For example, how adopting a dominant or confident posture increases testosterone levels and self-confidence. The mind-body link is very strong, and it goes both ways.

Well, the same thing happens with confidence.

I noticed it especially when it came to talking to someone I found attractive. At first, when you want to learn how to seduce, when you have to walk up and talk to someone you don't know with romantic interest, you're genuinely terrified. You think they're going to reject you and that it's going to be the end of the world. You know rationally that nothing bad will happen, but your brain makes you feel like rejection would be devastating.

What opened my eyes was understanding that I didn't feel confident simply because I hadn't done it many times. I didn't have to build confidence first and then do it. I had to do it, expose myself, see what happens when it goes badly, see what happens when it goes well. When your brain has done that 50 times, on the 51st it doesn't care. Because it looks back and says: we've done this 50 times, and even in the worst case it was nowhere near as bad as we thought it could be.

That's confidence based on evidence.

And this cleared up a lot of misconceptions for me, because I used to think: I need to build confidence before I can go talk to people, before I can take this exam, before anything. But when you understand that the cycle goes the other way around — that action comes first and confidence comes as a consequence of your actions — it takes a huge weight off. You no longer have to wait until you're ready. What you have to do is approach with zero confidence, see what happens, and do it many times. Confidence comes after.

And the good thing is that it transfers. When you've built confidence in seduction, in training, in academics, in your diet, in your projects, in your relationships, the next area that comes your way... your brain says: of course it's going to work out, we're superhumans and we've done all of this.

This also reminds me of something I've talked about many times: the motivation cycle. When you understand how the dopaminergic circuit works, you understand that motivation comes after the work, not before. First you set a goal, you work toward it, you advance, and when your brain sees the progress it rewards you to keep working. You have to work without wanting to, and the motivation will come. It's the same with confidence.

Start acting. Take steps. The more steps you take, the more you realize that failing isn't that bad. You fail and your life goes on. A lot of the time you'll realize you're worth more than you think. The pieces will fall into place, and in the end you'll build the type of person you want to be based on acting like that person.

Because, at the end of the day — what's the difference between someone who is something and someone who acts like they are? The inner dialogue is yours alone. If no one else knows about that inner dialogue, how important is it really, no matter how tangible it feels?

Eventually, when you've worked on many areas, when you've successfully tackled many fields, you arrive at something even better: you realize that things are going to work out regardless of how they go. Because you learn to shift the focus from the result to yourself. If you feel like you gave everything and you're at peace with how you acted, the outcome doesn't matter. You know that if something didn't work out, that's okay, you'll know how to act accordingly.

Act. Since you can't control the future, build now the type of person who — whatever circumstances your future self faces — will know how to handle them. And you build that yourself.

Keep going.
