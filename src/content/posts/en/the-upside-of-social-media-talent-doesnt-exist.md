Good morning, men, women, and otherwise. Today, for once, I wanted to talk to you about social media from a positive angle. For once.

Despite criticizing them so much and the fact that emotionally they bother me quite a bit — knowing the implications they have, the addiction they generate, the problems that come with constantly using them, the lack of privacy, the effect they have on your overall mood throughout the day — they have good things about them. And that's the reason I haven't cut them out. If for you they have no good things, cut them. But I think there are things you can get from them while minimizing the negative parts.

For me emotionally they're a disaster, they make me waste time, they make me feel bad. So I use them mainly for information, especially about training and my areas of work. I can't use them constantly. What I do is have the apps installed but not logged in: when I want to go in, I log in. That makes it harder to access out of habit and limits use, especially avoiding going to Instagram or Facebook while you're out and about.

One of the good things social media has is exposure. Being on them, as Fran Pascual rightly says, exposes you. It exposes you as a person to the world, but it also exposes the world to you. You see what's out there, and that can be a tremendous tool for motivation and awareness that there are people who work very hard. People who have, I'll come back to this word, a tremendous amount of talent. And if you want to compete with them you have to put in the work.

Now: that thing I just said — that word — talent doesn't exist. What exists is hard work over many, many, many years. As Richard Feynman said — who is one of my heroes for many reasons — there are no wonderful people. There are only people who have dedicated an enormous amount of time to learning about a problem that interested them. And because it interested them so much, they've put in a number of hours you can't even imagine. And what you see now, that result, looks like they've always been that way. But that's not true.

And I'm increasingly realizing it's not true in any field. The people at the top have simply put in an enormous number of hours. Some people are more capable in certain areas and less in others, but the difference isn't innate talent — it's a predisposition of interest. That a field interested them enough to put in such a huge number of hours that they became exceptional. And you'd be surprised by the number of hours certain fields require. I've been working on my handstand for two or three years and it's still shaky. On a good day I can hold a five-second set. That's with four or five sessions averaging an hour per week. To get a measly five seconds. That's the reality. Now go look at circus artists doing incredible things on their hands. Now look at that person who draws in an astonishing way. How many hours have they spent alone in their room drawing, analyzing charcoal, trying techniques, erasing and redoing?

How many hours have you put into the project you want? With how much obsession?

That's all there is to it. And that's how you can use social media well: to see people out there who have been so obsessed with what they want that they prove the limits of human capacity — both intellectually and physically — are much further than you expected.

The good and bad news is that it's your responsibility. You can no longer say: "It's just how they are," "that person has a special gift for physics," "she just paints well naturally." No. What there is, is people who have put in an enormous number of hours. That's the bad news because it puts the burden on the individual. And that's also the good news.
