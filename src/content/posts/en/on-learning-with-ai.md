Today I want to share a short video about artificial intelligence and its usefulness for learning. The origin of this video is that I've been researching different tools that allow you to synthesize certain content to get summaries, notes, and outlines. I've seen that there are a lot of tools designed to take a class and create a summary, take certain audio or podcasts and summarize them. And even though I think it's a fantastic and super useful tool, I think we have to remind ourselves why we do things.

When making a summary or taking notes or synthesizing certain content, the ultimate goal often isn't to have notes, because most of the time once you have the notes you barely look at them again. The value lies in the fact that you went through that content, digested it, distilled it, broke it down into its most basic constituent elements and organized and reproduced it in your own words. In other words, the goal is not to acquire the final product. It's the process. And the value is in the process, which is where you'll get the most benefit, which is what will make those ideas consolidate in your mind and integrate with existing knowledge.

I've talked about this in the past, in the video about books: the value of books isn't only in getting the information. The value, in large part, is that they present information to you slowly, gradually, repetitively, drilling into the details. That in the process of reading a book you spend two, three weeks, a month, thinking about the same ideas. You read a little in the morning, then you think: oh, how did this idea connect to that other one? Look at this here, and this alongside something I was thinking about in the past. And in that process you integrate knowledge much better than if you're simply presented with ideas quickly.

However well-made a YouTube video is, it's very hard to retain. Because you're giving your brain the signal of: this takes 15 minutes. And if something takes 15 minutes and never appears in your life again, you're not going to retain it, because you're telling your brain it's not something important. However, if you read a 1500-page book about the Ottoman Empire, you'll probably remember a lot more. And it's one of the reasons why I don't use book synthesis tools. There are platforms that offer summaries with the idea of these are the key ideas, you save yourself having to read it, you absorb it in 15 minutes and move on to the next one. And you can "read," in quotes, 50 books in a month. Well, the reality is that reality has no shortcuts. Consolidating knowledge takes time and it takes effort. If you read 50 books in a month with those shortcuts, you're not going to consolidate a damn thing. You're going to have the illusion of having learned a lot, but then you'll lack the ability to construct that discourse within your own thoughts.

And I think AI has a bit of this problem too. In our eagerness to consume more content, in our fear of not being good enough, in our desire to optimize time, we fall into this spiral of the more content I consume the better. Without stopping to think that we're not assimilating the content, we're not stopping to process it and integrate it with our own ideas, because it's so fast and so abundant that it doesn't last more than the fraction of a second we're paying attention to it.

So, when we want to integrate knowledge from a book, from a documentary, from class notes, we have to stop and think about what we want. Do we want the final result? Fine, AI is fantastic for that: synthesize, polish, make you a draft to work on and in half an hour you have notes done. Great. But do you want to integrate that information into yourself? Do you want to be able to generate that synthesis of ideas yourself? Do you want to properly connect those points with the ones already in your mind? Then there's no shortcut that works. What you have to do is read the content, underline it, annotate it, digest it, regurgitate it, write it again. If possible, you write it, you talk about it. All of that takes time, but it's the only possible way to truly learn.
