It had been a while since I'd published anything, but a few days ago I realized I'd been meditating for a thousand consecutive days and I think that's a good excuse to make a video about it. A while back I published a video on this when I was about a year in. Now that it's been three, it feels like a good moment to update my thoughts, my ideas, and share some new tools I've picked up along the way.

Personally I find that meditating has been one of the most important tools for learning to regulate my emotional and inner state, for increasing my productivity both at work and personally, and above all for helping me find direction in life. What do I want to do? Where do I want to go? And as stupid as it sounds, sometimes all you have to do is sit down and listen to what your head is telling you.

I want to start with mysticism. I'm a very scientific, very rational person, and I'm about as mystical as a potato. Meditation has to be approached with an open mind, free of prejudice. It's an exercise. A mental exercise, yes, but an exercise. It's not going to lead you to some kind of psychological transcendence. It can change your personality, but no more than starting to train or changing your diet. What you get out of it depends on you.

That's why I find a lot of traditional meditation texts pompous, hard to understand, and not very useful for actually showing what the practice is. I want this video to be my small contribution to cutting through that fog of mysticism.

I started meditating back in 2014, 2015. It was exam season and things were very hectic at university — we were completely overloaded, and I could feel my brain running at way higher revs than it could handle. My anxiety was pretty high and no matter what I did, I was always behind. There were always more problem sets to hand in, more lab reports, more things to study, and I was always, always behind. And I could feel it was unsustainable. I needed something to tell my brain: hey, stop. That's enough. Time is what it is. Let's do what we can. That's it.

I found meditation, sat down, did a basic meditation, and thought: wow, I feel great. And since then meditation has been more or less a constant. It came and went. Sometimes it was there, sometimes it wasn't. Until about four years ago I decided: every day. And that was that.

What is meditation? I think it's also important to say that meditation is not a relaxing practice. Its goal is not relaxation. Something whose goal is relaxation, like going to a spa, is not meditation. Meditation is an exercise. One of the consequences of the exercise might be that you end up more relaxed and handle stress better, but that's not the main point.

From my perspective, meditative practice is nothing more than a practice of conscious attention control. It's identifying where your attention is, and the exercise — the rep, if you want to call it that — is taking that attention and putting it where you want it. And what you want is generally not what your brain wants by default.

There's also an important therapeutic aspect. When you sit down to meditate and observe what's inside, you're going to realize a lot of things you weren't aware of. A response you gave someone and you suddenly think: I was kind of a jerk there. Something your boss said and you realize it affected you more than you thought. Or things from years ago can come up. The psychological literature is pretty clear on this: approach these thoughts with compassion and curiosity. You don't have to beat yourself up. You don't have to say: I'm a bad person and I handled this wrong. It's more: this is what's there, this is what happened, I'm going to try to make peace with it. And in a curious way, if there's something you're a little afraid to face, it's important to do it — but slowly. Give yourself time.

What do I get out of this practice? The first thing is that I've learned to identify distractions. At first it only happens while you're meditating, but little by little over time you learn to identify impulses: you're working and there's an urge to pick up your phone. And you learn to identify it and say: not now. And you bring your attention back to what you were doing. Or you say: do I actually want to pick up my phone? And you realize that actually you want to take a break because you've been working for a long time and what you want is to go outside and walk. Or sometimes you say: I want to pick up my phone because I don't want to do this because it's hard.

Also emotional impulses: a snappy response comes out impulsively, or aggression or irritation, and as it emerges you identify it and say: is this how I want to react to this situation? And you learn to give a different response. You learn to be more aware of when you're having a really happy moment and you're genuinely lucky. You learn to put things in perspective a bit when you're having a bad time.

Since I started meditating I argue a lot less. And I was already someone who argued little. But it's very hard to make me lose my temper emotionally. Something can irritate me, something can anger me, but you'll almost never get an uncontrolled emotional reaction from me that I later say: I regret acting that way.

You also learn that the world is full of things to do: endless tasks waiting. When you finish one there are ten thousand more. You could live in the queue of "I need to do more things" until the day you die. I think that's how a lot of people live, but I don't think it's the ideal way to live. You learn that you can sit and do nothing and that it's in your hands. You learn that there are lots of things to do and that it's you who has to say: yes, there's more, but right now I'm choosing to do this.

A good exercise once you're familiar with meditation: when you're expecting friends over, imagine you've got everything ready and there are still fifteen or twenty minutes before the guests arrive. Instead of picking up your phone or starting to do something else, just sit and wait. And when you do it, you'll notice a ton of small things, a ton of small thoughts, anxiety emerging. Have they run late and aren't telling me? Maybe they can't find the buzzer? I could be more productive... You realize how the brain doesn't know how to stay still.

And there are also a lot of things in our heads we're not aware of. There have been times I've woken up in a bad mood without knowing why. And I sit down to think, look inward, and say: ah, because of that thing that happened. Or this situation is frustrating me more than I thought. And when you identify that, when you sit with it and think about how to change your relationship with that situation to make it better — that's where I think it becomes a tremendously important tool for improving your life.

Tools for meditating? First: guided or unguided. Guided meditation is very useful and it's important to start with something guided. The Medito app has meditations in Spanish and is a good tool for beginners. That said, when you do a guided meditation, if you only return to the focus of attention when the voice tells you to, you're not practicing identifying when you're distracted. Guided meditations are fine, but they need to be complemented with unguided meditations to learn to be the one who says: hey, you were distracted, come back.

On sticking with it: my advice is super simple. Commit to yourself firmly that it's something you want to do. You can find ten minutes a day from under the couch cushions. There have been times I forgot to meditate, came back from a party at three in the morning and thought: damn, I haven't meditated. And you sit down and do it anyway. And what you get out of that isn't a punishment, it's a lesson: next time do it before you go out.

It's important to have some kind of reminder, like anchoring it to an existing habit. I used to do it before brushing my teeth, or right after training. An app with a persistent notification also helps: one that says "have you meditated today?" and stays on your screen until you tap yes.

Paradoxically I've found it harder to stick to it during relaxed, leisure times. You feel so comfortable that you say: I don't need it right now, I'm fine. And you let it slide. And by the end of the day you've skipped it. High-stress days are almost easier because you're dying to sit for ten minutes and clear your head. Be aware of these patterns.

On when to meditate: whenever you can. That simple. It's much better to do it at a non-ideal time than not to do it because you're trying to do it perfectly. Neuroscience says the prefrontal cortex — the part of the brain that directs conscious attention and willpower — gets tired throughout the day. It's affected by lack of sleep, the number of decisions you've made, and mental fatigue. That's why in the morning it's generally easier to focus. But if in the morning you're rushed or you're eager to get to work, don't force it. Times I personally find especially good: at the end of the morning as a break from the work hours, after training, or before sleep.

I do it before bed. Why? Because it's the only time where it fits without me feeling like I'm wasting time. Is it the best time to meditate? No, definitely not. But it's where it fits in my schedule. And doing it at a non-ideal time is a thousand times better than not doing it because you're trying to do it perfectly.

Meditation is a journey. It requires time, attention, and consistency. It's a mental exercise. Ten minutes a day is much better than one hour a week. Pick the minimum that works for you, commit to it a hundred percent, and start.
