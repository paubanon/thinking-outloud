Good and wonderful morning everyone. I'd been a while without posting things to this channel because to share something, to develop thoughts and have the motivation and the energy to share them, I have to be doing well. And I've been going through a stretch where I haven't been doing well. And in that stretch I've learned a lot. Because it had been a while since I'd been in a low point.

One of the things I've learned is that helping each other costs you nothing. Helping other people isn't something that weighs on you, isn't something that drains you. When you genuinely give it's a game where one plus one is more than two. I know this because lately in my close circles a lot of people have been doing badly. A lot of people have been depressed, going through personal difficulties. I know many of you probably are too because we're in a very difficult situation.

What I've discovered is that when I had a conversation with a friend where that person was doing badly and I was doing badly, we ended the conversation and we were both doing better. Helping each other doesn't subtract. It only adds. And even if you're both in the hole, talking about it and sharing it and supporting each other adds.

I wanted to make this video to add to you. I've been thinking about it for a while. And I wanted to share the strategies and tools I've developed to deal with moments of depression, of low points, and those moments when life feels like it's going more uphill than downhill.

The first one, number zero. Open up to the people you love. Have people around you and open up to the fact that you're not doing well. You'll be surprised how much they love you and support you. And you doing it opens the door for them to do it too. And when you both do it, you create a stronger relationship. Because you know that person is going to be there for the hardest moments. And I also recommend being there for others. Genuinely, it's a very hard time for everyone. Let's support each other and love each other.

At a practical level, the first thing is to take care of yourself. Take care of yourself and love yourself a lot. Do things carefully, patiently, and well for yourself. Take long, careful baths. Stay clean. Shave. Get rid of body hair. Dress well every day. Cook good meals. Go out to eat if you need to. And don't hesitate to spend money and effort and resources to feel good, decent. To feel that your room is clean and tidy.

Because we tend to think when we're in a low that we don't deserve any of that. And that's not true. When we're in a low we usually think we're worthless. And we have to convince ourselves that we are worth something to get out of there. And the best way to do that is with practical steps. You're not going to feel like it. Probably none of what I say here you're going to feel like doing. But all of these things help to convince yourself that you're okay. That you're a valuable individual. And that it's worth getting out of there.

The second point is to get out of the house. As much as possible. The outdoors works wonders. Every time you feel sad, tell yourself: I'm going to be sad outside. Go for walks. Make plans with people. Walk the dog three more times than you normally would. If you have nothing to do, leave the house. Work somewhere other than home. Shop at the furthest supermarket you can find. Take advantage of doing those things outside the house that you wouldn't do otherwise. Go fix your bike. Spend an entire morning outside.

Nature, fresh air and feeling yourself in a place where there are people around you, even if you're not interacting with them, like a gym or a café. Even if you're alone there, you don't feel alone. And low points are much easier to carry when you walk. Much easier.

Point three is: do what you have to do even when you don't feel like it. Given that your motivation is going to be through the floor when you're in a low, you can end the morning saying: I have absolutely no desire to work. But the options in front of me are to end the morning just as depressed as I started it, or to end the morning just as depressed, but also having done things that are worth something. So even if you don't feel like doing anything at all, sit down and work on your projects. Whatever project you want. But do things that when you look back are worth something. Because our brain is convinced that if you dedicate time to things that advance your life, your brain says: maybe things aren't so bad. At least this aspect of our life is moving forward. And that's going to give you, very little by little, positive feedback that's going to help you get out of the hole.

Every time you notice depression lurking around the corner, say: no, I'm going to focus on something. I'm going to direct my attention to a book I want to read, something I want to write, my work, developing anything. Focus your attention on something. The worst thing you can do is wander aimlessly around the house.

Point four: train. And train hard. Leave it all out there. You won't feel like it at all. You'll want to stay home turning into a lump on the couch. Don't do it. Go. Do sprints. Do squats. Finish completely exhausted. That's going to help you get out of the house, you'll be in a relatively social environment, not to mention the endorphins you're going to produce when you finish. Plus it's going to make you take care of yourself because you're investing effort and time in your health. It lets you focus on training and not think about other things. And it's going to help you sleep better. And sleeping better is one of the best tools you have for your brain to deal with life's dramas.

And point five is: be at peace with not being okay. Don't try to pretend you're fine. You're not fine. Okay. That's what's called for right now. People are not doing well sometimes. If you're like me, always fighting for your things and moving, you're going to feel very strange feeling bad. But that's what's called for now. Accept it. Look at yourself with introspection and say: okay, now it's time to get out of here. Little by little, with all these steps, with the help of the people who love me, we're going to get out. But right now we're not okay. We're not going to deny it.

Outside of this list what has also helped me a lot is not using my phone too much. Setting the phone aside as much as possible. So that when I'm doing something, I'm doing that thing. If I'm cooking, I'm cooking. If I'm writing, I'm writing. If I'm reading, I'm reading. And if I'm at home with nothing to do, I don't have my attention waiting on the outside world. I'm here in my house with myself. That has helped me a lot.

Meditating, of course. If you've never meditated, it can be a slightly hard moment to start. It might feel scary to face those things inside you. But precisely in facing them, in ruminating on them, turning them over, is where you gradually learn how you are, how you feel, what you want. And little by little you'll get out of the hole. Like we all do.

As I said, giving costs you nothing. And if with this small gesture and this small effort I can help any one of you, on some day, lighten a difficult moment even slightly, it's absolutely worth it. We're in a very hard time for everyone. Many people very separated from the people they love, from their loved ones, from everything. Let's love each other a little.
