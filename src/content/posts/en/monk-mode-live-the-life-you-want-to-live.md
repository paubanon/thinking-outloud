I just finished recording the previous video, and I was thinking about the topic of the primitive brain, high-level brain, low-level brain, and I realized that in large part what monk mode is, is exactly that. It's practices for building the brain you want to have, for building the brain that responds to impulses the way you want it to. To have a brain that, whatever the day-to-day decisions are, says: is this what we want to do? Yes, then we do it fully, we do it enjoying it 100%, we go all in. But if it's not what we want to do, we're capable of, first and hardest, identifying that that isn't what we want to do. And second, once we've identified it, taking the necessary actions to control ourselves and say: that's not what I want, step back, my life is going in this direction.

Part of the great value of monk mode is in creating a very high-level brain, a brain that has all the primary impulses under control. Which doesn't mean stopping having impulses, doesn't mean stopping having emotions, as a lot of people think. Quite the opposite. It means having those emotions, and when you want to have them, you have them fully. You're having sex with someone you want, so you enjoy it and give everything. You're having coffee with a lifelong friend, so you're completely present, appreciating the moment: damn, I feel good and how lucky am I to be here with this person. Completely in the moment, your brain isn't working. When you're working, you're working completely focused. When you're resting, you're resting. When you're training, you're training.

And all of that is achieved through abstinence. Abstinence is forcing your primitive brain, teaching it that it can't have everything it wants. You'd better decide carefully what it is you want. Because most things aren't going to be aligned with what you truly want. Most will be instant stimuli. And what you truly want to achieve in your life isn't achieved with instant stimuli. It's achieved with hard, consistent work over a long period of time.

And it gives so much satisfaction because you see that you're moving toward very high and very distant goals, but you can see they're reachable. Because all it takes is to keep working. When you train your brain to say: we're going to cut out masturbation, and when we do it it'll be because we want to. We're going to learn to fast. And when we eat, we'll eat what we truly want to eat. Then we'll ask: what do we want to eat? And we'll know that the food that isn't best for our system doesn't take us toward our ultimate goal of being the healthiest person we can be. When we sleep we pay attention to sleep because we know it's important for our long-term goals. We don't succumb to the primitive instinct of I want to eat sugar, of I want to stay home and masturbate all day, of I don't want to sit down and write, I want to scroll Instagram until I die. That's what your primitive brain says. What your high-level brain says is: that doesn't take us where we want to go.

The first thing you have to do is identify where you want to go. And then create a route to get there. And then train your brain so that today it's capable of making the decisions that put you on track toward where you want to end up.

And that's what this whole thing is about. It's not about: look how cool I am, I've got this Japanese aesthetic. No. It's about knowing that life is hard and creating the type of person you want to be. And to do that you have to go through the abstinence of things. Why? Because it's the only way to teach. Abstinence can be structured: nofap, eliminating social media or heavily restricting it, fasting. But abstinence has another level, which is the daily level. Those big abstinences are big, but the daily abstinence is saying: I want a chocolate bar, I know, I noticed I want it, but I'm not going to eat it. I want to open Facebook, I want to open it, but I'm not going to. And you sit down and try to work again. Those small decisions are what teach your brain. Now is the time to work. Little by little.

And over time you build that brain. Because imagine the work it takes to write a book. Think about it. Think that you're an author and you have a great idea, a brilliant idea. Maybe some of you have a project you say: if I actually pulled this off it would make me a millionaire. And you never pull it off. Which is totally normal. The book Originals said that the difference between people who make a difference isn't that they have great ideas, it's that they act on them. And that implies that almost everyone has very good ideas, but very few of us are capable of putting in the necessary effort to carry them out.

Imagine writing a book. What does it require? It requires every day, for months, it can be a year, years. Every day. Setting aside everything else you have to do. Sitting down. You alone with your thoughts. And writing them out. And rewriting them. And rereading them. And crossing things out and correcting. And facing the blank page. Over and over and over again. Every day.

Think about Brandon Sanderson, for example. He has an insane number of books, books of 1500 pages. Think about how long it takes to write 1500 pages. Not just to write them. Think about the work behind creating a plot structure that's coherent. How many times you have to write it. How many times you have to rewrite it. How many hours you've had to spend alone doing that, and nothing else.

Think about your brain right now. If you watch this channel, you probably have a brain like 99% of the population today, which is programmed to jump from one task to another. With a brain like that it's impossible to write a book. And the same if you want to start a business, if you want to go far in your career. You're not going to get there if you don't learn to sit down and work. That's the key to achieving most goals. For that not to be agony, you have to train your brain. So that what sets you apart from being a primate knows how to say: we're going to do only those things that align with our highest goals and ideals. And we're going to work for that.

And the great thing about this is that it has a cumulative effect. The more focused you work, the more you progress. The more you progress, the more motivated you are to work. Because you can see you're advancing. Since you're more motivated, you put in the hours it takes. When you're doing what you're doing, and not something else, you're more productive. And it spreads to different areas of your life.

Something as simple as deleting Instagram from your phone. Leaving your phone on a different table from where you're working. Not consciously opening Facebook. And you don't need app blockers.
