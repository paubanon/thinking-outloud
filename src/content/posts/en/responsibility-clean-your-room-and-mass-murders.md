Jordan Peterson has a phrase that a lot of people find ridiculous at first: "clean your room." And yet I think there's something very deep in it.

The idea is this: before trying to fix the world, fix what's immediately within your control. Your room. Your life. Your immediate surroundings. Because if you can't keep your own room in order, what moral authority do you have to tell others how to organize the world?

This connects with something Peterson develops extensively around mass murders and the figure of the "resentful man" — the man who blames the world for his problems and reaches a point where he decides that if he's going to suffer, everyone is going to suffer. It's not a new phenomenon, but it's become more visible.

What distinguishes these people, in Peterson's reading, isn't evil in the abstract sense, but the rejection of responsibility. The refusal to say: my life is this way partly because I've made these choices, and I can make different choices.

It's much more comfortable to blame others. Blame society, the system, women, the rich, whoever. Because if the blame is external, you don't have to change anything. You're a victim.

And there's a trap in that. Because being a victim exempts you from responsibility, but it also strips you of agency. If everything is everyone else's fault, you can't do anything. If, on the other hand, you acknowledge that part of what happens to you is a consequence of your own decisions — that hurts more, but it also means you can change it.

Cleaning your room is a symbol. It's the first concrete step. It's saying: in this one small thing within my control, I'm going to create order. And expand from there.

It's not that the world doesn't have problems. It does. But you change them from the center outward, not from the periphery inward. Start with yourself.
