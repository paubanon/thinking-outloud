I want to talk about something I find curious: the coexistence of two apparently contradictory narratives about men in contemporary culture.

On one hand, we have the Mr. Wonderful culture — that pastel aesthetic of motivational phrases that celebrates vulnerability, crying without shame, talking about feelings, being "a new man." Masculinity understood as softness, as emotional openness.

On the other hand, there's an implicit expectation that men be strong, capable, that they don't complain, that they solve problems. That they be providers, protectors. Masculinity understood as toughness and competence.

And these two narratives coexist simultaneously in the same culture, sometimes in the same individual, without anyone seeming uncomfortable with the contradiction.

That's cognitive dissonance. Holding two contradictory beliefs at the same time without resolving them.

The problem is that when men try to live by the first narrative they're often criticized for being soft or weak. And when they try to live by the second they're criticized for being macho or incapable of expressing emotions. It's a dead end.

I think the solution isn't to choose between the two extremes but to integrate them. A man can be emotionally intelligent and also capable and strong. He can cry when he needs to cry and also be a rock when someone needs that. Vulnerability and strength aren't opposites.

But getting to that integration requires thinking clearly, without getting swept up by trends. And that requires a certain discomfort, because it means not fitting perfectly into any of the available categories.

Masculinity, like anything else that's complex, doesn't fit in a meme.
