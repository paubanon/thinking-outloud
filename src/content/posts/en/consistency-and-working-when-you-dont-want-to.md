Good morning everyone. It's been a while since I stopped by here and I have a couple of stories I want to tell you.

The first one. Yesterday I was training in a calisthenics park and I ran into someone I know from the gym. We started talking and at some point he mentioned that the day before he'd smoked an enormous amount of weed and that day he was feeling blah, not much energy, and so he'd gone to train to clear his head a bit. This was already around 8 or 9 at night.

Anyway, we ended up talking a bit about training every day, about how to approach the day to day. I mentioned that in the end everything is a source of stress. If you have stress from work, studies, family, relationships, whatever, that stress adds up. And that training is another source of stress and that if there's a lot of stress on one side, sometimes you have to ease up on the sources of stress you can consciously control.

He said yeah, that's what I do, and more or less each day I train based on how I feel. And I said: well, not quite. I mean, one thing is consciously modulating the stress level, but if you train based on what you feel like, the truth is you're going to end up almost never training. Or you're going to end up doing a quarter of what you could do. Because the truth is the vast majority of times I go to train, you go without wanting to. Why? Because training, even though it's true it makes you feel better, is hard, it's difficult. What you do training is voluntarily put yourself in a stress situation and you improve. And in the end you get a certain reward of: I did something difficult and hard and something I didn't want to do, and I feel good about myself for having done it. But as a feeling, you're tired. And the body doesn't like getting tired.

This reminded me of something I'd been thinking about in recent weeks, because I'd been listening to certain talks by Joe Rogan and Jocko Willink on discipline. And there was a phrase I really liked: 90% is doing it. That is, not doing it when you feel like it. 90% of the work, 90% of what you achieve is: whether I want to or not, I show up and do it.

You see, I've realized one of my great strengths is consistency. I'm an extremely rational person and I'm very consistent. I take on an objective and I walk, and walk, and walk. And in the end you get there. But I walk many times without wanting to. And that consistency is what gets you there.

Imagine you want to write a book. 90% of writing a book is: I get up, I make myself a coffee, I sit down and write. Three hours, four hours every day. Some days will be wonderful, some days will be terrible. But every day you sit down. Every single day. And there's a point at which you don't even ask yourself whether you feel like it or not.

Asking yourself whether you feel like something that you have to do, or that you want to do at a deeper level, is stupid. If you've decided you want to do a handstand press, it doesn't matter whether you feel like it or not. You've set the goal. Do you want to take it seriously? You show up and go. I'm tired. I show up and go. I don't feel like it today. I show up and go. I'm on top of the world today. I show up and go. It doesn't matter how you feel. You show up and go. And you put in the sets. Consistently. Over the days, the weeks, the months. And that's how goals are achieved.

Picture other aspects of your life, like work. Or brushing your teeth. You don't ask yourself: do I feel like going to work today or not? Am I going to go to work based on how I feel? You don't do that. You get up, grab your stuff and go. And thanks to that your financial situation holds up. Things work when you do what you have to do, whether you feel like it or not.

There will be days that are wonderful and you'll set records. And there will be days that are truly awful. But you go. And at the end of the week, the months, the years, when people see the result, they don't see the thousands of days you went to train without wanting to train. They don't see the thousands of hours you sat down to write without wanting to write. They don't see the thousands of days you got up without wanting to get up. They only see the result.

The second story I wanted to tell you has to do with something that happened a few days ago. I was with a friend and we were talking about relationships and I was saying that sometimes people have the impression that relationships are easy. And we were talking about how in relationships, just like in everything else, you have to work. And that you don't always feel like being with your partner. You don't always feel like making the concessions you have to make. But if you do it consistently, if you put in the effort consistently, long-term the result is a better relationship. And that's no different from training, no different from writing. It's the same logic.

So that's it. 90% is showing up and starting. Period.
