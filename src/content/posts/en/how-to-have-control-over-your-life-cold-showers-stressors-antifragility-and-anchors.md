I was in the shower thinking. I was taking a cold shower, and well, you don't know... right now I'm living in Germany and it's November, and outside it's about 6, 4 degrees, which makes the water pretty cold. Taking advantage of that I've been experimenting with the Wim Hof method of cold exposure, breathing techniques and all that.

And I've realized that what all the things that make us feel good long-term have in common, everything that makes us stronger, all the training methodologies, all the things that promise health and wellbeing, all of it is based on one principle: antifragility. Those of you who have read Taleb already know what that is, but for those who haven't, antifragility is simply taking advantage of those systems that, when given a certain dose of stress, don't just hold up — they actually improve. An example is the human body, which if you isolate it doesn't stay the same, it gets worse. But on the other hand, if you give it stressors — and that's the key, in the right dose — the body keeps adapting, getting stronger and stronger, more resilient.

So what all the methods that promote health have in common — cold exposure, fasting, training — all of them consist of stressors, of making yourself suffer, of putting you through it. And then, apart from the physiological changes that happen, psychologically they create a very important shift: you realize what you're capable of.

Damn, if I got in the shower and stayed under cold water for a full five minutes, afterwards you feel very in control because you managed the situation. And when a problem shows up in your life, you'll know how to handle it. And that's something I've always said training has given me a lot: the ability to take a big problem, break it into little pieces, and say, okay, to get here I need to do this, this, this — one step, another, another, another. And I think that's what matters.

Same with fasting. Fasting, apart from all the hormonal benefits it has, gives you the feeling of: I'm in control, I'm not a slave to hunger. If one day I can't eat, if I skip a meal, it doesn't matter. And that makes you feel good, and psychologically too, because you feel powerful and you feel capable of overcoming one challenge and the next.

It's not that there's one discipline that's magic for the human body, or that cold exposure is especially wonderful, or that fasting is the magic pill. It's not like that. There's never a magic one. The benefit is simply getting out of what's comfortable and starting to expose yourself to uncomfortable things, first in a controlled way — cold showers, start training, throw in a fast.

Because later, when you have things stressing you out that aren't under your control — work, studies — you'll say: if I had control there, I'll have it here too. And you don't stress out, and you learn to deal with it much better. And that's when it leads to improvements in quality of life, happiness and all that.
