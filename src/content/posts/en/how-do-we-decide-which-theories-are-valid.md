Hey everyone. This is the second part of the series on principles inherent to nature. The first video is the Matthew principle, which is the previous one. At the end of that video I was saying that the Matthew principle, far from being an oppression of the system or something similar, is the way things have developed, and it's that way because it's the only — or most logical — way they could have developed. That's basically an appeal to Occam's razor, or the principle of simplicity in arguments.

Occam's razor — Ockham was, I think, a monk, I can't remember which century — his principle is basically formulated as: no more arguments than necessary should be used to explain a phenomenon. Roughly speaking. In plain terms: the simplest argument is the valid one, or the one we should consider first. And it's a principle that seems very innocent, but it governs all of our society. It governs the design of every physical theory we have and governs our understanding of the world at every level. And that in turn governs how society is built.

When you're making a theory, you basically need to invent something that explains the world to you. And the first thing you might think is: no matter how complex the world is, I can always make a theory with even more complexity that explains it. That is, I could use a theory in which the world we live in has 27 dimensions and I can explain absolutely everything. Well, Occam's razor tells you that's not valid. That you have to use the simplest possible argument, because it's the one closest to the truth.

The first thing is that your theory has to explain reality. If it doesn't, we throw it out immediately. But given different theories, the simpler one has a higher probability of being true and of making better predictions. If it's necessary to make a more complicated theory because the simpler one turns out to already be very complicated, then so be it. General relativity is tremendously complicated, but it's the only one we have that explains all the gravitational phenomena we can observe. So for now that's what there is. If we had a simpler theory that explained the same thing or more, we'd throw general relativity out. Literally pick it up and toss it.

Maybe some of you are saying: this has nothing to do with me. It should, because as I said, it's a deep truth inherent to reality. And it connects to another video I made a while back: assume ignorance before malice. In that video I argued that when someone does something that offends you, something that bothers you, you have to try to think about what most likely happened. And what's most likely, generally, is that person didn't realize the offense they were causing you. Rather than assuming they're a bad person and holding it against them. The idea that you should think in terms of the simplest possible arguments is, like the Matthew principle, transversal to nature.

It seems inherent to the way the human being is designed, or any animal. We don't have more muscles than we need. We have no muscles, bones, insertions, organs, proteins, or hormones to spare. None. Everything has a purpose. And every time we've tried to say something is unnecessary, we've realized it isn't. The appendix, for example. It seems to be a vestigial remnant. You remove it and you realize it serves as a reserve of microbiota, or something like that. I don't remember exactly. But it's necessary, even though it's prone to infections.

And I've heard many people say: the knee is poorly designed. How dare you say the knee is poorly designed? There have been billions of years of natural selection to get the knee you have. Maybe you're not using the knee the way it was meant to be used. In that sense it's poorly designed, because design goes hand in hand with the need to perform a function. If you change the function, you should change the design. But you don't have an extra ligament. You have nothing you could say: this serves no purpose. Even the little toe is essential. If you lose your little toe, you lose your balance. Everything we have is there for a reason.

Nothing in nature is superfluous. Every time we say this protein doesn't do anything, that's not true. You remove it and suddenly eczema appears on the skin, or the immune system goes haywire. A very typical unresolved example is when identifying DNA: it's said that 30 or 40% is useful DNA and the rest is junk DNA. That it's vestigial, that it's not active. How dare you say 70% of DNA is junk? If it's there, it has a function. You can be sure of it. If it didn't, it would have been discarded. Why? Because if you had an unnecessary muscle, that muscle consumes energy. And that imposes a difficulty in terms of survival. And that difficulty will make you less likely to reproduce. Integrated over billions of years, that translates to: those individuals who stopped having those unnecessary muscles were much more efficient at surviving and therefore survived more. And the same goes for absolutely every structure you can think of.

Structures not only at the physiological level, but mental structures with which we interpret the world. We have the simplest mental structures capable of understanding the world. And it seems that the world in turn operates on the simplest mechanisms necessary for the world to work.

An example could be an ecosystem. What we've discovered by studying ecosystems with complex systems physics is that if you remove one piece — a plant, reduce the amount of water, make a species go extinct, or introduce a species — suddenly the whole system changes. Because there's practically no piece you can remove without the whole system changing. If there were some piece you could remove without the system changing, by simple logic it would have already disappeared, because it has no function. And if something exists and has no function, it gets in the way. It obstructs growth.

So when we develop a physical theory, we have to try to find the minimum premises that make something hold. At the mathematical level this is established as axioms. Axioms are the minimum units of truth we have to assume for everything else to follow. A theorem is nothing more than saying: if we start from these axioms, this happens and this happens and this happens. And from theorems emerge corollaries, lemmas, propositions. Everything emerges from basic truths. And those truths have to be as simple as they can possibly be.

This is why, for example, I'm not fond of string theory. It strikes me as unnecessarily complicated, with eleven dimensions and certain compactified dimensions we can't see. If it were the only theory that lets us explain everything and make predictions about the rest of the world, I'd be the first to say: fair enough, that's what we've got, I'll accept it. But since it seems that's not the case, that there are many more theories, some simpler, trying to explain the same thing, that's where I lean.

The same goes for the dark matter problem. For those who don't know it: basically we calculate the mass of the universe or of galaxies through the light they emit, and then we measure the mass they have based on the speed at which the different rings that form a galaxy rotate. And we see the masses don't match. Mass is missing somewhere. What's been said is: well, there's a particle we'll call dark matter, which only interacts gravitationally, it's there and we can't see or detect it yet. And we've been searching for it for many years with very detailed experiments, and we haven't found it.

Since it's very hard to prove something doesn't exist — because absence of evidence is not evidence of absence — there are other schools of thought that say it's not that there's an invisible matter permeating the entire galaxy, but that our theoretical calculations have some flaw. And by the principle of Occam's razor, it seems much more logical to me to think that our theoretical model is deficient, that something has slipped past us — to err on the side of humans and our understanding of the world — than to search for elements in the world that we don't observe and leave no trace. If dark matter were discovered, I'd be the first to say: it's been measured, case closed, great.

Which is what has happened in other areas of physics, where there have been theoretical predictions, people have searched, and they've said: this theoretical prediction only works if this particle exists, with these characteristics, this mass, this charge. And they searched in those ranges and found it. That's one of the most celebratory moments of the human capacity to understand the world around us. But in the case of dark matter, that doesn't seem to be what's happening.

Occam's razor governs all scientific development. And when you think about it, it's a principle so simple and so elegant that you can't refute it. You can't refute that what moves things forward are the simplest mechanisms. Greater complexity is going to require problems.
