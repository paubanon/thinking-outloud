An idea. An idea is an abstract thing we have in our heads. Let's say it's a concept, a mental object, that a group of people share. But ideas, being mental objects, are shapeless. We can't fully delineate them or describe them. I like to picture it as a dark object you have no direct access to. You can't pick it up, look at it, and see exactly where its limits and shape are. You only have access to its projections. It's like having an object and shining a light on it from the side so you can see the shadow. You can rotate the object and see different projections and gradually get an idea of what it looks like. It generally doesn't have a sharp, defined edge. They tend to be diffuse, abstract, open to interpretation.

So when it comes to teaching something to someone, you have to understand it that way. The projection that seems most informative to you might not be the one that gives the other person the most information. Turning it around and presenting it a different way is how the other person better understands the concept you're trying to convey. It's something I've developed as I've given classes. I love physics, and I love teaching. They're complicated ideas. And sometimes you realize you don't have an idea as clear as you thought the moment you have to explain it to someone else. Because you present it the way you think is correct, the other person doesn't get it, and you have to explain it differently. And if you can't do it differently, that means you don't have the idea as consolidated as you thought.

This is the basis of the Feynman method: you study something, stop, explain it out loud, and the points you can't explain correctly are the ones you haven't fully understood. As Einstein said: you've only truly understood a concept if you can explain it to your grandmother. When you've completely grasped an idea, you're able to simplify it enough for the other person, without technical knowledge, to understand it. It's as if the image you're producing has greater or lesser resolution. Depending on the audience, you have to present it accordingly.

This leads me to the point that teaching doesn't only benefit the student. It benefits the teacher too. You learn to articulate your ideas in a way the rest of the world understands. And you include yourself in that world. When you force yourself to explain and articulate your thinking precisely, you're not only instructing the other person. You're learning to organize your own ideas.

How many times has it happened that you have something crystal clear in your head and you say: "Let me explain it to you," you start talking and suddenly stop and say "no wait, that's not where I'm going to start"? That "step back" is a brain connection you just made in real time: the logical order for presenting this idea is not this one. And on top of that you realize that this idea is connected to this other piece of knowledge that you implicitly had connected but weren't consciously aware of. That only develops through discourse. Doing it over and over with different audiences.

It's what we called the spiel at Show Movers. The discourse on a topic. A deep discourse that benefits not only the listener but also the one giving it. As you explain the same ideas more and more to more people, you learn to define them in your head and start discovering the edges: which ones are well-defined and which aren't as much. There are points where you say: "Oh! This area isn't as clear to me as I thought." And that's when tools like this channel, writing, explaining in person, help you interact better with the world.

You're moving things from the unknown unknown to the known unknown. That is, you're learning that there are things you don't know. And that's the best first step, because what you know you don't know you just have to sit down and learn. The hard part is being aware that there are things you don't know you don't know. The unknown unknown is vast. And when you enter that zone, stress and anxiety exist. Articulating your thinking well, knowing how far you reach and where your limits are, also helps you interact better with the world because you learn to express your ideas and your emotions precisely.

It's like being in a relationship where you're frustrated about something, but you don't know why. You only know you're not okay. You can't attack the problem because you don't know what it is. But if you try to talk about it, you'll start to narrow it down. Something that's "I'm not comfortable in this relationship" starts to become "I'm sometimes uncomfortable, and sometimes I'm uncomfortable because you do this thing." And maybe you also start to see that you're also taking it the wrong way, not how you should. And you keep narrowing the problem down to something within your reach. You don't throw the whole fleet overboard when all you have is a small hole in the hull.

There's nothing more frustrating than someone trying to tell you something they think they understand but don't, and can't articulate it. "But I understand it!" No they don't. They need to articulate it, reflect on it, sit with it.

For this it helps to explain things to people in person, to discuss things, because they can offer you perspectives you hadn't considered. Or to keep a blog, or to write. How many times have we heard that people write a diary and say that it helps them organize themselves, helps them see more clearly what they need to do? Everything seems very clear until you start doing it. Making a list of what you want to do in the morning makes it much more productive and satisfying, because as you write you notice the details: "well if I write that I want to study topics 4 and 5, topic 5 was long, so I won't go shopping now, I'll go later." You refine your ideas. And refining your ideas lets you better face the chaos.

As Jordan Peterson says, logos, consciousness, is the fundamental pinnacle of Western culture. That which lets you move from chaos to order. But beware — it's important that there be disorder so you can move to order. The unknown, uncertainty, chaos: from there you move to order. And order is the zone where you don't stress, where you're in control and can act.
