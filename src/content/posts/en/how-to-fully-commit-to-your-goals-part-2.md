In the previous video I talked about the inability to perceive what we leave behind when we engage in certain typically addictive behaviors, like social media, sugar, pornography, and masturbation. In this video I want to talk about how to stick to a behavior you want to change, or how to decide whether a habit is right for you and to what degree.

The mistake I see very often is that people make excuses like: yeah, but this gives you this benefit; yeah, but this isn't for me; I could never give this up; I think this is still bringing me value. And the problem is that it's a fallacy. You don't have perspective on how something affects you if you haven't tried. You can't know where a path leads before walking it.

And a lot of the time we make these excuses to not even start. We don't even begin. Because we feel like we have to make a decision before having the experience. We feel like we have to know for certain whether pornography is affecting us or not, whether social media makes us happier or unhappier. I need to know whether gluten affects me before cutting out gluten. And we feel like if we make a decision it's going to be forever, with no way back. All of this is a lie. All of this is one big lie.

What I'm here to tell you is: you don't need all that knowledge before starting to walk. Try it. Put it into your life. And put it in seriously. Take something you want to change and implement it in your life at 100%. If you want to cut sugar, completely out. Full fast. You want to stop watching YouTube and social media because you notice they're taking up your time, don't try a middle ground of I'll try to moderate my use. Out. 100%. For a month. Then look back.

You want to know how pornography affects your brain. Cut it for three months. Out, completely. Accept that you're going to have a hard time. Accept that you're going to have cravings. Accept that it's going to be uncomfortable. Accept that you're going to climb the walls. Accept that you're going to miss it. Accept all of that. And commit 100%. Then, when you've done what you set out to do at 100%, you look back. Give yourself the chance to change your attitude and your thinking.

Set yourself reasonably high goals. Don't be afraid. I'm going to meditate every day for ten minutes for sixty days. I won't miss a single one. No matter what. And if I miss one I'll start again. And once I've done it at 100%, I'll decide whether I want to keep meditating or not. And I'm completely at peace with my decision. If at that point I think it wasn't worth it, that it didn't bring me anything, fine. I tried it. Seriously. I gave it a real chance. It's not for me. I'll drop it. Done.

Cut out pornography for three months. Feel sexual desire. Learn to keep it under control. Learn what urges come from not wanting to work. Learn all of that. And then, once you've walked that path, give yourself the chance to look back and make the decision. Hey, it wasn't worth it for me. I preferred masturbating. Then go back to it. What do you lose? In the case of not having a partner, not having an orgasm for three months of your life. Yes, that's what you miss. But what do you learn? You learn to control your sexual desire. You learn that masturbation was an important part of your life. You learn how your perception of other people changes. Maybe you learn that you're more attracted to people in everyday life. Maybe you learn to concentrate better. Maybe you discover it was affecting you more than you thought. Or maybe you learn it wasn't affecting you at all. And fine, you had three months without orgasm. Okay.

Same thing I see with gluten. I just couldn't give up bread. Give it a chance. 100%. One month, two months without bread. Zero. And then give yourself the chance to look back and say: I'm going to try bread again. You take it, you try it. You see the effect it has now. You see if it causes intestinal discomfort. You see if you've lost fat or gained muscle. You see your mental clarity. And then you give yourself the possibility to decide.

This isn't about making a categorical decision. It's about walking the path. You try it. And you're perfectly at peace to make a decision afterward. But what I want to emphasize is that the decision is made after you've learned. Once you've taken something seriously and given it everything.

Personal example. I decided to sign up for salsa classes. And on the day I was supposed to go sign up for the first class, I'd had a tiring day, it was raining. I said: I really don't feel like it. I don't think salsa is for me. I'm going to feel embarrassed because I dance badly. And I told myself: everything you're telling yourself is a bullshit excuse because you're embarrassed. You're going. You're signing up. You're paying for it. You're going to all ten classes. You won't skip a single one. And then, when you've finished those ten classes, you think about whether you like dancing or not.

And that's what happened. Turns out I had a great time. I decided salsa isn't my obsession, isn't my passion. But it's something I enjoy, that I find stimulating. And it's about giving yourself a chance. But to give yourself that chance you have to commit 100%. If I'd said: well, I'll try it halfway... I would have gone to two classes and then started making excuses and quit.

Another personal example: fasting. Right now I fast about sixteen hours every day, more or less. I skip breakfast and only eat twice. But there was a period when I implemented fasts of more than twenty-four hours. I did several twenty-four-hour fasts and it went pretty well. Then there was a stretch where they started feeling long. I noticed I was a bit low on energy toward the end of the day, that I wasn't training with the same motivation, that past six or seven in the evening there wasn't much I felt like doing other than sitting on the couch staring at the ceiling, just waiting for it to be time to eat.

I could have told myself: yeah, but twenty-four-hour fasting is just like this, it's intrinsic to you, this is just what you are. Like a lot of people do. And become obsessed with that being the way it is. Instead, I gave myself the chance to change my mind. I gave myself the chance to say: I tried this at 100%, exactly as I wanted to. And I saw the effect it has. I'm not satisfied with it. So I'm dropping it. And I dropped it. No big deal.

But you can't know your own perspective until you've walked the path. It's true that you pay a price for walking it. But generally it's a pretty small price when you think about it long-term. Not eating bread for a month is a pretty small price. Not masturbating for three months is a pretty small price. What's the potential benefit? That you realize bread affects you more than you thought. That it irritates your gut. That it affects how you sleep, how you work, how much body fat you have. And that, if it works out, is a positive effect for life.

You're setting aside ten minutes a day to meditate for sixty days for the possibility of learning a practice that's going to make you sleep better, improve your personal relationships, let you live without stress, help you concentrate more. You're setting aside orgasms for three months but potentially gaining better concentration, becoming more attractive, increasing your confidence, learning about your sexual impulses. Those are potential benefits that stay with you for the rest of your life. And if they don't work out, I'm sure you'll come back to any of those practices differently, having learned something.

And there's one more point I want to emphasize: committing 100% is easier than committing 98%. Because if you commit 100%, you only have to make the decision once. Every time someone offers you a sweet, you simply say: no, thanks. It's automatic. You don't have to decide yes or no in the moment because you already decided.

However, if you commit 98%, every small interaction you have to decide. But this sweet... Today yes? Today no? How much counts as an exception? How often should exceptions be? When was the last time? Is my sister's birthday a sufficient exception? Is going out to dinner with my girlfriend a sufficient exception? Is having a hard day at work a sufficient exception? To eat a sweet, to masturbate, to whatever. You have to constantly be making decisions. And that's exhausting and makes you quit.

That's why I put so much emphasis on commitment having to be 100%. Now, you also can't expect to make a 100% commitment forever. That's why I also put emphasis on setting a date, a limit. I'm going to do this for sixty days and then I'll think about it. Because then you can afford to commit 100% without fear of missing something. After that date I'll re-evaluate. Not before. But you have to walk the path. You have to walk the path before making a decision.
