In the last few videos or seasons I've realized I've talked quite a bit about reflections, philosophies, thoughts, and not so much about tools or practices you can apply day to day. And many times when I've talked about productivity tools, I've mentioned that I've ended up going back to paper, which is what works best for me and what I like most in terms of feel.

However, I'm going to do something I don't usually do, which is talk about a couple of products — without any affiliation — for their notable contribution to my daily productivity. And because I think the best way to promote a product is when people recommend it because it's worked for them. And these two items have worked so extremely well for me, and have stuck around since they entered my workflow, displacing tools that had been doing their job for years, that I think they deserve an honest mention.

Those two tools are the Kindle Oasis and the reMarkable tablet.

I'll start with the Kindle Oasis. I was already a Kindle user: I had a Kindle 4 that when I decided to switch had about twelve years behind it. The battery died very quickly. Every time I looked at what the new versions could offer me that mine couldn't, I realized they had nothing the old one couldn't do when the device's goal is reading. However, when the battery died — which is one of the main features you want from an e-reader — I decided to switch. The Oasis was the top-of-the-range Kindle, and also the most expensive. What made me go for it was, honestly, the fact that I read a lot. If it's something you're going to use every day, that you want to take travelling, buy the device that does its job best.

And I have to say I'm very glad I went with the premium version because it makes a big difference. The large screen is a gift. The resolution is very good. The fact that it has a light that can be dimmed and turned reddish or orange, and brought down to very very low levels, is essential for me. It has I think 28 brightness levels and I never go above level 11. Most of the time I can read in complete darkness between levels 1 and 3 without it bothering my eyes.

Since I bought it, it has practically replaced paper books many times over. I still really like the feel of paper, but the practicality of this thing is incredible. It's so light and small that many times, even when I have the paper book, I also download it in digital format and read it on one or the other depending on the situation. If I'm at home and can grab the paper one, great. But if I want to take it to university, travelling, to sleep at someone's house, the Kindle is the thing.

And now the reMarkable. I didn't think it was going to have as big an impact as it did. It surprised me far more than pleasantly.

The idea of the reMarkable is to replace paper. Full stop. It's a paper notebook in digital form. You get everything paper gives you with the bonus of being able to export your notes as PDFs, save them, send them to someone, categorize them, organize them. It's not trying to be a miniature computer. It's not trying to be a tablet with e-ink and a thousand features. It's trying to be a digital paper notebook. And that's precisely its strength.

It has nothing to distract you. It's pure writing feel. You get into it and it absorbs you. You don't have the temptation of I'll open this website, I'll check my email, I'll install this temporarily. No. Just write.

The writing experience isn't like paper in the sense that paper is always better for me. The feel of writing with a pen on paper is unbeatable. However, it's very pleasant to write on this thing because it has a slightly textured surface that gives a certain friction to the writing. It's not slippery like an iPad might be. And the response speed is super fast, there's no delay between when you write and when it appears. Exactly as you write is how it appears on paper.

What I like most is the absence of distractions. Literally, when you sit down to write with it, the flow you enter is identical to the one you enter when you pick up paper. It's just you and the paper and the thought. It has nothing — it doesn't even have a clock when you're writing.

Why has this device won me over compared to paper? The ability to organize. Being able to take notes on different topics and put them in folders and subfolders that reflect my tree-like way of thinking — all the research I do in each area, topics, subtopics, ideas. Until now that was impossible. If I used paper, I needed to carry physical binders around. And a binder only has one level of organization. The ability to create folders within folders and tags for the different documents has been revolutionary for me.

I had no intention of it replacing paper. I bought it as a complement. And straight away, after two or three weeks, I realized I wasn't using paper for anything. And after three months of use, both personally and academically or professionally, there's nothing to compare it to. Being able to make my notes, add PDFs of articles and books in large format, read on e-ink which is so easy on the eyes, write, highlight, mark, draw, add notes, categorize it, send it to myself by email... it's priceless for me. It's been literally a revolution.

If I had to find any downside: it's a specialized device. If what you're looking for is multitasking, if you're someone who's constantly sending messages, it can be useful but it's not for that. It's a paper replacement. And the USB connectivity leaves a bit to be desired, at least on Linux. The only practical inconvenience is that to export things you have to email them to yourself.

These two tools share the characteristic that they have a relatively high price and it took me a long time to decide to buy them. And when I bought them, without any extra effort, they completely took over my workflow. Their greatest value lies in completely isolating you from possible distractions. You don't have to be restraining yourself from opening any app. There's no app to open. You can only read or write.
