Those of you who know me know that I'm very, very bad at being on time and I usually show up late. And those who don't, well, now you know — I'm someone who's always late. I don't say that with pride, it actually bothers me, but reflecting on why it happens I've come to the conclusion that while many people work by time — meaning from this hour to this hour I do this, from this hour to this hour I do that — my way of working is different. I work by tasks.

I do a task and when I finish it I move to the next one, and the next, and the next. What's the upside and downside? The upside is that you don't move on to the next task until you've finished the current one. That means you finish your tasks, you never leave something at 76%. You say, I've finished it, and if it took me 30 minutes or an hour longer than expected, well, that's what it is, but I needed to do this task and I did it.

And it's a problem with planning your time by time, because you might say from this hour to this hour I study, but what if you get through less than you expected? What if you didn't get as much done? What if you got stuck on a problem and didn't finish the problem sets? What do you do? Stop? That's the benefit of working by tasks. You say, I have to finish this problem set, I have to train, whatever it is. Until you're done, nothing else.

What's the downside? You're late.

And it's a somewhat selfish attitude. Something I'll keep exploring through the videos... My way of acting can sometimes be what you'd call selfish. But I think sometimes you have to prioritize yourself over others. And if what you want is productivity, you have to accept that others won't agree with you. Because they want to come first. And it's you who has to decide. No. This comes first. This task comes first. And when it's done, the next one.

Deciding whether you want to do what you need to do. Or whether you want to be on time.
