The ability to choose. That's what I wanted to talk about today, and specifically I wanted to talk about how it's overrated, in my opinion. Today we have a culture that exalts freedom above everything: the freedom to choose whatever you want in your life, to be absolutely anything you want to be. But a lot of people don't stop to think that the ability to choose comes with a weight: you have to decide. And deciding puts pressure on us as human beings, because choosing something means setting aside many other things. We always have that fear: what if there's something better? What if there's a better degree than the one I'm doing? What if this job isn't the right one? What if this brand of ketchup isn't the best? What an existential anxiety, what desperation.

I think we need to sometimes revalue the blessing of ignorance. It might not be the most popular opinion, but if you grew up in an environment where there was no possibility of choice — not that there was no ability, there was no possibility — if you had grown up in an era where you couldn't choose your job, where you were going to continue your father's trade: if he was a blacksmith, you were a blacksmith, because there was no other way to make a living. Your option was that or starving. So you didn't have to wonder whether you liked it, you didn't have to wonder if it was your passion, if you were doing something meaningful for the universe. You didn't think about it because you had no choice. And all those existential problems characteristic of these last generations disappeared through the simplicity of having no choice.

You lived your life working at what you worked at. You'd surely have hard days and good days, and in the end you'd die exactly the same as we all die now. But probably, knowing there's no other option, knowing you don't have to choose, through ignorance, you'd be a little bit happier. You'd be content with what you have, you wouldn't rack your brain about what you don't have and could be better, about whether you should push yourself more, about whether you should go abroad. Maybe your life is simple and plain, with the people you love and what you have around you, and that's enough.

And I increasingly think we're moving further in that direction. Things as banal as buying anything, just by virtue of having internet access, can turn into a real headache. I'll give a couple of examples. Not long ago I bought a backpack. What a few years ago would have been: you walk into a store, you see the backpack they have and you take it. Instead it gave me quite a problem, because of course I had hundreds of backpacks within reach, with these specs and those, all perfectly organized. This one has a few more liters, this one a few less, this fabric is a bit more resistant, this one has more reviews but this one has more positives. What do I value more: that more people have tried it, or that the people who tried it have a better opinion? You start comparing and in the end you can spend four days comparing something that isn't that important, because it's a backpack.

This usually happens especially when we have to commit to buying a device, something valuable: a phone or a television. This past holiday season, well, last year, my dad said to me: "Pablo, do a bit of research because we're going to buy a TV." And knowing it was a big investment, I started looking. First: what features do televisions have? What technology is better? And I realized that every manufacturer uses whatever naming convention they feel like, uses whatever technology they feel like, nothing is standardized, and it's incredibly hard to compare one thing to another. On top of that you have a million websites with a million televisions, with deals, with comparisons, each one with whatever naming system they fancy. It becomes an arduous and agonizing job.

In the end, the afternoon came and my dad said: "Grab your stuff, we're going to look at TVs." And we went out, checked three stores, found a good deal and said: this one. And we took it home. Was it the best deal of all? Probably not. Probably if I had kept searching online for five more days I would have found an amazing one. But once you have it at home, you don't notice that slight difference in color between slightly darker tones on this TV versus slightly brighter ones on that other one. You don't notice, because you only notice that when you have ten TVs and can compare them side by side. And what I'm saying is that sometimes making a decision — any decision — is much better than getting rid of the problem of still having to think about it. Sometimes it's better to make a decision than to make the best decision.

Our brain is remarkable at making peace with what it has. I read about an experiment a while back where a group of volunteers were asked to choose between some paintings. Two control groups: the first was told "the painting you choose is yours, period"; the second was told "the painting you choose, if after some time you want, you can exchange it for another one." The group that had no take-backs — by quite a margin, I think — after some time said they were happy with their decision, that it was the best painting, that they wouldn't change it. Meanwhile the other group, knowing they could change it, had been going back and forth: "I'm not sure this is the painting I like, there was another one that maybe was better, I could ask for the exchange." And they were more unhappy with their choice, simply because they still had the option to change it.

On the other hand, there's a factor we don't realize: we tend to value other people's opinions more than our own. And I think that's something we should start to change. Value your own opinion more, be aware of what you yourself know. Because when you read opinions online — about a movie, about anything — you have to be aware that you don't know who the person doing the judging is. There are people I know, and if they give me an opinion about a film I say: "Your opinion means nothing to me, because I know I don't share your values." But there are other people who, if they tell me a book is worth reading, I'll dive right into it, because I know them and I know what they like. That doesn't happen on the internet. We have hundreds of thousands of opinions from people we don't know, and we let ourselves be influenced too much.

And that, which initially seems like a positive thing — that the buyer can know other users' experiences — isn't quite so, because the buyer enters a loop of having to analyze so much information to choose a product that it becomes agonizing. And when you don't have to do this occasionally, but in every aspect of your life, because the internet is covering every aspect and you have two hundred million options for absolutely everything, decision paralysis sets in. The existential problems set in.

When you're at the supermarket and there's only one brand of ketchup, you grab it. When there are two brands of ketchup it's already not so easy. When there's a shelf with thirty-five brands of ketchup you stop to look at each one: this one has more tomato, this one has more sugar, this one has less fat, blah blah blah. You can go crazy. And that expands to the entire range of products we consume today. The number of difficult decisions we have to make is insane, and throughout the day that stresses us out.

Not to mention the great dilemma I mentioned at the start: today there's such ease of travel, such ease to choose whatever job you want, that it can be a serious shock to realize that you have to choose something. And it frightens us — me too — to choose something that turns out not to have been the job of your life. When the best we can do is change our perception and say: within the things I like, I choose one and I don't look back. And if you're able to do that with enough honesty, you'll be able to value what you've chosen and not wonder about what you left behind. I'm happy with my choice, and it probably wasn't the objectively best option, but there was no option that was objectively better in every aspect either, and if there was it was by some triviality that isn't worth the headache.

This has given me a little pang about books. A book is a good investment of time, effort, interest. It's something very intimate, because it's not just the book: it's your interaction with it, and that depends on your personal moment, your state of mind, the attention with which you read it. You see a film in an hour and a half and it's easier to pass judgment. A book depends on so many things that making a recommendation is a delicate matter. Personally, when someone asks me to recommend a book, I have to know that person very well to dare. My recommendations change depending on the person.

Having access today to opinions about books, combined with the fact that we have no idea who is judging us, I think is very counterproductive. You go to look for a book and check: this one has a 4.75 on Goodreads and this one has a 3.79, so I'll read the 4.75. Maybe it turns out the 3.79 is a bit more challenging and the average reader liked it less, but for you, who are an avid reader, it's exactly what you love. Those ratings bias the reader.

I think sometimes you need to cut yourself off from options for your mental health. And beyond that there's a romantic element I really like: going into an anonymous bookstore with no preconceived idea of what book you want. You just feel like reading something in a certain style. You browse, look at covers, read blurbs, and at most you ask the clerk: "How's this one?" "They say it's really good." And that's all your feedback. You go home with a book, without having searched for it online, without comparing it. The way people have done it for many years. And you'll probably like it, and you haven't gone down so many rabbit holes, and there's an element of intrigue, of not knowing what's coming. That gives it more flavor.

And that's not just about buying a book. It's about choosing your career, choosing your partner. Thinking "will there be someone better?"... I don't know. Probably, but better according to which values, better for whom. Maybe what you need to do is commit to the partner you're with now, both of you commit and build a good relationship. Not look for a perfect person, but bet on that person. I think a lot of couples break up because of this: "There's definitely someone better." The ease of contacting someone else, of chatting with someone, I think makes us put less effort into building a good relationship. Because a good relationship doesn't appear out of nowhere. There's no person you fit perfectly with in every way. What there is is an interpersonal relationship that sometimes goes very well and sometimes something's off, and what you have to do is work to understand that the other person is a complex person, and you have to learn to understand each other, to work with each other, to live together. Stop obsessing over "maybe she's not the person of my life."

Same with your career. Maybe there's no career that is your passion. Maybe what you need is to pick one you like and throw yourself into it, and commit to it. Because in the end, as I always say, we're all going to die the same. And that also gives us the freedom to say: since we're here in the world, choose something you like. But if you have many options you like and they're driving you crazy, just think it through and choose. Don't let inaction from information overload block you, because then you've lost.
