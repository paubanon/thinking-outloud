Today I want to talk about standardization, individualism, and school uniforms. Which might seem like separate topics but I think are very much connected.

Standardization is a very powerful modern phenomenon. It's what allows you to produce millions of identical cars, millions of identical phones, millions of identical forms. It's the foundation of industrial efficiency.

The problem is when we apply that logic to people.

The education system, in its current form, is built on the logic of standardization. All children learn the same thing, at the same pace, evaluated by the same criteria. The implicit goal is to produce people who fit the mold society needs.

And that has costs.

The individual has unique characteristics: interests, abilities, learning rhythms. When you put everyone in the same mold, those who fit well come out ahead and those who don't learn that there's something wrong with them.

School uniforms are a good microcosm of this debate. The arguments in favor are well known: they reduce visible differences in economic level, they eliminate the pressure to be fashionable, they foster a sense of belonging to the group. Those are reasonable arguments.

But there's an argument against that I think is important: clothing is a form of individual expression. For adolescents, who are in the process of constructing an identity, being able to express themselves through how they dress has real value. The uniform suppresses that.

I don't know whether I'm for or against uniforms. I think it depends on the context. But I do think the debate reveals something deeper: how much individualism do we want in our society and how much conformity?

Too much conformism flattens human potential. Too much individualism makes social cohesion impossible. Somewhere in between is the balance.

What I am clear on is that a society that values independent thinking should be very careful about how much it conditions its members to think and behave the same way from a young age.
