It's been a long time since I uploaded a video just reflecting, sharing thoughts. Partly because I was busy, partly because I felt like I had things to say but they weren't that important. I kept putting it off, in line with my commitment to this channel of not putting any kind of social pressure on myself to publish. But you can't judge the value of what you share by measuring it against yourself, and I've gotten a lot of learning at many points in my life from people who shared things that were more or less important. Not only what's fundamental to life is worth sharing. Sometimes it's worth sharing small reflections or small ideas without needing them to be infinitely profound. So with that spirit I decided to start recording again, with very little idea of what I'm going to talk about beyond the general topic, which is rest.

I'm a very restless person, and maybe many of you listening to this channel are too. I have a lot of ambitions, a lot of projects, a lot of things to do and a lot of desire to do them. At other points in my life I called this stress. Wanting to do many things and never getting there, because for every spoonful you take you add two more to the bowl. These days I call it more like excitement. It's wanting to eat the world. It's not "I need to do this," it's "I want to do it," but I want it so badly that sometimes the wave even sweeps me away.

This is more pronounced now, in this period where I am absolutely my own boss, my own dictator, and my own therapist. All the time. And that's something complicated for people who are, let's say, autonomous in the vital sense. If you're the one deciding what to do and how to do it, you're also the one responsible for setting your own limits. And that's very hard. A boss can tell you: at 5 you go home. And your brain indexes that from 5 onward you don't have to think about work. When you're your own boss, what stops you from working until midnight? Nothing. And where's the limit when the social boundaries around what a weekend means start to dissolve?

I've been arriving at certain conclusions I want to share. The first is that it's important to stop. There's a point at which stopping is not just better for you but makes you more productive, because you arrive at work moments with more energy and new ideas. The second is that rest has to be scheduled with the same rigidity, or even more, than work. Because work tends, at least in my case, to spread infinitely across all areas and every second of my life. Something as simple as walking to the gym can very quickly turn into: I have this idea, I'll ask the AI while I walk and I'll ruminate on it the whole way there. Those are hours of work-thinking that keep you from relaxing.

The break between work and rest has to be radical. When you work, you work. When you rest, you rest. And you have to forbid yourself by martial law from touching anything work-related during rest hours. If something occurs to you, write it down, and when the time comes you sit down and work it out. This has helped me immensely to have real moments of disconnection.

These breaks, for me, have to happen at several levels. At the daily level, for example, in the morning I have my coffee reading, with no digital devices. Then I tidy the house, go for a walk, and get to work. At midday I stop, eat, and take a nap. And then in the afternoon, depending on the day, one or two hours of extra work, and then I stop and go to the gym. At the weekly level, there have to be at least 24 hours, ideally 48. That has to be religiously protected. I fall into that mistake over and over and over. My partner is sleeping, since I'm up anyway I'll open the laptop and write a few lines of code. No. That's a catastrophic mistake. Before you know it it's 1 in the morning and you never went to the woods. And then, at various timescales: monthly, a different weekend where you go to a country house or another city. Quarterly, four or five days of absolute rest. And annually, a longer vacation where you completely disconnect.

And here comes the main thing I wanted to share. It's not just that you have to forbid yourself from working. You have to decide that you're not going to work. There's a big difference between a day when you don't work and a day when you decide not to work.

When you don't work on a day because the day was full, if throughout that process you've been thinking: if I get a moment, maybe I can, let's see if we finish early so I can get to work — the brain doesn't disconnect. It's waiting for a free moment to get back to it. And that's tremendously toxic: you don't rest, the mental buffer doesn't clear.

On the other hand, when you decide you're not going to work, when you say "on Sunday I'm not going to work, I'm forbidding myself," you enter a different mode. Maybe you have two or three free hours in the morning. When you haven't forbidden yourself from working, you'll end up working if you're like me. But if you've forbidden yourself, you enter a diffuse, creative, open-minded mode of thinking. That's the level of block you need to achieve: as if your work life were in another city and you'd have to get in the car to get there.

Then you start thinking: well, I'll go for a walk, I'll read a book, I'll lie in the sun. And sometimes there's nothing to do. And those moments where there's nothing to do and you've forbidden yourself from working are the most restorative. They're the moments when you do the things that don't occur to you when you're thinking about work. The "this afternoon I have nothing to do" and you go to a café in the city center, alone, to read, watch people go by, write, chat with someone you meet. That only happens when you mentally block off the time.

Free time isn't limited to the time you're not working. Free time is blocked as free time and then you decide what to do with it. And with that I hope this message reaches someone. Someone who's burnt out, someone who doesn't know how to manage their time. That it opens them up toward those more creative, wider paths, where wasted time doesn't matter, where you go train outdoors and you're not running thinking you have to finish in an hour, but if it's two hours, great, and if it's three because you feel like lying in the sun after, also great. That for me is what real rest is. And it's only in those moments that I've managed to shift from ultra-productive, work-obsessed person to someone who lets go a little more. It took me many years to learn that. I hope that with this video some of you manage it a little sooner than I did.
