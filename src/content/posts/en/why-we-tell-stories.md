As you know, I'm someone who's very interested in evolutionary biology and all the elements that have accompanied us across billions of years. Because every time I've looked inside something that has been with us throughout all that time, it's helped me understand myself better, understand who I am and why, and why I have the desires and concerns I have. And how to live a better life.

Some of you may know that I've been interested in symbolism for a few years now. That interest came from discovering the concept of the hero's journey. That is, the idea that there's a meta-structure behind many of our stories. The idea that certain stories resonate with us even when we're told them a million times, and that certain types of stories don't.

And many stories don't resonate. Which is surprising, because when you think about it you might say: there are tons of stories, each one is different. True, but not really. Each story is different in its details, but inside, the vast majority of stories are the same. There are very few stories where the hero gets to the end, dies, and doesn't get what they wanted. It's a valid story, it's something that can happen, but it doesn't resonate. Something grates. Stories where nothing happens. Stories where the hero gets corrupted and everything ends badly. Stories where the main character doesn't try and simply gets what they need and moves on with their life. All of those are valid stories. They could pass for stories, but they don't resonate. Something in them tells us: no, this isn't a story I'd want to hear.

And I wondered why. What's going on here? It's not that it resonates with me but not you or vice versa. It's that collectively, as human beings, there are stories that resonate with us. And they're stories that deserve to be told again and again and again, given a fresh coat of paint and told with this character or that one, this villain or another, this adventure or a different one. And we love hearing them. We vibrate. We've been telling stories since humans were humans and gathered around the fire.

This led me to research Joseph Campbell, who was the first to develop this idea. That at an evolutionary level, just as we've developed fingers, just as we've developed certain types of eyes, we've developed certain types of neural patterns. Certain neural connections that are built into our brains from the moment we're born, inherited after millions of years of evolution. And they're neural patterns associated with behavior: how you should act to be successful in life, which patterns are right for society and for yourself.

The problem is that until very recently we weren't the rational animals we think we are. We weren't trying to rationalize the world. And it turns out that stories are the best way we have of expressing these behavioral patterns. That is, through stories we bring into the cognitive, conscious realm those patterns we all carry internally in an unconscious and collective way. We all know what it means to behave properly. We all know which behavioral patterns are appropriate and which aren't. Lying, deceiving, betraying, hiding. We all know those aren't good behavioral patterns.

Well, it seems that over millions of years we've assembled the right patterns for living and bundled them into something called the figure of the hero. And those behaviors we shouldn't have we've grouped into the figure of the villain. And those unknown elements of the world we've grouped into the figure of the dragon or of chaos. And we tell ourselves the same stories over and over about how we should behave. Because it's a way of helping ourselves understand how the world works and how we work.

A very clear example of these meta-stories is the hero's journey. The hero's journey is a pattern that's not only common in the stories we tell today, but common in stories you can find in hundreds of religions around the world. It's common to Star Wars, to Harry Potter, to biblical stories, to Greek, Egyptian, Mesopotamian stories, and even tribes cut off from civilization in the middle of the desert tell the same story in one form or another. And that's remarkable. Because when you think about it, some of these cultures have had no contact with each other. And yet you find the same pattern.

The story of the hero's journey starts with the hero in their village. The hero is in their town, living their life peacefully. They don't know how to be a hero. They're an ordinary person. And that village represents their comfort zone, their stability, their life as it is at that moment. Then they receive a call to adventure. Something happens that gives the hero a need to go out into the world and fight. This can be anything. A princess has to be rescued, there's a dragon. Here the dragon symbolizes any difficulty, any problem that can arise in life. It can be your boss telling you something needs to be done within a month. That's the dragon. It can be that you suddenly meet someone you want to get to know and suddenly your life is no longer stable. There's an adventure to be lived. All that chaos, that uncertainty, that adventure is symbolized by the dragon.

Then the hero decides to leave their comfort zone and fight the dragon. It's important to note that the hero goes after the dragon. They don't wait for the dragon to come. And this symbolizes the need to face problems consciously and deliberately. Because if you wait for the dragon to come, it may be too big by the time it arrives. If you wait for the deadline to come to you, rather than going after it and working it actively, it'll probably be too late.

Dragons, archetypically, hoard gold or a maiden or princess. And this is notable because when you think about it — what does a dragon want with gold? Why does it hoard gold and a maiden it can't use? It seems like a stupid question because the idea of the dragon hoarding gold is so deeply embedded in us, so natural, that the mere act of posing it as a question is surprising. But it's a reptile that can't talk, breathes fire, and hoards gold. If you think about it rationally, it's not obvious at all. Well, dragons hoard gold because gold is a symbol of what you most need, of those goods that will help you if you obtain them by going through the dragon. A very clear symbolism: if you get through the project, you get paid. As simple as that.

And it's no coincidence that the dragon is a reptile. It can be a serpent, it can be a leviathan. The reptile is an embodiment of those animals that were our main predators when we were apes. It was what we feared most because it was our greatest danger. So all of these ideas together reflect: there is a very serious danger, it's the most dangerous thing there can be, and if you go and fight it voluntarily, you may get what you most need.

In this confrontation the hero undergoes a personal transformation. There's an evolution, and it generally involves some moment of near-death or pseudo-death followed by rebirth. Sometimes in films this is literal. In Harry Potter, when he goes down into the depths to rescue Ginny, he has to be bitten by the basilisk to be reborn through the phoenix and defeat it. That process of death and rebirth symbolizes that in the course of the adventure, changes will happen to you — painful and difficult ones — that will change you irrevocably.

And once you've obtained what you need, the hero doesn't keep the gold, revel in it, and turn into a tyrant. The hero takes what they need and returns to their home city, where they improve their community and their world. That is, you have to go through this whole process of adventure to return to where you were and make the world better. It's a symbolic idea that throughout your life you have to deal with adventures, you have to deal with difficulties. And each time you deal with a difficulty successfully, you return to where you were — but you return changed, and you return better.

Even meta-stories, like the process of finishing a degree, are made up of micro-stories that are each year, which in turn are made up of micro-stories that are each exam. And each one aligns positively with the archetype of the hero.

All this symbolism is encapsulated within all the stories we hear throughout our lives. And what these stories do is tell us: this is the right way to act. This is the way you have to move through the world, which is hard and complex, but this is the way you'll advance optimally.

And I've lived all of this recently. I'm reading a book by Joe Abercrombie. Because when you study history they tell you: yes, this kind of trial took place where it didn't matter what you said, you're guilty. And you think: yeah, that's awful. But when you read about characters you identify with, when you hear the arguments being used in their own voice, when you see what it would be like to be there, you have a much deeper understanding.

It's like the difference between conceptual knowledge and integrated knowledge that I mentioned a few videos ago. Listening to stories lets you empathize with those people. And it seems that telling stories is our way of simulating different lives. Or better yet, the way of simulating the most fundamental elements from which we can extract the most information. Because in life you learn almost nothing except through experience. You can give people a thousand pieces of advice, but until they go, fail, mess up, and then think about your advice, they're not going to change. Myself included.

Well, it seems that stories are the medium through which we're able to pass on this knowledge. It's the way I can make you feel what it's like to go down that path. And in doing that, you evoke the empathy of what it's like to be there and maybe you learn something.

And it's remarkably curious how stories grab us and capture all our attention. Some of us have had the fantastic experience of being in front of a group of friends and starting to tell a story and suddenly the whole circle turns and looks at you and pays close attention. And when that happens it's because you're telling something that resonates with people. A kind of small bubble is created where we all vibrate around something. It's like we're sitting around a fire.

For example: when you look back, our brain reconstructs memories and makes things up to make our life into a coherent narrative. Your brain wants your life to be a story.
