Since I uploaded the video about danger, I'm going to make an addendum, a postscript on the subject. Part of why we like danger is that it forces us to be in the present. If you have to walk along a railing that's up high and you know that if you fall there's a real risk of getting hurt, you're not thinking about anything other than your body on that railing. Nothing else.

It's something I love about climbing. The danger, the stress, yanks you back to reality with a kick in the ass. You're climbing, especially if you're leading, and you're not thinking about the work you have to do for university, or the exam tomorrow, or what you're having for dinner. All those problems shut off the moment you're attached to the wall. Your reality shrinks to a circle of roughly a meter around you. Nothing else exists. There's only the balance, the strength you have left, the agitation in your body, the holds you can reach, whether your fingers are slipping.

And that, in a world where we're rushing around from one thing to another, doing things that we generally perceive as having little value because nothing's on the line yet they still stress us out — I think all of that together is what makes activities with a certain risk very attractive as tools for personal development. You learn to flirt with fear, you learn the difference between fear and danger, you learn your capabilities, you learn where your limits are, you learn that it's okay to fail, or you learn that sometimes failing has a price.

It's also a bit like what happens when you take a truly cold shower, or when you get into ice-cold water: it drags you back to reality instantly. Suddenly nothing exists except the cold, because there's nothing more real than suffering. And you're capable of concentrating like you never have before. You have to be completely and totally immersed in the moment. You can't do a precision jump at your limit in a half-present state. You have to concentrate to give everything you have, because you know that if you fail it may have consequences.

You see it on people's faces. They're laughing, chatting about something, and suddenly they look, they breathe, they shake themselves out a little, they go serious, and they do what they need to do. That discipline, that concentration, can be acquired in other ways — meditation, for example. But with an external element like danger, it's instant.
