Second video of the day. I wanted to talk to you about fears and the voluntary acceptance of fears.

If someone had seen me in the hallway before an exam throughout university, they'd have seen most people bouncing on their heels, nervous, scratching their heads, looking down, faces serious, going over their notes one last time. And if that person were an attentive observer, they'd look at the faces and see mine. Tall and upright — not that tall — with a smile on my face. A fierce smile. A smile that says: we came to fight and we're ready. I had a playlist for exams, a playlist with basically epic music, that told me: we're going into battle. And we're going into battle to win. We're not going to crawl and survive. We're going to crush the enemy.

When it comes to doing something that scares you, when it comes to doing something difficult, there are three possible reactions. One is you freeze and do nothing. Another is you try to run. And another is you decide to face it. Let's group freezing and running together as defensive attitudes, as a "I can't do anything against this."

What research in psychology shows is that whether you voluntarily confront your fears or let your fears wash over you and try to run away affects the physiological processes of the body. It affects which genes are activated. That affects protein coding. It affects your stress levels, your hormones. It affects the secretion of substances that support the development of new neural connections. And it affects how you assimilate the experience in a subsequent situation.

If an experience is difficult or terrifying and your response is: I want to get out of here right now, I can't take it anymore, please get me out of here, it's very easy for trauma to form. It's very easy for the next time it happens, you to freeze. And that's exactly how a paralyzing fear is created, a terror, a phobia of elevators, of open spaces, a phobia of whatever.

However, if instead of that you choose to voluntarily face that fear, you generally discover that you are stronger than you might have believed. And even if you don't come out of the battle victorious, you're proud to know how far you got, that you gave everything. And you never walk away with that paralyzing feeling of fear.

I've also experienced this a lot in climbing lately. For those who haven't done it: it's very scary. Scary to the point where it's easy to panic. There's a moment when you're on the rock and you say: get me out of here, lower me down, I can't take it anymore. And it happens. It's happened to all of us who've done climbing.

However, it's very easy to identify that there are days and there are people where you feel like you can take on the world. And the same scary situation you face with a "let's go." If we fall, we fall. You go for it, you fall, and you say: that was exciting! Again! And you open new routes.

And there are days where that's not there. There are days where you're mentally fatigued. You get on the wall and you fight, but you say: I'm not comfortable facing my fears today.

And not just that. It's very interesting because the number of bullets you have in a day to face fears is quite limited. You have a certain amount of fears you can confront in a day. You might have opened two or three routes with moments where you say: real fear, I'm way above the last clip, I have to keep going. You can fight through five or six of those. After that, your brain says: we can't handle any more of this. Rest. Please. And by the end of the day it's very common to feel more afraid than at the beginning.

What's very important is that the physiopsychological processes that follow when you take one path or the other are very different. And that when you turn that fear into aggression, into competitiveness, into valor, into courage, things come together. And you generally realize that you're far more capable than you thought.

An example I find very illustrative is how people with elevator phobia are taught to stop being afraid of them. You're not going to convince them that it's not scary. They'll argue that it is. And they're not wrong. It is scary. The solution is to expose them voluntarily, gradually. You put them in front of an elevator. First just looking at it. Then stepping in and stepping out. Then riding it one floor. Then two. And through voluntary, gradual exposure, the fear disappears. Because the brain learns that what scared me didn't kill me. And if it didn't kill me, I can handle it.

This applies to everything. The difficult conversations you have to have. The uncomfortable situations you have to face. Exams, projects that feel overwhelming to start. Voluntary confrontation. Putting yourself in the situation and saying: I'm going to fight. That's what makes you grow.
