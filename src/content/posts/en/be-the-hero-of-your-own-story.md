Be the hero of your own story. When you have to make a difficult decision, think: if my life were a movie and I were the hero of that movie, what would I do? This is an idea I heard from Joe Rogan a while back and I keep coming back to it regularly whenever I have to make a tough decision. I think it's a good mental model for acting and for making decisions.

What I want to tell you today is based on three books: The Storytelling Animal, by Jonathan Gottschall; The Hero with a Thousand Faces, by Joseph Campbell; and The Power of Myth, also by Campbell. All three were incredibly interesting and extremely illuminating to me when I was deep in the idea of myths, of stories, and how they persist in our culture and shape it.

The core idea is about what the figure of the hero is, how we've built it, and what role stories play in our lives. You could make the argument that stories have played an important role in our evolution in that they are our natural way of transmitting ideas, of sharing information from one generation to the next. And the reason for using stories instead of raw abstract knowledge is that when you tell a story, you're transmitting knowledge, but you're also transmitting emotion. A well-told story gives you the feeling that you're living what the character is living. And in that sense, what a story does is create a virtual reality environment where you experience, in a small dose, the difficulties, the highs and lows that the characters go through. This creates experience.

You don't learn without experience. You can memorize the concepts in any situation, but until you live it in your own skin you haven't absorbed it. An example that always amused me: a psychologist friend of mine, very much in the habit of giving relationship advice to her friends, with everything very clear in her head, was in a long relationship. The moment the relationship ended, she fell apart. And she herself told me she was surprised because, even though she conceptually knew everything she needed to do, she knew the psychological tools, she didn't know how to do it for herself. Because she had never lived it. And if you haven't lived it, you don't know it deeply.

When we hear a good story, we partially relive what the character experiences. This knowledge, while inferior to living the experiences themselves, is superior to merely knowing. And it has another advantage: you can live many stories, from different situations, situations you don't currently have access to but might have in the future. And you can live and relive them now in story form.

Where does the figure of the hero come from? The idea is that the hero is not a truthful description of a real person. The hero is a distillation of those positive characteristics that have led people to act correctly in life. We've seen a person do something well and move forward. We've identified certain traits. Then another person. And another. We do cross-matching over thousands and thousands of years, we eliminate the traits that aren't common and keep the ones that are. We build a figure that is an amalgam of positive personality characteristics. A white knight. A distillation.

These stories, these myths, these ideas are distillations of reality. They are not reality itself, they are almost more real than reality itself. They are what lies beneath it. Those common elements that have moved people forward over the millennia, in different situations and cultures.

So, taking this framework, I think it makes sense that understanding your life as a story and trying to act as the hero of that story is a very good framework for action. Because you're telling yourself what the ideal is, what's the most you can aspire to right now. It can be something as simple as wanting to skip the gym and thinking: what would the hero of this movie do? The hero would go. What would the hero do after a breakup? He'd rebuild himself. He'd get up and say: the situation is hard, but I can handle it and I can move forward. What would the hero do when someone betrays him and he feels like getting back at them? He'd be honest, he'd be clear, he'd have integrity. And even though life beats him up, he can take it and endure it and be a moral person in a way that gives him nothing to regret. The hero can make mistakes — the best stories are like that — but the hero is capable of asking for forgiveness no matter how hard it is.

This guide moves you away from those behaviors you don't want to do at a deep level. Think about what the hero would do. The hero wouldn't do it. And if you're the hero, you shouldn't either.

And it also orients you toward reaching your full potential. Many times it's not about avoiding bad behavior. It means voluntarily embarking on a difficult story where you're not sure you'll complete it, but you believe it's right at the edge of your abilities and that it's going to push you to grow. And when you face that challenge, that possible adventure, it's very easy to set it aside because these days we can set everything aside. We can live a mediocre life. But what would the hero do? Would the hero cower before these difficulties? Or, despite being afraid, would he embark on that journey? He would suffer. And he would come out victorious on the other side, with new learning, being a better person than he was before. Because the hero knows it's never enough, that there's always more to learn, always more room to grow.

If you think of yourself as the hero of your own story, you'll maximize your learning, you'll make better decisions, you'll improve your relationships. And after several years of doing it, I can assure you that you feel like the hero of the movie. And I can assure you that when you look back you won't have any regrets about the decisions you made.
