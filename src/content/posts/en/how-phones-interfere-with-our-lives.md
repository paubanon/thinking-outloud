Hey, humans. You all know, if you follow me a little, that I have a pretty complicated relationship with technology. I tend to think a lot about it and about the way it changes our behavior, our brain structure — as the book The Shallows describes — as well as our human relationships. It's something I've thought about a lot, because I think it's one of the biggest revolutions the human being has gone through. It's changing everything, and it's tremendously present in every aspect of our lives. It's filtering its tentacles into each and every one of them.

The ubiquity and ease of access are what make it a revolution the human being has never experienced before. No technology has influenced our lives so much and in so many ways. The other technologies humans have created were more localized, for something specific. For example, when we invented writing, it changed the way we relate to each other, but not that much, because it takes time to write and time to move things from one place to another. It made us more reflective, it made us learn to concentrate on a single task. The phone lets you communicate at a distance, but it's also something expensive and limited to the corner where the phone is. But with the internet access we have on our smartphones, the possibility of communicating with any person indirectly, anywhere, is completely changing everything the human being has been up to now in terms of brain structure and personal relationships.

When was the last time you were alone at home without your phone in your hands? When was the last time you were in your room with your phone off and you didn't look at it when you woke up either? Being at home on a weekend with your phone connected doesn't count. I mean a Saturday without a phone, disconnected, doing whatever you have to do: cleaning your room, organizing it, making your food, eating, taking a nap, studying, watching a movie, without needing to be connected.

When was the last time you went shopping without headphones in?

I think what we're often doing is running away from our own thoughts. Running away from ourselves. Running away from being alone with myself. Being alone with my own mind, and my mind wanders, and suddenly I ask myself: what am I doing with my life? Is this what I want? Is this the relationship I want? Do I really want to spend time on this and not on that? We get foggy, and in any small moment of stillness we pull out the phone. Any empty moment of contemplating the world, we reach for the phone.

And I think that makes us tremendously unhappy. Because even though in the moment it takes away the responsibility of thinking about what you're doing with your life, in the long run it creates a feeling of helplessness, of lack of control over your own life. That decisions are happening and you're not aware that you're making them, or that someone else is making them for you. By isolating ourselves from the present with the phone, what's happening now — which is what defines how your future will be — passes fleetingly between one screen and another, and you don't stop to think about it.

We get up, pick up the phone, a coffee, you brush your teeth with the phone, go shopping looking at the phone, oh this app isn't updating, I'll fix that, and the morning passes without having done absolutely anything.

I think the great value of meditation, for example, is partly that: there's nothing mystical about it, it's simply being able to be alone with yourself, in silence, in a room, without distractions. Without letting notifications and alerts interrupt you constantly.

And I have the feeling that this constant interruption corrupts reality. It corrupts certain magical moments that shouldn't be broken. A special moment with your partner — a moment of tenderness, of warmth, just the two of you, showing each other love — should not be interrupted by a notification in the background, because it breaks it instantly. It kills the mood, as they say. And even if you say "I won't look at it," there's already been friction. Like a gear grinding: it no longer runs as smoothly.

Reading a book should never be interrupted. A sunset or a particularly beautiful scene in nature should not be broken by a ping. Because you feel it inside you when you're in the present: you're at the top of a mountain and you say "how beautiful the world is, how incredible to be here." You're climbing or walking through the woods, you hear the silence. But if someone interrupts you and you respond and then come back, you wonder: was I even in the woods? I feel calm or I don't. I went physically, that's a fact, but I wasn't conscious of having gone. I wasn't there in that moment.

It reminds me of something a friend told me: that when you go somewhere, touch things. The more varied stimuli you give your body, the more you remember it. If on top of seeing, you smell, you pay attention to the smell, you touch the wall of a church, you keep a more vivid memory of it.

And I think that's part of the great unhappiness: not being conscious of the moment, and having the world pass by like a whirlwind while we look at a screen in our hands. Not being aware that you're making decisions unconsciously, or worse, that you're not making them and therefore someone else is making them for you.

So courage to everyone to have more moments of calm, more moments of personal reflection. To turn off the phone and say: I'm here, now. Or I'm with this person, with a friend, and I'm not going to let a phone interrupt that. I'm having dinner with my family and it's a moment I'm dedicating to them. I'm not available to anyone else.

The fact that you can be available to everyone else doesn't mean you have to be. That phrase changed my perspective. You can, because that possibility exists, but you have the power to decide whether to do it or not. You're perfectly free to say "everyone else can go to hell, I'm not available." And it can be for a reason as selfish as: I'm at home relaxing, I'm out for a walk, and I don't have to answer to anything else.

Tell me in the comments if you've ever reflected on how this affects us, if you've noticed changes, if you've had periods where you went on a technology detox and how it felt.
