Today I want to talk to you once more about distractions, procrastination, the internet, social media, purpose, and that whole package.

Since I finished my PhD I've been a victim, let's put it that way, of more distractions than I'd suffered before. I attribute it to more slack in my schedule. When I have a very tight schedule and many goals to meet, it's much harder for me to get distracted, because my focus is very clear: this goes here and if I don't do it now, it won't happen this week. Since I finished my PhD I have more free time and that's paradoxically made me less productive. Because now I have the option of putting off the work to some other moment when I feel more like it. And that's a problem, because there's never a moment when you feel more like doing things.

I have the sense that many of us regularly work below our potential simply because with more margin, work is almost like a viscous liquid that spreads through our schedule occupying as much time as possible. And we're only capable of concentrated work when we force ourselves with some kind of time constraint.

My capital sins are YouTube and Reddit. I had my appendix burst and while I was in the hospital recovering, I realized something. In that moment when I had nothing to do, bedridden for several days, I didn't use those forms of entertainment. All I did was read like a madman. And that made me reflect: if now that you have all the time in the world you're not using YouTube or Reddit, why do you use them when you don't have as much time? The answer comes pretty quickly. I realized I use these forms of entertainment not to be entertained, not because I like them, not because they give me something meaningful. I use them as an escape route to avoid doing what I know I want to do or need to do, but which is hard and requires concentration and effort.

It can be something as simple as starting a task. You look up and YouTube is already open. Sometimes not even to watch a specific video, just to open the feed and save four things to "watch later" and then close it. Why did you do that? To avoid the discomfort of: where do I go now, what do I have to do now, what are the next steps?

All these activities, these apps, are designed precisely for that: to steal your attention, with very low activation energy. It takes very, very, very little to open your phone. Whereas starting a rewarding task, like going to train, requires just a little more energy. And we end up falling into the pit of sloth.

It's like the situation where cleaning is incredibly tedious and boring — until the alternative is studying for final exams. Then cleaning the house is a party. Activities aren't so much hard or rewarding in absolute terms. It depends on what you compare them with. Your brain is constantly mapping all the possible things it has to do and making a constant evaluation of what's most worthwhile right now. And since the brain's process is very lazy, most of the time it picks the easiest option, even if it's not the most rewarding one.

How can we reduce the noise? My experience tells me that if the option is there, you'll take it. Fighting it through sheer willpower is almost futile. So how do we make life easier for ourselves?

One option is to raise the initial energy required to start the distracting activity. I regularly used to turn my phone off, put it between the shirts in the wardrobe, and close the wardrobe on the other side of the house. And it works really well. Throughout the morning it's very rare that you go and get it. And if you do, it's something very intentional. Airplane mode doesn't work — it has to be off, because what we want is for it to be hard.

Another option is to eliminate the possibility entirely. If I don't have Instagram installed, I won't use it. There are apps that block certain websites during certain time windows.

And the last action I've taken is to rob it of its distraction power. Since I often wasn't going to YouTube or Reddit intending to watch something specific, just "to see what's there," I installed a feed blocker. When I go to YouTube or Reddit, no suggestions come up. It doesn't show me "maybe you'd like to watch this." It doesn't suggest anything. I only have the search bar. That's it. And this is very useful, because when you eliminate the distraction options and only leave the ones you want, it's the equivalent of wanting to stop eating junk food and, instead of having it on the shelf, throwing it all out and only keeping healthy options.

I wanted to share that I get distracted too. Sometimes on channels or social media, people come across a certain way that isn't how they really are. I also struggle to focus, constantly. It's a constant battle for everyone, to a greater or lesser degree. And as you can see, sometimes just changing jobs throws off your whole schedule and suddenly you find yourself procrastinating.

This summer I've decided to eliminate all the futile distractions — Instagram, YouTube, Reddit — and only allow myself reading as a distraction. And above all, I want to cultivate presence in the moment. So that when I finish a task and don't know how to start the next one, instead of opening my phone, I take a walk and think about it. I want to enjoy life and have it bring me that peace I know I've had at other times.
