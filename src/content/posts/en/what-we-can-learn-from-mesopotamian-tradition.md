Today I want to talk about Mesopotamian mythology, elaborating on Jordan Peterson's interpretation along with some examples that come to mind. I'm basically going to tell you a story.

In Mesopotamian mythology — which is important to understand was the first great civilization we have a record of — everything begins with two gods: Apsu and Tiamat. Apsu is the embodiment of masculinity, of order. And Tiamat of femininity and chaos. This duality is one we find tremendously present in our day-to-day lives and that seems inherent to how we not only perceive the world but how it actually functions. In opposing pairs. Day and night, life and death, masculine and feminine, order and chaos, yin and yang.

When you think about it, it makes sense that the world would work this way. Because the world tends to operate on the simplest laws it can. Mathematically you can build a dynamic system with three elements, but the world tends toward the simplest thing that works. Bilateral symmetry appears everywhere: hips, brain hemispheres. Each hemisphere specializes in one of these interpretations of order and chaos.

It's important to understand that this doesn't mean one force is good and the other is bad. They are primordial forces that permeate the world. Everything else emerges from them and both are always present. Like when The Way of the Superior Man talks about masculinity and femininity: it doesn't say that men are masculine and women are feminine, but rather that archetypically men tend toward the masculine and women toward the feminine. Both exist in every being, just in varying proportions.

These two gods, Apsu and Tiamat, have children. And this first generation of offspring are something more earthly: the elements that make up the world. Water, fire, earth, metal, wood. It's a recurring theme in Taoist and Greek cosmology: from the primordial gods emerge the titans and the elements of the world. It's a way of describing how from the most fundamental — chaos and order — the earthly plane emerges.

Now, these first gods fight among themselves, a war develops, and in the course of this war they decide to eliminate Apsu — that is, their father, order. Because of this, Tiamat, chaos, becomes furious and creates an army of eleven monsters to fight her children. At the head of these monsters is Kingu, to whom she gives the tablet of destinies — to be interpreted as the crown, the one who reigns, the one who holds power. And Tiamat unites with Kingu.

This arc, in which the first gods kill the father and as a result chaos creates an army of monsters, represents the idea that when you behave in a childish and irresponsible way, without considering the consequences of your actions, and you wrongly destroy order, chaos is unleashed in ways you cannot control.

Facing the threat of Tiamat's army, the gods search for a champion who can confront her. And that champion is Marduk. Marduk is the one with eyes in every direction, which is interpreted as meaning he has total vision, he can see in all directions simultaneously. He can see the past and the future, he can see what's coming. He is the god of attention, we could say.

The gods ask Marduk to save them. And Marduk says: I'll help you, but in exchange I want the highest position among the gods. The gods agree. And Marduk confronts Tiamat, the embodiment of chaos, and defeats her. And from Tiamat's body he builds the world as we know it.

I find this a powerful image. Order doesn't defeat chaos by destroying it. Order defeats chaos by incorporating it, transforming it, building with it. Marduk doesn't eliminate chaos. He uses chaos as construction material. The world is made of ordered chaos.

And there's another dimension. Marduk, to accept the mission, demands to become the supreme god. This represents the idea that to voluntarily face the most terrifying thing, the unknown, the chaos that threatens to destroy everything, you have to have something very valuable to gain. You don't do it out of abstract altruism. You do it because the wager is your entire destiny.

I think these stories have been telling us something for thousands of years that we still need to hear.
