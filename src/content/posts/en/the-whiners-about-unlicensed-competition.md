I'm going to do a short video about something that has really annoyed me and that I think is a problem in our society: unlicensed competition. There's a lot of talk about this, people complaining and so on, but this video is a take from the other direction. This video is in favor of unlicensed competition.

Why? Because I think what people have is a desire for comfort that's off the charts. That is, if you with four years of a degree in any subject can't compete with someone who has studied a topic at an amateur level, what have those four years been good for?

This came up because I saw an Instagram story from a nutritionist complaining: "nutritionists are qualified professionals, with four years of training, etc., no to unlicensed competition." And it's like: no, no, no. Not no to unlicensed competition. Do your job so well that people without the degree can't compete with you. Personally, I couldn't care less whether someone has a degree. Especially in the field of nutrition, where they teach theories that have been obsolete for fifty or sixty years, and you want no unlicensed competition... that doesn't seem right to me at all.

I think what you have to do, if there's unlicensed competition in your field, is be so competent that people can say: this is a professional and this is an amateur. And, sorry to say, but if there's an amateur who has put in so much work that they've reached professional level, even without having paid for a degree and without having the certificate, from my point of view, go for it.

I think a free market does a lot of good for people. And it seems to me that the anti-unlicensed-competition stance is crying. It seems like what people don't want is competition. And I'm sorry, but the world, and especially the world today, is like that. There's going to be competition, there's going to be fair competition and there's going to be unfair competition. Your job is not to cry because you have competition from people without a title. Your job is to make your title so valuable that those people without a title can't compete with you. That's it.

Even more so today, where there are media that give you infinite access to information, that allow the user to form a critical opinion and analyze everything that's out there. Which prevents professionals from using the argument from authority to back up their knowledge. And also allows people who, for whatever reason, haven't had access to higher education to empower themselves. They have access to free education if they have enough will to do it. And from my point of view, if you've reached that level and you can demonstrate that what you're doing is done correctly, go ahead.

In my field, for example, I would never complain about someone not having formal education. In fact, many people throughout the history of physics haven't had formal education and have developed some of the most important theories in all of history. If I'm not mistaken, Ampere, or Maxwell, was one of those who deduced some of the most fundamental equations in physics heuristically. They had intuition, they had the drive to learn, they had obsession, and they developed it, and as far as I'm concerned that's wonderful.

A degree shouldn't be a piece of paper fetish. It shouldn't be: love me because I have the university certificate. A degree should be useful years invested in which you've learned enough to be an extremely competent professional in your field, so that you know much more than average and much more than people who train on their own, and therefore the client, the user, will prefer you because you're offering incomparable quality.

Some of you will say: sure, but the user's ignorance means people go to cheaper practitioners because they charge less. It's also your responsibility as a professional to inform the user of why they should work with you and not with others. And all of this is a call to individual responsibility rather than crying. Stop posting stories on Instagram. Stop complaining. Take ownership of the problems and say: I am responsible for this. And if there's a user who doesn't know who to go to, it's your duty to inform them. And if you haven't done it properly, it's because your website isn't well designed, your marketing strategy isn't well designed, and because you haven't put enough emphasis on results with clients.

So please, stop complaining and take ownership of your responsibilities. We all know how to complain. What's hard is accepting that maybe it's your responsibility and that maybe this person, even though they haven't invested four years of study in a degree, has dedicated enough hours on their own to be a trained professional.

I think the unlicensed competition we have today in many fields, mainly due to the liberation of information thanks to the internet, is something wonderful. It's a good way to give a nudge to certain groups who had become dormant and comfortable in their position of power, of knowledge, of authority, and tell them: hey, if you want to compete, wake up. Because people today are waking up and reading and researching. And I think that's great.
