It's been a long time since I posted a video, and part of that is because I've been traveling. I spent a month alone with my van exploring the Alps, and it was an absolutely fantastic experience. During that time I had a chance to reflect on a lot of things, and today I'm coming to you with one of them: the question of who you want to be.

Many of us have an image of someone we'd like to become. It might be training more, eating a certain way, quitting smoking, reading more, being more punctual, behaving better toward the people around us, being more generous, spending less time on social media, having a certain kind of job. Usually it's a million things.

One of the things I've always wanted to be is the kind of person who can travel alone. I'd never taken such a long trip alone, and it generated a certain anxiety and fear in me. My mind circled around things like: I'm going to get bored, I'm going to feel lonely, what if I feel insecure. But I've always admired people who travel solo. I've always sensed that they have something — a calm, a serenity. And I said: I want to be that kind of person. Simple as that. Without any further deliberation, I grabbed the van, grabbed the bike, the crash pad, and went off alone through the Alps. And it was absolutely fantastic.

Why am I telling you this? Because a lot of the time the "who do I want to be?" is framed in abstract qualifiers. Being punctual. Being disciplined. Being organized. And I say they're useless for the following reason: they're circular adjectives. Someone is organized because they keep their home in order. But why do they keep their home in order? Because they're organized. Those adjectives tell us nothing about what we actually have to do.

However, from the standpoint of cognitive-behavioral psychology, the only thing that matters is behavior. And by modifying behavior you modify your identity. Identity is not the abstract floating element through which our actions are filtered. Often our actions are what our brain uses to infer how we are. Your brain takes data from day-to-day life and concludes: therefore, I am X. And this means that to become who you want to be, you have to behave like the person you want to be. The popular saying: fake it till you make it.

Being organized isn't an abstract quality. Everyone knows how to organize. You designate a space for each object. The camera goes here, the computer goes there, the clothes go here in this order. And then you pick up the camera that's on the dresser and put it in its case. You pick up the computer that's on the couch and put it on the desk. Again and again and again. At no point do you need to invoke some supreme inner essence declaring you to be organized. The difference is people who do it and people who don't. And a lot of the time we use labels as excuses not to do it ourselves.

Apply this to my trip. Since I'd never done it, I had the label of "you're not the kind of person who takes these trips." Fine. You go and you do it. If it's within your financial and time resources, you go and you do it. And based on doing the things that the people you want to be do, you become those people.

So, orienting yourself toward the person you want to be comes down to something as simple as making a list of how that person would behave. Not just what traits, but what exact behaviors you'd like them to have. They're noble, they're honest, they always tell the truth, they train, they eat well, they spend time in nature, they know how to balance work and leisure, they show appreciation for the people around them, they know how to talk to strangers and make them feel comfortable. You break it down as much as possible. And every time you find yourself in one of those situations where you'd like to act differently, instead of acting the way you would, you act the way that person would. Regardless of the consequences.

Why do I say "regardless of the consequences"? Because a lot of the time we say: yeah, but I'm very tired. I don't feel like it. All of that is excuses. For a very simple reason: we tend to think that people who do the things that inspire us don't face any of the same barriers. We think that because they're disciplined, that person has no difficulty doing what they've committed to. But when you talk to people who have those characteristics and ask them how they got that way, most likely they'll tell you: I often don't feel like going to the gym either. I have to do it too when I'd rather stay on the couch. The difference is that they do it anyway.

When you orient yourself toward the person you want to be, you do things regardless of the consequences. Because a lot of the time being that person has fine print in the contract that you're not aware of. We might admire a singer, an actor, an intellectual figure, thinking their life is so much easier being where they are. We often don't see the doubts, we don't see what it cost to get there, we don't see what they've had to sacrifice. And it's up to you to decide whether you accept that fine print or not.

Because to do the things you have to do, you simply have to do them. In the vast majority of cases. People who meditate for years without interruption don't do it because every single day they love sitting down. They do it because they're stubborn as mules. And when they got back from a bar at 3 in the morning they said: no, I haven't meditated, I'm sitting down. And many times it was a terrible session, but they did it anyway. The difference is people who do it and people who don't. It's that simple.

Make a list. Set the behaviors in motion. And if among the things you want to be is someone who knows themselves better, I can't recommend highly enough the book ¿Por qué pollas haces esto? by Ramón Nogueras, which is what inspired this video.
