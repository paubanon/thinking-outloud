Atoms surround us. Atoms are everywhere and we take them for granted. What is the origin of all this?

To understand the origin of atoms, you first have to understand what an atom is. An atom is made up of a nucleus and a cloud of electrons that surrounds it. The nucleus itself is made up of protons and neutrons held together by what's called the strong nuclear force. Protons and neutrons in turn break down into smaller units. And the number of protons in the nucleus determines what element it is.

How do the first atoms originate? The first atoms originate at a very early stage of the universe, when the universe was tremendously hot, that is, it had enormous amounts of energy, so much that the individual components of protons and neutrons were constantly breaking apart and coming together. As the universe cooled, these components would come together but no longer break apart, because they're more stable when joined. And little by little, the first atomic nuclei formed, the first protons joining with each other and with neutrons.

The first element to form was hydrogen, being the simplest: just a proton, or a proton and a neutron in its variants. Why is it the simplest? Because any other element with more protons in the nucleus requires those protons to come together. And that's hard and requires a lot of energy. Remember when you were little and you grabbed two magnets and tried to push them together and they repelled? Well, that's a consequence of the electromagnetic force. When you take two protons, since they have the same electrical charge, when you try to push them together, they repel each other. Unless you push them together with so much force that they overcome that barrier, and then the strong nuclear force starts to interact and holds them together.

As the universe advanced and cooled, the hydrogen nuclei were still moving very fast and had enough energy that when they collided, they'd join together. And so the first elements started to form: hydrogen, helium and lithium, which are those with one, two and three protons in the nucleus. Beyond that, no more elements could form because the electromagnetic repulsion between them, with the universe now cooler, was insurmountable.

How do we explain that right now there's carbon? How do we explain that there's oxygen, that there's iron, that there's gold? Well, it turns out that in our universe there's only one thing capable of generating this. There's only one event, one entity, one object that has enough energy to take these primordial elements, this helium, this hydrogen, and heat them so much and subject them to such enormous pressure that they fuse together and start generating new elements. These nuclear plants are stars.

Stars are basically accumulations of hydrogen that, through gravitational collapse, start to heat up, and they heat up and heat up until they start burning, fusing. Because it's so hot and under such pressure that atoms start fusing with each other. And that releases an enormous amount of energy. That's how a star works: it's basically a nuclear fusion reactor. As they fuse, the atoms generate denser and denser atoms, until new elements are obtained.

What's the problem? Two. One, in this process elements beyond iron are not produced. Iron has certain characteristics that make it not worth fusing beyond it. So in the core of a star we find mainly iron. But on Earth we have elements heavier than iron. And that needs to be explained. And the other problem is: okay, elements are generated in stars, but we don't live in the core of a star. How did they get here?

Well, the cores of stars, as they become more massive and dense from the production of heavier elements, start to collapse gravitationally. If the star doesn't have enough mass, it can cool down and become a giant ball of iron floating in the universe. Those are brown dwarfs.

But if the star is massive enough, it weighs so much that it starts to collapse under its own enormous gravity. It squashes down so much, so much, so much, that the elements inside are subjected to tremendous gravitational pressure and start fusing, generating elements beyond iron. And this collapse produces so much pressure that the gravitational force can no longer hold it and it explodes, sending everything it had inside out into its surroundings. That's a supernova.

In the case of the star being even more massive, a neutron star could form, which is basically like an atomic nucleus the size of a star. Or even a black hole: it collapses so much that, let's say, it breaks spacetime and collapses into a point beyond which we can't see anything. And that's a black hole. That's why they're called black.

This supernova explosion is the only mechanism that exists in the entire universe for generating these heavy elements and distributing them. So the only way that right now on this planet we have metal in cars, silicon that makes this phone work, iron, carbon, oxygen, that we're able to live, the only way that could have happened is that in this region of space there was a star, or several, that generated all those elements, collapsed, expelled them into their surroundings, and those elements accreted until they formed our planet Earth.
