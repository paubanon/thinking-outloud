It had been a long time since I'd recorded a video. But it's been a turbulent time and my head wasn't ready. Ideas weren't coming, plain and simple. And that's fine. I waited until I had something to say before saying it.

Today I wanted to tell you that the last three times I went to Decathlon I didn't buy anything. Same at MediaMarkt. This might seem irrelevant, but it's interesting. The emotions it triggered in me are interesting, and how I identified and handled them.

For those of you who don't know me, Decathlon or MediaMarkt for me is basically a toy store. I have a great time browsing the little gadgets, the new things, all the little bits and pieces there might be. There's so much stuff where I look at it and think: wow, that's cool, that's great, I'd love to have that, tweak the camping gear a bit, look at this mug, this pot, this sleeping pad, good lord, so much smaller.

And the last three times I went, without any intention to buy something specific, just to browse, I didn't buy anything. And that makes me feel good. That's what I wanted to tell you.

I saw a lot of things that attracted me, a lot of things that could be nice, and I said: either you don't need it, or it's not the right time, or you could get it cheaper through Aliexpress or something else. But the main reason most of the time was: you don't need it.

It's a very strange feeling in the consumer economy we're in. When someone asks me: what do you want for Christmas? And I tell them: I don't want anything. Because I genuinely don't need anything. I'm content. And not only do I not want anything, but almost anything someone gives me is going to be clutter that'll cause me more trouble to have and carry around than pleasure.

For several years I've been choosing very carefully what I buy. Everything I buy is exactly what I need. And if it's expensive, so be it. But expensive because of quality. There's research behind every purchase I make. When I bought my laptop, I did a huge search across different models, brands, specs. I thought very carefully about what I need. Eventually I landed on Thinkpads, T series. I sat down and said: what do you need? I decided I didn't need a graphics card because I'm not going to do graphics work. What I needed was good battery, portability, and build quality and durability. And that's what I bought.

When I bought my backpack I said: what do you need? A do-it-all backpack I can take to Germany, to Greece, to Italy, to Spain, to the mountains, camping, that handles rain. Result: 5.11 Rush 72. It's expensive. I know it's going to last me many years.

And these trips to Decathlon, I saw a lot of things I'd like, a lot of little gadgets. But I didn't need them. Conceptually someone might say: you're a minimalist. Maybe. But I don't personally identify with any of these movements. It's more a consequence of being at peace with your own life and not needing any kind of object to feel excited about improving or to feel more valuable.

Because for example with all the gear sold for training, what they're selling you is not something you need. In the vast majority of cases they sell it with the idea of: buy this and you'll improve faster, this is what you're missing to improve. But the reality is no. What you're most likely missing to improve is consistency with your program. With the most basic equipment you can do a brutal workout.

Right now people ask me: should I get you something for training? No, no, no. Why? Because I have rings, I have resistance bands, and I have handstand blocks. I don't need anything else.

Same with clothes: with these clothes you'll run faster because you'll be lighter. Not true. What you need is to run more. With this stylish clothing you'll be more attractive to the opposite sex. It's a lie. It's all a lie. You'll buy the clothes, for one day you'll feel slightly more attractive because I'm wearing a new shirt. And the next day you'll be you. Just as happy or just as miserable as you were yesterday.

I suppose someone calls it self-esteem. Having enormous acceptance of yourself, while also having an enormous desire to improve. And knowing that to improve you don't need anything more than your own person and the desire to work on what you want to improve. You don't need new yoga pants, or a €400 Garmin, or a fitness tracker, or an Oura Ring, or a new dress. Nothing else. The attractiveness is in your person. And the potential to improve is in your person.

When you understand all of that, deeply embedded in your person, you go into stores and you see what's really there. And what's really there is that they're selling you need, not solutions. I went to MediaMarkt today too and there were tons of gadgets, all very cool. And you say: man, I'd love to have this connected to the TV, and some Philips Hue bulbs so I can change the color of the room. And you stop yourself and say: but I didn't have that need. When I'm at home, I don't have that need.

So a good rule I think is: only buy what's going to solve an existing problem. You're living your daily life and you identify a problem: I'd like this to be different, this would really help. For example right now I could really use a tripod that grips the mount properly so I can rotate my phone and camera without it falling. I could really use that. And I'll buy it because I'm going to get use out of it. A problem arose and I said: for this problem I need this object. And I'll buy it. And I'll buy a good tripod. A good tripod doesn't mean an expensive tripod. It means a good tripod.

But if I go to MediaMarkt I'm going to see a huge selection of things that solve problems I haven't had. Which is another way of saying they create need. Because what they're saying is: you haven't thought about this, but you have this problem, and here's the solution.

Generally when I want something I give myself a period of several weeks to think it over and do research. I never buy anything on the spot. If something's been on my mind for several months, it's probably worth buying. But not before. And those are my rules for buying. Hope you enjoyed it. Take care.
