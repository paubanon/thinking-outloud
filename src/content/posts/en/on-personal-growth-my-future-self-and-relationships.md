Good morning, evening. As you can see, I'm in Elche. Today I wanted to talk about something that has helped me a great deal to understand myself over the years and to manage the uncertainty of the future, which is something I think we all have a bit of existential anxiety about — the way things unfold — and sometimes we don't know how to handle it well, because ultimately it's hard to make decisions and know they're going to have repercussions in the future.

The idea is to understand that you're not going to be the same person now that you will be in the future. That seems trivial, but it isn't. What happens is that the changes are small, so you identify with your self from an hour ago. But do you identify with your self from 4 years ago? You'll probably say: no, I was young, I had short hair and I was much more naive and knew less about life. But you knew that person, you knew how they felt and you can still empathize with them. However, we're terrible at empathizing with our future self. Partly because we don't know them, but also because that future person isn't well defined. We don't know what they're like, we don't know how they'll analyze the options in front of them, or how they'll feel.

So, understanding that in the future it won't be your current self who decides helps relieve a little of the existential pressure of daily life. There's no point in thinking now about what decisions I want to make three years from now. Because when I have to make those decisions, I'll be a different person, with different experiences, with a different way of thinking and a completely different situation. It's not worth getting worked up about it now. Because not only is it not worth it, it's completely useless.

For example, the PhD question. I don't want to do a physics PhD, in principle. However, maybe a year and a half from now, when the opportunity presents itself, my situation will be completely different. Maybe I'll want to do it. Maybe in this year of master's I have left, I'll discover that I like research. I'm very happy in the research group I'm in. And I'll say, hey, why not? However, if they told me now: do you want to sign up to do a PhD in a year and a half?, I'd say no. But it's not now that I have to make that decision.

This process of understanding that you're going to evolve as a person has many angles worth observing. For example, it teaches you that people change: when it comes to others, it teaches you that people can regret their actions or can change for the worse. But it also helps you understand yourself and teaches you to let go. It teaches you to be a person who knows they need to evolve to continue on their path in life. You can't hold on to what you have and try to cling to it for the rest of your life. Sometimes to build you have to tear down. You have to remove things, let go of ideas, ways of thinking, decisions you thought you were going to make one way, and accept that now you've changed your mind.

And it's perfectly legitimate for a person to change their mind. It's not very ingrained in society because it's a common criticism for someone to say: "Well, a few years ago you didn't think that way." And you say: of course, that's what living does to you. The labels you impose on yourself weigh on you and can prevent you from seeing the range of possibilities open to you. If you've been saying for years "I'm the kind of person who doesn't want to do a PhD" and the moment comes when you do want to, you have an unnecessary identity crisis. Understanding that you're a person who evolves, you accept that before you didn't want to and now you do. And that's perfectly legitimate.

This also applies to personal relationships. To maintain a long-term relationship you have to accept that your partner changes and that you change, and that despite the changes you're willing to stay together because you know the sum is more than the parts. You like facing life as a couple better than alone. The moment someone doesn't accept that their partner has to evolve, the typical thing happens: "You've changed." Of course I've changed. A much bigger problem would be if the person I am five years from now were the same person sitting in this rocking chair, because it would mean those five years hadn't been worth anything.

And it's also understandable that many relationships are finite, that they end, not because the other person isn't the right one, but because they were the right person for this stage of your life and now you've changed. That doesn't mean throwing away a valuable person. I'm a strong advocate that relationships are built and you have to work at them. But to accept the changes you have to talk things through and decide whether it's worth continuing or not.

Something else I wanted to mention: being aware of this makes us a better person in the present too, because it teaches you to let go emotionally and intellectually in conversations. Accepting that you can change your mind is a very valuable skill. When you talk to someone you have to keep in mind that that other person might know something you don't, and that to learn that thing you might have to accept that you were wrong. It's not at all easy to go into a conversation with the mindset that you might be wrong and to weigh the other person's arguments trying to think: maybe this person is right. But it's a tremendously valuable skill because it lets you evolve more. Everything you change should be passed through a critical filter, and that allows you to tear down and build something better.

And going back to the beginning, how does this help you day to day to not stress about decisions that aren't yet within your reach? By postponing that decision. Not thinking about it. Being aware that you don't have to make it now and not making plans too far into the future, because those plans are unpredictable both because of the chaos of the world and because of your own personal evolution. Don't try to decide now what your life is going to look like. Plans one or two years out, yes. There will be times you embark on longer projects, a degree, a master's, and that's different: those are very serious projects you decide to take on knowing they're for the long haul. But don't get ahead of yourself making decisions that aren't yours to make yet. That's also important for living better in the present and handling the future better when it comes.
