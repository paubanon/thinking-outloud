Good morning everyone. A while back I recorded a video called "Music had a price," about the effect of music on different aspects of life, with an emphasis on its role as filler between tasks. Lately I found myself in a similar situation and wanted to reflect on it.

I've been putting in a lot of hours on my master's, working on a single topic, which means my head is constantly churning through that topic and also getting quite saturated with it. So it becomes very automatic: finish work, go for lunch, and immediately put on music, a video, a podcast, anything. Immediately after. Especially when I go from the university to home or go for lunch alone.

And one of the things I've learned is that this is a mistake. The brain needs silence and emptiness to process what you've been working on and to arrive at new ideas. This is something that's given absolutely no value in our culture. Nobody stops to think that doing nothing can have value, that interesting things can emerge from doing nothing. And these are things you're not aware you're missing because your brain doesn't notice what it's missing if it's never had it.

You can tell when someone is ignoring you face to face while you look at your phone. You detect that. But what your brain doesn't detect well are the missed opportunities that aren't planned. If you go out with earbuds in, you're in your own world. You go from one place to another, do what you had to do, get home. All fine. But if you go without earbuds and without looking at your phone, you open the door to things happening. They're random, unplanned things, and because they're unplanned your brain doesn't notice missing them. Maybe you run into someone, or you overhear a conversation and join in, or simply the fact that that emptiness exists is enough to make you start talking to the person in front of you. Maybe they're unfriendly and it goes no further. Maybe they're the love of your life. It's like that meme: one day you'll cross paths with the love of your life and you'll both walk past because you're both looking at your phone searching for the love of your life. The act of leaving space opens up opportunities that aren't planned and therefore you can't conceive of them and therefore you don't have the feeling of missing them. And that's dangerous.

But there's also another effect. When you do concentrated, serious work, you can't just jump from one task to another like that. If you arrive at the office or to study having come with your music, you'll most likely arrive restless, your head elsewhere, and need 20-30 minutes before your brain says: okay, now it's this. Whereas if I come to the university in silence, that emptiness on the way makes my brain start turning over what I need to do, how I'm going to organize it, and ideas start appearing, connections that wouldn't appear if I had music on. I've found that in those moments of emptiness I've had very good ideas that have significantly shaped how I study or given me ideas for new projects.

Our brain has what's called working memory. It's like the RAM of a computer: a temporary memory in which everything you're working on at that moment is stored. Once you finish the work, your brain takes what's in that working memory, sifts through it, refines it, connects it with the memories and knowledge you already have, and decides what to store and how. But that requires time.

A very clear example of why working memory is so volatile: if you're doing serious, concentrated work with a 30-minute argumentative thread and a large volume of knowledge in your head, and suddenly someone enters the room or the phone rings, all that knowledge evaporates. Then you sit back down and say: where was I? You need 10-15 minutes to rebuild all of that. It's like a house of cards. As you go deeper into deep work you're building a more complex, taller house of cards that requires more attention and more care. A breath of distraction and the whole thing collapses.

The same happens when you finish working. Finishing work and putting on music, looking at your phone, switching to something else: big mistake. You're not giving your brain time to process the working memory.

I've learned this especially around going for lunch. If I finish work, go for lunch, and don't put on music, don't put on a video, just do what I need to do without any additional distraction, what happens? It relieves a lot of stress. And many times, in the middle of the meal, something clicks in my head. Oh, look, this connected with that. Or: if I present this first and this second, the presentation is more intuitive. So I take out the paper notebook — because if I grab my phone the chance of a notification pulling out of my head whatever I've got is very high — I write down whatever it is, put it away, and keep eating.

Lately I listen to very little music. And when I listen to music it's because I genuinely say: I want to listen to it right now and right now I can. The fact that music can be present everywhere at any point in the day doesn't mean it has to be, or that it's harmless. Previous generations didn't have that option. Silence and solitude were an integral part of life — they have been throughout all of human history — and it makes sense that our brain does something valuable with that time even though it seems wasted.

We don't know to what extent filling every moment of solitude and silence with some kind of distraction affects our stress levels, our happiness, our ability to work in a focused and productive way. This is my contribution on this point. I hope it's useful to you.
