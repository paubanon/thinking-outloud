Good morning. Following on from yesterday's video, I'd like to talk about the mindset I think will take you furthest when facing an exam or test, so you can perform as well as possible while minimizing the stress it brings. We could call it, in Jordan Peterson's words, the hero's journey.

First: understand the exam as a challenge to be overcome. Not as a bad moment you're going to drag yourself through like a worm crawling through mud, but as a test where you have to prove your worth and you're going to do it as well as you can. With a fighting attitude. "No, no, I'm going to show you who's in charge here." Not the "oh, let's see what happens... oh, I don't know... oh, I'm scared." No. I'm going to go in and do it as well as I can. And that means always going for the 10. Always. With that said, being aware of reality and your options. You can go for the 10 knowing you've had three days to study because you had another exam before, and knowing the most likely outcome is a 7. But I'm going for the 10. As Oscar Wilde said: I have very simple tastes, I only want the best. Don't settle for less.

Another thing that helps manage the stress as it builds up: having given absolutely everything you had. Every last bit. Having put it all into studying, prepared yourself as well as you could with the time and tools you had. So that whatever happens, you have no regrets. So that if they gave you the same time and the same material again, you'd study it the same way because you did it as well as you could. For me that means sleeping eight or nine hours and training two hours a day. Eating and sleeping. But not wasting time on trivialities. Not putting on a film if you know it's going to hurt you in the morning. Not procrastinating on your phone. Not going out partying if you know it's going to ruin the next day.

In the worst case — the case where you fail — you need to have a clear conscience that you did everything possible, that you gave your best self, that you fought to the end. If you fail being able to say "I did absolutely everything I could with all the tools and time I had," there's no regret. On to the next one. That will make you go into the exam much more at ease.

It's also important to understand the relative importance of the exam. They're generally much less decisive tests than they appear. Stop and think: what actually happens if I fail? You're still going to have a roof over your head. Your friends are still going to be your friends. Your family is still going to love you the same. You're going to be the same person. You'll probably have another chance. Don't let fear dominate you because the impact of failing is minimal on what matters in your life.

Think, for example, that throughout history there have been people who had limbs amputated without anaesthetic. That's a lot worse than your exam. It might sound stupid, but knowing that people have been through much worse things and got through it helps quite a bit.

And finally: approach the exam as something you're doing voluntarily, not as something being imposed on you. "I'm going to take the exam. I'm going voluntarily." For your mind it's very different. Because it's not letting the fear rain down on you waiting to see if you get through it. It's that you're going to face the fear. When something scares you, facing it voluntarily, rather than letting it happen and seeing what comes of it, has completely different consequences for your brain. When you face it voluntarily, your brain activates the learning and optimal performance mechanisms, it says "let's do this, it's a fight and we're going to fight." Whereas if the fear comes to you, you go into panic mode, paralysis mode, other hormones get released and you're unable to perform. That's what blocks a lot of people in exams.

It's the hero's journey. A hero in the archetypal story never waits at home, cozy under their blanket, for the dragon to arrive. Never. They go out looking for the dragon.
