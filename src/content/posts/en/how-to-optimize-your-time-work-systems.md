Last week I had an unproductive day. It was a day where, despite there being a lot to do — the Enso Movers trimester was starting, I had paperwork to handle, things for the doctorate — it was a very unproductive day. I woke up, tried to sit down and work, started on one thing, didn't really know where to go with it, started on another, looked at the task lists and thought: yeah, there are things to do, but I don't know what's done and what isn't. And I ended up falling into an internet rabbit hole and losing part of the morning and part of the afternoon.

This made me reflect. Why did I have such an unproductive day? And the answer was that I hadn't thought in advance about what needed to happen that day. I didn't have a plan for the day, no idea of: I need to do this, this, and this, in this order of priority, in these time blocks. And because of that, faced with an enormous number of options, I was unable to distinguish what was important and what wasn't.

This brings me to this video, where I want to talk about the importance of having a work system. The importance of thinking about what to do, when, how, and in what order. And doing it beforehand.

It connects a lot to other videos I've made before about the importance of having a calendar and blocking time. And it comes from a book I recently started by Cal Newport called A World Without Email. The title, I have to say, doesn't do it justice, and that's one of the reasons I hadn't picked it up yet, even though it had been on my reading list since 2015 when it came out. I had the feeling it was mainly about email, as the title suggests. But it turns out the book isn't only about email. It's about the importance of having a work system, of having a hierarchy of values and objectives, and of having a well-defined structure that minimizes messaging between different parties to maximize the time everyone spends actually working.

Even though the book focuses on organizational work formats at scale — like a company — I found it extremely useful when thinking about it on an individual level too. The book's thesis is that if there's no clear, specific, hierarchical work structure — one where you think before working about how you're going to work — people end up in a constant send-and-receive of messages that just means nobody works properly. Everyone constantly jumping between work, email, email, work, with a torrent of information that overwhelms you and prevents you from concentrating.

But in this particular video I don't want to talk so much about that point, but about how to apply it at the individual level. Not as an individual within an organization, but what can I do with myself to have a better, more focused life and be more productive in any area.

And this is where the importance of planning comes in. A while back I realized that in a household it's much more efficient to have a cleaning schedule than not to have one. It sounds idyllic to think that people will clean when they see something dirty, that we'll all pitch in. But in my experience it's a terrible plan. People don't clean, people delegate responsibility, and you all end up annoyed at each other because nobody knows who's supposed to do what or when. I've found it tremendously useful to make a strict cleaning schedule where it says: you're in charge of this, you of this, you of this. And we all have it done by Sunday. Because not only do things get done and get done on time, but I know exactly what I need to do and when, and I also know what I don't need to do. If I know you're going to do your part and I'm going to do mine, I stop worrying about yours and focus only on mine.

This brings me to the point that thinking about what to do, when to do it, and how to do it is a fundamental element of productivity. You can't not have a schedule or a daily plan if you want to know what to do. Because not having a plan means not having a hierarchy. And if you don't have a hierarchy of values, how do you decide what's most important in the face of all the options in front of you? You don't know. And when you're in the middle of executing, you can't be evaluating what to do and how at the same time. It just doesn't work. Our brains are single-threaded.

I've found enormous value in having clearly differentiated moments for planning and moments for executing. You can't, as I've said many times in other videos, do your training and decide what to do in that training at the same time. Whether it's leg day or upper body today. Whether it's six sets or eight today. Whether it's a deload week or not. Because besides not having a hierarchy of values in the moment, you don't have a macro-structure, you don't have coherence. And if you don't have coherence at scale, there's no way to advance the project.

You need to have a goal, a starting point, and a map of what lies in between. That's what you do in your planning moment. I want to get here. In any area of my life: I want to learn to play guitar. Okay, how are you going to do it? Which days? Which course? With how much attention? You have to decide it now, so that you're not going through the week thinking: do I practice guitar now or not? Do I practice more? Less? An hour? Three? If you're constantly making decisions, you'll most likely make bad decisions because you haven't worked out what's important and what isn't. And you end up with a general dissatisfaction because you didn't do what you wanted with the guitar, didn't do what you wanted with training, and spent a ton of mental energy deciding when to do things.

So I think it's essential to know what to do and when. Because this means that even if you're advancing less than you think in an area, you're doing so knowing it's a conscious decision. For example, if you have to skip some Jiu-Jitsu classes to work more, if you haven't planned it you feel bad. You say: damn, I wanted to do four or five days and I couldn't because work got in the way. However, if you've made a plan in advance and you have a hierarchy of what's more important compared to what, you can consciously say: I'm going to skip this class because this other thing is more worth my time. And I know that will affect my performance, but I'm going to gain this from this other thing and I'm temporarily willing to make that trade-off. If you do it consciously and plan it, cognitively it's manageable. But without a structure, it can be terrible.

And when you don't have a hierarchy, you can't see the big picture. And if you can't see the big picture, you're lost at sea with no idea where you are or where you're going. You need a map and you need to locate yourself on the map.

And how do you build your schedule? You open Google Calendar and start with the most important thing. How much time do I want and can I dedicate to work? Put those blocks in. Next: training. How much time would I like to train? Given the calendar, how much is actually feasible? Put that in specifically. Sleep: I know that if I sleep fewer than this many hours I don't perform well, so sleep goes here. Food, rest, naps. Social relationships: maybe they don't fit perfectly, but then I trim a bit here and add a bit more there. That's how you build your schedule.

And once you have the blocks, for everything that can be planned within each block, also plan that. Once you have your training time blocked off, go through the same process for the training itself. Where am I? What do I want to do? Where do I want to go? Is this realistic given how much training I can fit in? No, revise. Yes, implement. A couple of weeks later, check, repeat.

People think they want free time, but that's not true. You don't want time where you do nothing. You don't want a vacation where you don't know what to do and time passes and you've done nothing and you have that feeling of: I could have gotten three times as much out of this time. You want time to do the things you want to do. You want to go on holiday, but you want to consciously say: today I'm going to rest. Tomorrow I'm going to that place. The day after I'm spending the whole day on the beach reading. This afternoon I'm going diving. Consciously.

My experience tells me that moderately organized free time — where you have time to rest, to read in the sun, to nap without an alarm, to talk to a friend without rushing, but where at the end you can look back and say: it was worth it, I did things — is much better than free time where you're in your room not knowing whether to go get a coffee or go for a walk or record a video.

And another thing that sometimes gets overlooked: having a system frees up cognitive capacity. If you don't have a system that says when to work, when to rest, what comes first, you're constantly making decisions. Do I sit down to work at 8 or 9? Do I work until 1 or 2? What do I work on first? This is chaos and it's terribly wasteful. However, if you know that this part of your work is going to happen on Thursdays at 11, from Monday to Wednesday you don't think about that thing. You don't have to constantly decide: do I do it now? Later? Tomorrow? No. You know that on Thursday at 11 you tackle that problem. And the rest of the time is cognitively free to focus on whatever's at hand.

To close: I wanted to emphasize the importance of having a system you stick to. And above all the importance of separating execution moments from planning moments. They have to be clearly separated. In my experience, the more separated they are, the better. If you can block one hour a week — Sunday, for example — to say: what am I going to do this week? When? How? The benefits are enormous in terms of how much work gets done, the quality of that work, and the amount of real free time you have afterward.
