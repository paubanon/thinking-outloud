Today I want to talk about an old concept, but one I've been turning over lately and want to develop a bit further. It's the idea of stagnation. As a personal trainer I've dealt many times with people who were stagnant or who said they were, and in my own practice I've been stagnant a million times and have had to develop tools to get out of it. Not just at the level of physical training, but professionally too. Off the back of a recent stretch in my PhD where things were especially rough, with feelings of stagnation, I've gone deeper on this idea.

And I'm here to tell you that if you feel stuck, the most likely thing is that you're not. It's a perception.

Because the first thing we need to say is: when we talk about being stuck, stuck relative to what? What's the frame of reference? When figuring that out, you have to consider your expectations of progress and how many metrics you're measuring. If you don't have either of those things clear, you can't say you're stuck. And that's why I'm here to give you what I think is the best tool for dealing with stagnation: measure. You need to measure more. If you're not measuring, you can't say you're stuck.

I'll use the training example because it's very clear and simple, and then I'll extend it to the professional and cognitive realm.

When you're training and you feel stuck, the first thing is to determine what you're measuring. If you're only measuring the weight you're lifting, it's very easy to say you're stuck. But what if you also measure reps? You might be lifting the same weight, but you can lift it more times. You're no longer stuck. But let's say you're lifting the same weight at the same reps. How many times can you do that set? Going from being able to do it four times to seven, eight, ten times is progress. And if you're doing it with less effort, that's also progress. And if you're resting less between sets, you're probably progressing. And then you can start factoring in externals: how much you slept, how much fatigue you have that day. There's a whole world of metrics that when you look at them together reveal many ways to make progress.

When we realize this, we see that stagnation is often not real stagnation. It's stagnation from a lack of resolution. When something is blurry, two things can look the same. But as you bring the image into focus, the nuances reveal themselves and progress — especially in very advanced plateaus — becomes more evident.

What are your expectations of progress? If you've been doing a movement for three or five years and you haven't advanced in three weeks, that's probably normal. When you've been in a domain for a long time, when you've become highly specialized, the amount of time you need to invest to get a certain return is very large. Progress might not come until a month has passed. And that doesn't mean you're stuck, it means the margin of return is smaller because you're more specialized. The more specialized you are, the more advanced you are in a domain, the more you have to measure, the more detail-oriented you have to be.

A while ago I went through a stretch where I felt pretty stuck. In the PhD I'd show up to work each day and leave at the end of the day feeling like I hadn't moved forward. And I decided to apply this strategy. I decided to treat research work like training. Before starting to work I'd describe in detail what I expected from that session, and when I finished I'd mark what I'd done. You're setting a goal, breaking it down into smaller goals, each of those into micro-goals. What do I need to do today to move this forward? What do I need to do this week? And in a month? And when I started doing this, I realized I wasn't stuck. At the end of the morning I'd done things that weren't finishing the project, but that were part of the project. If you focus on the large-scale goal, you lose the richness of those details. And when you notice you're advancing, your mood lifts. You're more upbeat, happier, more motivated. And the project moves forward more and better.

The same applies to any area. If someone tries to become more extroverted and sets that as a goal, it's a bad goal: when are you extroverted enough? You never know. You have to say: I want to reach the point where I don't hold myself back from talking to strangers. Okay, we have a target. How do we get there? By talking to one stranger a day. And you keep a record. Then two or three. Then you only count interactions that last more than a minute. And so on. The more you measure, the more you'll be able to tell whether you're advancing toward your goal or not.

This is the key to achieving pretty much anything we set our minds to: consistency and measurement. If you're consistent and measuring properly, I guarantee you'll move toward the goals you set. Or if you're not advancing, you'll know why and you'll be able to adjust the plan.
