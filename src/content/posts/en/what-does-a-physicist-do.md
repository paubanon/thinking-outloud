Good morning everyone. It's a beautiful day today, as you can see. Gorgeous sunshine, which doesn't happen often here. And to celebrate I'm going to record a video. I'd actually been thinking about it since this morning when it was still cloudy.

The video is about what a physicist does. What do we physicists do? People have an idea of what a physicist is, but I think those ideas are wrong, because they're too narrow. People tend to think physicists research the universe, particles, cosmology, the evolution of galaxies. We observe the sky, we observe elementary particles, we establish relationships between them. And yes, that is physics, but it's one part of physics. That's research physics. And those are the fields that are currently most in vogue and that get the most attention. People like hearing about this stuff because it's very fancy, very wonderful, almost science fiction. But what a physicist does and what a physicist is goes far beyond that.

What a physicist does is... learn to think. A physicist isn't someone who has a billion pieces of data and information stored in their head. No. When someone says physicists are smart people, you usually think they know a ton. And yes, but not that kind of knowing. It's not the kind of knowing someone has who's read a lot of history. It's a different kind of knowing. It's a knowing of interconnection. What we're very good at is analyzing problems: taking them, dissecting them, breaking them apart, modeling them, simplifying the parts that can be simplified, squeezing them and figuring out how they're solved. Our ability, what we're good at, isn't knowing things. It's knowing where things are and what to do with them.

I always say that a physicist's job is to do what a computer can't do: think. To draw logical conclusions: well, from here I do this, from here I move to this, from here I get this. A classic example — well, it's classic now because I'm about to tell it — is from a physics of the atmosphere exam. The problem said: "Knowing that such-and-such amount of water evaporates per day on Earth, calculate the temperature of the Sun." And you build up arguments from the basic ones, little by little, and you arrive at things you never would have imagined.

A physicist is someone who only knows how to ask why. And why does this work like that? And how does this work? And once they explain it to you: "Okay, but why? And why do we assume this? And why can we assume this but not that?" It's not enough to say "this affects that thing." It's: why does this affect that thing? Maybe we have a model and we say: "Fine, but this model only explains it up to a certain point. Can we make a better one?" That's what a physicist does. Ask why.

It's a very vocational degree, but very human at the same time. You go into physics because you say: "I don't know, I'd like to understand how the universe works." And when you finish the degree you realize it's incredible how many things you can understand. And notice — it's not that you know how everything works. It's that you know you're capable of understanding how everything works. When you finish physics, you're capable of understanding how a television works, a lamp, an electrical circuit, a radiator, a car. Not that you know it in advance, but you have the tools to understand it. And I'm certainly not putting down other professions, like engineering, which specialize in specific things and obviously know more about those things than we do. But you acquire a general understanding of how the world works at its most fundamental level, and that's basically our strong suit: knowing how to explain things and analyze them.

This quality gives a physicist an invaluable ability. Having someone in your circle who can analyze problems efficiently and find solutions, even without having encountered that problem before. That makes us very versatile.

When someone says to me: "You study physics, and what's that good for?", the answer is hard not because there isn't one, but because there's an excess of things you can do. Apart from the stereotypical physicists — who exist, and there are quite a few, people who work in labs, in research, in offices — there are physicists everywhere. There are physicists in medicine: we're the ones in charge of radiation protection equipment, creating imaging systems for areas we can't physically access, like nuclear magnetic resonance. There are physicists in all kinds of companies, from Mercedes to an elevator company. A physicist can work in a bank, can be a data analyst at Facebook or Google, can work in financial forecasting. Having so many tools and knowing how to use them means you can develop yourself in almost any situation. There are physicists who've specialized in psychology, in economics, because their strong suit is analyzing problems. And it doesn't matter what kind of problems.

The only fields where maybe there aren't as many physicists are the arts. But it's very interesting that this division — arts and sciences — doesn't really exist so much, because the physicists I know are incredibly intelligent people, but not only at a technical level. They're tremendously cultured people, tremendously interested in learning, capable of appreciating beauty. They read poetry, they study art, they study history. People who are very capable in many areas. And it's a curious asymmetry: I don't think there's much crossover of arts people studying physics in their free time. I'm not saying that to stir debate, just as a passing thought.

For me, being a physicist helped me build the company Show Movers. Without my mechanics and logic skills I wouldn't have been able to improvise with biomechanics, which to me is straightforward. You look at anatomy and if you're a physicist you already know biomechanics. It's helped me develop filters for what information sources are worth paying attention to. A physicist has tools to filter what's worth it and what's not. Evaluating possibilities and probabilities: is it possible that the lights people see at night are flying saucers? Yes, it's a possibility. But we have another option — atmospheric phenomena, or drones, or helicopters. Much less fun explanations, but infinitely more probable given what we know about the universe. And that capacity for analysis, which in that example seems trivial, when it gets to fields like nutrition, health, or psychology, is no longer trivial at all.

Anyway, a physics degree gives you the tools to know that maybe you don't know how to do things, but you know you're capable of learning to do almost anything. And that confidence, that autonomy of saying "I don't know how to do this, but I'll learn" — I think that's one of the best tools it's given me. You know that if you're a physicist and you put in the work, you can find a job in almost any field you set your mind to.

I hope you liked the video. It's not as educational as the others, but I hope I've cleared up some doubts about the degree and knocked down a few myths about what a physicist does. We're not all Sheldon Coopers locked in offices studying string theory. Take care.
