In this video I'd like to share some thoughts on Poor Things, a film that's generated a lot of debate and that I think is therefore worth debating. Unlike other videos, I'd explicitly like to hear your opinion, because it's a complicated film — I have a different view from several of my friends. Since I'm going to talk about it in detail, this video will contain major spoilers. If you haven't seen it, pause the video, save it for later, go to the cinema, watch it, form your own opinion, and then come back and comment.

The film is about a woman who has the mental age of a five-year-old, and over the course of the film she discovers the world, progresses, enriches herself, and matures. It's been widely praised for its feminist elements. And those of you who know me know I'm not a big fan of the word feminism — not because I think it's wrong, not at all — but because there are so many currents within feminism and using the word depends too much on the listener to make for effective communication. I'd rather debate point by point, individually.

I have to start by saying that, personally, I didn't enjoy the film much. I found it boring, the humor isn't my kind of humor, and I've never liked the dreamlike quality it has. But despite all that, I think the film is good. What I disagree with is the message it conveys.

The film revolves largely, from my perspective, around sexual liberation. It's true that part of it is about this character going out to explore the world, interacting, and maturing. But from my point of view, that liberation is completely overshadowed by the emphasis on an unbounded sexuality. The message being conveyed is that sexual freedom is total — no barriers, no taboos, you can do whatever you want, with whoever you want, whenever you want, and anything that holds you back is just social repression that should be discarded. To me, that's a bad message.

I'm completely in favor of sexual freedom among mature, responsible adults who take responsibility for their actions. And that's not what the film shows. It's the sexual freedom of someone with the mental age of ten. And that's somewhat disturbing throughout the film, because it also whitewashes prostitution in a very pronounced way, which I don't think is a great idea. The protagonist decides she really likes sex, needs money, goes to a brothel, becomes a prostitute, and the film paints it as something positive. I don't think that's a good message, and I think it does feminism a disservice.

The character's maturity is treated with a complete absence of responsibility. The film presents it as the character going from absolute innocence to an independent, mature adult, but to me it looks like nothing more than a person who suddenly has sex without consequences and does whatever she wants in the world without considering other people's feelings, the effect she has on her surroundings, or the impact she has on society. The message is: to mature, to free ourselves from society, you have to not give a damn about anything, do what you want, sleep with who you want. That doesn't seem like a good message to me. It doesn't seem like a rich one either. It feels pretty shallow. It lacks complexity.

For example, the main character never acquires what I'd call intellectual maturity. At no point does she learn that her actions have consequences, that you can't treat people as objects, that the world works better if you empathize. At no point is there any acknowledgment of the importance of things beyond sex and instant gratification. Friendship, the sense of depth that comes from doing worthwhile things, contributing something to society. None of that appears. And yet the final feeling is that the character has matured. It's an infantilized kind of maturation, from my perspective.

To give credit where it's due: it is true that the main character is shown as an intelligent person despite having this younger mental state — she likes medicine and becomes a surgeon. In that aspect I completely agree that it's something tremendously positive.

Another thing that bothered me quite a bit is that every man in the film is either a doormat or an abuser and a control freak who only wants to put this free creature that is the protagonist under his thumb. I fully agree that people like that exist and that we should have the right mechanisms so that when it happens, the person can safely escape that toxic relationship. But to make a film where every single man tries to dominate her, drools over her, and the moment she shows a hint of freedom outside the relationship becomes jealous, domineering, and possessive — that's a bad representation. It implies that all men are like this, which is wrong. Demonizing half the population is harmful. The same criticism could be made of many other films where the only women who appear are blonde and dumb, and I fully agree with that criticism too.

All of this together gives this otherwise good film a political character that I simply disagree with. I think it's a film that could have been better, could have been richer, and that dresses up a caricature of the feminist movement as intellectualism. When you dig even a little, there's a sense that there's nothing underneath.

That said, I'd like to hear your thoughts on this, because maybe I'm wrong. As a final disclaimer: I'm someone who is completely in favor of women's rights, of equal treatment, of treating people as people and not by their sex or gender. I think any kind of oppression or violence within a relationship should be prevented, and I think it's fantastic that we have a social system that supports any member of a relationship being able to safely leave it. I think there's still a way to go in that area. But I don't think the solution is to demonize either sex. And I don't think it helps the idea of a world where men and women treat each other properly — as individuals, with equal capacities, and humanely.
