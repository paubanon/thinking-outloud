Good morning everyone. We're back in cold and foggy Germany. I wanted to make a video to shake off some of the dust from being in front of a camera, it's been a while.

Since coming back to Germany after a long, wonderful, very eventful summer, I've found myself having to get back to work. And when you get back to serious work, you have to readjust. As I've talked about many times, the brain is very plastic and adapts to whatever stimulus you give it. If the stimulus you give it is that it doesn't need to make a big effort to concentrate for long stretches of time, the brain doesn't do it. After several months without needing to make this effort, I've found myself in the difficult task of reconnecting. We've all experienced this: after a vacation period, coming back and thinking, it's so hard to focus and put in the necessary hours.

Based on my experience with previous periods of study and all the difficult tasks I've tackled, I've designed a strategy for reconnecting with Deep Work. For those who don't know it, Deep Work is a concept developed by Cal Newport in the book of the same name: the importance of working in a very focused way during certain periods of time, generally no longer than 90 minutes or two hours, a total of around 3 or 4 hours per day maximum, and how critically important it is not to get distracted.

The brain adapts. This is very clear in Nicolas Carr's book *The Shallows*, in which he talks about how a constantly distracted attention throughout the day, produced by social media and the internet, has generated completely superficial thinking in us.

My first approach for getting back to focused work is to reduce the level of stimulation not only while I'm working, but throughout the entire day. I try to make each task I do completely single-focus. If I'm at the gym, I don't look at my phone. If I'm shopping, I don't look at my phone. If I'm working, I don't look at my phone. And if I'm waiting for the bus, neither. I only look at my phone at specific moments of the day when I consciously say: okay, I'm going to check my phone and reply to messages.

But the point I wanted to address here is: how do you do what you want done, but don't want to do? How do you sit down to work when you don't feel like it? Even though you know you have to sit down to work, you have these two layers: the layer that cares about your future self that says "you have to sit down to work because you're going to build this thing that will help you," and then the surface layer that says "yeah, but ugh, I can't be bothered." And you ask that surface layer: do you want to do something else? And it says: no. In reality the only thing it wants is to avoid hard work. You don't even actually feel like opening Instagram or checking email. Not really. You're just using it as a distraction from what you don't want to do. Sometimes it's even cleaning your room. It's not that you want to do that, it's that you don't want to do the other thing.

Life works by doing the things you know you have to do but generally don't want to do. And you don't want to do them simply because it's hard work, because it requires effort, because it requires concentration. And that produces discomfort.

How do we reduce that friction to start?

The first thing to understand is that it's not all willpower. Part of it does require discipline and willpower, but they're slightly different concepts. Studies clearly show that willpower is a limited resource. The more conscious decisions you have to make throughout the day, the less willpower you have left and the harder it's going to be to do the things that are hard for you. However, what many of these studies overlook when this is discussed, even though they do use the analogy, is that they say: willpower is like a muscle, it gets fatigued. But you train a muscle. By repeatedly making a difficult effort in different areas, you get used to facing that effort and you build positive feedback momentum.

What we mainly want is to turn those tasks into a habit. A habit you just put on autopilot and do. It's like brushing your teeth. You don't think about whether you feel like putting shoes on today or not. You just pick up the shoe and put it on.

What matters most is reaching the point where those tasks stop being optional. You want to reach the point where you stop asking yourself whether you feel like doing that task or not. You just do it. The question is no longer "do I feel like it?" The question disappears.

And the best way to get there is a schedule. A fixed schedule. Not "today I'll work if I feel like it." Not "today I'll work when I'm in the mood." At 9 in the morning I sit down. Full stop. And this brings me to the title of the video: how do you stick to your schedule when you don't want to? Same way. Your past self already made the decision. Your present self only has to execute it. You don't have to decide again. The decision is already made. And not having to decide enormously reduces the mental cost of starting. You reduce friction to a minimum.

And then there's a very simple trick that works really well for me: you just have to start. You just have to sit down and write the first sentence. You just have to open the file and read the first few lines of where you left off. 90% of the time, once you're inside, the motivation comes on its own. Work attracts work. Concentration attracts concentration. The only threshold that needs to be crossed is starting.
