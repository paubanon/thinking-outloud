Well, I hadn't planned to record this, but you left me a comment that gave me a small but important thought. I want it documented on this channel. It's the duality that exists when sitting down to work.

On one hand, you want to wait for a moment when you're relatively inspired or motivated. And you have to know when that's not the right moment, when it's the moment to say: today isn't the day for this, today I need to clear my head, today I need to go for a walk first, today I'm going to train, tomorrow I'll sit down and be more productive. That makes sense.

But on the other hand there's the absence of the perfect moment. The fact that many times there isn't a moment where you say: right now I'm at peak readiness to work. That moment never exists, there's always something better to do. And the more you put something off, the more you say "now isn't the time," then later you don't feel like it either, you leave it aside, and so on — the less suitable the moment becomes. Because what you have to learn is that everything is hard to start. But once you start, you have to learn to make whatever moment the moment. It's not something that happens to you, it's not something where you say: "it's time to work now because divine grace has fallen on me and I feel like it." It doesn't work that way. It works another way: regardless of how you feel, you sit down and make it happen.

You can adapt a little. For example, if you're writing, some days you dedicate to writing, some to calculating, some to making figures, depending on your mood. If you're very on, you write. If you're a bit more scattered, you design the figures you need to add. But you don't wait for the perfect moment. Because if you wait, that moment gets pushed back. It gets further and further away. Each day you feel less like it. Until there comes a point when you're incapable of picking up a project and moving it forward.

That said, as I mentioned before, there are times when you have to say: I'm not in the right state for this, I need to train, I need to go for a walk. But if you see it happening more than one day — not for a week, but more than one day — the "I'm not in the mood today," and you've sorted out your sleep, you're still training and eating well... It's not that you're not in the mood today. It's that you have to get in the mood. Time to work. Period. I sit down and I work. There's no other trick.

The motivation circuit works like this: I work even though I don't want to. That gets me closer to a goal I value. Then my brain rewards me with motivation because it sees that what I did is moving me toward the goal. What this translates to is sitting down to work without wanting to, starting without wanting to, the first things come out mediocre. And when you've been working half an hour, suddenly... boom! You look up and two hours have gone by.

This happens neurologically because when we think about sitting down to do a task that takes effort, the same areas that activate when you feel physical pain are literally activated in the brain. It's something unpleasant, something your body rejects. But what research has also found is that if you force yourself to sit down and work, after five or ten minutes that activation disappears.

The inability to sit down and work is a very common problem today. Partly because it's very easy to avoid: emails, the phone, whatever nonsense. Doing deep work is very unpleasant at first. Enormously. And it's a skill that has to be developed. The skill of: I'm not going to feel like doing this, but I do it. And as I say many other times, it transfers. Because it's the same neural pattern you strengthen when you don't feel like meditating at two in the morning and you do it anyway. The same one you strengthen when you have a donut in front of you and you don't eat it. The same one you strengthen when you don't feel like the cold shower but you take it. It's the neural pattern of: I'm capable of facing what causes me discomfort. And the surprising thing is that once you get past that initial barrier, everything becomes easier.

When I push myself to sit down and study without wanting to, I organize the environment so I can't allow myself any distractions: phone off and away, just paper on the table, in silence, in a room alone, nothing else on the table except what I'm going to work on. And that makes me sit down and only be able to work. The results are magical. But you have to get past that initial bar.

How do you avoid procrastination? I'm sure you've been in the situation of saying: I want to do this project, I want to get this done, yes, yes, yes. But every time you think about sitting down, you let it pass. And suddenly a month has gone by and you haven't sat down a single day.
