Hello, abricomaniacos. Today I wanted to tackle a heavy philosophical topic. This is a video with substance. I wanted to talk about language and the capacity of words to create our reality, and to what extent we are subject to our expression in constructing the world around us.

In Genesis, when God creates the world, he does it with words. He begins by naming the animals, naming the elements, naming the plants, and as he names them, he creates them. Finally, he names man. It's a symbolism representing the sacred character of the word. If you can't name something, it doesn't exist. It might seem like a radical idea, but it isn't really. You might say: "Well, if you don't tell me this is a sofa, I know what it is, I can touch it, it exists." Yes, for tangible objects. But there are other elements that aren't so straightforward, like the idea of freedom. If you don't know what freedom is, if that concept doesn't exist in your mind, does freedom exist for you? It's not a trivial question, and it's precisely one of the tools Big Brother uses in *1984* to control the masses: the idea of freedom isn't banned — it simply doesn't exist, because there's no word to name it.

Another author who explores this a lot is Ted Chiang, in his short story collection. It's wonderful, by the way, especially if you're a physicist — there are some brilliant passages. One of his stories is the basis for the film *Arrival*, also fantastic. It explores the concept of language from different angles. In the film — without spoiling it — the structure of language modifies the way reality and time are understood, moving from a linear structure like ours, where we write consecutively and understand reality as consecutive moments, second by second, one after another, to a simultaneous conception of reality, because the beings who use that language have no linear conception of time.

I'll pause for a moment on the physics side, because it's cool: there are certain formulations in physics where you don't understand reality as a sequence through time, but as a condition that has to be satisfied, and time is an irrelevant variable. The clearest example is Fermat's principle: light, when it goes from point A to point B, does so along the path that takes the least time. You can understand this linearly — the light travels and arrives — but you can also understand it as if the light hypothetically explores every possible path to reach point B and chooses the one that takes the minimum time. As if the light knew where it had to go. It's a bit unsettling, but it's mathematically equivalent.

Ted Chiang also has another story in which a person is injected with a drug and becomes tremendously intelligent. And the first thing they discover is that their intellectual capacity is limited not by their intelligence itself, but by language. The language they have is insufficient to express all the ideas they need and to establish connections between them. So the first thing they develop, as they become more intelligent, is a new, more complex and complete language. It's a very interesting idea: the word as the creator and organizer of our reality. And we don't usually appreciate it.

For example, in Spanish we have white, off-white, beige, greyish white, pearl white. Not much more. However, Inuit peoples have up to twenty different shades of white that for us don't exist. You can see them, but your brain doesn't identify them as different. It says "it's white" and that's that. For them, it's practically a different color.

Same with the concept of freedom: if it doesn't exist in your mind, you can't fully understand it. In Japanese there's the word *komorebi*, which describes sunlight filtering through leaves — not just any sunlight, but that sunlight interspersed with shadow, with the slight, shifting pattern characteristic of summer days with a hammock nap. We all know it's not the same to lie there as to lie in direct sunlight. It has different connotations. But until you name it, the element doesn't achieve completeness in your mind.

Another Japanese word is *shinrin-yoku*, which means "forest bath": that feeling of calm and peace we get when, after a long time in society, we temporarily retreat to nature and reconnect with ourselves, stress levels drop. We've all felt that feeling. But because our language has no word to define it, the idea has a vague boundary. It floats like a cloud. It doesn't exist as a compact element: "This is this, and I tell you and you understand perfectly what it is."

Language and its structure profoundly modify the way we understand our reality. And we're not aware of it. Words have great power. With words you can hurt a person, cause real damage, and we have no doubt that damage is real. What you say matters. Be aware of what you say.

And finally, as a practical conclusion: expanding your vocabulary is a very good investment of time. Read a lot, to expand it. Not to say "I know more words than you," but because different words describe different elements with different nuances. And until you have a word to define it, those nuances are lost. You can't express yourself with that clarity, and above all you can't perceive reality with that complexity. The more vocabulary you have, the greater the nuance with which you'll understand reality. And the better you understand reality, the better you'll be able to face it.
