The art of sharpening swords is an art very few people practice, and one I think should be practiced more. Because sharpening your swords lets you know your weapons better and lets you defend yourself and fight when it's necessary. Maybe some of you are a little confused by the title, but maybe not so much once we talk about what a sword actually is.

A sword is your tool for combat. It's not a weapon made to cause harm — it's a tool you have to learn to use. We all have swords, but most people use sticks. Blunt sticks, fragile sticks, chipped swords.

It started a bit from a scientific writing course I did recently at the university, where we were discussing the critical aspect and open debate. We talked about how a certain professor, in a course on critical thinking, every year invited anti-vaccine groups to participate in his class and debate with students who dismissed them with great conviction as crazy people who have nothing to say. And repeatedly, every year, when the anti-vaccine groups fought with the pro-vaccine students, they won the argument. And they won because they had sharper swords. From having to expose themselves regularly to combat, they'd been forced to refine and perfect their arguments over and over. To the point where, despite being wrong, they were capable of winning the fight. Meanwhile a mind convinced by its own ego that it possesses the truth, with no need to face combat, develops weak tools. Blunt, rusty swords that are useless when the moment comes.

And when the moment to fight arrives, you can't sharpen your sword. Because sharpening your sword requires time, care, space, reflection, attention.

All of us have convictions about different topics. Yet rarely do we stop to think about what those convictions are based on. Why do I think this way? What happens if someone attacks my convictions? How can I defend myself? Most people don't stop to think about what their arguments are in support of a position. Many times they don't even stop to think about what their position is. It's just something that happens.

I think this is a very bad way to move through the world. Because you're not stopping to think critically: what do I think about this? Are my arguments logical? Are they arguments based on emotional impulses? Which might be the case. And in that case, stopping to reflect on them, observing your sword, examining the blade and the guard, means you might change your mind on a topic. But many other times what you do is reinforce and elaborate in greater detail why you think the way you do. And then you'll be more effective when it comes to fighting.

How do you sharpen your sword? The first step is finding a problem. Something that irritates you, or something you don't have a clear position on and want to develop one. Or a topic that comes up repeatedly in your daily life. It can be political, personal, any kind. Something where you've found yourself lacking the tools to defend your position well. You've felt that yes, more or less this, but deep down you know you haven't refined your argument.

Then you identify your sword: what your position is, what your tools are, their strengths and their weaknesses. This is a very important critical perspective: thinking about what you think about things, as if from a third-person view. Don't just accept a position. Think about what do I think about this and why do I think it. Sometimes this exercise can lead you to discard your position on a topic. In that case you have to find a more solid one. If you hammer that position hard and you're consistently able to break it without being able to reforge it, maybe you have to abandon it and adapt to a more antifragile one, more robust, capable of withstanding the blows of the hammer. And even though it's not pleasant to say "I have no choice but to accept this is better that way," that's a critical attitude.

Once you have a piece of iron, something to forge, you want to know if you're articulate enough about this position. Can you argue it robustly? Without fallacies, without personal attacks, without creating strawmen? You can start introspectively: if I had to attack this position, how would you defend it? And then do it with others. Try to debate. Bring the topic up in conversations with people who hold opposing positions. Something that can spark a light, friendly debate. That's combat practice. You don't want to jump straight into combat with someone who has a lot of experience. You want to test the waters, see what counterarguments come up, identify your weak points. And when you have them clear, when you leave a debate saying "I didn't articulate this well, I need to think about it," when you get home, grab a book, look things up, argue, repeat.

Iterate this process regularly. You can read, listen, talk, think, write. All of them are tools for sharpening your sword.

Once you have a sharp sword — and sometimes sharpening it requires years of work — you want to know when to use it. You don't want to be someone who enters combat brandishing the sword like a madman. Because you'll cut yourself. And they'll cut you. The sword has a purpose and can be used in many ways. I'll borrow a metaphor from Patrick Rothfuss: Kvothe, the protagonist, talks with his sword master and asks that if he uses the sword to cut a wooden stick or to prop open a window, isn't that an improper use? And the master laughs and says: the sword is a tool. How terrible would it be to have a sword that you only used to kill people?

When you use it, you want to use it with humility, elegance, and precision. You want to know exactly where to cut, how to cut, and how deep. To maximize the effect you want to make. And that sometimes means not going as hard as you can go. In order to create a greater opening in your opponent and be able to cut deeper later in the argument.

As I say, having a sword and not knowing how to use it is stupid. So, just as you learn to sharpen it, you have to learn to use it. And that only comes hand in hand with humility, with honesty in debate, with not just wanting to win but wanting to have a good fight. There are times when you have to accept that you've lost. And you want to be a good samurai. You want to be able to have an honest fight and win honestly.
