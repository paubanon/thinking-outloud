Have you ever sat down to work or study and felt like what you were doing was maximally interesting? Like everything was flowing, no friction? One thing leading to the next and the next and time just disappeared? And you didn't have time to think about picking up your phone or not? No distractions? Have you ever felt that state of flow?

Probably yes. But probably other days you've felt the opposite. Days when motivation is through the floor, every step costs an enormous amount, checking your phone or the distractions or looking at the clock to see how much time is left of this suffering. Probably that too. But probably very few of you have stopped to think: why? What did I do right on those days when I was totally focused? And what did I do wrong on those other days when things weren't flowing?

A couple of weeks ago I was in that phase where everything was flowing, one thing leading to another, every time you finished working you ended the day satisfied. I took a vacation and when I came back I had a week of classes, I was a bit sick and because I was sick I didn't have much energy to do other things. And when I finally recovered and got back to work, I realized it was really hard to focus. What had happened? From having low energy, I'd indulged more in some behaviors I'd had extinct in the past: browsing entertainment websites, watching a series. And I'd gotten used to that feeling of instant reward. My brain quickly picked up the dynamic: this is the speed at which we get the reward and this is the amount of effort we need. And it's very little. The moment I had to ask my brain to make an effort, to concentrate, to do the things that take work, my brain was saying: what if we don't? What if we do something else?

To understand why this happens, we have to talk about the dopaminergic circuit. Dopamine is a neurotransmitter that generates motivation. It's produced in anticipation of a possible reward. The amount of dopamine you generate is proportional to the motivation and the size of the reward you're going to get.

And here's the key: there's no separate dopamine circuit for the morning and another for the afternoon. There's no separate one for vacation and another for work periods. There's one dopamine circuit that manages your motivation and reward in relation to everything you do. So if you indulge in activities that provide a very fast and fairly large stimulus — because they're designed for that, to keep you spending a lot of time on them — when the next morning you tell your brain, now we're going to sit down and write for three hours in complete silence, well, your brain is probably going to resist. Because it'll say: yeah, but yesterday we were doing the other thing. Why aren't you giving me what you gave me before?

If you're constantly switching between the need for total focus and cheap entertainment, it's going to be very hard to get into that flow state in the focused moments when the brain is maximally motivated. The brain tends to prioritize activities that give it an easy and quick reward for evolutionary reasons. If we can get something similar in terms of brain interest, even though we know it's not as good long-term, with less work, the brain says: let's do that.

How do we get out of this loop? Over the years, paying a lot of attention to my behaviors, I've developed what I call monk mode. In principle it's a life structure designed for exam periods, which I developed when I was at university and realized that if I entered a bubble of total focus — and not just the moments of focus on the task, but all the rest of the day organized in a way that supported those moments of focus — those moments were tremendously satisfying and productive. Paradoxically, they could be shorter, I could get more done, and I'd have more time to train.

How do we do this? If what we're asking our brain to do is maintain uninterrupted attention on a low-stimulation task, let's try to make the fast-reward stimuli throughout the rest of the day as minimal as possible. For example, what if we replace series with a book? A book can be very addictive, but it's less stimulating. It requires visual attention, has no sound, fewer colors. What if when I'm walking to the shop I don't put on music? The stimulus I have is the walk itself, my thoughts, interacting with people. What if I train without music some days? I turn my daily life into a way of living where the need to concentrate is greater. And that way, I train my brain to: this is the pace of life, this is how long things take, and I don't need a small spike of novelty every two minutes.

Our dopamine circuit is very malleable. It adapts very fast. And it adapts to the bad stuff even faster than to the good. But the point is it's adaptable in both directions. If you get used to your daily life having little stimulus, to having limited phone use, to eliminating empty distractions like Reddit or Instagram, you adapt very quickly. And when you adapt, your daily life isn't boring. It becomes normal. It's your standard. And you're just as fine, but without those stimuli. And in return, when you need to concentrate, you'll be able to focus much better and you'll find work more motivating.

The same way someone who goes to the gym gets used to progress coming at a certain rate: if your expectation of progress is small, when you collect those rewards it's going to be very motivating, and that's going to make the workouts themselves more attractive and you'll enjoy them much more.

It's about creating an ecosystem that supports the kind of attention you want to have. And understanding that we can't ask our brain to be totally focused when at the same time we're giving it constant, quick distraction. That also happens on a smaller scale: if you spend half the morning answering emails and suddenly the emails run out and you say, finally I can get to that project, and you sit down and don't know where to start and feel scattered... you've habituated your brain to a very frantic pace of many things one after another, and now you're asking it to sit down and write. It's not going to happen. You have to decouple those tasks, you have to put spacers in between.

Understanding how our brain works so we can modify our behavior and make it work for us, motivating us and making our lives as interesting as possible in pursuit of the goals that matter to us.
