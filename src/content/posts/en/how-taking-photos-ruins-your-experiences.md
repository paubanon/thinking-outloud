I find this super interesting because it's something I've experimented with for a long time and played around with — living experiences without documenting them. I did it at first because in today's society, with social media and everything, documenting something is basically what makes it exist. It's like if you don't post it, it didn't happen. Since I had the feeling that was the case, and every time I find something that makes me uncomfortable I try to address it or understand why it is, I decided: okay, you're going to live some experience and not post it. Or you're going to be having a great time with someone and you're not going to be thinking: we should, let's take a photo, let me take some photos of this.

The first shift is taking photos because you like taking photos, not taking photos thinking: I'm taking this photo because I'm going to post it later. That's the first mental shift. And the second mental shift is being there and saying: look how beautiful this is. I'm not going to take photos.

Technology is so ubiquitous in our daily lives that we don't realize how it affects us. We take it for granted and think it's harmless. But it isn't.

And it's happened to me, and I'm sure it's happened to you too, of being with someone and having a really good moment, laughing, or it's not even a good moment, it's a moment of silence because the conversation topic runs out or whatever, and someone picks up their phone. If when you're together and alone one of you picks up the phone, you don't get those moments of both of you just sitting there, looking at the ceiling, thinking: we've run out of things to talk about, let's see where things go from here. It's something that also really bothers me in my family. My dad in particular is more into watching TV than anyone else in the family, and I really don't like it when he turns it on. Because the act of putting the TV on while we're eating means that no matter how much you try, you're going to have half your attention on it. And that means conversation topics don't emerge. Because many times, without the television on, you sit down to eat and, since there's no TV, random conversation topics come up. And that makes you forge better bonds with your family, because unexpected topics of conversation arise. It's not that you arrive and say: I want to talk about how my day went, I'll tell you and that's it. No. You start talking and since you don't have the TV distracting you, you start to wander and pull on a small thread, and you say: oh, this thing happened to me. And that's how bonds are forged.

I also remember another similar example that talked about how phones were preventing relationships from forming in companies. If you don't have your phone, you sit down and since you've got five or ten minutes before it starts, you say: hey, how are things? How's that project you were working on? And you've talked, and you know that person knows about your problems and cared about them, and they remember, and they ask. And that's how friendships develop.

You're not aware of what you're missing when you use social media and technology, because you can't see it coming. The future is too complex to project. I remember one of the first times I did this was on the Camino de Santiago, which I did with three friends. And we established a rule, after finishing the camino, that no photos from the camino would be posted on social media. Because we had the feeling it was something so intimate, something so personal, something that had marked us so deeply — living so many days together and walking... a relatively spiritual experience. That we refused to post it. It felt like prostituting the camino, because then you were no longer doing it for the camino itself, you had the feeling you were doing it for people to see that you'd done it.

And it's not easy at all to separate those two things. In the moment you get a reward from something like the likes when you post a photo. It's not easy to step back and say: no, I did this because I wanted to. The brain tends to think that if it has a quantifiable reward, that's why you do it. So to save myself from that, I decide not to post it.

With this I'm not saying don't post photos on Instagram or Facebook. Do whatever you want with your life.
