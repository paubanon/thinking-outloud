It's nearly 11 at night. Very late here in Germany. I just spent an hour finishing studying cosmic inflation, because I didn't want to leave it half done and wanted to finish before dinner. It took me a long time to understand. It was an idea I'd read about quite a bit, but it didn't make sense in my head. I couldn't explain why we postulate that there's an inflationary period and why it works the way it works.

I think it was Feynman who said that you understand an idea well when you're able to explain it in plain terms. That connects well with his study method: you study something, you explain it, and where you see that you can't explain it is where you haven't understood it well. You go back and back and study it again. And the fact that I just spent a good while — hence the tired face — studying inflation, turning it over and over until I understood it, is the reason I love physics.

I love physics because it's hard. And also out of a human curiosity about how the universe works. Those questions you step out of the shower and think: and how does the universe work? Well it turns out that physics, alongside philosophy — which go hand in hand, I'll talk about that one day — are the only disciplines that have arguments so complex that you have to struggle to understand them. Other disciplines can be hard in the sense that the experimental methods are complicated, or that you have to memorize a lot. But for an idea to be so complex that it simply doesn't make sense to your mind, and you have to reshape your mind to understand it — that's beautiful.

You can almost physically feel your brain stretching. As Mario Alonso Puig said: when the brain has to stretch to accommodate a new idea, and once stretched it never returns to its initial position. Once you've absorbed a new idea and let it sink in, you're never the same person again. Your brain has changed to make room for that new idea.

It's a brutal effort. You feel pressure in your head. You say: I don't get it. I don't get it. You take the idea and turn it around, illuminate it from this angle and that one, look at it from different projections. Some shed more light than others, and you start to build a picture of the concept you're trying to grasp. But your mind doesn't need to reshape itself if the idea isn't complex enough. If you're reading things you already know, or in terms that are too simple, you don't grow. You read it and think: fine, I've learned a little bit, but you haven't had to struggle.

As Jordan Peterson said: life takes on meaning when you have one foot in order and one foot in chaos. When you have enough capacity and the material you're working with is comprehensible enough that you understand something, but it has a point where you don't understand. You have to work at it. It has a point of chaos, of uncertainty, where you have to reshape your mind to accommodate the idea or to understand it well enough to critique it. To see why it makes sense and where it stops making sense.

And it's a very satisfying feeling to spend hours and hours turning over a complex idea and finally understand it. Super rewarding. The same has happened to me with understanding time dilation, with learning to work in multiple dimensions. In university it's almost a daily thing: you see something and think I know what you're trying to say, but I'm not getting it. You have to keep turning it over until you understand it. And if I don't understand it I'm not satisfied.

Same with philosophy: I need to turn an idea over, understand the author's point of view, why they construct reality the way they do. And it's very beautiful. To the point that when I get into it, the hours fly by and I don't mind spending my time on it.

I think that's when you find something that gives your life meaning, at least in part. And it's something worth spending time on.
