In today's video I want to share one of my rules that I've been applying for a while now, but that I don't think I've ever made a specific video about. The rule is very simple. Complaining is forbidden. Absolutely forbidden.

This was also rule number one for coach Charles Poliquin, who called it No Pissing, No Moaning. This rule came out of a difficult period I had while working on my master's thesis. I was complaining a bit too much. And I was with a partner who was also going through a hard time, partly because of COVID, and she was also complaining more than usual. And the result was nothing positive for either of us. After that experience, I decided to make it a rule that complaining is forbidden. Because I realized that complaining brought nothing to me and brings nothing to the people around me.

My first experience with this rule was on one of my trips back and forth between Spain and Germany, on a trip I wasn't very happy about. People ask you: so you're off again? Are you looking forward to it? And the natural response was: no, I'm sick of it, I'm going back and it's going to be cold, it's going to be dark, I'm going to be working while here I have sunshine, I have my dog, I have my friends — the last thing I want is to go back there. That was my script. But I decided it wasn't a good script because every time I said it I reinforced the idea that I really didn't want to go.

So I decided to do the opposite. Every time someone asked, I'd say: I'm really looking forward to going, because I have a job there that I really like, I'm very lucky to have a great house, I have good friends, I have a forest, when it snows it's really beautiful, it's an international community, I'm learning a lot. And I'd list, mentally or out loud, the series of positive reasons. And curiously, after saying it two or three times, I felt much better than when I said I didn't want to go.

Over time I've been noticing this behavior in myself and in others: sometimes we complain and all we're doing is reinforcing the idea of a bad situation. We find ourselves in a non-ideal situation and we want to share it with the people around us. But that sharing often ends up being a victimization, a constant lament. "How are you doing?" "Terrible, I'm fed up, this is weighing on me so much." And you haven't shared any new information. You've just told a story where you're doing badly and the other person has to comfort you.

We humans have a tendency to focus on the negative. Evolutionarily it makes sense: if something is going well, it's cognitively efficient to ignore it. What's going well is going well. You don't need to pay attention to it. However, if something is going badly, focus all your attention there, because you need to fix it. The problem is that when a situation can't be fixed in the moment or drags on for a long time, that focus stays there. And when you stay focused on that topic for too long, you lose sight of the whole. Of all the good things you have, of all the wonderful things.

When you want to evaluate the story of your life — which is something we do regularly every time we think about who we are — if you only focus on the bad, the narrative is that everything is going wrong. And when you project to the world that everything is going wrong, the brain says: everything must be going wrong. We're miserable and our life is a disaster.

When you put things in real perspective, when you step back and look at everything and accurately describe what's actually happening, the brain says: most things are going well. We don't need to stress. There's only one small thing that needs attention and we're working on it.

What's the difference between complaining and sharing? For me the big difference is that when you share you're providing new information about a situation in your life to others. You're talking about what's happening. But what I categorize as complaining is repeating the same story to people who already know that story and adding nothing new. You're wallowing in the mud. The first time you tell them: "Hey, I'm in the mud!" And people say: "Poor thing!" But the second time: "Hey, I'm still in the mud!" Another "Poor thing!" It feels good, that little pat on the back, but it helps nothing objectively. And it even makes things worse, because you're telling yourself the story that you're in the mud, when the reality is that maybe you only have one foot in the mud and the rest of your body is fine.

So my rule: you tell people once. When something goes wrong, you talk about it, of course. It's not at all healthy to keep things entirely to yourself, or to repress them, or to ignore them. I think when someone asks you "how are you?" it's possible to say: "well, it depends. Generally good, but in this one area things aren't going so well, I have these problems, but we're working on it." That's already a different narrative. You're putting things in perspective.

And when the people around you ask again — because if they care about you they will regularly — "if there are any updates, I'll let you know. If you don't hear from me, it means there are no updates." Don't generate focus on the negative topic beyond once. Because when you have something going badly, not only does your attention focus on the bad, but if you have people who care about you, the attention of all those people focuses on the same thing. And every conversation with your parents, with your friends, with your partner revolves around it. Every conversation: "how's it going?" And you tell the same story. And without meaning to you slip into a spiral of negative narrative.

My rule is: you tell them once, and when they ask again you say you'd rather not talk about it. And in the meantime you talk about something else, because there are a thousand things to talk about. Which leaves you cognitively free to focus on how good dinner was last night, or how well that class went yesterday, or anything that lets you separate your attention from that negative narrative you've created.

This also has a lot to do with meditation. Many meditation practices consist of this: when you sit down to meditate and a negative thought appears, you don't dodge it, you look at it. You don't let it worry you — you turn and look at it and say: you, what do you want? I've already heard it, what are we doing with this thought? And the mind says: oh, they're looking at me. Well, I just wanted to remind you that this thing is going badly. And you say: I know it's going badly. Anything else? Any new information? Anything we're going to do? And since you're alone in your room the mind says: well, no. I just wanted to remind you. And then it tends to go quiet when you look it in the eye.

So, by all means, share your problems with the people who care about you. Don't keep things to yourself — it's hugely positive to have people you can lean on. But don't wallow in the mud. The victim mentality helps absolutely nobody, ever. And you'll see over time how the internal narrative and the internal physiological response change once the external narrative has been modified.
