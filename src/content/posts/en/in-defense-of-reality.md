Ladies and gentlemen of the jury, I find myself here today to argue in defense of reality. It might seem very trivial because ultimately we all think reality is real and we don't think much about it. But I think it's important to emphasize that reality is the most important thing. And to remind ourselves that reality is the only real thing.

What do I mean by this? It started with a book by Alain de Botton called The Art of Traveling. In this book the philosopher talks about travel and philosophizes about it: about anticipation, about the journey itself, about how it transforms us, about tourism. And the first chapter I found, aside from entertaining, illuminating.

In the first chapter he talked about the anticipation of travel. He had taken a trip, I don't know if it was to the Bahamas or Barbados, he had an idea of what the island was like because he had seen photographs. Whenever he saw a poster of Barbados, he saw the white beach with the little palm tree and the sunset and the completely empty sea, and he found it fantastic. And when he arrived, he realized something that many of us have experienced: that an image or an idea doesn't capture the reality of the situation. Despite being in the same spot as the photograph, with the same visual scene, it turns out it smells rotten because there's a dead seagull next to you, there's a child screaming behind you, there's the noise from the beach bar's air conditioning. And even though you're in the same place, you're not in Nirvana like you expected.

And another thing he mentions is that on one of these beaches, he realizes he wasn't enjoying the moment, that his head was ruminating about work stuff. He realized that when imagining the trip, he had forgotten that the eyes that were going to take in that scene came attached to a body. And that body was going to make the trip too.

I found it very illustrative to think about something that has happened to me many times: being in a situation and wanting to capture that moment with a photograph to share it, then seeing the photograph and saying, it doesn't do justice to what it was. It could be a moment of watching the stars with someone, it could be a mountain, it could be a sunset. And precisely in that attempt to capture it, many times we let the moment pass and we didn't enjoy it.

But that wasn't what I wanted to talk to you about today. I wanted to talk about expectations and the difference they have with reality. And the problems this causes.

For example, when planning a trip: the expectation that when I get there, because it looks so paradisiacal, I won't have problems, I won't have worries, everything is going to be great. And it's not true. Because when you get there, you arrive and you have the same problems and the same headaches you had here.

I've mentioned this in other videos too: the expectation of not enjoying the moment and postponing it for the next step. I can't take time off now because I'm in my last year of school. I can't now because I have college entrance exams. I can't now because in university the grades count toward the scholarship. I can't now because they count for the master's. Now the master's, now the PhD, now the promotion. And like that you've spent your life postponing enjoying the moment, thinking that at the next step you'll be fine. And it's a fallacy.

You have to make the day-to-day full and absolutely enjoyable. That doesn't mean you don't have to put in effort, but the day-to-day should keep you happy. And the expectations that in the future that's going to change are false expectations that aren't going to happen if you don't do anything to change it.

Many times we tend to idealize our partner, to think that when I have a partner everything will be fixed. You don't think it like that, but you have that feeling of I wish I had this. And the reality is no. When you get it, if by some chance you got what you want, you'd have it and 10 seconds later you'd say: okay, but I still have all the same problems and I still have to put up with myself.

It also happens with buying an object. How many of you have experienced this? I want to change my phone. I really want to change my phone. You go ahead, spend 300, 400 euros on a phone. You turn it on and say: how beautiful, how elegant! Only to 15 seconds later say: how many more things can I do with the same phone? The same as before. The dopamine hits you up to there. And a certain frustration.

The same thing can happen with people buying clothes, buying sports equipment: "no, no, I'm missing this to be able to train, I just can't have a good workout without this." And they get that thing, they start using it a little and right away: I need pants with more reflective material. And so on infinitely.

It also happens with the expectation of a job versus actually doing it. This happened to me with Enso Movers. Thinking about doing a job is usually very exciting, very cool, until you actually have to do it. Until it becomes the monotony of waking up every day to sit down and work on the Enso Movers programs, in silence, in my house, at my computer. Nobody pats me on the back, nobody encourages me. I have to design my own website, I have to set up the video for social media, I have to edit them. All of this you do alone in your house with your computer. There are no coworkers to talk to, no cafeteria to go down to for a walk.

I've also seen it a lot in training. A lot of people who want to achieve certain benchmarks and then aren't able to put in the hours. They can't understand that to get that thing they wanted, what you have to do is, consistently, train alone in your house, or in the park, or at the gym. And the vast majority of those training sessions are boring. It's showing up and doing what you have to do. Plus you leave tired, because it was a good workout. No rush, no dopamine.

What people want is to wake up one day and be able to do a bunch of physical things they couldn't before, feel exultant. And that's wonderful. But life isn't like that. The reality is that every time you achieve a new skill, you don't explode with joy. You're happy, but it was such a gradual, little-by-little progress, and you've put so much work behind it, that the day you achieve it, for you it's obvious that you were going to achieve it. It's like, of course it was going to happen, because I've put all this work in. It's not that exciting because it's not a surprise.

I think the problem is an idealization, a lack of realistic expectations, and I think it's influenced by the consumer culture we move in, in which you have access to everything instantly. I want to eat a donut, I have it. I want to buy this, Amazon brings it tomorrow. Is it sex right now? Pornography. We've been badly spoiled by immediacy. And the problem happens when there are things that aren't immediate, that require work and effort.

I'd like to suggest you play at identifying when you engage in this behavior of idealizing an object, a trip, a situation. And think about whether that's really what would happen if you got it. Put yourself in the shoes of your future self who gets it and think about whether it's actually going to be like that or if it was an excuse. Because for me the answer most of the time is: when I buy this I'm going to keep being the same person but with one more piece of junk in my house.

But other times the answer is no, no. That thing is going to add a lot of value to my daily life and it's worth it to me. And you buy it and say: how great to have this, I use it regularly, it lets me have a richer life. Yes, that sometimes happens.

Life is wonderful and the closer you are to it I think the more fully you live it. With its difficulties, with its "this is going to take me six months." So let's start today. Because if we have false expectations that writing a book is going to be easier than it's going to be, we'll never write it, we'll never start training or eating well or taking care of our health. It will always be postponed.

So start today. Accept that it's like this. Accept that everyone who has written a book found it to be like this. Accept that everyone who does things worth doing has walked the same path. Because there are no shortcuts, there's no immediacy, there's no magic pill. And knowing when we're conjuring magic pills in our heads, identifying them and eliminating them, for me has been a great help in my life and in how happy I am.

I hope you liked it. Cheers.
