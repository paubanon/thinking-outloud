What if I told you there's one thing you can do with your phone that will boost your productivity, improve your mental health, improve your relationships with family, make you sleep better, and help you know yourself and stabilize your emotions? You'd probably do it. And if you know me, you probably already know what the answer is. It's turning it off.

I'm a creature of habit, conservative as that might sound, and I like having rules. One of my rules is that the phone gets turned off at night. And when I say turned off, not airplane mode: it actually turns off. Another rule I implemented a couple of months ago is that on Sundays the phone turns off. The phone doesn't exist on Sundays. Sundays are for me. The nights rule ensures I sleep peacefully, and the Sundays one means those days are reflective and calm.

Outside of that, I always have my phone on. Sometimes I put it in airplane mode to work, but it's always on. And today I realized I have a certain need to feel it has to be that way. Like you have to be accessible to others. Today, for personal reasons, I had a strange morning and put the phone in airplane mode on the table. I got on with my things and noticed that a small part of my attention was still on the phone. And I stopped and thought: you can turn it off. You have the freedom to turn off your phone whenever you want. You don't have to be connected all the time. Hey, if you feel like turning it off one afternoon, you can turn it off. Because the phone is a tool. It's not a vital organ, it's not an obligation. You don't have to feel that obligation to be available to others. You decide when you're available.

You can turn it off. And turning it off lets you be with people a hundred percent, or be alone a hundred percent. Either way it forces you to be present in what you're doing. And unlike airplane mode — which is very easy to undo, you just tap it and that's it, and even in airplane mode you can still pick it up and fidget with it and open apps or look at photos or whatever pointless thing lets you avoid being alone or being productive — if it's off, you can't. And when it's off it takes time to turn on. That's good. Because the time it takes to turn on means there's a big initial barrier. It gives you a lot of time to think: do I really want to turn this on? What for? What do I want to do with it on? And when you turn it on you're very conscious of it. You understand it, it does what it has to do and you put it down.

When you're with someone — with friends, with a partner, talking with someone — it lets you do what I've talked about in other videos: be completely present, not have your head somewhere else. And not just meaning not picking it up. Turning it off lets you not even think about the fact that it exists. Not think about: ugh, I can see a light blinking at the other end of the room... no, right now I'm with this person. Turning it off means knowing for certain that there's nothing waiting for you there, that there's nothing imminent. And it opens up the possibility that, for example, if you want to explain something to someone and normally you'd say: let me look it up on my phone and show you. If it's off, sometimes you say: let me draw it for you. And you draw it. And then you have a memory on paper. Sounds like a small thing but it's something we've lost.

When you're working, same thing: it forces you to focus and to use other resources instead of reaching for the phone. Because when you're working and you want to procrastinate, it takes a second to turn airplane mode off. If it's off, no.

And when you're alone it lets you feel completely alone. That you're home alone and you're doing whatever you're doing — looking out the window, tidying the room, cooking. You're completely present in that. And in your head the things that need to ruminate are ruminating: your personal problems, your ideas, your emotions, the ideas about your projects. None of that gets processed if your head is in the phone.

I find it very beneficial to know how to be comfortable alone. Very much so. Not just because it's made me more whole as a person, but because it's improved my personal relationships. And I think it's something more people should understand. The problem is that being alone is hard and you have to work at it. It's not something to take for granted. It's not just a matter of: I turn off my phone and stay home. It requires the will to want to be alone and being open to learning what solitude can teach you. It means knowing sometimes how to recognize when your body is asking for solitude. Sometimes it's just for the challenge. I've gone to the cinema alone, simply because I wanted to see a film and no one was up for it. And I had a good time. Being alone at home in silence doesn't mean you always have to be. But I think it's fundamental to having a well-furnished mind.

And in that sense, as I say, turning off the phone is a radical change. I wanted to make this video to put on the table the radical idea that you can turn off your phone whenever you want. And I say it's radical because I know people who never turn off their phone. Who at night put it on silent, not airplane mode. And when you think about it: you put it on silent because you want information to keep coming to your phone. You just don't want it to bother you. Notice how far the addiction goes. Break that. Allow yourself to turn it off. Don't be a slave to the social need of: this has to be this way. You're fine being disconnected.

You don't have to be available to people all the time. You can reserve the right to not be available. And it doesn't have to be because you're in a work meeting. It can simply be: I don't feel like talking to people, this is my time. Same deal: you have the ability to turn off your phone.
