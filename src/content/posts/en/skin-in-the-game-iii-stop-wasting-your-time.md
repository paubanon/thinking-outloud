I didn't plan on making a third part to this video, but the ending of the previous one left me with this feeling that the cake is missing its cherry.

You'll have noticed that I don't edit my YouTube videos, I don't cut them, I don't add titles, I don't add background music, I leave the silences, I leave my doubts, I leave my tongue-ties, I leave my mistakes, I leave everything. I decided to do this because I reached the conclusion that I don't have time to edit. Editing takes a lot of time and a lot of effort. And I realized I don't have time because I'm busy living my life instead of worrying about what I give to others. That sounds very selfish, but it's the reality. I have a very full life, I try to keep it that way, not because I have a big ego, but because I like having my projects, working on them, experimenting with them. And I'm not going to spend two hours every time I make a video editing it. I'm going to make an effort to put my opinion out there, what I've learned, my experiences. But I'm not going to waste that time because I'm going to be busy living life.

What I realized from watching a lot of YouTube is that YouTube creates the false illusion that you're living life, that you're learning things. When that's not the case. YouTube is better than the other social networks, certainly, but it's still consumption. And I've realized that I feel fulfilled not when I consume, but when I create. When I create content or when I experience it. When instead of listening to an Alan Watts video about how wonderful it would be how beautiful life is, instead of watching videos of people like me giving you these talks, I go live it, I stop rationalizing.

I stop thinking that I need more things to learn. I stop thinking that maybe I'm still missing something. That's the YouTube trap: you always think a little more, a little more. A little more of Peterson's talk. A little more of the Alan Watts interview. One more video explaining the importance of fasting. One more talk from such-and-such university intellectualizing this concept. A little more about the effect of exercise on cognition. A little more of: I'm not quite sure how to structure my training, not quite sure which exercises to include. A little more of: is it a 16-hour fast or 18? Or when 24? And if I do a three-day fast once a month is that better or worse than one of 16?

When what you really need to worry about is living your life. Putting it into practice. Playing, discovering. Experience is far more gratifying, produces results, and will teach you much more than YouTube ever will.

YouTube is great because you can put up quality content and it teaches you things that last over time. But what we see on social networks is an excess of intellectualizing, of rationalizing, instead of a real desire to live. On Instagram what you see is people who want to look like they're living that life. People who are actually living don't worry about showing it. They're preoccupied and occupied with living their life to the fullest. If you're at peace with yourself, if you have nothing to prove to anyone, taking ten photos until you get the perfect one and posting it seems like a waste of time. Because you have more important things to do.

And YouTube fools you with the feeling that there's always something more to learn. I watch a video about 12 Rules for Life and I get this feeling of: yes, yes, that's right, motivation, fire. But that's not real. That's an illusion. That's intellect. That's potential. And as I was saying in the previous two videos, action always beats potential. It's better to take those twelve rules and try to implement them in your life, see how they fit together, what nuances they have when you apply them, than to watch another video of another Peterson talk going over the same ground.

What your body, your brain, is asking you is: live it. Put it in motion. Play it out. See how it goes. And that will teach you more than many YouTube videos. But YouTube has that drawback: it promotes the feeling, the illusion of learning, when what you need to do is go out and live your life.
