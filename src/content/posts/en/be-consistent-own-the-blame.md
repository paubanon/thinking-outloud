Good morning everyone. A little Sunday reflection. The other day I suggested a sprint program to my roommate. For those of you who don't train, start training. And for those who don't know, sprints are a brutal workout. You finish dead. It's super hard, but very effective and useful. Anyway, I suggested this workout to my roommate the other day. And one of the days he had sprints scheduled, we ended up playing volleyball with some friends. Three hours. He'd also gone to play soccer before. And at the end of the volleyball, at nine-thirty at night, he asks me: "Ugh, I played soccer earlier and now with the volleyball... does this count as the sprints?" And I said: No. And he said: Come on!

What do I mean by this? That we tend to play mental tricks on ourselves all the time to avoid feeling guilty. He asked me if it counted as sprints because he didn't want to feel bad about himself. If I say yes, he doesn't feel like he skipped the workout. But the reality is that he had skipped the sprints and he knew it.

Be consistent with your decisions. You decided to skip the sprints to play volleyball and soccer. Period. You decided that. Whether you did it consciously or unconsciously is another matter. Start taking your decisions seriously and being coherent with what you actually want. Start learning to say no. And start identifying when you make conscious decisions and when you make unconscious ones.

Faced with a situation like the one the other day you can do several things. Either, at the end of the volleyball at nine-thirty at night and being exhausted, you say: damn, I still have the sprints to do, and you do them anyway. That's the attitude I always take. And it's the attitude that has kept me from missing meditation almost a single day in a year and a half. The only thing that has saved me is saying: it's one in the morning, you're very tired, and you haven't meditated. Then you suck it up and meditate now. And next time you'll have done it earlier. Period.

Or you learn to say no to the social commitment: I'll do my sprints and then join the volleyball. Or you move the workout: if I have to meet someone at eight, I train at five. Two or three hours, and then we meet up. But instead, if what you do is say "well, I'll skip the workout today," you're not taking yourself seriously. And if you don't take the workout seriously — something you're doing because you want to, in principle — you're not going to take yourself seriously at work, in your life, in anything.

What makes a team workout more important than an individual one? That if you bail, you let everyone else down. You have a responsibility to others. The workout is imposed by others, from this time to this time. All of that makes it possible to delegate the feeling of guilt. You're a servant, in that sense: another person is making the decisions for you. A person gains total control over themselves when they learn to value themselves and their own decisions as much as or more than other people's decisions. And learn to say: this is my workout, it comes before social commitments, because it brings me closer to my goals. And if I have to say no to volleyball today, I do my sprints and then join. And you say it: I have to do sprints. But you could skip it. Yes, I could. But I don't want to. Because it's my workout and I have a commitment to myself. And that commitment should be even more important than the ones you have to other people. Because you are the center of your world. Either you take yourself seriously and fully commit to your purposes, or life's ups and downs will take you wherever they want.

It's also very important when working from home, which a lot of people are dealing with now. Why is working in an office easier? Because another person imposes their will on you. The hard part is imposing your own will on yourself. Saying: I am going to wake up every day at this time and I am going to work this many hours uninterrupted because I want it that way. And despite there being no external factor, my internal motivation is strong enough that I stick to it.

We always look for excuses to skip it. Like my roommate: well, does this count as sprints? No, it doesn't count. They're not sprints. You skipped them. Period. So you can do the sprints later and suffer, which is hard when you're very tired. Or you could have said no to the social commitment and done the sprints first. Or you accept that you skipped them and carry the guilt and the discomfort of: I had this commitment to myself and I broke it.

Which can actually be something positive if done consciously. In my case I did it consciously: I had to do acrobatics, but I've had no social contact for three months, today I'm going to prioritize building friendships over my acrobatics. I decided that and I decided it consciously. The problem is when you do it unconsciously and then look back and say: damn, I screwed that up.

My best recommendation in those cases is: do it anyway. As hard as it is. If you have to meditate at three in the morning after coming home from a night out, do it. If you have to train after an hour of soccer and two hours of volleyball, do it. And next time you'll think about it before. Or own the blame. Own the blame. Let that guilt gnaw at you, let it make you feel bad, and say: I let myself down, I let my decisions down. And use that energy, that frustration, to make better decisions next time.
