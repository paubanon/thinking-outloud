There's a principle I find very useful in human relationships and that I keep trying to apply: when someone does something that bothers or hurts you, assume ignorance before malice.

That is, before assuming that the person did something with the intention of hurting you, consider the possibility that they simply didn't know. That they weren't aware of the effect their words or actions would have. That they were acting from their own limitations, their own fears, their own way of seeing the world.

This doesn't mean excusing everything. There are unacceptable behaviors that deserve to be called out regardless of intent. And there are people who act in bad faith, that also exists.

But in the majority of everyday conflicts, in the majority of misunderstandings, in the majority of situations where someone disappoints you, the most likely explanation is not that they're evil. It's that they're human, and humans make mistakes, have blind spots, aren't always aware of how they affect others.

Why does this matter?

Because the interpretation you make of what happens determines how you react. If you interpret that someone did something to you on purpose, your emotional reaction is going to be much more intense. You're going to feel betrayed, attacked. And that reaction will damage the relationship.

If instead you start from the premise that it probably wasn't their intention, you can have a conversation. You can tell them what happened from the perspective of "this affected me this way" instead of "you did this to me." And from there it's much easier to work things out.

It also has the benefit of freeing you. Holding onto the narrative that someone hurt you with bad intent is exhausting. It's much lighter to assume that people do the best they can with what they have.

Assume ignorance before malice. You'll be wrong sometimes. But on average you'll be closer to the truth and you'll live with less resentment.
